## 1. Optimal Item Count

### Recommendation: 21 items (3 per behavioral domain)

### Reasoning

**The tension:** Coverage versus fatigue versus sensitivity.

This instrument is completed twice in a single training day — once before any content (when participants are likely skeptical and defensive), and once at the end (when they are tired from a full day). Both administrations must be quick enough that participants complete them honestly rather than pattern-filling.

**Research basis for 21 items:**

1. **Fatigue onset.** Research by Galesic and Bosnjak (2009) demonstrated that response quality degrades significantly after approximately 20-30 minutes of survey completion. At 15-20 seconds per item, 21 items yields 5-7 minutes — well within the quality window.

2. **Minimum per construct.** Measurement theory requires a minimum of 3 items per latent construct to achieve adequate internal consistency (Cronbach's alpha above 0.70). With 7 behavioral domains, 3 items each yields exactly 21 items.

3. **Sensitivity context premium on brevity.** When assessment content is ego-threatening, longer instruments produce *more* social desirability bias, not less. The participant's internal resistance accumulates item by item.

4. **Pre/post design doubles the burden.** The same 21 items will be answered twice. At 5-7 minutes per administration, total assessment burden is 10-14 minutes across the day.

**Why not fewer?** 14 items (2 per domain) provides insufficient within-domain reliability. A single random response out of two distorts the domain score by 50%. With 3 items, it distorts by 33%.

**Why not more?** 28 items creates an 8-10 minute instrument. In the defensive population at 9:00 AM, longer forms signal "they're testing us" rather than "reflect on yourself."

### Distribution Across Domains

| Domain | Items | Rationale |
|--------|-------|-----------|
| 1. Foundation awareness | 3 | Attitudinal/cognitive — important baseline |
| 2. Self-awareness | 3 | Metacognitive — items about noticing one's own patterns |
| 3. Empathy | 3 | Perspective-taking behaviors, active listening |
| 4. Daily interactions | 3 | Most concrete, most directly tied to complaints |
| 5. Health sensitivity | 3 | Most sensitive domain — handled carefully |
| 6. Feedback and conflict | 3 | Skill-oriented behaviors |
| 7. Team inclusion | 3 | Structural/systemic behaviors |
| **Total** | **21** | |

**Note on Domain 5 (Health sensitivity):** Three items covers the behavioral range (noticing discomfort, offering accommodation, maintaining dignity) without over-indexing on a topic that could trigger defensive shutdown if it dominates the instrument.

## 2. Item Wording — The Hard Part

### The Central Design Challenge

These participants know exactly why they are here. Women filed complaints about their behavior. The pre-assessment is literally the first thing they encounter. Every item will be read through a lens of: *"Is this accusing me of something?"*

Any item that feels like an accusation triggers one of two defensive responses:
1. **Overclaiming:** Rating themselves unrealistically high
2. **Dismissal:** Rating everything neutrally without engagement

### Wording Principles

**Principle 1: First-person behavioral specificity**

Every item must describe a concrete, observable behavior. Abstract attitudes are both harder to self-assess honestly and easier to fake.

| Bad (abstract) | Good (behavioral) |
|---|---|
| "I respect women in my team" | "When a woman on my team speaks in a meeting, I give her the same attention I give male colleagues" |
| "I am inclusive" | "I assign high-visibility tasks to women team members, not just routine work" |
| "I care about my team's wellbeing" | "When I notice a team member seems unwell, I ask if they need any support" |

The engineering mindset of this audience actually helps here. Engineers are comfortable with specific, measurable statements.

**Principle 2: Growth framing, not deficit framing**

Each item should describe a positive behavior the participant does to some degree, not a negative behavior they should stop.

| Deficit framing (avoid) | Growth framing (use) |
|---|---|
| "I don't talk over women in meetings" | "I make sure every team member gets a chance to finish their point before I respond" |
| "I avoid assigning only menial tasks to women" | "I distribute challenging assignments across my team regardless of gender" |
| "I don't ignore health-related requests" | "When a team member requests accommodation for health reasons, I respond promptly and supportively" |

**Principle 3: Universal subject where possible**

Where the behavior applies to all team members, frame items with "all team members" or "every person on my team" rather than "women employees." This positions inclusive behavior as good management, not special treatment.

Recommended ratio: **60% universal framing, 40% gender-specific framing.**

**Principle 4: Frequency-based, not agreement-based**

Items should describe behaviors occurring with measurable frequency.

| Agreement-based (avoid) | Frequency-based (use) |
|---|---|
| "I believe all team members deserve respect" | "I speak to all team members with the same level of courtesy and professionalism" |
| "I think health accommodations are important" | "When a team member appears in physical discomfort, I offer to adjust their work for the day" |

Agreement scales push toward ceiling effects. Almost everyone agrees with prosocial statements. Frequency scales create honest variance.

**Principle 5: Situational anchoring for difficult domains**

Embed the behavior in a concrete situation rather than stating it abstractly. This makes the item feel like a scenario description rather than a moral judgment.

| Abstract | Situationally anchored |
|---|---|
| "I accommodate women's health needs" | "If a team member is having a difficult day due to a health issue, I offer them a seat or lighter tasks without making a fuss" |
| "I check my biases" | "Before making a task assignment, I consider whether my choice might be influenced by assumptions about who can handle what" |

**Principle 6:** Avoid double-barreled items. Each item assesses exactly one behavior.

**Principle 7:** Items should be 15-25 words. Avoid jargon from diversity/inclusion discourse. Use plain workplace English.

## 3. Scale Anchors

### Recommendation: 5-point frequency scale

**1 = Almost Never | 2 = Rarely | 3 = Sometimes | 4 = Often | 5 = Almost Always**

### Why These Specific Anchors

**"Almost Never" / "Almost Always" instead of "Never" / "Always":**
Absolutes create ceiling/floor problems. Honest participants avoid "Always" because they know there have been exceptions. "Almost" gives reflective participants permission to use the extremes while maintaining honesty. Additionally, "Never" implies moral failure, triggering defensiveness.

**"Sometimes" as midpoint:** A genuine, honest midpoint. Many participants DO behave courteously sometimes and not others. We want to capture that truthfully.

**Why not agreement scales ("Strongly Disagree to Strongly Agree"):**
1. Agreement scales push toward ceiling effects with prosocial items.
2. Frequency scales match the behavioral specificity of the items.

**Why 5 points:**
- 4-point (forced choice): Increases social desirability bias in sensitive domains
- 6-7 point: Creates decision paralysis, especially at 4:45 PM; labels become fuzzy
- 5-point: International default for behavioral frequency scales; well-understood; adequate granularity

### Indian Engineering Context Considerations

1. **Numerical comfort.** Display both number and verbal label: "1 (Almost Never)" through "5 (Almost Always)."
2. **Visual layout.** Present horizontally with clear circles/boxes to fill in.
3. **Scale direction.** Left-to-right, low-to-high (1 on left, 5 on right). Do not alternate direction between items.
4. **Language.** The form remains English-only per project constraints. Facilitator may verbally provide Hindi equivalents of scale anchors.

## 4. Reverse-Scored Items

### Recommendation: Include exactly 3 reverse-scored items (out of 21 total, ~14%)

### What Reverse-Scored Items Do

A reverse-scored item describes a behavior that is the *opposite* of the desired direction. A high frequency rating on a reverse-scored item indicates a problem, not a strength. Responses are inverted (1→5, 2→4, etc.) before summing.

**Example:**
- Forward: "I give all team members equal opportunity to take on challenging work" (higher = better)
- Reverse: "I tend to assign lighter or simpler tasks to women team members" (higher = worse — reverse-scored)

### Why Include Them

1. **Acquiescence bias detection.** Some participants rate 4-5 on every item without reading carefully. A high rating on reverse items contradicts high ratings on forward items — flagging inattentive or defensive responding.
2. **Reflection disruption.** A run of similarly-framed items creates a response set. Reverse items break this pattern by requiring the participant to actually parse the statement.
3. **Facilitator diagnostic value.** A room where participants score high on both forward and reverse items tells the facilitator honest engagement hasn't occurred yet.

### Why Only 3

1. **Confusion risk.** Reverse-scored items are inherently confusing; some participants will misread them. Research shows they frequently produce worse model fit (van Sonderen et al., 2013).
2. **Negative framing exposure.** Each reverse item describes an undesirable behavior. In this sensitive context, every negatively-framed item risks triggering defensiveness.
3. **Proportion.** 14% is conservative but adequate for acquiescence checking without poisoning the overall growth-oriented tone.

### Placement Strategy

Distribute — do NOT cluster:
- One in the first third (items 5-7)
- One in the middle third (items 10-12)
- One in the final third (items 16-18)

Do not place reverse-scored items first or last.

### Which Domains

| Domain | Suitable? | Rationale |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Daily interactions | ✓ YES | Common default behaviors recognizable without ego-threat |
| Team inclusion | ✓ YES | Task allocation biases often unexamined; honest endorsement likely |
| Self-awareness | ✓ YES | Metacognitive "not reflecting" can work as time/habit issue |
| Health sensitivity | ✗ NO | Too ego-threatening; direct connection to the specific complaints |
| Empathy | ✗ NO | Reverse empathy items too stark; universally denied |

### On the Participant Form

The participant form contains **NO indication** of which items are reverse-scored. No asterisks, no different formatting, no footnotes.

The facilitator guide marks reverse items with **(R)** after the item number and includes a conversion table: 1→5, 2→4, 3→3, 4→2, 5→1.

### Mitigation for Confused Responses

Facilitator verbal instruction: "Please read each statement carefully. Some describe behaviors you might do frequently, others describe behaviors you might do rarely. There are no trick questions — just rate how often you actually do each behavior."

## 5. Behavioral Domains Structure

### Recommendation: Group items by domain with unlabeled section breaks

### The Tradeoff

**Grouping by domain (pro-reflection):** Helps participant think systematically about one area at a time; makes pre/post comparison meaningful within domains.

**Random ordering (pro-honesty):** Harder to game; prevents response sets within a domain.

### Why Grouping Wins in This Context

This is a self-reflection tool, not a high-stakes psychometric assessment. The participant keeps their own form. Nobody else sees it. Helping the participant reflect systematically within each domain is more valuable than making the instrument harder to game.

The pre/post comparison is most powerful when the participant can see their shift within specific domains: "I moved from 2 to 4 on the Daily Interactions items" is actionable. "My total score went from 55 to 72" is abstract.

### Implementation

Use visual separators (horizontal line or extra white space) between groups of 3 items. **Do NOT label sections** as "Section 3: Empathy" or "Health Sensitivity Items." This telegraphs what is being measured and can trigger defensive responding in sensitive sections.

The facilitator guide maps sections to domains. The participant form does not.

### Recommended Domain Ordering

Mirror the emotional arc of the course: start safe, build to sensitive, end with agency.

| Position | Domain | Rationale |
|----------|--------|-----------|
| 1st (Items 1-3) | Foundation awareness | Least threatening. Eases into behavioral self-assessment. |
| 2nd (Items 4-6) | Daily interactions | Concrete, tangible, relatively low-threat. Gets participants into the mode. |
| 3rd (Items 7-9) | Self-awareness | Introduces introspection. Personal but not accused-behavior territory. |
| 4th (Items 10-12) | Empathy | Asks participants to consider others' perspectives. Moderately challenging. |
| 5th (Items 13-15) | Feedback and conflict | Skill-oriented, feels like professional development not personal attack. |
| 6th (Items 16-18) | Health sensitivity | Most sensitive. By item 15, participant is warmed up. Not first (risk of shutdown), not last (feels like "gotcha"). |
| 7th (Items 19-21) | Team inclusion | Ends on a systemic, leadership-oriented note. Agency and empowerment framing. |

## 6. Scoring for Pre/Post Comparison

### Recommendation: Side-by-side column format on a single form

### Form Layout

The participant receives ONE physical form with three columns:

```
+------------------------------------------+-------+--------+
| Statement                                | Pre   | Post   |
|                                          | (AM)  | (PM)   |
+------------------------------------------+-------+--------+
| 1. I greet all team members by name      | [ ]   | [ ]    |
|    when the shift begins.                |       |        |
+------------------------------------------+-------+--------+
| 2. ...                                   | [ ]   | [ ]    |
+------------------------------------------+-------+--------+
```

### Why Single Form, Side-by-Side

1. **Immediate visual comparison.** The entire point of this instrument is for the participant to see their own shift. Side-by-side makes this comparison effortless and visceral.
2. **No lost forms.** With separate forms, participants may lose their pre-assessment during the day, making comparison impossible.
3. **Reduces re-reading time.** At the post-assessment, participants scan the item, see their pre-response, and evaluate current response — 3-4 minutes instead of 5-7.

### Critical Design Decision: Pre-Scores Visible During Post

**Yes. Deliberately.**

Some designers argue for hiding pre-scores to prevent anchoring. This concern is valid in a research context. But this is a self-reflection tool. The visible pre-score serves the learning objective: the participant sees they checked "2" on unconscious bias awareness, spent the day learning about it, and now honestly considers whether they have moved to 3 or 4. The visible discrepancy between pre and post IS the reflection.

**Risk mitigation for anchoring:** Facilitator instruction: "For the post-assessment, please rate yourself based on how you feel RIGHT NOW — not based on what you put this morning. It is completely fine to give the same rating, higher, or even lower. Honest reflection is what matters."

### The Self-Comparison Guide

At the bottom of the form:

```
------------------------------------------------------------
SELF-REFLECTION (complete after filling in the Post column)
------------------------------------------------------------

Look at both columns. For any item where your rating CHANGED:

1. Which domain showed your biggest shift?
   _________________________________________________

2. Which item surprised you the most?
   _________________________________________________

3. What ONE behavior will you focus on first when you
   return to the shop floor?
   _________________________________________________
```

This guided comparison feeds directly into Module 7 (Action Planning).

### Color Coding

- **Pre-column:** Standard black print, no shading
- **Post-column:** Light blue or grey shading

Prevents accidental marking in the wrong column.

### Facilitator Aggregate Data

Participants keep their forms — so how does the facilitator get aggregate data?

**Option A (Recommended): Verbal show of hands.**
"Without sharing your exact numbers, raise your hand if you noticed a shift of 2 or more points on any item in the Daily Interactions section." Provides rough aggregate data without surrendering forms.

**Option B: Anonymous tear-off strip.**
Perforated strip at the bottom: participants record total pre-score, total post-score, and largest single-item shift. Tear off and drop into a box.

**Option C: Facilitator observation only.**
No quantitative data collected. Facilitator reads the room through engagement level and action plan quality.

**Recommendation:** Option A primarily; Option B available if quantitative aggregate data is desired.

### Scoring Arithmetic (Optional Self-Scoring on Form)

```
OPTIONAL: Calculate your domain averages

Domain 1 (Items 1-3):  Pre avg: __ / Post avg: __
Domain 2 (Items 4-6):  Pre avg: __ / Post avg: __
Domain 3 (Items 7-9):  Pre avg: __ / Post avg: __
Domain 4 (Items 10-12): Pre avg: __ / Post avg: __
Domain 5 (Items 13-15): Pre avg: __ / Post avg: __
Domain 6 (Items 16-18): Pre avg: __ / Post avg: __
Domain 7 (Items 19-21): Pre avg: __ / Post avg: __

Total Pre: __ / 105    Total Post: __ / 105
```

**Note:** The participant form does NOT ask participants to reverse-score. Simple averages work for self-reflection purposes. Only the facilitator guide handles reverse scoring for aggregate analysis.

## 8. Cultural Deep-Dive: Indian Manufacturing Context

### 8.1 The Hierarchy Dynamic

Indian manufacturing operates within a steep hierarchical structure. Being asked to self-assess behaviors that subordinates complained about creates a hierarchy violation. Participants may over-rate themselves out of a deeply internalized belief that a person of their position *should* already behave correctly.

**Design mitigations:**
- Frame the instrument as a "management practices inventory" not a "behavioral assessment." "Inventory" implies self-knowledge; "assessment" implies judgment from above.
- Include items where high scores are genuinely aspirational for anyone (e.g., emotional self-regulation under pressure). This communicates that even excellent managers have room to grow.
- Facilitator opening: "This is not a test of whether you are a good or bad manager. It is a snapshot of where your management practices are today, so you can see your own growth by the end of the day."

### 8.2 Face-Saving (Izzat)

The concept of *izzat* (dignity/face/honor) means admitting behavioral failures in writing carries significant weight. If participants feel "caught," they will either over-rate defensively or disengage entirely.

**Design mitigations:**
- Never write an item that reads as a paraphrase of the known complaints. If the complaint was "he didn't offer a seat during my period," do NOT write "I offer seats to women who are menstruating." Instead, abstract the behavior: "If a team member appears unwell or in discomfort, I privately offer to adjust their tasks."
- The privacy guarantee must be emphatic and credible. Stated verbally, repeatedly, at each instrument transition.

### 8.3 Collective Identity

Indian workplace culture has collectivist dimensions. Two competing effects:
1. **Honesty can be contagious.** If a few participants visibly engage honestly, others may follow.
2. **Minimization can be contagious.** If the group's body language signals performative compliance, individuals pattern-match to the group's disengagement.

**Design mitigations:**
- The facilitator controls the collective tone through her opening framing and energy.
- U-shape seating (per requirements) naturally limits participants' ability to see each other's responses.
- Self-reflection questions should be completeable silently and individually, not discussed during completion.

### 8.4 Gender Dynamics with a Female Facilitator

A female consultant asking male supervisors to rate their behavior toward women is a loaded power dynamic. Some participants will performatively rate themselves highly to signal to the facilitator ("I'm one of the good ones"). Others may use low ratings as hostile compliance.

**Design mitigations:**
- The 60/40 universal-to-gender-specific ratio reduces the accusation feel. "Rate your management practices, some of which involve gender dynamics" is less threatening than "rate how good you are to women."
- The facilitator should hand out the form with minimal preamble and let participants fill it out in silence. Extended verbal introduction allows defensive framing to crystallize.

### 8.5 Engineering Mindset Interactions

**Positive:** Engineers engage seriously with well-structured numbered scales.

**Negative:** Engineers may approach the assessment as a problem to solve — selecting the "right" answer rather than reflecting honestly. The tendency toward solution-orientation means they may rate based on what they *intend* to do rather than what they *actually* do.

**Design mitigations:**
- Items must be phrased in past/current behavior: "I offer..." not "I would offer..." or "I am willing to offer..."
- Facilitator instruction: "Rate each statement based on what you have ACTUALLY DONE in the past month, not what you believe you would do."

### 8.6 The "Voluntary but Not Really" Dynamic

The training is listed as voluntary, but declining would be noticed. Participants who feel coerced are less likely to engage honestly.

**Design mitigations:**
- Physical design should feel personal and workbook-like, not corporate-compliance-like. Avoid logos, formal headers, or anything resembling an HR document.
- Recommended title: "My Management Practices — Self-Reflection" (not "Pre-Training Behavioral Assessment")
- Form header: "This is your personal reflection tool. Your responses are for your eyes only. There are no right or wrong answers."

### 8.7 Menstrual Health — The Elephant in the Room

The specific complaint about not offering seats during menstrual days is likely known to all participants. Directly mentioning menstruation on a 9 AM self-assessment form will trigger one of: extreme discomfort → avoidance, performative over-rating, or resentment.

**Design approach:**
- Use health-general language on the form: "appears unwell," "in discomfort," "health reasons."
- The course content (Module 5) educates about menstrual health explicitly. The assessment measures the behavioral outcome without requiring participants to engage with the taboo topic before they have the vocabulary and framework to process it.
- The post-assessment, completed AFTER Module 5, captures the shift. The participant who learned about menstrual health accommodations will now read the health-sensitivity item with full understanding of what it covers.

**This is sequencing, not avoidance.** The pre-assessment happens before Module 5. Asking participants to self-assess on menstrual health before the learning is unfair and counterproductive.

## 9. Additional Design Recommendations

### 9.1 Instrument Title and Header

**Recommended title:** "My Management Practices"
**Subtitle:** "A personal reflection tool — for your eyes only"
**Avoid:** "Assessment," "Evaluation," "Test," "Survey," "Questionnaire"

```
Name: _________________ (optional — you may leave this blank)
Date: _________________
Department: ____________ (optional)

This is a personal reflection tool to help you understand your
current management practices. You will complete it twice today --
once now and once at the end of the session.

There are no right or wrong answers. Rate each statement based
on what you ACTUALLY DO, not what you think you should do.
Your responses are completely private -- this form is yours to keep.
```

The "Name: optional" detail matters. Making the name optional signals genuine respect for autonomy. Most participants will write their name anyway, but the optionality communicates trust.

### 9.2 Facilitator Pre-Assessment Script

> "Before we begin today's content, I'd like you to take five minutes for a personal reflection exercise. You'll find a form in your workbook called 'My Management Practices.' This is your private reflection tool — I will not collect it, nobody else will see it, and you take it home with you today.
>
> Please read each statement and circle the number that best describes how often you actually do that behavior in a typical work week. Be honest with yourself — this is for YOUR benefit, to help you track your own growth by the end of today.
>
> A few notes: Rate what you ACTUALLY do, not what you think you should do. There are no right or wrong answers. Please rate every item.
>
> You have five minutes. Please work silently and independently."

### 9.3 Post-Assessment Framing

> "We've now reached the end of our learning together today. Please turn back to your 'My Management Practices' form. You'll see that the right column is still blank — that's your post-column.
>
> Please read each statement again and rate yourself based on how you feel RIGHT NOW. You can see your morning responses in the left column. It is completely fine to give the same rating, a higher rating, or even a lower rating. What matters is honesty.
>
> After you've completed the right column, please take a moment with the reflection questions at the bottom.
>
> You have five minutes."

### 9.4 Print Layout Specifications

- **Paper:** A4, front-and-back if needed
- **Font:** 11pt minimum for items, 10pt for instructions. Clean sans-serif (Calibri, Arial).
- **Column widths:** Statement = 65%, Pre = 15%, Post = 15%, margin = 5%
- **Row height:** Enough for 2 lines of text per item
- **Scale display:** Numbers 1-5 with circles to fill in
- **Post column shading:** Light grey or light blue background to visually distinguish
- **Section separators:** Thin horizontal rule between each group of 3 items. No section labels.
- **Bottom section:** Self-Reflection questions and optional scoring grid
- **No stapling:** If two-sided, fold or leave loose

### 9.5 What This Instrument Cannot Do

1. **Cannot measure actual behavior change.** Measures self-reported behavioral intention and self-awareness shift. A necessary precursor to behavior change, but not behavior change itself. Actual behavioral follow-up requires the 30/60/90-day instruments (v1.2 scope).

2. **Cannot identify specific individuals who need intervention.** Participants keep their forms. This is by design.

3. **Pre/post shifts include demand characteristics.** Some of the shift reflects social desirability ("I should score higher after training"). Unavoidable in single-day pre/post designs. The self-reflection questions help convert even socially desirable responses into genuine commitment.

4. **Domain subscale reliability is minimal (3 items minimum).** Adequate for individual self-reflection; not suitable for research-grade measurement.

## 10. Summary of All Recommendations

| Design Element | Recommendation | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Item count | 21 (3 per domain) | Minimum for within-domain reliability; 5-7 min per administration avoids fatigue |
| Scale | 5-point frequency (Almost Never to Almost Always) | Avoids ceiling effects of agreement scales; natural fit for behavioral items |
| Reverse items | 3 out of 21 (14%) | Catches acquiescence without overwhelming growth framing |
| Item grouping | By domain, unlabeled sections | Supports systematic reflection; visual separators without section labels |
| Domain order | Foundation → Daily → Self-awareness → Empathy → Feedback → Health → Team | Safe to sensitive to empowering |
| Form format | Single form, Pre/Post side-by-side columns | Enables visual self-comparison; prevents lost forms |
| Pre-score visibility | Visible during post-assessment | The visible delta IS the learning moment |
| Scoring | Optional self-scoring on form; no participant-facing reverse scoring | Keeps it simple; facilitator guide handles reverse scoring for aggregate |
| Aggregate data | Show-of-hands primary; optional anonymous tear-off secondary | Preserves trust while giving facilitator room-level data |
| Item framing | 60% universal, 40% gender-specific | Reduces accusation feel while addressing actual gender dynamics |
| Menstrual health language | Health-general on form; Module 5 content addresses specifics | Sequences sensitivity appropriately; pre-assessment is before Module 5 |
| Title | "My Management Practices" | Avoids loaded assessment language |

## 11. Open Questions for the Assessment Designer

1. **Pilot testing:** Can we arrange for 3-5 Indian manufacturing managers (outside the training group) to complete a draft and provide feedback on item clarity and emotional reaction? Cognitive interviewing would significantly improve item quality.

2. **Hindi supplementation:** Should the facilitator prepare Hindi paraphrases of each item for verbal clarification? Depends on English fluency comfort level of this specific participant group.

3. **Tear-off strip decision:** Does the facilitator want aggregate quantitative data? If yes, include anonymous tear-off. If not, omit it for a cleaner privacy message.

4. **Follow-up instruments (v1.2):** The 30/60/90-day follow-up should use the same 21 items, enabling longitudinal tracking. Design the current form with future reuse in mind — keep numbering consistent, don't change item wording between versions.

5. **Action plan integration:** The self-reflection questions at the bottom feed into Module 7. Coordinate with the action plan template designer to ensure reflection outputs flow naturally into the action plan format.
