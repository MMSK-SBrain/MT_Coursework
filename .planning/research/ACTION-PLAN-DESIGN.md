## 1. Theoretical Foundation

### Why This Design Matters

The action plan is the single most critical document for post-training behavior transfer. Research consistently shows that training without structured commitment planning yields near-zero behavioral change at 30 days. Three mechanisms make action plans effective:

**Implementation Intentions (Gollwitzer, 1999).** The "if-then" planning framework transforms abstract goals into automatic behavioral responses. When a person writes "When [situation], I will [behavior]" — and writes it in their own hand — the brain begins encoding the situation as a cue for the new behavior. Meta-analyses show implementation intentions approximately double the probability of goal attainment compared to motivation alone.

**Public Commitment Effect (Cialdini, 2001; Deutsch & Gerard, 1955).** When people make commitments publicly — especially in front of peers they will see again — the commitment becomes tied to identity, not just intention. The Commitment Circle after the action plan leverages this: participants have already written their plan privately (low threat), then share one commitment publicly (identity lock-in).

**Self-Concordance (Sheldon & Elliot, 1999).** Commitments that participants choose themselves (rather than being assigned) are 3-4x more likely to be pursued with sustained effort. The action plan must feel like the participant's own choice, not a compliance exercise.

### The Specificity Problem

The central design challenge: **left to their own devices, 80%+ of participants will write vague platitudes.** Examples from actual training evaluations in similar programs:

- "I will be more respectful" (vague — what does "more" mean? Respectful how?)
- "I will try to be inclusive" (hedged with "try," no observable behavior)
- "I will remember today's training" (not a behavioral commitment at all)

These fail because they provide no behavioral cue, no observable action, and no self-accountability mechanism. The template must **architecturally force specificity** through its structure while maintaining a developmental, empowering tone.

### Timing and Energy Considerations

Module 7 runs 4:45–5:00 PM. This is the end of a long, emotionally intense day. Design implications:

- Participants are fatigued. The template must be **simple to navigate** — no complex instructions.
- Energy is low for writing. **Structured fields with short answers** beat blank pages.
- But emotional openness may be high after a full day of reflection. The template should **capitalize on the emotional state** without demanding complex cognition.
- The 15-minute window is tight. Realistic expectation: 8-10 minutes writing, 5-7 minutes for the Commitment Circle. The template must be completable in 8-10 minutes by someone writing by hand.

### Cultural Context: Indian Manufacturing Male Managers

Several cultural factors shape the design:

- **Engineering mindset.** These participants are trained to think in structured, systematic ways. A structured template (fields, formulas, examples) will feel natural. Open-ended journaling will not.
- **Hierarchical workplace culture.** The participants may perceive the action plan as something being "done to them" by management. The framing must make clear this is FOR them, not FROM management.
- **Face and dignity.** The action plan must never feel like an admission of guilt. Framing: "I am choosing to grow" not "I admit I was wrong."
- **Reactive training context.** These men were sent here because women filed complaints. Some participants may be defensive, resistant, or resentful. The template cannot afford to trigger defensiveness. It must offer agency and choice.
- **Collectivist orientation.** Framing commitments in terms of "my team" and "my people" will resonate more than pure individualistic framing.

## 2. Template Structure Design

### Recommended Structure (7 Sections)

The template is a single-sided or double-sided A4 sheet (printable). Every section has a clear purpose.

---

#### Section 0: Header and Intent Statement

> **MY PERSONAL ACTION PLAN**
> *Inclusive Management for Shop Floor Supervisors*
>
> This plan is yours. It is not submitted to HR, your manager, or anyone else. It stays with you as a personal commitment to the kind of leader you want to be.
>
> Today you have reflected on how your daily actions shape your team's experience. This plan turns that reflection into specific actions you will take starting tomorrow.
>
> **Name:** __________________ **Date:** __________________
> **Department / Area:** __________________

**Design rationale:**
- "MY Personal Action Plan" — possessive language reinforces ownership.
- "This plan is yours" — immediately defuses anxiety about surveillance.
- "Not submitted to HR" — critical for honesty. Must be stated explicitly.
- "The kind of leader you want to be" — aspirational identity framing.
- "Starting tomorrow" — anchors commitments in immediate future, not vague "someday."
- Name field: the act of writing your name on a commitment document activates the consistency principle (Cialdini). It is a mild self-binding mechanism.

**Signature line:** YES, include. Research on behavioral contracts shows signing increases follow-through by approximately 20-30%. The participant is signing a commitment TO THEMSELVES, not a document for authority.

---

#### Section 1: Reflection Anchor (1-2 minutes)

> **Before you write your commitments, pause and think:**
>
> Think of ONE specific moment in the past month when you could have handled an interaction with a woman colleague differently. What happened? (Write 1-2 sentences — this is for your eyes only.)
>
> _____________________________________________________________
> _____________________________________________________________
>
> What would you do differently now?
>
> _____________________________________________________________

**Design rationale:**
- Forces a concrete memory before abstract planning.
- "Past month" narrows the window — prevents "I can't think of anything."
- "For your eyes only" — lowers defensiveness.
- "What would you do differently NOW" — bridges reflection to commitment.
- Two lines, not a paragraph box. Signals brevity — this is not an essay.

---

#### Section 2: Behavioral Commitments (5-6 minutes) — THE CORE

**The Commitment Writing Formula** (printed at the top of this section):

> **How to write a strong commitment:**
>
> Use this structure: **"When [specific situation], I will [specific action] instead of [old habit]."**
>
> Example: *"When I see a woman colleague standing while I am seated in a meeting, I will offer my seat or arrange an additional chair, instead of assuming she is fine."*

**Each commitment slot contains these four fields:**

> **Commitment #[1/2/3]**
>
> **The situation I am addressing:**
> (What specific workplace moment does this commitment relate to?)
> _____________________________________________________________
>
> **When this situation happens, I will:**
> (What specifically will I DO? Describe the action someone could observe.)
> _____________________________________________________________
> _____________________________________________________________
>
> **Instead of my old pattern of:**
> (What did I tend to do before, or what will I stop doing?)
> _____________________________________________________________
>
> **I will know I am succeeding when:**
> (What will be different? How will I check myself?)
> _____________________________________________________________

**Design rationale for the four sub-fields:**

1. **"The situation I am addressing"** — Forces a trigger/cue. Without a cue, the commitment floats unanchored. Implementation intention research shows the cue is the most critical element.

2. **"When this situation happens, I will"** — Forces an observable behavior. The prompt "something someone could observe" prevents attitudinal commitments like "I will feel more empathy." Observable = accountable.

3. **"Instead of my old pattern of"** — Requires honest self-reflection about current behavior. Contrasting old and new behavior makes the change concrete and memorable. Implicitly acknowledges that change is happening FROM something TO something, not from nothing.

4. **"I will know I am succeeding when"** — Creates a self-evaluation criterion. Without this, participants have no way to check their own progress. This is the self-accountability mechanism.

**Why three commitments minimum, not one or five:**
- One is too easy to make trivially.
- Five is too many for 8 minutes and creates shallow commitments.
- Three is the sweet spot: enough to cover different behavioral areas, few enough to write with care.

**Optional fourth slot:**

> **Bonus Commitment (optional):**
> When _________________________, I will _________________________
> instead of _________________________.

---

#### Section 3: Behavioral Area Coverage Check (30 seconds)

> **Quick check — my commitments cover:** (tick all that apply)
>
> [ ] How I greet and interact daily
> [ ] Health / comfort accommodations
> [ ] How I give feedback
> [ ] How I listen to my team
> [ ] How I assign tasks fairly
> [ ] How I respond when others are disrespectful
> [ ] How I make my team feel welcomed
>
> *Aim for at least 2-3 different areas across your commitments.*

**Design rationale:** A nudge, not a requirement. Prevents the common failure mode where a participant writes three variants of "I will say good morning" and ignores harder behavioral areas.

---

#### Section 4: Commitment Circle Preparation (1 minute)

> **For the Commitment Circle:**
>
> Choose ONE commitment from above that you are willing to share with the group. Pick the one that matters most to you personally.
>
> **The commitment I will share:** (circle: #1 / #2 / #3 / Bonus)
>
> **In one sentence, my commitment is:**
> _____________________________________________________________
>
> *Tip: Start with "Starting tomorrow, I will..." and keep it to one clear action.*

**Design rationale:**
- "Willing to share" — respects agency. Participants choose what to make public.
- "Matters most to you personally" — not the easiest or most performative, but the most meaningful.
- The one-sentence field forces distillation. Verbal commitments that are too long lose impact.
- "Starting tomorrow" anchors temporal immediacy.

---

#### Section 5: Peer Commitments (During Commitment Circle)

> **What my colleagues committed to:**
>
> | Name | Their Commitment |
> |------|-----------------|
> |      |                 |
> |      |                 |
> |      |                 |
> |      |                 |
> |      |                 |

**Design rationale:** Recording peers' commitments creates mutual accountability. When a participant sees a colleague doing (or not doing) what they committed to, it reinforces the culture of change.

---

#### Section 6: My 2-Week Self-Check (Post-Training)

> **2-Week Self-Check** *(Put this date in your phone calendar now: ____________)*
>
> Two weeks from today, look at this plan and honestly answer:
>
> | Commitment | Did I follow through? | What happened? |
> |------------|----------------------|----------------|
> | #1 | Yes / Partly / No |                |
> | #2 | Yes / Partly / No |                |
> | #3 | Yes / Partly / No |                |
>
> **If I followed through:** What made it possible? How can I sustain it?
> _____________________________________________________________
>
> **If I did not:** What got in the way? What will I do differently?
> _____________________________________________________________
>
> **My updated commitment going forward:**
> _____________________________________________________________

**Design rationale:**
- "Put this date in your phone calendar NOW" — behavioral nudge executed in the room. The facilitator should explicitly ask participants to set a phone reminder. This is the single most important design feature for post-training use.
- Yes / Partly / No — simple, honest, non-judgmental.
- "Updated commitment" — acknowledges that plans evolve. Not a pass/fail.

---

#### Section 7: Signature and Commitment Statement

> **My commitment to myself:**
>
> I, __________________, choose to be a more inclusive leader. These are my personal commitments. I make them not because I was told to, but because I want my team to thrive.
>
> **Signature:** __________________ **Date:** __________________

**Design rationale:**
- "Choose to be" — agency language.
- "Not because I was told to" — directly addresses the reactive training context. Reframes the action from compliance to choice. Psychologically critical for this audience.
- "My team to thrive" — collectivist, outcome-oriented framing.

---

### Physical Layout Recommendation

**Format:** Single A4 sheet, double-sided.
- **Front:** Sections 0–2 (Header, Reflection, Three Commitment Slots)
- **Back:** Sections 3–7 (Coverage Check, Commitment Circle Prep, Peer Commitments, 2-Week Check, Signature)

**Typography:** Clean, professional, not clinical. Adequate line spacing for handwriting. Box or line fields, not blank white space.

**Visual tone:** Professional development document, not a worksheet. No clip art. This is a document for experienced engineering managers.

## 3. Scaffolding Prompts — The Most Important Design Element

### The Commitment Writing Formula

**Formula:** "When [specific situation], I will [specific observable action] instead of [old pattern]."

This is based on implementation intention research (Gollwitzer & Sheeran, 2006). The three-part structure works because:
- **"When [situation]"** creates a behavioral trigger/cue
- **"I will [action]"** specifies the replacement behavior
- **"Instead of [old pattern]"** creates contrast that makes the change salient

### Three Model Examples (Printed on Template)

> **Examples of strong commitments:**
>
> *"When I see a woman team member standing during a shift meeting while chairs are available, I will offer the seat or bring an additional chair, instead of assuming she does not need one."*
>
> *"When I need to give corrective feedback to a woman employee, I will call her to my office for a private conversation and start with what she is doing well, instead of correcting her in front of the team on the shop floor."*
>
> *"When I am assigning overtime or physically lighter tasks, I will rotate assignments based on skill and turn, instead of automatically assigning 'easier' tasks to women and 'harder' tasks to men."*

### Situation-Identification Prompts (Facilitator Only — Not Printed on Template)

**Prompt 1 — The Replay Prompt:**
"Think of a time in the last two weeks when a woman on your team seemed uncomfortable, frustrated, or withdrew from a conversation. What was happening?"

**Prompt 2 — The Routine Prompt:**
"Think about your daily routine — morning meeting, shift handover, lunch break, task assignments. Where in your routine do you interact with women colleagues? What typically happens?"

**Prompt 3 — The Bystander Prompt:**
"Think of a time you saw another supervisor behave rudely or dismissively toward a woman employee. What did you do? What could you have done?"

**Prompt 4 — The Reverse Prompt:**
"If a woman on your team were filling out this form about how she wished her supervisor would act, what would she write?"

**Prompt 5 — The Module Recall Prompt:**
"Looking back at today's sessions — which topic made you think, 'Yes, I do that' or 'I need to work on this'? Start your commitment there."

### The Specificity Ladder (Facilitator Coaching Tool)

| If participant writes... | Facilitator asks... |
|--------------------------|---------------------|
| "I will be more respectful" | "Respectful how? In what situation? What would someone see you doing?" |
| "I will be polite to women" | "When specifically? Morning greeting? Feedback? Meetings? Pick one moment." |
| "I will try to offer seats" | "Remove 'try.' When exactly will you offer? What's the trigger?" |
| "I will listen better" | "What does better listening look like? What will you DO differently?" |
| "I will treat everyone equally" | "That's a great goal. What's ONE specific action that would show equal treatment?" |

## 4. Good vs Poor Commitments — Quality Spectrum

### Rating Levels Defined

**Level 4 — Excellent:** Specific situation + specific observable behavior + contrast with old pattern + self-check criterion. Another person could observe whether it is being followed.

**Level 3 — Good:** Specific situation + specific behavior. Missing either the old-pattern contrast or the success criterion, but the core commitment is clear and actionable.

**Level 2 — Weak:** Has some specificity but remains largely attitudinal or uses hedge words ("try," "more," "better"). Could not be objectively observed by another person.

**Level 1 — Poor:** Platitude, generic statement, or shows no personal reflection. Could have been written by anyone without attending the training.

### Examples Across Three Behavioral Areas

#### Area A: Daily Greetings and Professional Courtesy

| Level | Example | Why This Rating |
|-------|---------|-----------------|
| **4 - Excellent** | "When I arrive at the shop floor each morning, I will greet every team member by name — including women operators — with 'Good morning, [name]' and eye contact, instead of only greeting the male supervisors and walking past the women's stations." | Specific trigger, specific action, specific contrast, observable. |
| **3 - Good** | "I will make it a point to greet the women on my team every morning when I arrive at the shop floor." | Specific timing and action. Missing contrast and success criterion, but actionable. |
| **2 - Weak** | "I will be more polite to everyone on my team." | No specific situation, no specific action. "More polite" is not observable. |
| **1 - Poor** | "I believe in treating all people with respect." | A belief statement, not a behavioral commitment. |

#### Area B: Health Accommodations (Menstrual Needs)

| Level | Example | Why This Rating |
|-------|---------|-----------------|
| **4 - Excellent** | "When a woman team member requests to sit down or take a short break, I will immediately say 'Of course, take the time you need' without asking for a reason, instead of questioning why or making her feel she needs to justify it." | Specific trigger, specific verbal response, specific contrast, addresses dignity. |
| **3 - Good** | "When a woman on my team looks unwell or tired, I will proactively offer her a chair or suggest she take a 10-minute break." | Specific trigger and action. Proactive. Missing contrast. |
| **2 - Weak** | "I will try to be more sensitive to women's health needs." | "Try," "more sensitive," and "health needs" are all vague. No observable action. |
| **1 - Poor** | "I now understand about menstrual health." | A knowledge statement, not a commitment. |

#### Area C: Fair Task Allocation

| Level | Example | Why This Rating |
|-------|---------|-----------------|
| **4 - Excellent** | "When assigning weekly tasks, I will use a rotation chart visible to the whole team so everyone takes turns on all tasks, instead of automatically giving documentation work to women and machine tasks to men." | Specific mechanism (rotation chart), specific task types, specific contrast, includes accountability tool. |
| **3 - Good** | "I will review my task assignment sheet every Friday to check if I have been distributing work fairly between men and women on my team." | Specific timing, mechanism, and criterion. Missing contrast, but has a self-check. |
| **2 - Weak** | "I will give equal opportunities to women employees." | No specific action, no timing, no mechanism. "Equal opportunities" is abstract. |
| **1 - Poor** | "Women can do any job a man can." | An opinion statement. No personal commitment to behavior change. |

## 5. Facilitator Evaluation Rubric

### Design Principles

The facilitator circulates during Module 7 (approximately 8 minutes of writing time). The rubric must:
1. Be usable **at a glance** — the facilitator is scanning plans while walking around a room.
2. Focus on **coaching**, not grading — the purpose is to improve commitments in real-time.
3. Be **warm and developmental** in tone — the facilitator is a supportive coach, not an examiner.

### The Rubric: Two Dimensions, Four Levels

**Dimension 1: SPECIFICITY — Can I picture this happening?**

| Rating | Description | Facilitator Sees... |
|--------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Strong** | Commitment names a specific situation, a specific action, and contrasts with old behavior. You can visualize the moment. | Filled-in trigger, clear action verb, "instead of" field completed |
| **Developing** | Commitment has a situation OR an action but not both. Partially specific. | One or two fields filled well, others vague or empty |
| **Vague** | Commitment is about attitudes, feelings, or general intentions. Cannot be observed. | Abstract language ("be more," "try to," "always") |

**Dimension 2: DEPTH — Does this reflect real self-awareness?**

| Rating | Description | Facilitator Sees... |
|--------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Strong** | Commitment connects to a real personal situation. The "old pattern" feels honest, not performative. | Personal detail, honest admission in "instead of" field, reflection section filled meaningfully |
| **Developing** | Commitment is reasonable but generic. Could apply to anyone in the room. | Correct-sounding commitments that lack individual specificity |
| **Vague** | Commitment feels copy-pasted from the examples or is clearly written to "get through the exercise." | Near-verbatim copies of template examples, or single-word answers |

### The Coaching Decision Matrix

| Specificity | Depth | Facilitator Action |
|-------------|-------|--------------------|
| Strong | Strong | Affirm: "This is powerful. I can see you really thought about this." Move on. |
| Strong | Developing | Light nudge: "Good specific actions. Is there a real moment that prompted this?" |
| Developing | Strong | Coach specificity: "I can see this matters to you. Let's make it more concrete — when exactly will you do this?" |
| Developing | Developing | Coach both: "This is a good start. Can you tell me about a specific time this came up? And what exactly will you do differently?" |
| Vague | Any | Direct coaching intervention (see prompts below). Spend 60-90 seconds. |

### Coaching Questions for Vague Commitments (Sequence)

1. **"Tell me about this one."** (Open — lets the participant explain verbally. Often the verbal version is more specific than what they wrote.)
2. **"When did this last happen?"** (Anchors to a real event.)
3. **"What would I see you doing differently?"** (Forces observable behavior.)
4. **"Can you rewrite this as: When [situation], I will [action]?"** (Provides the formula directly.)

**Tone guidance:**
- Crouch or sit beside the participant, not standing over them.
- Use "this is a good start" before any suggestion to improve.
- Never say "this is too vague." Instead: "Let's make this even more powerful."
- If a participant seems resistant, respect their process: "Take your time. You can always refine this after the circle."

### When to Intervene vs. Let the Participant Struggle

**Intervene when:**
- A participant has been staring at a blank form for more than 2 minutes (stuck, not thinking).
- All three commitments are Level 1 (the participant is not engaging).
- A participant is copying template examples verbatim.
- A participant asks for help.

**Let them struggle when:**
- A participant is writing slowly but thoughtfully (deep processing).
- A participant pauses between commitments (selecting what matters).
- A commitment is Level 2 but shows genuine effort.
- A participant crosses things out and rewrites (self-correction is working).

### Facilitator Quick-Scan Checklist (During Circulation)

For each participant's plan, mentally check:
- [ ] Reflection anchor (Section 1) has specific content (not blank or one word)
- [ ] At least 2 of 3 commitments are Level 3+ (Good or Excellent)
- [ ] "Instead of" field is filled in at least twice (shows self-awareness)
- [ ] Commitment Circle selection (Section 4) is marked
- [ ] Coverage check (Section 3) shows 2+ areas ticked

If 3+ of these are missing, prioritize coaching this participant.

## 6. Commitment Circle Design

### Purpose and Mechanics

The Commitment Circle transforms private written commitment into public verbal commitment. Two psychological mechanisms:

1. **Social accountability:** Others heard what you said. You are accountable to peers.
2. **Identity statement:** Saying "I will..." aloud in front of others makes the commitment part of your public identity.

### Format Recommendation

**Physical setup:** Participants stand in a circle (if room permits) or remain seated in a U-shape. Standing is preferred — it adds formality and physical commitment.

**Facilitator opens:**
> "Each of you has written your personal commitments. Now I invite you to share ONE — the one that matters most to you. This is not about impressing anyone. It is about saying out loud what you are committing to. When you share, start with 'Starting tomorrow, I will...' Keep it to one clear sentence."

**Format:** Go around the circle. Each person shares one commitment. No commentary from others. Facilitator says "Thank you, [name]" after each one. No applause, no questions — keep it solemn and respectful.

**Time estimate:** For 15-20 participants, 15-20 seconds each = 4-7 minutes.

### How to Choose Which Commitment to Share

Communicate to participants:
> "Choose the commitment that feels most personal to you. Not the easiest one. Not the one that sounds best. The one that, if you actually follow through, would make the biggest difference to your team."

### Verbal Phrasing Template

The action plan includes in Section 4:

> **In one sentence, my commitment is:**
> "Starting tomorrow, I will _________________________________."

The "starting tomorrow" anchor prevents "I will always..." (too abstract) and "I will try..." (too hedged). It grounds the commitment in immediate action.

### Facilitator Closure

> "Thank you. Every commitment in this room is a step toward the kind of workplace where everyone — every person on your team — feels respected and valued. Your plan stays with you. Check it in two weeks. Hold yourselves to it. Hold each other to it. You are the leaders who shape your shop floor culture every single day."

## 7. Post-Training Self-Use

### The Core Problem: Plans Get Filed Away

Within 48 hours, the training fades. Within a week, old behaviors reassert themselves. Three design features prevent this.

### Feature 1: The Phone Calendar Nudge (Most Important)

During Module 7, the facilitator explicitly says:
> "Take out your phone. Set a calendar reminder for two weeks from today, [specific date]. Title it 'Check my action plan.' Do it now."

This is the single highest-leverage post-training design feature. The template includes a blank: *"Put this date in your phone calendar now: ____________"*

### Feature 2: Wallet-Card Commitment

Include a small perforated section at the bottom (or a separate small card):

> **MY COMMITMENT** (tear off and keep in your wallet/phone case)
>
> Starting tomorrow, I will: _________________________________
>
> Date: __________ Signed: __________

A business-card-sized commitment that lives in a wallet creates daily visual reinforcement.

### Feature 3: Physical Design That Invites Re-Reading

- **Quality paper stock** — not flimsy photocopy paper. Heavier cardstock communicates "this matters."
- **Clean design** — the document should look like something worth keeping.
- **The participant's own handwriting** — handwritten commitments are recalled more vividly than typed text. The physical act of writing creates stronger memory traces.

### What NOT to Include

- **Do NOT include a manager-sharing requirement.** The moment the plan becomes something shared with a boss, honesty drops to zero.
- **Do NOT include a "report back" mechanism.** This shifts from self-accountability to external accountability, undermining the psychological contract.
- **Do NOT include a scoring rubric for self-evaluation.** The check-in is reflective, not evaluative.

## 8. Full Sample Action Plan: Rajan

### Participant Profile

**Name:** Rajan Krishnamurthy | **Age:** 38 | **Role:** Production Supervisor, Assembly Line B
**Experience:** 10 years manufacturing, 7 years as supervisor | **Team:** 24 operators (8 women, 16 men)
**Context:** Competent technically; received informal feedback that women on his team feel he is "cold." Never formally complained about, but knows colleagues who have. Entered training cautiously — not hostile but skeptical.

---

> **MY PERSONAL ACTION PLAN**
>
> **Name:** Rajan K. **Date:** 17 Feb 2026 **Department / Area:** Assembly Line B, Unit 3

---

> **Before you write your commitments:**
>
> *Last Tuesday, Meena came to my desk and said she had a headache and asked if she could sit in the break room for 10 minutes. I said "okay" but I was annoyed and I think she could tell. I didn't ask if she was alright.*
>
> What would you do differently now?
>
> *I would say "of course, take the time you need" and ask if she needs anything, instead of acting irritated.*

**FACILITATOR NOTE:** Excellent reflection. Specific moment, honest about his emotional response ("annoyed"), identifies both verbal and emotional change needed. This participant is engaged. This reflection will naturally flow into a strong Commitment #1.

---

> **Commitment #1**
>
> **The situation:** *When a woman team member asks for a break or says she is not feeling well*
>
> **I will:** *Immediately say "Of course, take what you need" with a normal tone. If she is on the line, I will arrange cover without making it a big deal. I will check on her after 15 minutes — just a quick "feeling better?"*
>
> **Instead of:** *Saying "okay" in an irritated voice, or looking annoyed, or making her feel like she is creating a problem for the line*
>
> **I will know I am succeeding when:** *Women on my team ask for breaks without looking nervous. Right now I can see they hesitate before asking me.*

**FACILITATOR ASSESSMENT — Level 4 (Excellent)**
Specific trigger, specific verbal response, specific follow-up action, specific old behavior contrast. Success criterion shows genuine empathy and awareness. Coaching needed: None. Affirm.

---

> **Commitment #2**
>
> **The situation:** *Morning shift start when I walk onto the floor*
>
> **I will:** *Greet all operators on my line, including the women, by name. Not just the men I usually talk to first.*
>
> **Instead of:** *Going straight to the men team leads and only talking to women operators if there is a work issue*
>
> **I will know I am succeeding when:** *I can name all 8 women on my team without thinking. Right now I know only 4-5 names properly.*

**FACILITATOR ASSESSMENT — Level 3 (Good)**
Specific timing, specific action, specific contrast. Success criterion shows honest self-awareness. Optional coaching opportunity: "Do you have a natural route you walk when you enter the floor? Could you plan your greeting route to start from the women's stations?" Would elevate to Level 4, but Level 3 is strong enough.

## 9. Implementation Notes

### For Template Production

1. **Page count:** 2 pages (1 sheet, double-sided)
2. **Paper:** 120gsm or heavier. Communicates "this matters."
3. **Print:** Black and white is fine.
4. **Font:** 11pt body, 13pt headers. Minimum 8mm line height for handwriting.
5. **Field design:** Lines for short answers, small boxes for longer responses.
6. **Wallet card:** Include perforated business-card-sized tear-off at the bottom of page 2.

### Timing Script for Module 7

| Time | Activity | Facilitator Action |
|------|----------|--------------------|
| 4:45 | Introduction | Distribute plans. Read intent statement aloud. Explain "this is yours, not HR's." (1 min) |
| 4:46 | Reflection anchor | "Take 1 minute to think about that one moment..." (1 min) |
| 4:47 | Commitment writing | "Now write your three commitments. I'll come around if you want help." (6 min) |
| 4:53 | Coverage check + Circle prep | "Check the back of your form. Choose one commitment for the circle." (1 min) |
| 4:54 | Phone calendar reminder | "Take out your phone right now. Set a reminder for [date]." (1 min) |
| 4:55 | Commitment Circle | Each person shares. "Starting tomorrow, I will..." (5 min) |
| 5:00 | Closure | Facilitator closing statement. Signature. End. |

### Critical Success Factors

1. **The facilitator MUST read the intent statement aloud.** If participants think this is collected by HR, honesty drops to zero.
2. **The facilitator MUST circulate during writing time.** Without coaching, 30-40% of commitments will be Level 1-2. With coaching, under 10%.
3. **The phone calendar reminder MUST happen in the room.** "Set a reminder later" means no one will.
4. **The Commitment Circle MUST happen standing** (if physically possible). Standing adds solemnity.
5. **Examples on the template MUST be culturally relevant.** Indian workplace scenarios, names, and dynamics. Not Western examples.

### Potential Failure Modes and Mitigations

| Failure Mode | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|--------------|------------|------------|
| Participants rush through, write vague commitments | HIGH (without coaching) | Facilitator circulation + coaching questions |
| Participants copy template examples verbatim | MEDIUM | Facilitator spots this, prompts personalization |
| Participants refuse to share in Commitment Circle | LOW | Frame as invitation. Have 1-2 willing participants go first. |
| Time runs out before all commitments written | MEDIUM | Prioritize quality of 2 over quantity of 3. Announce "2 more minutes" at 4:52. |
| Plan gets lost/discarded after training | HIGH | Phone calendar reminder + wallet card + quality paper stock |
| Defensive participant writes hostile/sarcastic responses | LOW | Reframe: "What kind of leader do you want your team to describe you as?" |
