## 1. Scenario Count and Time Architecture

### The Core Constraint

60 minutes total. Two activities must fit: individual response AND group debrief. The debrief is where growth happens — it cannot be rushed. The individual response phase is merely the setup for the debrief.

### Time Budget Analysis

| Activity | Time per scenario | Notes |
|----------|-------------------|-------|
| Read scenario + think | 2 minutes | Engineers read fast; vignettes should be concise |
| Select answer | 1 minute | Decisive audience; don't overthink individually |
| **Individual total** | **3 minutes** | Per scenario |
| Facilitator reveals best answer | 1 minute | Quick announcement with brief rationale |
| Group debrief/discussion | 5-7 minutes | The heart of the learning |
| **Debrief total** | **6-8 minutes** | Per scenario |
| **Full cycle per scenario** | **9-11 minutes** | Combined |

### Recommended: 5 Scenarios

**Total time at 5 scenarios:**
- Individual phase (all 5 at once): 15 minutes
- Debrief phase (5 scenarios x 8 min avg): 40 minutes
- Buffer/intro/wrap: 5 minutes
- **Total: 60 minutes**

**Why 5, not fewer or more:**

| Count | Pros | Cons | Verdict |
|-------|------|------|---------|
| 3 | Deep debrief per scenario (12-15 min each) | Covers only 3 of 8 topic areas; feels too thin | Too few |
| 4 | Good debrief time (10 min each) | Still misses significant topic coverage | Viable but suboptimal |
| **5** | **Covers 5 of 8 topics; 8 min debrief each; manageable cognitive load** | **Slightly less debrief depth than 4** | **Recommended** |
| 6 | Covers 6 of 8 topics | Only 6 min debrief each — too rushed for behavioral reflection | Too many |
| 7-8 | Covers everything | Quiz fatigue; debrief becomes token; cognitive overload at 3:45 PM | Far too many |

### Execution Model: Answer All First, Then Debrief All

**Do NOT debrief each scenario immediately after answering it.** Instead:

**Phase 1 — Individual Response (15 minutes):**
- Participants receive printed scenario booklet (all 5 scenarios)
- They read and answer all 5 independently
- No discussion, no conferring
- Facilitator circulates but does not help

**Phase 2 — Sequential Debrief (40 minutes):**
- Facilitator goes through scenarios one by one
- Reveals best answer, walks through rationale
- Opens floor for discussion, questions, pushback
- Manages time strictly (8 minutes per scenario average, with flexibility)

**Phase 3 — Score and Reflect (5 minutes):**
- Participants self-score using answer key on screen/board
- Brief quiet reflection moment
- Transition to Module 7 (Action Planning)

**Why this model works:**
1. **Uncontaminated responses** — Group discussion of Scenario 1 would bias answers to Scenarios 2-5
2. **Efficient** — No repeated context-switching between individual and group modes
3. **Protects psychological safety** — Nobody sees others' answers during individual phase
4. **Facilitator control** — Debrief pace is entirely in facilitator's hands

## 2. Scenario Structure — The Anatomy

### The Template

Each scenario follows a rigid 4-part structure. Consistency reduces cognitive load — by Scenario 3, participants know exactly what to expect and can focus on the content, not the format.

```
SCENARIO [N]: [Short Title]

[Setting — 1-2 sentences establishing WHERE and WHO]
[Context — 1-2 sentences establishing WHAT HAS BEEN HAPPENING]
[Trigger — 1-2 sentences establishing WHAT JUST HAPPENED RIGHT NOW]

As [Role], what is the BEST course of action?

A) [Option A]
B) [Option B]
C) [Option C]
D) [Option D]
```

### Component Specifications

#### Setting (1-2 sentences, 15-30 words)
Establishes the physical location and the people involved. Must feel immediately recognizable.

**Good:** "You are the shift supervisor in the assembly section. Your team of 14 includes three women operators who joined six months ago."

**Bad:** "You work in a manufacturing company where diversity and inclusion are important values." (Too abstract, too corporate-speak)

**Rules:**
- Name the specific area (assembly line, quality lab, tool room, packaging section)
- Name the protagonist by role ("you are the shift supervisor"), not abstractly
- Mention team composition only if relevant to the scenario
- Use present tense

#### Context (1-2 sentences, 20-40 words)
Establishes what has been happening — the background tension or pattern. This is where the scenario gets its texture.

**Good:** "Over the past week, Priya, one of your operators, has seemed quieter than usual. She completed her tasks but avoided the team lunch area and left immediately at shift end."

**Bad:** "One of your employees has been having personal problems that are affecting her work." (Vague, assumes too much)

**Rules:**
- Use observable behaviors, not interpretations ("seemed quieter" not "was upset")
- Keep the protagonist (participant) in the observer role — they are noticing something
- The context should create enough information to make the trigger meaningful
- Include a named character (always a real Indian name, with role/designation)

#### Trigger (1-2 sentences, 20-35 words)
The immediate event that demands a response. This is the "what do you do NOW?" moment.

**Good:** "During the morning toolbox meeting, Priya asks if she can sit on the bench near the wall instead of standing with the rest of the team. Two male operators exchange glances."

**Bad:** "Priya asks for special treatment." (Loaded language; not behavioral)

**Rules:**
- A specific, concrete event — not a vague situation
- Something that requires an immediate response from the participant
- Include social dynamics (who else is watching, what's the group dynamic)
- The trigger should create mild tension — the right answer shouldn't be instantly obvious

#### Total Vignette Length
**Target: 60-90 words per vignette.** This is approximately 3-5 sentences total across Setting + Context + Trigger.

**Why this length:**
- Engineers dislike padding — they scan for the relevant data
- At 3:45 PM after 6 hours of training, reading stamina is low
- Shorter vignettes force precision, which makes the scenario feel more realistic
- 60-90 words can be read in under 30 seconds by this audience
- Enough space for authentic detail without becoming a reading comprehension test

#### Question Stem
Always the same: **"As [specific role], what is the BEST course of action?"**

**Why "BEST" not "correct":**
- "Correct" implies there is one objectively right answer and everything else is wrong — this triggers the engineer's binary thinking
- "BEST" acknowledges that multiple approaches have merit, but one is demonstrably better — this mirrors real management judgment
- "BEST" opens the debrief to discuss WHY it is better, not just THAT it is right
- "BEST" language is standard in Situational Judgment Tests (SJTs) across organizational psychology

**Why "course of action" not "response":**
- "Course of action" implies doing something deliberate, not just reacting
- It frames the participant as an agent, not a reactor
- It signals that management requires proactive choices

## 3. Answer Options Design — The Critical Part

### Four Options (Not Three, Not Five)

**Recommendation: 4 options per scenario.** This is a firm recommendation.

| Count | Assessment impact | Practical impact |
|-------|-------------------|------------------|
| 3 | 33% random chance; too easy to eliminate one and guess | Feels too simple for engineers |
| **4** | **25% random chance; standard for SJTs; enough to create meaningful gradation** | **Fits the page well; manageable to debrief** |
| 5 | 20% random chance; better discrimination | Takes longer to read; harder to write 5 truly distinct options; debrief becomes unwieldy |

### The Option Gradation Model

This is the most critical design decision in the entire assessment. The four options are NOT "one right + three wrong." They are a **behavioral gradation** — a spectrum from best to worst.

**The four-tier model:**

| Tier | Label | Description | Points |
|------|-------|-------------|--------|
| **Best** | The inclusive, professionally skilled response | Demonstrates the behavior the course teaches. Balances empathy with professionalism. Is neither passive nor aggressive. | 3 |
| **Adequate** | The decent but incomplete response | Shows good intent but misses a key element — maybe addresses the surface issue but not the underlying dynamic. Many participants will choose this. | 2 |
| **Poor** | The well-intentioned but counterproductive response | Feels like it might be right because it comes from a caring place, but actually causes harm through paternalism, public embarrassment, or overstepping. This is the critical distractor. | 1 |
| **Harmful** | The response that perpetuates the problem | Not cartoonishly evil — it is the "business as usual" response that feels normal in the current culture but is exactly the behavior the training aims to change. | 0 |

### Why This Gradation Matters

**The "Adequate" option (Tier 2) is the teaching tool.** Most participants will choose either Best or Adequate. The debrief discussion centers on: "Why is the Best answer better than the Adequate one? What does the Adequate answer miss?" This is where real behavioral insight happens.

**The "Poor" option (Tier 3) reveals paternalism and over-correction.** In gender sensitivity training, a common failure mode is swinging from rudeness to overprotection. Options like "Immediately excuse Priya from all standing tasks for the rest of the week" feel caring but are paternalistic and singling out. Participants who choose these options need the debrief to understand the difference between accommodation and infantilization.

**The "Harmful" option (Tier 4) should NOT be cartoonishly awful.** It should be the normalized behavior — the thing these managers were doing before the training that felt completely normal to them. This is important: if the worst option is obviously terrible ("Yell at her in front of everyone"), nobody picks it and it teaches nothing. The worst option should be something like "Tell her that everyone stands during toolbox meetings — there are no exceptions" — a response that sounds fair and rule-based but ignores the accommodation need.

### Distractor Design Principles

**Principle 1: Every wrong answer should feel defensible to someone.**
If a participant cannot articulate why they picked a wrong answer, the option is poorly designed. Every distractor should have a logic that makes sense from a particular worldview — the debrief unpacks that worldview.

**Principle 2: Wrong answers should reflect real reasoning patterns.**
Common reasoning patterns to embed in distractors:
- "Rules are rules — everyone must be treated exactly the same" (equality vs equity confusion)
- "I should protect her by giving her lighter work" (paternalistic benevolence)
- "This is a personal matter; I should not get involved" (avoidance/discomfort)
- "I will handle it quietly later" (avoids public action but delays necessary accommodation)
- "I need to address this in front of the team so everyone learns" (uses a person as a teaching example)
- "I will ask her directly what is wrong" (probing into private health matters publicly)

**Principle 3: The "best" answer should not always be the "nicest" answer.**
Sometimes the best answer involves setting a boundary, having a direct conversation, or making a decision that feels uncomfortable. If the best answer is always "be the most empathetic person possible," participants learn to game the test by picking the warmest-sounding option. Build in scenarios where the best answer involves professional firmness, or where over-empathy is actually the trap.

**Principle 4: Randomize the position of the best answer.**
Across 5 scenarios, the best answer should appear in different positions (A, B, C, D). Do not let a pattern form. A common SJT design flaw is clustering best answers in the B/C positions.

### Option Length and Format

- Each option: 15-30 words (one to two sentences)
- Begin with an action verb ("Tell...", "Ask...", "Approach...", "Announce...")
- Do not include option-internal reasoning ("Tell her to sit down because everyone deserves comfort") — the reasoning is for the debrief, not the option
- Keep all four options approximately the same length — a noticeably longer or shorter option is a psychometric tell

## 4. Difficulty and Sequencing

### Sequencing Strategy: Warm-Up, Core, Complex

For 5 scenarios, the sequence is:

| Position | Difficulty | Purpose | Characteristics |
|----------|-----------|---------|-----------------|
| Scenario 1 | **Easy** | Build confidence, establish format | Clear best answer; harmful option is obviously poor; debrief is quick and energizing |
| Scenario 2 | **Easy-Medium** | Deepen engagement | Best answer is clear but Adequate distractor is very tempting |
| Scenario 3 | **Medium** | Core challenge | Best and Adequate answers are close; requires nuanced judgment |
| Scenario 4 | **Medium-Hard** | Maximum stretch | Paternalism trap is strong; best answer feels counterintuitive |
| Scenario 5 | **Medium** | Consolidation | Integrates multiple concepts; clear best answer but requires applying several course ideas together |

### Why NOT End on the Hardest Scenario

Ending on the hardest scenario leaves participants feeling confused or defeated heading into Module 7 (Action Planning). The last scenario should feel consolidating — "I can apply what I have learned" — not destabilizing.

The hardest scenario is Position 4. This creates a pattern:
```
Confidence -> Engagement -> Challenge -> Peak Difficulty -> Resolution
```
This mirrors a narrative arc. It also matches attention science — attention peaks mid-session and dips at the end.

### Topic Sequencing Across 5 Scenarios

**Recommended topic selection and order:**

| # | Topic | Why this position | Course situation # |
|---|-------|-------------------|--------------------|
| 1 | Daily greeting / professional interaction | Easy entry point; everyone has this experience daily | #4 |
| 2 | Health accommodation (menstrual needs) | Central to the training trigger; moderate difficulty | #1 |
| 3 | Giving feedback to a woman employee | High skill requirement; medium difficulty | #2 |
| 4 | Responding when a male peer is rude to a woman | Hardest — requires intervening with a peer, not a subordinate; power dynamics shift | #8 |
| 5 | Fair task allocation | Consolidation — systemic fairness integrates multiple concepts | #6 |

**Topics NOT selected (and why):**
- #3 (Handling conflict when a woman pushes back) — overlaps significantly with #2 (feedback)
- #5 (Recognizing discomfort) — woven into Scenario 2 naturally
- #7 (Public vs private feedback) — woven into Scenario 3 naturally

## 5. The Debrief Guide

### Debrief Structure Per Scenario

For each scenario, the facilitator guide provides a structured debrief in this exact format:

```
SCENARIO [N] DEBRIEF — [Title]
Time allocation: [X] minutes

BEST ANSWER: [Letter]
[Full rationale — 3-4 sentences explaining WHY this is the best response.
Connects back to specific course concepts taught earlier in the day.]

WHY NOT [Letter — Adequate option]:
[2-3 sentences. "This is a reasonable response, but it falls short because..."]
Key teaching point: [One sentence the facilitator can say out loud]

WHY NOT [Letter — Poor option]:
[2-3 sentences. "This might feel caring, but it actually..."]
Key teaching point: [One sentence]

WHY NOT [Letter — Harmful option]:
[2-3 sentences. "This is the normalized response we are trying to change.
It feels fair because... but it actually..."]
Key teaching point: [One sentence]

DISCUSSION QUESTIONS (pick 1-2, not all):
1. [Question that invites personal reflection]
2. [Question that connects to participants' real workplace]
3. [Question that challenges the "adequate" answer specifically]

WATCH FOR IN THE ROOM:
- [Defensive behavior to anticipate and how to redirect]
- [Common justification to listen for]
- [Breakthrough signal — what indicates real insight is happening]

FACILITATOR NOTES:
[Any cultural sensitivity notes, things to avoid saying, ways to validate
struggle without accepting the harmful behavior]
```

### Debrief Time Allocation Across 5 Scenarios

| Scenario | Debrief Time | Rationale |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| 1 (Greeting) | 6 minutes | Easy — quick win, brief discussion |
| 2 (Health accommodation) | 10 minutes | Central issue, likely most discomfort, needs space |
| 3 (Feedback) | 8 minutes | Skill-based, good for exploring technique |
| 4 (Peer behavior) | 10 minutes | Hardest scenario, most defensiveness expected |
| 5 (Task allocation) | 6 minutes | Consolidation, shorter but connecting |
| **Total** | **40 minutes** | |

### Facilitation Techniques for Debrief

**The "Show of Hands" Method:**
Before revealing the best answer, ask: "How many chose A? B? C? D?" This makes the distribution visible and normalizes that people disagree. It also gives the facilitator data on where the group's judgment clusters.

**The "Devil's Advocate" Reframe:**
When discussing why the Adequate answer is not the Best: "I know many of you chose B, and it is a good instinct. But let me push on this — what does B miss that A addresses?"

**The "Real Story" Bridge:**
After the rationale, ask: "Has anyone faced a situation like this? What did you actually do?" This grounds the abstract scenario in lived experience.

**The "No Shame" Frame:**
Before the debrief begins, the facilitator should say: "There are no trick questions here. Every option was designed to reflect real thinking that real managers have. The goal is not to catch you out — it is to sharpen your judgment for when these situations happen on Monday morning."

## 6. Scoring for Self-Accountability

### Scoring Model: Weighted, Not Binary

**Recommendation: Use the 0-1-2-3 point scale per scenario, NOT binary right/wrong.**

**Why not binary (right/wrong):**
- Binary scoring punishes the participant who chose "Adequate" the same as the one who chose "Harmful"
- In behavioral assessment, there IS a meaningful difference between "close but not quite" and "completely wrong"
- Binary creates a pass/fail mindset; weighted scoring reveals a profile
- A participant who scores 10/15 (chose "Adequate" on most) has a very different development need than one who scores 3/15

**Scoring per option:**

| Tier | Points | What it means |
|------|--------|---------------|
| Best | 3 | You applied the inclusive management framework effectively |
| Adequate | 2 | You showed good instinct but missed a key element |
| Poor | 1 | Your intent was good but the approach would cause unintended harm |
| Harmful | 0 | This is the pattern we are working to change |

**Total possible: 15 points (5 scenarios x 3 points)**

### Score Interpretation Bands

| Score | Band | Framing Language |
|-------|------|-----------------|
| 13-15 | Strong foundation | "Your judgment aligns well with inclusive management principles. Your challenge is consistency — applying this every day, not just when you are thinking about it." |
| 9-12 | Growing awareness | "You are on the right track. The gaps in your score show where your instincts need sharpening — these are your specific growth areas for the Action Plan." |
| 5-8 | Developing | "You showed honesty in your responses. Some of your choices reflect patterns that were normal in your workplace until now — the training today gave you a new lens. Your Action Plan should target these specific areas." |
| 0-4 | Starting point | "This score tells you where you are starting from, not where you will stay. The fact that you are in this room doing this work matters. Your Action Plan is especially important — focus on 1-2 specific changes." |

### How the Score is Delivered

1. Facilitator displays the answer key on screen/projector (or reads aloud)
2. Participants self-score on their own answer sheet
3. Participants total their own score using interpretation bands on the sheet
4. **No public announcement of scores. Ever.**

The facilitator should explicitly say: "Your score is yours alone. I will not ask what you got. Your company will not see it. This is for you — it shows you where your growth edges are, which feeds directly into the Action Plan we are building next."

## 7. Three Full Sample Scenarios

### SCENARIO 2: The Morning Seating Request (Health Accommodation)
**Difficulty:** Easy-Medium | **Position:** 2 of 5 | **Topic:** Menstrual accommodation

---

**Setting:** You are Rajesh, the shift supervisor of the stamping section at an auto-components plant. Your section has 18 operators, including four women.

**Context:** It is 7:15 AM. You are conducting the daily toolbox talk. All operators are standing in a semicircle near the whiteboard as usual. Sunita, a press operator with three years of experience and consistently good output, is standing at the back, leaning against the pillar. She looks pale and is pressing her hand against her lower abdomen.

**Trigger:** Sunita quietly asks, "Sir, can I sit on the stool during the meeting today? I am not feeling well." Two male operators nearby glance at each other.

**As the shift supervisor, what is the BEST course of action?**

A) Ask Sunita what is wrong and whether she needs to go to the medical room before deciding whether to let her sit.

B) Tell Sunita that toolbox talks are only 10 minutes and it is important for everyone to stand together to maintain team discipline.

C) Tell the group that anyone who needs to sit during toolbox talks can always pull up a stool, and gesture to Sunita to grab one.

D) Tell Sunita she can sit on the stool, and continue the toolbox talk normally without drawing further attention to it.

---

#### ANSWER KEY — Scenario 2

**BEST ANSWER: C (3 points)**

**Full rationale:** Option C normalizes accommodation without singling Sunita out. By making the announcement to the whole group — "anyone who needs to sit can always pull up a stool" — Rajesh creates a standing policy rather than a one-time exception. This protects Sunita's privacy (he does not ask why she needs to sit), maintains her dignity (she is not receiving "special treatment"), and establishes an inclusive norm that benefits everyone. This is the principle of universal design applied to workplace micro-interactions.

**Why not D (2 points — Adequate):**
"This is a decent response — it grants the accommodation immediately, which is good. But it frames this as a one-time favor to Sunita specifically, which means she has to ask again next time, and every other woman who needs this has to make her own individual request. It does not solve the systemic issue."
*Key teaching point:* "Good managers solve the problem once for everyone, not one-at-a-time for each person who asks."

**Why not A (1 point — Poor):**
"This feels caring — you want to know if she needs medical attention. But publicly asking 'what is wrong' puts Sunita in a position where she must either disclose a private health matter in front of 17 colleagues or lie. If she is menstruating, she should not have to explain that to her male supervisor in front of the team to earn the right to sit down."
*Key teaching point:* "Asking 'what is wrong' in front of others forces disclosure. Accommodation should not require explanation."

**Why not B (0 points — Harmful):**
"This is the 'rules are rules' response. It sounds fair — everyone stands, no exceptions. But equal treatment is not the same as equitable treatment. When a team member is in physical discomfort and requests a minor accommodation, denying it for the sake of 'discipline' prioritizes optics over people. This is the exact behavior pattern that generated the complaints that led to this training."
*Key teaching point:* "Fairness is not treating everyone identically — it is ensuring everyone can participate fully."

**DISCUSSION QUESTIONS (pick 1-2):**
1. "What stops us from making 'you can always sit if you need to' a permanent team norm? What are we actually afraid of?"
2. "Think about your own toolbox talks. Has something like this happened? What did you do?"
3. "Some of you may have chosen D. That is a good instinct. But what is the difference between solving a problem for one person and solving it for the team?"

**WATCH FOR IN THE ROOM:**
- **Defensiveness about "discipline":** Some participants may argue that allowing sitting undermines authority. Redirect: "Is your authority built on people standing, or on their respect for your leadership?"
- **"But what if everyone starts sitting?":** Address directly: "If your entire team needs to sit, maybe the format needs rethinking. But in practice, people stand when they can and sit when they need to."
- **Breakthrough signal:** A participant says "I never thought about making it a general rule instead of a one-off thing."

**FACILITATOR NOTES:**
Do not assume participants know the scenario implies menstruation. Some may genuinely read it as a general illness scenario — that is fine, the correct answer is the same regardless of cause. If a participant asks "But how do we know it is her period?", respond: "You do not. And you do not need to. The accommodation is the same whether it is menstrual pain, a headache, or a bad knee. That is the point."

### SCENARIO 3: The Quality Report Feedback (Professional Feedback to a Woman Employee)
**Difficulty:** Medium | **Position:** 3 of 5 | **Topic:** Giving feedback professionally

---

**Setting:** You are Vikram, a production supervisor at a consumer electronics assembly plant. Meera, a quality inspector on your line, has been in the role for one year.

**Context:** Meera submitted her weekly quality report yesterday. You found two significant errors — she miscounted the rejection figures for one batch and left another section incomplete. The report goes to the plant manager every Monday. This is the second time in a month that Meera's report has had errors. You know Meera is generally careful, and her inspection work on the floor is solid.

**Trigger:** It is 9:30 AM. Meera is at her workstation reviewing today's batch samples. Three other inspectors are working at adjacent stations. You need to address the report errors before the Monday submission deadline at 11 AM.

**As the production supervisor, what is the BEST course of action?**

A) Walk to Meera's station and quietly ask her to step into the small meeting room for five minutes, then show her the specific errors and ask her to correct them before 11 AM.

B) Send Meera a text message listing the errors and asking her to fix and resubmit the report by 10:30 AM.

C) Walk to Meera's station and point out the errors at her desk so she can fix them quickly, keeping your voice low so others do not overhear.

D) Ask one of the senior male inspectors to review and correct Meera's report, since the deadline is tight and you want to ensure accuracy.

---

#### ANSWER KEY — Scenario 3

**BEST ANSWER: A (3 points)**

**Full rationale:** Option A follows the principle of private feedback with personal respect. Asking Meera to step into the meeting room creates a private space where she can receive the feedback without being overheard by colleagues. Showing her the specific errors treats her as a professional who can self-correct. Giving her the 11 AM deadline is clear and actionable. This is direct, respectful, professional, and efficient.

**Why not C (2 points — Adequate):**
"This shows good instinct — Vikram is going to her directly, keeping his voice low. But 'keeping your voice low' in an open workstation area is not the same as privacy. Other inspectors will notice the supervisor leaning over Meera's desk. In a shop floor environment, everyone notices these interactions. The visual alone signals 'Meera made a mistake' to her peers, even if they cannot hear the words."
*Key teaching point:* "Privacy means a private space, not just a quiet voice. What your team sees matters as much as what they hear."

**Why not B (1 point — Poor):**
"Text messaging avoids public embarrassment entirely, which is good intent. But it removes the human element of feedback completely. Feedback about repeated errors deserves a conversation, not a text. Meera may read the message as cold, or may have questions she cannot ask via text."
*Key teaching point:* "Feedback is a relationship act, not an information transfer. The medium matters."

**Why not D (0 points — Harmful):**
"This bypasses Meera entirely. Having a male colleague fix her work without telling her what was wrong denies her the chance to learn from the error, undermines her professional standing, and signals that when a woman makes a mistake, a man needs to fix it."
*Key teaching point:* "Correcting someone's work behind their back is not help — it is erasure. She cannot grow from mistakes she never learns about."

**DISCUSSION QUESTIONS (pick 1-2):**
1. "Be honest: When you have feedback for a woman employee, do you give it the same way you would to a male employee? What changes, if anything?"
2. "Some of you may have chosen C — going to her desk and keeping your voice down. What is the real difference between that and a private conversation?"
3. "Why do we sometimes avoid giving women direct feedback? What are we afraid of? And what happens to her career if we never give her honest feedback?"

**WATCH FOR IN THE ROOM:**
- **"I treat everyone the same":** Probe gently: "Same words, same setting, same directness? Or do you soften it?"
- **Deflection to "But the deadline":** Acknowledge the constraint, then redirect: "How long does it take to say 'Meera, can you step in here for 5 minutes'?"
- **Breakthrough signal:** A participant admits "I actually avoid giving tough feedback to women on my team."

**FACILITATOR NOTES:**
This scenario subtly tests whether participants treat women as professionals who can handle direct feedback. The paternalism trap is Option D — the instinct to "fix it for her." Be prepared for the argument that "women get more upset when you give feedback" — address directly: "That is a stereotype, and even if a particular individual is sensitive to feedback, the answer is to improve how you deliver it, not to stop delivering it."

### SCENARIO 5: The Heavy Lifting Rotation (Fair Task Allocation)
**Difficulty:** Medium | **Position:** 5 of 5 | **Topic:** Fair task allocation

---

**Setting:** You are Anil, the section in-charge of the packaging department at a pharma manufacturing unit. Your section has 12 operators — four women and eight men. The work involves both machine operation (moderate physical effort) and end-of-line packing (heavy lifting, carrying 15-20 kg cartons to the palletizing area).

**Context:** You have been scheduling the weekly task rotation. Until now, you have always assigned the four women to machine operation only, and rotated the heavy packing among the eight men. Nobody has complained. Last week during the training session, the trainer discussed "benevolent bias" — the idea that protecting women from challenging work can limit their growth and signal that they are less capable.

**Trigger:** It is Monday morning. You are preparing this week's rotation chart. Kavitha, one of the women operators, approaches you and says, "Sir, I noticed that we are never assigned to the packing station. I have done this work before at my previous company. I would like the chance to rotate through all stations like everyone else."

**As the section in-charge, what is the BEST course of action?**

A) Include Kavitha in the full rotation starting this week, and announce to the team that going forward, all operators — regardless of gender — will rotate through all stations based on capability, with appropriate safety supports available to everyone.

B) Tell Kavitha you appreciate her initiative and will include her in the packing rotation this week on a trial basis to see how it goes.

C) Explain to Kavitha that the packing work involves heavy lifting and you were trying to be considerate, but tell her you will think about it and discuss with your manager first.

D) Tell Kavitha that the current arrangement works fine and nobody has complained, so there is no need to change the rotation system.

---

#### ANSWER KEY — Scenario 5

**BEST ANSWER: A (3 points)**

**Full rationale:** Option A addresses both the individual request and the systemic problem. By opening the rotation to all operators based on capability (not gender) and providing safety supports for everyone, Anil fixes the system, not just one person's complaint. The announcement normalizes this as a professional operational decision, not a favor to Kavitha. This also applies the "benevolent bias" concept taught earlier in the training — translating classroom learning to real action.

**Why not B (2 points — Adequate):**
"This responds positively to Kavitha and gives her what she asked for. But framing it as a 'trial basis' implies doubt — it says 'let me see if you can handle it,' which is a test that male operators never had to pass. It also does not address the systemic issue: the other three women are still excluded."
*Key teaching point:* "A 'trial basis' for something male employees do every week without question is not equal treatment — it is a conditional invitation."

**Why not C (1 point — Poor):**
"Anil says he was 'being considerate,' which is the textbook definition of benevolent bias — assuming women need protection from work they have not asked to be protected from. Suddenly needing management approval only when a woman asks to be included signals that her request is unusual or risky."
*Key teaching point:* "If you would not need permission from your manager to add a new male operator to the rotation, you do not need permission for a female operator either."

**Why not D (0 points — Harmful):**
"'Nobody has complained' is the defense of every unfair system. The women may not have complained because they did not feel empowered to. Kavitha IS requesting change right now — in the most professional way possible — and this option dismisses her."
*Key teaching point:* "Absence of complaints is not evidence of fairness. It may be evidence that people have given up on fairness."

**DISCUSSION QUESTIONS (pick 1-2):**
1. "Think about your own section's task assignments. Are there tasks you only assign to men or only to women? Why? Is it about actual capability, or assumption?"
2. "The concept of 'benevolent bias' came up today. Can you think of a time you were 'being considerate' in a way that might have actually limited someone's opportunity?"
3. "What would it take to implement a truly gender-neutral rotation in your section? What are the real barriers versus the imagined ones?"

**WATCH FOR IN THE ROOM:**
- **"But the lifting IS heavy — women WILL get hurt":** Address directly: "Are your male operators getting hurt? If so, the issue is ergonomic safety, not gender. Proper lifting aids and training should be available to everyone."
- **"I was trying to be nice":** Validate the intention, challenge the impact: "Your instinct to protect comes from a good place. But when protection becomes exclusion, the impact contradicts the intent."
- **Breakthrough signal:** A participant says "I just realized I have been doing exactly this."

**FACILITATOR NOTES:**
This scenario deliberately references course content (benevolent bias) from earlier in the day. The facilitator can call this out explicitly: "Remember the discussion about benevolent bias this morning? This is what it looks like in practice."

Note on heavy lifting: The scenario specifically says Kavitha has done this work before AND includes "appropriate safety supports" in the best answer. The facilitator should not dismiss legitimate safety concerns — redirect them: "Safety is solved with equipment, training, and protocols — not with gender-based exclusion."

## 8. Indian Shop Floor Authenticity Guide

### Names and Roles

Use names that are common, pan-Indian, and recognizable across regions. Avoid names exclusively associated with one caste, religion, or linguistic group.

**Recommended male names (supervisors/managers):**
Rajesh, Vikram, Anil, Suresh, Manoj, Sanjay, Deepak, Prakash, Ajay, Karthik

**Recommended female names (operators/inspectors):**
Sunita, Meera, Kavitha, Priya, Lakshmi, Divya, Anita, Rekha, Swathi, Pooja

**Roles and designations (actual Indian manufacturing titles):**
- Shift Supervisor / Shift In-Charge
- Section In-Charge / Production Supervisor
- Quality Inspector / Press Operator / Machine Operator
- Assembly Operator / Packaging Operator
- Plant Manager (off-stage authority figure)

**Forms of address:**
- Subordinates to supervisor: "Sir" (universal on Indian shop floors)
- Supervisor to operator: First name is standard
- The facilitator guide should note: The use of "Sir" reflects actual workplace hierarchy, not a problem to fix

### Physical Environment Details

| Element | Authentic detail |
|---------|-----------------|
| Morning meeting | "Toolbox talk" — daily 10-15 min stand-up at shift start |
| Workspace | Stations (not desks); adjacent stations (not cubicles) |
| Break area | Canteen/pantry (not "break room") |
| Timing | General shift: 8/8:30 AM–5/5:30 PM |
| Documentation | Quality reports, batch records, rotation charts |
| Hierarchy | Operator → Shift Supervisor → Section In-Charge → Department Manager → Plant Manager |
| Formality | "Sir/Madam" upward; first names downward |

### Cultural Context Notes for Scenario Writers

1. **Menstruation is still a taboo topic** in many Indian workplaces. Design scenarios so the correct response works WITHOUT requiring the supervisor to name or discuss menstruation.

2. **"Being considerate" toward women is seen as virtuous.** Benevolent bias is culturally reinforced. Scenarios must honor this intent while challenging the impact.

3. **Engineering mindset means systems thinking.** Frame best answers in terms of systems and efficiency, not just empathy. "Because it prevents recurring problems" is more compelling than "because it is the right thing to do."

4. **"What will the team think?" is a genuine concern.** Address head-on in debriefs. The answer is normalization through universal policies rather than individual exceptions.

5. **Seniority matters.** A 45-year-old supervisor being told by a 30-year-old female consultant that his behavior needs to change is a sensitive dynamic. The facilitator guide must acknowledge this without apologizing for the content.

## Appendix D: Scenario Writing Checklist (for remaining 2 scenarios)

For Scenario 1 (Daily greeting) and Scenario 4 (Peer rudeness), follow these principles:

1. **Vignette length:** 60-90 words (3-5 sentences)
2. **Structure:** Setting + Context + Trigger + Question stem
3. **Options:** 4 per scenario, graded 3-2-1-0 (Best-Adequate-Poor-Harmful)
4. **Option length:** 15-30 words each, starting with action verb, roughly equal length
5. **No cartoonish villains:** The 0-point option should feel "normal" (pre-training normal)
6. **The 2-point option is the teaching tool:** Most participants will cluster between 3 and 2
7. **Authentic details:** Real job titles, real meeting types, real plant areas
8. **"Sir" is okay:** It reflects reality, not a problem to fix
9. **The question is always:** "As [role], what is the BEST course of action?"
10. **Debrief > Test:** The assessment exists to fuel discussion; the score is secondary
