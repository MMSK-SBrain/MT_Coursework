## Story Scenario

### Part 1: The Customer Complaint - 10 minutes
**"The Oil Pressure Warning Light"**

**Date:** Thursday morning, 9:30 AM
**Location:** Tata Motors Authorized Service Center, Pune
**Service Advisor:** Calls Ravi to service desk

**Customer:** Mrs. Deshmukh, Tata Nexon 1.5L diesel, 45,000 km
**Complaint:** "Oil pressure warning light comes on at idle after engine warms up. Light goes off when I rev the engine or drive. I'm worried the engine will be damaged."

**Service History Check:**
- Last oil change: 5,000 km ago (40,000 km service)
- Oil type used: 5W-30 synthetic (correct specification)
- No previous oil pressure complaints
- Regular scheduled maintenance (customer is conscientious)

Ravi reviewed the complaint and nodded professionally. "Mrs. Deshmukh, you're right to bring this in immediately. Low oil pressure can cause serious engine damage. Let me perform a thorough inspection and diagnosis. I'll call you with my findings in 2-3 hours."

**Ravi's Initial Thoughts:**

He walked to the vehicle bay and pulled up the technical specifications on his tablet:
- **Tata Nexon 1.5L Diesel**
- **Oil Pressure Specification:**
  - Idle (900 RPM, hot): Minimum 1.0 bar (14 psi)
  - 3000 RPM: Minimum 3.5 bar (50 psi)
- **Oil Capacity:** 5.5 liters with filter
- **Oil Type:** 5W-30 or 5W-40 diesel engine oil

**Possible Causes (Ravi's Mental Checklist):**
1. Low oil level (simple check first)
2. Wrong oil viscosity (too thin when hot)
3. Worn oil pump (can't generate pressure)
4. Stuck pressure relief valve (releases pressure prematurely)
5. Blocked oil pickup screen (restricts flow to pump)
6. Worn engine bearings (excessive clearance allows pressure loss)
7. Faulty oil pressure sensor/switch (false warning)

**Ravi's Approach:**
"I'll start with the simple checks—oil level, condition, and sensor verification. Then I'll measure actual oil pressure with a mechanical gauge. If pressure is truly low, I'll need to diagnose the cause: pump, relief valve, pickup screen, or bearings."

### Part 2: Initial Inspection and Oil Pressure Testing - 20 minutes
**"Verify the Problem First"**

**Step 1: Visual Inspection**

Ravi opened the hood and began his systematic inspection:

**Oil Level Check:**
- Engine cold (hasn't been started yet today)
- Pulled dipstick, wiped clean, reinserted fully, pulled again
- **Reading:** Between MIN and MAX marks (OK)
- **Oil color:** Dark brown/black (normal for diesel with 5,000 km since change)

**Oil Condition Visual Check:**
- Rubbed oil between fingers: Smooth texture, no grittiness (no metal contamination detected)
- Smell test: Normal diesel engine oil smell, no fuel smell (no fuel dilution)
- Held dipstick at angle in sunlight: No milky appearance or droplets (no coolant contamination)

**External Inspection:**
- No oil leaks visible at oil filter, pan gasket, or valve cover
- Oil pressure sensor location identified (near oil filter housing)

**Step 2: Verify Warning Light Operation**

Ravi started the engine:
- Cold idle (initial startup): Warning light OFF (oil pressure OK when cold)
- Let engine warm to operating temperature (10 minutes, coolant temp 90°C)
- Hot idle: Warning light **ON** (pressure dropped below switch threshold)
- Increased RPM to 2000: Warning light **OFF** (pressure increased)

**Conclusion:** Warning light behavior matches customer complaint. Not a false alarm. Actual low oil pressure at hot idle.

**Step 3: Mechanical Oil Pressure Test**

Ravi retrieved the oil pressure gauge kit from the tool cabinet. This would give him the actual pressure reading, not just the on/off signal from the warning light switch.

**Equipment:**
- Mechanical oil pressure gauge (0-10 bar / 0-150 psi range)
- Adapter fittings (match sensor thread—M10x1.0 for Nexon)
- Thread sealant tape
- Rag and drain pan

**Test Procedure:**

**1. Engine off, cool down 5 minutes** (reduce burn risk)

**2. Locate oil pressure sensor** (near oil filter on Nexon)

**3. Disconnect electrical connector** from sensor

**4. Unscrew oil pressure sensor** (12mm wrench, catch oil drip with rag)

**5. Install mechanical gauge adapter:**
   - Wrap threads with 2-3 layers of sealant tape
   - Screw adapter into sensor port (hand-tight plus 1/4 turn)

**6. Connect mechanical gauge** to adapter

**7. Route gauge hose away from hot exhaust** and moving belts

**8. Start engine and test:**

**Cold Idle (1st Minute After Start):**
- RPM: 900
- Coolant temp: 40°C
- Oil pressure: **3.2 bar** (46 psi) — Good

**Hot Idle (After 10 Minutes):**
- RPM: 900
- Coolant temp: 90°C
- Oil pressure: **0.6 bar** (8.5 psi) — **LOW** (spec: minimum 1.0 bar)

**2000 RPM (Hot):**
- RPM: 2000
- Oil pressure: **2.8 bar** (40 psi) — Acceptable

**3000 RPM (Hot):**
- RPM: 3000
- Oil pressure: **3.8 bar** (55 psi) — Good (spec: minimum 3.5 bar)

**Ravi's Analysis:**

**Problem Confirmed:** Oil pressure is definitely low at hot idle (0.6 bar vs 1.0 bar minimum). However, pressure is acceptable at higher RPM.

**What This Tells Ravi:**
- Oil pump **can** generate pressure (3.8 bar at 3000 RPM proves pump isn't completely failed)
- Pressure loss at idle suggests:
  - Worn pump (reduced efficiency at low RPM)
  - Pressure relief valve stuck open (releasing pressure prematurely)
  - Excessive bearing clearance (oil escaping through worn bearings faster than pump can supply at low RPM)
  - Blocked pickup screen (restricting flow at low pump speed)

**Next Step:** Remove oil pan and inspect oil pump, pickup screen, and pressure relief valve.

### Part 3: Oil Pan Removal and Inspection - 25 minutes
**"Finding the Root Cause"**

**Oil Pan Removal Procedure:**

Ravi lifted the Nexon on the 4-post hoist and positioned himself under the vehicle.

**1. Drain engine oil:**
   - Positioned drain pan (10-liter capacity)
   - Removed drain plug (19mm socket)
   - Allowed oil to drain fully (15 minutes)
   - **Oil collected:** 5.2 liters (close to 5.5L capacity, confirms oil level was OK)

**2. Remove oil pan bolts:**
   - 16 bolts total (10mm socket)
   - Followed service manual sequence (start from center, work outward)
   - Used plastic scraper to break gasket seal (no metal tools—prevents pan damage)

**3. Lower oil pan carefully** (oil residue still inside)

**Inspection 1: Oil Pan Interior**

Ravi examined the oil pan under a work light:
- **Bottom of pan:** Small amount of sludge (normal for 5,000 km since change)
- **Magnet on drain plug:** Very fine metal particles (normal wear—not excessive)
- **No large chunks of metal** (bearings not catastrophically failing)

**Inspection 2: Oil Pickup Screen**

The oil pickup screen was still bolted to the bottom of the engine block. Ravi removed the 2 bolts and pulled it free.

**Finding #1: PARTIALLY BLOCKED PICKUP SCREEN**

The mesh screen was covered with a thick layer of sludge and fine debris, blocking approximately 30-40% of the screen area.

**"There's the problem,"** Ravi thought. **"Restricted pickup screen reduces oil flow to the pump. At high RPM, the pump works hard enough to pull oil through the restriction. At idle, it can't generate sufficient suction, so pressure drops."**

But Ravi knew better than to stop at one finding. **"Blocked screen is a symptom. What caused the sludge buildup? And is the pump itself worn?"**

**Inspection 3: Oil Pump Removal and Measurement**

The oil pump on the Nexon is mounted inside the front timing cover. Ravi removed:
- Timing cover bolts (12 bolts, 10mm socket)
- Carefully separated cover from block (gasket seal)
- Exposed oil pump (gear-type pump driven by crankshaft)

**Oil Pump Type:** Internal gear pump (crescent design)
- Inner rotor (driven by crankshaft)
- Outer rotor (driven by inner rotor via gear teeth)
- Crescent (separates rotors, creates pumping chambers)
- Pump housing

**Oil Pump Measurement (Using Feeler Gauges and Straightedge):**

**Measurement 1: Rotor-to-Crescent Clearance**
- Specification: 0.10-0.15 mm
- Measured: **0.18 mm** — **OUT OF SPEC** (worn)

**Measurement 2: Rotor End Clearance (Rotor-to-Cover Face)**
- Specification: 0.03-0.08 mm
- Measured: **0.11 mm** — **OUT OF SPEC** (worn)

**Measurement 3: Outer Rotor-to-Housing Clearance**
- Specification: 0.15-0.25 mm
- Measured: **0.28 mm** — **OUT OF SPEC** (worn)

**Finding #2: WORN OIL PUMP**

All three critical clearances exceeded specification. Excessive clearances allow oil to leak past the rotors instead of being pumped forward, reducing pressure output (especially at low RPM where leakage is a higher percentage of total flow).

**Inspection 4: Pressure Relief Valve**

Ravi removed the pressure relief valve (threaded plug in pump housing):
- Spring and piston removed
- **Spring:** Free length measured with caliper: 42mm (spec: 44-46mm) — **WEAK**
- **Piston:** Smooth movement in bore, no scoring
- **Spring seat:** Clean, no debris

**Finding #3: WEAK PRESSURE RELIEF VALVE SPRING**

The spring's job is to hold the valve closed until pressure exceeds the setpoint (typically 4.5-5.0 bar). A weak spring allows the valve to open at lower pressure, reducing maximum pressure and causing pressure loss at idle.

**Root Cause Diagnosis:**

**Primary Cause:** Worn oil pump (excessive clearances)
**Contributing Causes:**
- Blocked oil pickup screen (restricts flow)
- Weak pressure relief valve spring (allows premature pressure release)

**Why Did This Happen?**

Ravi reviewed the service history again. Oil changes were on time, correct oil used. But:
- Vehicle driven in Pune city traffic (short trips, lots of idling, engine runs hot)
- Diesel engine (produces more soot and contaminants than petrol)
- 45,000 km on original oil pump (normal wear over time)

**The sludge on the pickup screen likely came from:**
- Extended oil change intervals early in vehicle's life (if previous owner didn't maintain well)
- Short-trip driving (oil doesn't get hot enough to evaporate moisture and fuel contamination)
- Diesel engine byproducts (soot accumulation)

### Part 4: Oil Analysis - 15 minutes
**"What Does the Oil Tell Us?"**

Before discarding the drained oil, Ravi performed a visual oil analysis—a skill every technician should master.

**Visual Oil Analysis Procedure:**

**1. Color Assessment**
- Fresh diesel oil: Golden amber
- Used diesel oil (5,000 km): **Dark brown/black** — Normal (diesel soot)
- If milky/tan: Coolant contamination (head gasket leak) — **Not observed**
- If very black and thick: Excessive soot (DPF regeneration issues) — **Not observed**

**2. Smell Test**
- Normal diesel oil: Sulfur smell (diesel byproducts) — **Observed**
- If strong gasoline/fuel smell: Fuel dilution (injector leaking, DPF regeneration issues) — **Not detected**

**3. Texture Test**
- Rubbed between fingers:
  - Smooth, slippery: Normal — **Confirmed**
  - Gritty: Metal particles (severe wear) — **Not detected**

**4. Magnetic Particle Test**
- Dragged strong magnet through oil sample
- **Result:** Very fine ferrous particles adhered to magnet (normal wear)
- Large chunks would indicate bearing or gear failure — **Not observed**

**5. Blotter Test (Professional Method)**
- Placed 2-3 drops of oil on clean white paper towel
- Allowed to absorb for 30 minutes
- **Observation:**
  - Center dark (soot and contaminants) — Normal
  - Outer ring lighter (oil base stock) — Normal
  - No separation ring (would indicate high contamination) — Normal
  - No metallic particles visible — Normal

**6. Pour Test (Viscosity Check)**
- Poured oil from container
- **Flow rate:** Flows smoothly, not excessively thick or thin
- **Conclusion:** Viscosity appears normal for 5W-30 at room temperature

**Ravi's Oil Analysis Conclusion:**

**Oil condition is acceptable** for 5,000 km use in diesel engine. No severe contamination detected (no coolant, no excessive fuel dilution, no catastrophic metal wear). However, sludge on pickup screen indicates some oil degradation over time.

**Recommendation:** After pump replacement, use high-quality diesel engine oil with detergent additives to help prevent future sludge buildup.

**If Professional Oil Analysis Were Performed:**

Ravi knew that for fleet vehicles or high-value engines, oil samples would be sent to a lab for detailed analysis:

**Typical Oil Analysis Report Includes:**
- **Viscosity:** Check if oil has thinned (shear) or thickened (contamination)
- **Wear Metals (ppm):**
  - Iron (Fe): Cylinder wear, ring wear, bearing wear
  - Copper (Cu): Bearing wear (bronze bushings)
  - Aluminum (Al): Piston wear, bearing wear
  - Lead (Pb): Bearing overlay wear
  - Chrome (Cr): Piston ring wear
- **Contaminants (ppm):**
  - Silicon (Si): Dirt ingestion, coolant contamination
  - Sodium (Na), Potassium (K): Coolant contamination
  - Fuel dilution (%): Excessive fuel in oil
- **Additives (ppm):** Calcium (Ca), Zinc (Zn), Phosphorus (P) — Detergents, anti-wear agents
- **TBN (Total Base Number):** Oil's remaining ability to neutralize acids (low TBN = oil degraded)
- **Soot (%):** Diesel soot contamination

**Typical Interpretation:**

| Parameter | Normal | High | Indicates |
|-----------|--------|------|-----------|
| Iron (Fe) | <50 ppm | >100 ppm | Cylinder/bearing wear |
| Copper (Cu) | <20 ppm | >50 ppm | Bearing wear |
| Fuel Dilution | <2% | >5% | Injector leaks, DPF regen issues |
| Soot | <3% | >5% | Incomplete combustion, short trips |
| TBN | >3.0 | <2.0 | Oil degraded, change immediately |

**For Today's Scenario:**
Ravi's visual analysis was sufficient to rule out catastrophic contamination. The root cause was mechanical (worn pump, blocked screen), not oil quality.

### Part 7: Lessons Learned - 10 minutes
**"What Ravi's Scenario Teaches Us"**

**Key Takeaways from Today's Scenario:**

**1. Systematic Diagnosis Saves Time and Money**
   - Ravi didn't guess; he tested and measured
   - Mechanical pressure test verified low pressure (not sensor fault)
   - Inspection found three issues: pump, screen, valve

**2. Oil Pressure Diagnosis Sequence:**
   - Verify complaint (warning light, mechanical gauge test)
   - Check oil level and condition (simple first)
   - Test actual pressure at idle and high RPM
   - Remove pan and inspect pump, pickup screen, relief valve
   - Measure pump clearances (determines if worn)

   - Ravi found worn pump + blocked screen + weak valve
   - Fixing only one wouldn't fully solve the problem
   - Thorough inspection identifies all contributing factors

   - Regular oil changes with quality oil prevent sludge buildup
   - Sludge blocks pickup screen → starves pump → low pressure → bearing wear → engine damage
   - ₹11,600 repair vs ₹80,000-₹1,20,000 engine replacement (if ignored)

   - Ravi explained findings clearly (with photos, measurements)

## Assessment Content

### Quiz Questions (5 Questions - PowerPoint Slide Format)

**Question 1: Oil Pump Measurement (Apply Level)**
You measure an oil pump's outer rotor-to-housing clearance using feeler gauges. Specification: 0.15-0.25 mm. Your measurement: 0.32 mm. What should you do?

A) Install the pump—measurement is close enough
B) Replace the oil pump—clearance exceeds specification
C) Machine the housing to reduce clearance
D) Install thicker gasket to compensate

**Answer:** B) Replace the oil pump—clearance exceeds specification
**Explanation:** 0.32 mm exceeds maximum specification (0.25 mm). Excessive clearance allows oil to leak past rotors instead of being pumped, reducing pressure output. Pump must be replaced. Clearances cannot be corrected by machining or gaskets.

---

**Question 2: Oil Pressure Diagnosis (Analyze Level)**
A vehicle has good oil pressure at 3000 RPM (4.0 bar) but low pressure at hot idle (0.5 bar, spec: 1.0 bar minimum). Oil level is correct. What is the MOST likely cause?

A) Completely failed oil pump
B) Wrong oil viscosity (too thick)
C) Worn oil pump or excessive bearing clearance
D) Overfilled engine oil

**Answer:** C) Worn oil pump or excessive bearing clearance
**Explanation:** Pressure is adequate at high RPM but low at idle. This indicates pump can generate pressure when spinning fast but loses efficiency at low RPM due to internal leakage (worn clearances). Alternatively, bearing clearances are excessive, allowing oil to escape faster than pump can supply at low speed. If pump were completely failed, pressure would be low at all RPM.

---

**Question 3: Oil Pickup Screen Blockage (Apply Level)**
A partially blocked oil pickup screen will cause which symptom?

A) High oil pressure at all RPM
B) Low oil pressure, especially at idle
C) Oil leaks from valve cover
D) Excessive oil consumption

**Answer:** B) Low oil pressure, especially at idle
**Explanation:** Blocked pickup screen restricts oil flow to pump inlet. At high RPM, pump works harder and can pull oil through restriction. At idle (low pump speed), insufficient suction causes flow reduction and pressure drop. Similar to Ravi's Nexon diagnosis.

---

**Question 4: Pressure Relief Valve Function (Understand Level)**
What is the primary function of the pressure relief valve in an engine lubrication system?

A) Maintain minimum oil pressure at idle
B) Limit maximum oil pressure to prevent damage
C) Control oil temperature
D) Filter oil before it enters bearings

**Answer:** B) Limit maximum oil pressure to prevent damage
**Explanation:** Pressure relief valve opens when pressure exceeds a setpoint (typically 4.5-5.0 bar) to prevent damage to oil filter, seals, and oil cooler lines. It's a safety device. A weak or stuck-open valve allows pressure to bleed off prematurely, causing low pressure (as in Ravi's scenario).

---

**Question 5: Oil Analysis Interpretation (Analyze Level)**
During visual oil analysis, you find oil has a milky, tan appearance and the level on the dipstick is above MAX. What does this indicate?

A) Oil is fresh and in good condition
B) Coolant contamination (head gasket leak or cracked head)
C) Diesel fuel dilution (injector leak)
D) Excessive soot from diesel combustion

**Answer:** B) Coolant contamination (head gasket leak or cracked head)
**Explanation:** Milky, tan appearance is classic sign of coolant mixing with oil (emulsification). Level rises above MAX because coolant is being added to oil. Causes: head gasket failure, cracked head, or intake manifold gasket leak. Requires immediate attention—oil loses lubricating ability and engine will fail.

5. Start engine (instructor supervision)
6. Record pressure at:
   - Cold idle (immediate startup)
   - Hot idle (after 10 minutes warm-up)
   - 2000 RPM (hot)
   - 3000 RPM (hot)
7. Compare to specifications provided on worksheet
8. Diagnose: Pressure within spec, low at idle only, or low at all RPM

**Expected Results:**
- Engine A (worn pump): Low at idle (0.6 bar), acceptable at 3000 RPM (3.5 bar)
- Engine B (blocked screen): Low at idle (0.7 bar), marginal at 3000 RPM (3.2 bar)
- Engine C (weak valve): Low at all RPM (max 2.5 bar)
- Engine D (normal): Good at all RPM (1.5 bar idle, 4.5 bar at 3000 RPM)

**Station 2: Oil Pan Removal and Pickup Screen Inspection (45 min)**

**Procedure:**
1. Position drain pan under engine
2. Remove oil drain plug (drain oil—already drained in training setup)
3. Remove oil pan bolts (10mm socket, start from center and work outward)
4. Carefully separate oil pan from block using plastic scraper
5. Inspect oil pan interior for sludge, metal particles
6. Check drain plug magnet for metal particles
7. Remove oil pickup screen (2 bolts)
8. Inspect screen for blockage (estimate % of screen area blocked)
9. Clean screen in solvent tank if blocked
10. Dry with compressed air and inspect under light

**Station 3: Oil Pump Removal and Measurement (60 min)**

**Procedure:**
1. Remove timing cover bolts (instructor supervision)
2. Carefully separate timing cover from block
3. Locate oil pump (internal gear or rotor-type)
4. Remove oil pump mounting bolts
5. Remove pump from engine

**Measurement Procedure (Rotor-Type Pump):**

**Measurement 1: Rotor-to-Crescent Clearance**
- Insert feeler gauge between inner rotor and crescent
- Find thickest gauge that just fits
- Record measurement
- Compare to spec (typically 0.10-0.15 mm)

**Measurement 2: Rotor End Clearance**
- Lay precision straightedge across pump housing face
- Insert feeler gauge between straightedge and rotor face
- Record measurement
- Compare to spec (typically 0.03-0.08 mm)

**Measurement 3: Outer Rotor-to-Housing Clearance**
- Insert feeler gauge between outer rotor and housing
- Measure at tightest point
- Record measurement
- Compare to spec (typically 0.15-0.25 mm)

**Diagnosis:**
- All clearances within spec: Pump OK
- Any clearance exceeds spec: Pump worn, requires replacement

**Station 4: Visual Oil Analysis (30 min)**

**Procedure:**
1. Obtain oil sample from training engines (pre-labeled samples with different conditions)
2. Perform visual analysis tests:

**Test 1: Color Assessment**
- Pour sample into clear glass beaker
- Observe color: Amber (clean), Dark brown (used), Black (very used), Milky (coolant contamination)

**Test 2: Smell Test**
- Carefully smell sample (don't inhale deeply)
- Normal: Petroleum smell
- Fuel dilution: Strong gasoline/diesel smell
- Coolant: Sweet smell

**Test 3: Texture Test**
- Rub oil between thumb and forefinger
- Smooth: Normal
- Gritty: Metal particles present (severe wear)

**Test 4: Magnetic Particle Test**
- Drag strong magnet through oil sample
- Observe particles adhering to magnet
- Fine particles: Normal wear
- Large chunks: Bearing/gear failure

**Test 5: Blotter Test**
- Place 2-3 drops on clean white paper towel
- Allow to absorb for 15 minutes
- Observe pattern:
  - Center dark, outer ring light: Normal
  - Uniform dark: High contamination
  - Metallic particles visible: Severe wear

**Oil Samples Provided:**
- Sample A: Normal used oil (5,000 km)
- Sample B: Coolant-contaminated oil (milky)
- Sample C: Fuel-diluted oil (strong fuel smell, thin consistency)
- Sample D: Metal-contaminated oil (gritty texture, magnetic particles)

**Students Must Identify Each Sample's Condition**

**Pass Criteria:**
- Oil pressure test performed safely and accurately (all readings recorded)
- Oil pan and pickup screen inspection completed (blockage identified if present)
- Oil pump measurements performed with correct technique (feeler gauges used properly)
- All measurements within ±0.01mm accuracy
- Visual oil analysis completed with correct identification of all 4 sample conditions
- Diagnosis of assigned engine fault correct (worn pump, blocked screen, weak valve, or normal)
- Safety protocols followed 100% (no engine started without instructor present, proper tool use)

**Safety Reminders:**
- Never start engine without instructor supervision
- Mechanical gauge hose must be routed away from moving parts (belts, pulleys)
- Oil is slippery—clean up spills immediately
- Wear gloves when handling used oil (contaminants, skin irritation)
- Dispose of used oil properly (designated waste oil containers)
- Feeler gauges are precision tools—don't bend or force them
