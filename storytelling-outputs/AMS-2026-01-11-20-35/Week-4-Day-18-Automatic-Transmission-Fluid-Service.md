## Problem-Framing Framework

### Problem Statement

**The Challenge:**

Automatic transmissions are precision hydraulic and mechanical systems that rely on transmission fluid for:
1. **Hydraulic pressure:** Operating clutches and bands to engage gears
2. **Lubrication:** Reducing friction between gears, bearings, and bushings
3. **Cooling:** Dissipating heat generated during torque converter operation
4. **Cleaning:** Suspending wear particles to prevent component damage

**Critical Issue:**

Contaminated, degraded, or incorrect transmission fluid causes:
- Harsh shifting (delayed or abrupt gear changes)
- Slipping (transmission unable to transmit full engine torque)
- Overheating (reduced cooling capacity, accelerated component wear)
- Complete failure (internal clutch/band wear, valve body blockage)

**Why ATF Service is Essential:**

Unlike manual transmission fluid (changed every 40,000-60,000 km), automatic transmission fluid degrades faster due to:
- Higher operating temperatures (90-110°C vs. 60-80°C in manual transmissions)
- Hydraulic stress (fluid under constant pressure in torque converter and valve body)
- Torque converter heat generation (fluid slippage converts energy to heat)

**Service Intervals (India Context):**
- **Manufacturer recommendation:** 40,000-60,000 km (normal driving)
- **Severe service:** 30,000-40,000 km (city traffic, hot climate, towing)
- **Reality in India:** Many vehicles never get ATF changed until problems occur

**Your Objective:**

By the end of this session, you will be able to:
1. Perform drain-and-refill ATF service on conventional automatic transmissions
2. Replace transmission filter and pan gasket
3. Check ATF level using correct procedure (temperature, engine running, gear position)
4. Inspect ATF condition (color, smell, contamination) to recommend flush vs. drain-and-refill
5. Perform complete ATF flush using exchange machine
6. Differentiate service procedures for conventional automatic, AMT, CVT, and DSG systems

## Technical Content - Detailed Procedures

### Section 1: Understanding Automatic Transmission Fluid (ATF)

#### 1.1 ATF Functions and Properties

**Four Critical Functions:**

1. **Hydraulic Medium:**
   - Transmits pressure to actuate clutches, bands, and servos
   - Pressure range: 5-20 bar (depending on gear and load)
   - Must maintain viscosity across temperature range (0-130°C)

2. **Lubricant:**
   - Reduces friction between planetary gears, bearings, bushings
   - Prevents metal-to-metal contact that causes wear

3. **Coolant:**
   - Absorbs heat from torque converter (primary heat source)
   - Circulates through external transmission cooler (integrated with radiator)
   - Temperature management critical (above 130°C causes fluid breakdown)

4. **Cleaning Agent:**
   - Suspends wear particles (metal, friction material)
   - Carries contaminants to filter for removal
   - Prevents sludge buildup in valve body passages

**ATF Properties:**

- **Viscosity:** Thinner than engine oil (similar to 5W-30 motor oil)
- **Color:** Red or pink (dyed for identification—not engine oil)
- **Additives:** Friction modifiers, detergents, anti-wear compounds, anti-foaming agents
- **Lifespan:** 40,000-60,000 km under normal conditions, degrades faster under stress

#### 1.2 ATF Types and Specifications

**Common ATF Specifications (India Market):**

| Specification | Used In | Characteristics |
|---------------|---------|-----------------|
| **Dexron III** | Older GM-based automatics (Chevrolet Tavera, Optra) | General-purpose ATF, widely available |
| **Dexron VI** | Newer GM automatics (Chevrolet Beat, Sail) | Synthetic, longer life, better high-temp performance |
| **Mercon V** | Ford automatics (EcoSport, Figo) | Similar to Dexron III, Ford-specific formulation |
| **ATF Type T-IV** | Toyota/Lexus vehicles (Innova, Fortuner, Camry) | Specific to Toyota automatics, not interchangeable |
| **Honda ATF-DW1** | Honda vehicles (City, Amaze, CR-V) | Honda-specific, do NOT substitute |
| **Hyundai SP-IV** | Hyundai/Kia vehicles (Verna, Creta, Elantra) | Manufacturer-specific formulation |

**CRITICAL RULE:**

**Always use manufacturer-specified ATF type.** Mixing incompatible ATF causes:
- Seal swelling or shrinkage (leaks)
- Friction modifier incompatibility (harsh shifting, shudder)
- Additive package conflicts (reduced protection, accelerated wear)

**How to Verify Correct ATF:**

1. Check owner's manual or service manual
2. Check ATF dipstick cap (often marked with specification)
3. Call dealership parts department with VIN
4. Use ATF application guide (available from fluid manufacturers)

#### 1.3 ATF Condition Inspection

**Visual and Sensory Inspection:**

| Condition | Normal | Service Needed | Severe |
|-----------|--------|----------------|--------|
| **Color** | Bright red or pink | Dark red/brown | Black |
| **Smell** | Mild petroleum smell | Slightly burnt smell | Strong burnt smell (like burnt toast) |
| **Transparency** | Transparent, can see through on dipstick | Cloudy, slightly opaque | Opaque, cannot see through |
| **Particles** | None visible | Fine metallic particles | Large particles, debris, sludge |
| **Recommendation** | Normal service interval | Drain and refill recommended | Flush recommended (if transmission functional); investigate cause |

**White Paper Test:**

1. Place drop of ATF on white paper towel
2. Observe color and particle distribution
3. **Normal:** Even red color, no particles
4. **Worn friction material:** Brown particles dispersed in fluid
5. **Metal wear:** Metallic particles (shiny, magnetic if steel)

**Magnet Test (Metal Contamination):**

1. Place strong magnet near ATF sample
2. Ferrous particles (steel from gears/bearings) attract to magnet
3. Non-ferrous particles (bronze bushings, aluminum) do not attract

### Section 2: Drain-and-Refill ATF Service (Conventional Automatic)

#### 2.1 Tools and Materials Required

**Tools:**
- Floor jack and jack stands (or vehicle lift)
- Drain pan (minimum 8-liter capacity)
- Torque wrench (10-50 Nm range)
- Funnel with long spout (for filling through dipstick tube)
- Transmission fluid pump (if no dipstick tube—sealed transmissions)
- Socket set (metric, 10mm-19mm typical)
- Scraper or gasket remover (for pan gasket removal)
- Clean rags and brake cleaner

**Materials:**
- Correct ATF type and quantity (typically 4-6 liters for drain-and-refill)
- Transmission pan gasket (or RTV silicone if specified)
- Transmission filter (if accessible from pan)
- Drain plug washer/gasket (if equipped)

**Safety Equipment:**
- Safety glasses (ATF can splash)
- Gloves (ATF is slippery and contains additives)
- Shop towels

#### 2.2 Step-by-Step Procedure (Example: Maruti Ciaz/Ertiga 4-Speed Automatic)

**Step 1: Pre-Service Checks (5 min)**

1. Verify vehicle identification and correct ATF specification
   - Maruti Ciaz/Ertiga 4-speed automatic: **Dexron III** or equivalent
   - Capacity: **5.8 liters total, 4.0 liters drain-and-refill**

2. Check current ATF level and condition
   - Engine warm, running at idle, transmission in Park
   - Pull dipstick, wipe clean, reinsert fully, pull again
   - Note level and color/smell

3. Warm up transmission (if not already at operating temperature)
   - Drive vehicle 10-15 minutes to reach 70-80°C fluid temperature
   - Warm fluid drains more completely (thinner viscosity)

**Step 2: Drain ATF (15 min)**

1. Lift vehicle and secure on jack stands (or lift)
2. Locate transmission oil pan (underneath vehicle, towards rear)
3. Position drain pan under transmission pan

**If equipped with drain plug:**
4. Remove drain plug (typically 17mm or 19mm hex)
5. Allow fluid to drain completely (3-5 minutes)
6. Inspect drained fluid (color, smell, particles)
7. Install new drain plug washer, reinstall plug
8. Torque drain plug: **35-40 Nm** (typical, verify in service manual)

**If NOT equipped with drain plug:**
4. Loosen all pan bolts gradually (start from corners, work inward)
5. Leave two bolts finger-tight on one side (pan hangs at angle)
6. Carefully lower one side of pan to drain fluid (will spill—pan tilts into drain pan)
7. Remove remaining bolts, lower pan completely
8. Clean gasket mating surface on transmission case (scraper, brake cleaner)

**Step 3: Replace Transmission Filter (If Accessible) (15 min)**

**Note:** Not all automatic transmissions have serviceable filters from the pan. Some have internal filters requiring transmission disassembly (dealer service).

1. Locate filter inside transmission pan (bolted or snap-fit to valve body)
2. Remove filter mounting bolts (typically 2-3 bolts, 10mm)
3. Lower filter carefully (may contain residual fluid)
4. Inspect old filter for contamination (metallic particles, debris, sludge)
5. Clean filter mounting surface on valve body
6. Install new filter with new O-ring or gasket
7. Torque filter bolts: **8-10 Nm** (hand-tight plus 1/4 turn—verify in manual)

**Step 4: Inspect Transmission Pan and Magnets (5 min)**

1. Clean transmission pan thoroughly (brake cleaner, shop towels)
2. Inspect magnetic drain plug or pan magnets (if equipped)
   - Fine metallic fuzz: Normal (minimal wear)
   - Large chunks or excessive material: Indicates internal wear, investigate further
3. Inspect pan for sludge, debris, or unusual contamination
   - Clean pan with solvent, dry completely

**Step 5: Install Transmission Pan (10 min)**

**Gasket Type (Check Service Manual):**

- **Cork or paper gasket:** Apply thin coat of ATF to both sides, install
- **RTV silicone (if specified):** Apply 2-3mm bead to pan flange, install within 5 minutes

**Installation:**

1. Position new gasket on pan or apply RTV to pan flange
2. Lift pan carefully, align with bolt holes
3. Install all pan bolts finger-tight
4. Torque pan bolts in spiral pattern (start from center, work outward)
   - **Torque:** 10-15 Nm (typical for steel pan, verify in manual)
   - **CAUTION:** Overtightening distorts pan, causes leaks

**Step 6: Refill ATF (15 min)**

1. Lower vehicle (or remain on lift if filling from below)

**Filling Procedure (Vehicles with Dipstick Tube):**

2. Locate ATF fill point (usually dipstick tube on top of transmission)
3. Insert funnel with long spout into dipstick tube
4. Pour in 80% of drained amount initially (if drained 4.0L, refill 3.2L first)
5. Start engine, shift through all gears (P-R-N-D-3-2-1-2-3-D-N-R-P), 5 seconds each
6. Return to Park, engine running at idle
7. Check ATF level on dipstick (wipe, reinsert, pull, read)
8. Add ATF in 200ml increments until level reaches "HOT" mark
9. Recheck after 5-minute idle (level should stabilize)

**Critical: Temperature Matters**

- ATF expands when hot (70-80°C operating temperature)
- "COLD" mark on dipstick: Check before engine startup (reference only)
- "HOT" mark on dipstick: Check at operating temperature (70-80°C), engine running
- Overfilling causes foaming (air bubbles reduce hydraulic pressure, damage clutches)
- Underfilling causes pump cavitation (no lubrication, overheating, failure)

**Filling Procedure (Sealed Transmissions—No Dipstick):**

2. Locate fill plug (usually on side of transmission case)
3. Remove fill plug, install transmission fluid pump adapter
4. Pump ATF until fluid drips from fill hole (level correct when fluid overflows)
5. Install fill plug, torque to specification

**Step 7: Road Test and Verification (15 min)**

1. Drive vehicle 10-15 minutes (light to moderate load)
2. Monitor transmission operation:
   - Smooth shifting through all gears
   - No slipping (RPM increases without speed increase)
   - No harsh engagement or delayed shifts
   - Normal operating temperature (check gauge if equipped)

3. Return to workshop, recheck ATF level (engine running, in Park, at operating temp)
4. Adjust level if necessary (add or remove ATF)
5. Inspect pan area for leaks (clean underside, run engine, inspect for seepage)

**Step 8: Documentation**

1. Record on service card:
   - ATF type and quantity used
   - Filter replaced (yes/no)
   - Fluid condition before service (color, contamination)
   - Post-service road test results
   - Next service interval recommendation (40,000 km or 2 years)

### Section 3: ATF Flush Service (Exchange Machine Method)

#### 3.1 When to Recommend Flush vs. Drain-and-Refill

**Drain-and-Refill:**
- Removes 40-60% of total ATF (only fluid in pan drains, fluid in torque converter and cooler lines remains)
- Suitable for regular maintenance (40,000-60,000 km intervals)
- Less expensive (₹2,500-₹4,000 total cost)
- Recommended when fluid is still red/light brown, no severe contamination

**ATF Flush (Exchange Machine):**
- Removes 95-100% of total ATF (machine exchanges fluid dynamically while transmission operates)
- Recommended when fluid is dark brown/black, contaminated, or vehicle has missed service intervals
- More expensive (₹5,000-₹8,000 total cost, 10-12 liters ATF required)
- Restores transmission performance if internal components are still functional

**CAUTION: When NOT to Flush**

- **Never flush if transmission is already slipping or shifting harshly.** Flush may dislodge debris that temporarily "fills gaps" in worn clutches, causing complete failure within days.
- If fluid is black with burnt smell and transmission has shifting problems, flush won't fix internal wear—rebuild or replacement needed.

#### 3.2 ATF Flush Procedure (Exchange Machine)

**Equipment Required:**
- ATF exchange machine (e.g., Wynn's TransServe, BG, Mahle)
- Correct ATF type (10-12 liters)
- Transmission cooler line adapters

**Step-by-Step Procedure:**

**Step 1: Connect Exchange Machine (10 min)**

1. Locate transmission cooler lines (connect radiator to transmission)
   - Usually two rubber hoses with clamps near radiator
   - Identify flow direction (one is "supply" from transmission, one is "return" to transmission)

2. Disconnect cooler lines from transmission or radiator
3. Connect machine inlet hose to "supply" line (fluid flowing from transmission)
4. Connect machine outlet hose to "return" line (fluid flowing to transmission)
5. Fill exchange machine reservoir with new ATF (10-12 liters)

**Step 2: Operate Exchange Machine (20-30 min)**

1. Start vehicle engine, transmission in Park or Neutral
2. Activate exchange machine (follows machine-specific procedure)
3. Machine pumps new ATF into transmission while extracting old ATF simultaneously
4. Monitor machine display:
   - Old ATF collects in waste tank (observe color changing from dark to red)
   - New ATF level decreases in supply tank
   - Exchange ratio displayed (gallons/liters exchanged)

5. Cycle transmission through gears (P-R-N-D-3-2-1) several times
   - Helps flush fluid from valve body and torque converter

6. Continue exchange until new ATF color appears in return line (indicates old fluid purged)
7. Stop machine, disconnect hoses
8. Reconnect transmission cooler lines to original positions
9. Secure hose clamps, verify no leaks

**Step 3: Verify ATF Level and Road Test (15 min)**

1. Check ATF level using dipstick (engine running, operating temperature)
2. Adjust level if necessary
3. Road test vehicle (verify smooth shifting, no issues)
4. Recheck level, inspect for leaks

**Step 4: Documentation**

1. Record on service card:
   - ATF flush completed (exchange machine method)
   - ATF type and quantity used (typically 10-12 liters)
   - Old fluid condition (color, contamination level)
   - Post-service road test results
   - Next service recommendation (60,000 km or 3 years)

### Section 4: Service Procedures for Different Transmission Types

#### 4.1 AMT (Automated Manual Transmission)

**Examples:** Maruti Celerio AMT, Alto K10 AMT, WagonR AMT

**Fluid Service:**
- AMT uses standard manual transmission gear oil (NOT ATF)
- Fluid type: **75W-90 GL-4 gear oil** (same as manual transmission)
- Service interval: 40,000 km
- Procedure: Same as manual transmission fluid change (drain plug, refill, check level)

**Additional AMT-Specific Service:**
- Clutch actuator calibration (using scan tool after fluid change)
- Hydraulic clutch bleeding (if equipped with hydraulic clutch)

**IMPORTANT:** AMT is NOT an automatic transmission—it's a manual transmission with automated clutch and shift actuators. Do NOT use ATF.

#### 4.2 CVT (Continuously Variable Transmission)

**Examples:** Maruti Baleno CVT, Honda City CVT, Nissan Magnite CVT

**Fluid Service:**
- CVT uses specialized CVT fluid (NOT standard ATF)
- Fluid type: **Manufacturer-specific CVT fluid** (e.g., Honda CVT Fluid, Nissan NS-2 CVT Fluid)
- **DO NOT substitute ATF or mix CVT fluid types** (causes belt/chain slippage, transmission failure)

**Service Procedure:**
- Similar to conventional automatic (drain from pan, refill)
- Some CVTs have drain plug, others require pan removal
- Typically no serviceable filter from pan (internal filter)
- Service interval: 40,000-60,000 km (manufacturer-specific)

**Critical CVT Considerations:**
- CVT fluid level MUST be exact (more critical than conventional automatic)
- Use sealed transmission fill procedure or dipstick (check service manual)
- Overfilling or underfilling causes belt slippage and failure

#### 4.3 DSG/DCT (Dual-Clutch Transmission)

**Examples:** VW Polo DSG, Skoda Rapid DSG, Audi A3 S-Tronic

**Fluid Service:**
- DSG uses specialized dual-clutch transmission fluid
- Two types: Wet clutch (uses DSG fluid) and dry clutch (no fluid in clutch housing)
- Fluid type: **VW G 052 182 A2** (wet clutch DSG) or equivalent
- Service interval: 60,000 km (manufacturer-specific)

**Service Procedure:**
- Drain from pan (similar to conventional automatic)
- Replace filter and mechatronic unit filter (if serviceable)
- Refill using scan tool-assisted procedure (some DSG require scan tool to enter fill mode)
- Perform adaptation procedure using scan tool after fluid service

**NOTE:** DSG service is complex and typically requires dealer-level equipment (covered in detail on Day 20).

### Section 5: Common Problems and Troubleshooting

#### 5.1 ATF Leaks

**Common Leak Points:**

1. **Transmission pan gasket**
   - Cause: Overtightened bolts (distorted pan), old gasket, incorrect RTV application
   - Fix: Replace gasket, torque bolts correctly

2. **Dipstick tube O-ring**
   - Cause: Worn O-ring at tube base
   - Fix: Replace O-ring, ensure tube seated properly

3. **Transmission cooler lines**
   - Cause: Worn rubber hoses, loose clamps, damaged fittings
   - Fix: Replace hoses, tighten or replace clamps

4. **Output shaft seal**
   - Cause: Worn seal, driveshaft imbalance (causes seal wear)
   - Fix: Replace seal, inspect driveshaft for runout

5. **Torque converter seal (input shaft seal)**
   - Cause: Worn seal, requires transmission removal to replace
   - Fix: Replace seal during transmission service or rebuild

**Leak Detection:**
- Clean underside of transmission thoroughly
- Add UV dye to ATF (if available)
- Run engine, shift through gears
- Inspect with UV light (dye glows at leak source)

#### 5.2 Incorrect ATF Level

**Symptoms of Overfilling:**
- Foaming (visible on dipstick—bubbles in fluid)
- Rough shifting (air in hydraulic system reduces pressure)
- Fluid coming out of vent or dipstick tube
- Overheating (foaming reduces cooling efficiency)

**Symptoms of Underfilling:**
- Slipping (pump cannot generate full pressure)
- Delayed engagement (low pressure delays clutch engagement)
- Overheating (insufficient fluid for cooling)
- Noise (pump cavitation—sucking air)

**Solution:**
- Drain excess fluid (if overfilled) using turkey baster or pump through dipstick tube
- Add ATF in small increments (if underfilled) until correct level reached
- Always check level at operating temperature, engine running, in Park

#### 5.3 Wrong ATF Type Installed

**Problem:**
- Customer or previous technician used incorrect ATF type
- Symptoms: Harsh shifting, shudder, slipping, burnt smell

**Solution:**
1. Identify correct ATF type (service manual, dipstick cap marking, dealer parts department)
2. Perform complete ATF flush to remove incorrect fluid
3. Refill with correct ATF type
4. Test drive and monitor for improvement
5. If problems persist after correct fluid installed, internal damage may have occurred

### Section 6: Safety and Environmental Considerations

#### 6.1 Safety Precautions

1. **Hot ATF:**
   - Operating temperature: 70-110°C
   - Allow vehicle to cool 30 minutes before draining (if possible)
   - Wear gloves to prevent burns from hot fluid or components

2. **Vehicle Support:**
   - Always use jack stands or lift safety locks
   - Never work under vehicle supported only by floor jack

3. **Eye Protection:**
   - Wear safety glasses (fluid can splash during drain or pan removal)

4. **Fire Hazard:**
   - ATF is flammable at high temperatures
   - Keep away from hot exhaust components
   - No smoking or open flames in service area

#### 6.2 Environmental Responsibility

**Used ATF Disposal:**
- Used ATF is hazardous waste (contains heavy metals, additives)
- Never pour down drain, on ground, or mix with other waste
- Collect in designated waste oil tank (separate from engine oil if possible)
- Arrange pickup by licensed waste oil recycler
- Comply with local environmental regulations

**Spill Cleanup:**
- Use absorbent pads or floor dry to clean spills
- Dispose of contaminated absorbent as hazardous waste
- Prevent ATF from entering storm drains or waterways

A customer brings a 2020 Honda City with a conventional automatic transmission for ATF service. Which fluid should you use?

A) Dexron III (universal ATF)
B) Honda ATF-DW1 (Honda-specific)

**Question 2: ATF Level Checking Procedure (Apply Level)**

What is the CORRECT procedure for checking ATF level on a conventional automatic transmission with dipstick?

A) Engine off, cold transmission, dipstick read at "COLD" mark
B) Engine running, transmission in Park, at operating temperature (70-80°C), dipstick read at "HOT" mark
C) Engine running, transmission in Drive, at any temperature
D) Engine off, transmission in Neutral, warm engine

**Answer:** B) Engine running, transmission in Park, at operating temperature (70-80°C), dipstick read at "HOT" mark

**Explanation:** Correct ATF level check requires: (1) Engine running (circulates fluid through system), (2) Transmission in Park (fluid settles in pan), (3) Operating temperature 70-80°C (fluid expands when hot—"HOT" mark compensates for expansion), (4) Dipstick wiped clean, reinserted fully, pulled and read. Checking when cold or engine off gives inaccurate reading.

**Question 3: ATF Condition Analysis (Analyze Level)**

During ATF service, you drain fluid that is black in color with a strong burnt smell. The transmission is currently shifting harshly with occasional slipping. What is the BEST recommendation?

A) Perform complete ATF flush to restore transmission performance
B) Perform drain-and-refill service only (flush not needed)
C) Drain and refill ATF, but advise customer that internal damage likely exists—monitor closely
D) Add ATF additive to old fluid (no service needed yet)

**Answer:** C) Drain and refill ATF, but advise customer that internal damage likely exists—monitor closely

**Explanation:** Black ATF with burnt smell indicates severe overheating and friction material breakdown (clutch/band wear). Harsh shifting and slipping confirm internal damage. Flush is NOT recommended when transmission already has shifting problems—dislodging debris may cause complete failure. Drain-and-refill with fresh ATF may provide temporary improvement, but customer must be informed that internal wear exists and rebuild/replacement may be needed soon. Continuing to drive without service will cause complete failure. Additives won't repair worn clutches.

**Question 4: Drain-and-Refill vs. Flush (Understand Level)**

A drain-and-refill ATF service removes approximately 40-60% of total ATF. Where does the remaining 40-60% of old fluid remain?

A) Transmission pan and filter only
B) Torque converter, valve body, and cooler lines
C) Engine oil pan (ATF mixes with engine oil)
D) Overflow reservoir tank

**Answer:** B) Torque converter, valve body, and cooler lines

**Explanation:** When transmission pan is drained, only fluid in the pan drains out (40-60% of total). Fluid remains in: (1) Torque converter (largest reservoir, holds 3-4 liters), (2) Valve body passages, (3) Transmission cooler and cooler lines, (4) Clutch packs and internal passages. This is why complete fluid exchange requires a flush machine (exchanges fluid dynamically while transmission operates, pumping out old fluid from torque converter and entire system).

---

**Question 5: Transmission Pan Torque (Apply Level)**

When installing a transmission pan with a new gasket, what is the CORRECT torque sequence and specification?

A) Torque all bolts to 25 Nm in any order
B) Torque bolts in spiral pattern starting from center outward, 10-15 Nm typical (verify in service manual)
C) Hand-tighten all bolts (torque wrench not needed)
D) Torque corner bolts first to 20 Nm, then remaining bolts to 15 Nm

**Answer:** B) Torque bolts in spiral pattern starting from center outward, 10-15 Nm typical (verify in service manual)

**Explanation:** Transmission pan bolts must be torqued in spiral/cross pattern starting from center and working outward to evenly distribute gasket compression and prevent pan distortion. Typical torque: 10-15 Nm for steel pans (aluminum pans may be lower—always verify in service manual). Overtightening distorts pan, causes leaks. Undertightening also causes leaks. Hand-tightening is insufficient. Random torque sequence distorts pan unevenly.

### Hands-On Exercise Preview (Afternoon Practical - 3 Hours)

**Exercise Title:** Complete ATF Drain-and-Refill Service (Maruti Ciaz/Ertiga 4-Speed Automatic)

**Objective:**
Perform complete ATF drain-and-refill service including fluid inspection, filter replacement, pan gasket replacement, refill procedure, level checking, and road test verification.

**Step-by-Step Activity:**

**Setup (10 min):**
1. Students divided into groups of 4
2. Each group receives:
   - Maruti Ciaz or Ertiga with 4-speed automatic transmission (40,000+ km service due)
   - Dexron III ATF (5 liters)
   - Transmission filter kit (filter, pan gasket, drain plug washer)
   - Tool set (socket set, torque wrench, funnel, drain pan, scraper)
   - Service manual with specifications and torque values
   - ATF service worksheet

**Phase 1 - Pre-Service Inspection (20 min):**
1. Verify vehicle VIN and correct ATF specification (Dexron III for Ciaz/Ertiga)
2. Warm up transmission (10-minute drive to reach 70-80°C)
3. Check current ATF level (engine running, in Park, at operating temp)
4. Pull dipstick, inspect ATF condition (color, smell, transparency)
5. Perform white paper test (place drop on paper towel, observe)
6. Record findings on worksheet (level, color, contamination)
7. Instructor reviews findings before proceeding

**Phase 2 - Drain ATF and Remove Pan (30 min):**
8. Lift vehicle and secure on safety locks (or use lift)
9. Position drain pan under transmission pan
10. Remove drain plug (19mm hex), allow ATF to drain completely (3-5 minutes)
11. Measure drained quantity (record on worksheet)
12. Inspect drained fluid (color, particles, metal contamination)
13. Remove transmission pan bolts gradually (loosen all, then remove)
14. Lower pan, clean gasket mating surface (scraper, brake cleaner)
15. Instructor checks: Clean mating surface, no gouges or damage

**Phase 3 - Filter Replacement and Pan Inspection (25 min):**
16. Locate transmission filter (bolted to valve body inside pan)
17. Remove filter mounting bolts (10mm, typically 2-3 bolts)
18. Lower filter, inspect for contamination (particles, debris, sludge)
19. Clean filter mounting surface on valve body
20. Install new filter with new O-ring/gasket
21. Torque filter bolts: 8-10 Nm (hand-tight plus 1/4 turn)
22. Inspect transmission pan magnets (if equipped)—note metal contamination level
23. Clean transmission pan thoroughly (solvent, shop towels, dry)
24. Instructor checks: Filter installed correctly, pan clean, magnets inspected

**Phase 4 - Install Pan and Refill ATF (35 min):**
25. Install new drain plug washer on drain plug
26. Install drain plug in pan, torque to 35-40 Nm
27. Position new pan gasket on pan (apply thin coat of ATF to both sides)
28. Lift pan carefully, align with bolt holes on transmission case
29. Install all pan bolts finger-tight
30. Torque pan bolts in spiral pattern (start from center, work outward): 10-12 Nm
31. Lower vehicle (or remain on lift if filling from above)
32. Locate ATF dipstick tube, insert funnel
33. Pour 3.5 liters of ATF initially (80% of drained amount)
34. Start engine, shift through all gears (P-R-N-D-3-2-1-2-3-D-N-R-P), 5 seconds each position
35. Return to Park, engine running at idle, check ATF level
36. Add ATF in 200ml increments until level reaches "HOT" mark on dipstick
37. Allow engine to idle 5 minutes, recheck level (should stabilize)
38. Instructor checks: Level correct, no overfilling, proper procedure followed

**Phase 5 - Road Test and Final Verification (30 min):**
39. Road test vehicle 10-15 minutes (light to moderate acceleration, all gears)
40. Monitor transmission operation:
    - Smooth shifting through all gears (no harshness or delay)
    - No slipping (RPM increases proportionally with speed)
    - Normal operating temperature (if gauge equipped)
41. Return to workshop, recheck ATF level (engine running, in Park, operating temp)
42. Adjust level if necessary (add or remove ATF to reach "HOT" mark)
43. Inspect transmission pan area for leaks (wipe clean, run engine, inspect for seepage)
44. Complete service worksheet:
    - ATF type and quantity used
    - Filter replaced (yes/no)
    - Pan gasket replaced (yes/no)
    - Fluid condition before service (color, contamination level)
    - Post-service road test results
    - Next service interval recommendation (40,000 km or 2 years)
45. Instructor signs off on completed service

**Success Criteria:**
- **Pre-Service Inspection:** Correct ATF specification verified, fluid condition documented accurately
- **Drain and Pan Removal:** All ATF drained, pan removed without damage to mating surface
- **Filter Replacement:** Filter installed correctly with proper torque, pan clean
- **Pan Installation:** Gasket installed correctly, bolts torqued in spiral pattern to specification, no leaks
- **ATF Level:** Final level at "HOT" mark on dipstick at operating temperature, no overfilling or underfilling
- **Road Test:** Smooth shifting, no slipping, no leaks observed after service
- **Documentation:** Complete and accurate service worksheet

**Common Challenges (Instructor Will Address):**

1. **Pan Bolt Overtightening:** Distorts pan, causes immediate or future leaks
   - **Solution:** Use torque wrench, spiral pattern, 10-12 Nm (light torque)

2. **Overfilling ATF:** Causes foaming, rough shifting
   - **Solution:** Remove excess using pump or turkey baster through dipstick tube

3. **Underfilling ATF:** Causes slipping, delayed engagement
   - **Solution:** Add ATF in small increments until level correct at operating temp

4. **Checking Level at Wrong Temperature:** Inaccurate reading (too low if cold, correct only when hot)
   - **Solution:** Always check at operating temperature (70-80°C), engine running

5. **Forgetting to Shift Through Gears Before Checking Level:** Fluid remains in torque converter, pan level appears low
   - **Solution:** Shift through all gears 5 seconds each to circulate fluid, then check level

**Safety Reminders:**
- Wear safety glasses and gloves (ATF can be hot, causes skin irritation)
- Use proper vehicle support (jack stands or lift safety locks—never work under vehicle on jack alone)
- Dispose of used ATF properly (collect in waste oil tank, arrange licensed recycler pickup)
- Avoid spilling ATF on floor (slippery, creates hazard)
- Do not overfill ATF (causes foaming, pressure buildup, potential leaks)

Using wrong ATF causes seal incompatibility (leaks), friction modifier problems (harsh shifting, shudder, torque converter lockup issues), and accelerated wear. *Example for Slide 39 (Temperature Matters: ATF Expands When Hot):*
"Understanding temperature is critical for accurate ATF level checking. Look at this diagram: ATF volume increases approximately 10-15% from cold (20°C) to operating temperature (80°C). The 'HOT' mark on the dipstick is calibrated for 70-80°C fluid temperature. If you check level when transmission is cold, it will appear low—you might add ATF and overfill. This procedure ensures accurate level and prevents overfilling (causes foaming, rough shifting) or underfilling (causes slipping, overheating)."
