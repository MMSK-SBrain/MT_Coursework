#### Rising Action (Guided Journey) - 75 minutes
**"Walk with me through the diagnostic process, then the complete overhaul. I'll show you the systematic approach that separates good mechanics from master technicians."**

**Part 1: Diagnostic Road Test (15 min)**

**The First Rule: Never Skip the Road Test**

When a customer says "my transmission has a problem," you must experience it yourself. Here's my systematic road test procedure:

**Road Test Checklist (Maruti Swift 5-Speed Example):**

1. **Clutch Operation:**
   - Engagement point (smooth or grabby?)
   - Pedal effort (normal or heavy?)
   - Slip test (hold in 3rd gear, full throttle—RPM rises without speed = clutch slip, not transmission)

2. **Shift Quality—Each Gear:**
   - 1st gear: Engages smoothly from neutral?
   - 2nd gear: Grinding during upshift from 1st? (Most common synchronizer failure)
   - 3rd gear: Hard to engage or jumps out under load?
   - 4th gear: Noise in gear? (Bearing wear)
   - 5th gear: Pops out on highway? (Shift fork or detent wear)
   - Reverse: Grinds when engaging? (No synchronizer on reverse—driver technique or worn gear)

3. **Transmission Noise Analysis:**
   - **In neutral, clutch released:** Rattling = input shaft bearing or pilot bearing
   - **In neutral, clutch depressed:** Noise stops = confirms bearing diagnosis
   - **In gear, under load:** Whining = gear wear or bearing failure
   - **During shifts:** Grinding = synchronizer failure

4. **Shift Linkage Check:**
   - External linkage loose or worn? (Feels like internal problem but isn't)
   - Shift cable stretched? (Common on older vehicles)

**Real Scenario from My Experience:**

Customer complaint: "Second gear grinds during upshifts, especially when cold."

**My road test findings:**
- Cold engine: Second gear grinds 80% of upshifts
- Warm engine: Second gear grinds 30% of upshifts
- All other gears: Smooth, no issues
- Noise in neutral: None
- Clutch operation: Normal

**Diagnosis:** Second gear synchronizer cone worn. Cold transmission oil (thicker viscosity) makes synchronizer work harder, exposing wear. Warm oil (thinner) helps worn synchronizer function better temporarily.

**Part 2: Oil Analysis and External Inspection (10 min)**

**Before Disassembly, Check the Oil**

Drain the transmission oil into a clean pan. What you find tells a story:

**Oil Condition Analysis:**

| Finding | Meaning | Next Steps |
|---------|---------|------------|
| Clean, amber oil at correct level | Good lubrication, internal wear less likely | Proceed with disassembly to confirm diagnosis |
| Metal particles (fine, magnetic) | Bearing wear (steel particles) | Expect bearing replacement, check for root cause |
| Bronze particles (non-magnetic) | Synchronizer wear (brass synchro rings) | Confirms synchronizer diagnosis |
| Gear oil low level | Leak or inadequate service, caused accelerated wear | Find leak source, expect multiple component damage |
| Milky, contaminated oil | Water intrusion (seal failure) | Expect corrosion, rust on components |

**I once drained oil from a Hyundai i20 transmission:**
- Bronze particles throughout
- Low oil level (1.5 liters instead of 2.0 liters)
- Customer never serviced transmission in 80,000 km

**Result:** All three synchronizers worn, two bearings damaged. If oil had been changed at 40,000 km intervals (manufacturer spec), only synchronizers would need replacement. **Lack of maintenance turned a ₹12,000 repair into a ₹28,000 repair.**

**Part 3: Transmission Removal (10 min)**

**Systematic Removal Procedure (Maruti Swift 1.2L Example):**

1. **Safety First:**
   - Vehicle on lift, secured with jack stands
   - Battery disconnected (prevent starter engagement)
   - Drain transmission oil into proper container

2. **Disconnection Sequence:**
   - Shift linkage (rod or cable—photograph position before removal)
   - Clutch slave cylinder (hydraulic line or pushrod)
   - Reverse light switch connector
   - Vehicle Speed Sensor (VSS) connector
   - Driveshafts (mark position, support with wire—do NOT let hang on CV joints)
   - Engine mounts (support engine with jack before removing transmission mount)

3. **Transmission-to-Engine Separation:**
   - Remove bell housing bolts (note different lengths—take photo)
   - Support transmission with jack
   - Slide transmission straight back (do NOT let input shaft hang on clutch disc)
   - Lower carefully (transmissions are heavy: 35-50 kg)

**Common Mistakes I See:**

- **Mistake 1:** Letting driveshafts hang unsupported—damages CV joints (₹3,000 replacement cost per side)
- **Mistake 2:** Forcing transmission during removal—bends clutch disc, damages input shaft bearing
- **Mistake 3:** Not photographing shift linkage—can't reassemble correctly, customer complains of "different shifting feel"

**Part 4: Systematic Disassembly (20 min)**

**Now the critical work begins. This is where systematic approach matters most.**

**Disassembly Sequence (5-Speed Manual Transmission):**

**Step 1: External Components**
- Clutch release bearing and fork
- Bell housing/front cover
- Shift linkage and selector mechanism (photograph position)
- Rear extension housing

**Step 2: Shift Rail and Fork Removal**
- Remove detent springs and balls (careful—springs can launch across room)
- Slide out shift rails in correct sequence (reverse → 5th/4th → 3rd/2nd → 1st)
- Mark shift fork positions (not interchangeable between gears)

**The Lesson I Learned the Hard Way:**

I once removed shift rails without marking them. During reassembly, I mixed up 2nd and 3rd gear forks. The transmission shifted, but the detent engagement was wrong—gears would jump out under load.

**Two hours of rework because I didn't spend 30 seconds marking components.**

Now I use paint pens to mark everything: shift forks, bearing caps, case halves.

**Step 3: Case Separation**
- Remove case bolts (note different lengths and positions)
- Use soft mallet to tap case halves apart (never pry—damages sealing surface)
- Lift top case half carefully (gears, shafts, and bearings may fall)

**Step 4: Shaft and Gear Removal**
- Remove output shaft assembly (lift straight up with gears attached)
- Remove input shaft and bearing
- Remove countershaft (lay out all components in sequence on clean bench)

**Organization is Critical:**

I use magnetic trays with labels:
- "Input Shaft Components"
- "Output Shaft Gears—1st to 5th"
- "Synchronizers—By Gear"
- "Bearings—By Position"
- "Fasteners—By Length"

**Why?** A 5-speed transmission has 40-60 individual parts. Mix them up, and reassembly becomes a nightmare.

**Part 5: Component Inspection and Measurement (20 min)**

**This is where your Week 1 measurement skills come back.**

**Synchronizer Inspection (Most Common Failure):**

**What is a Synchronizer?**

It's a friction cone that matches the speed of the gear to the shaft before the dog teeth engage. Without synchronizers, you'd have to "double clutch" every shift like old trucks.

**Synchronizer Components:**
- Brass synchro ring (friction cone)
- Hub and sleeve (sliding engagement mechanism)
- Blocker ring (prevents engagement until speeds match)

**Measurement Procedure:**

1. **Synchro Ring-to-Gear Cone Clearance:**
   - Install synchro ring on gear cone
   - Measure gap with feeler gauge
   - **Specification:** 0.8-1.5mm (new), Reject if < 0.5mm
   - **What I measured on Swift:** 0.3mm (2nd gear), 0.9mm (other gears)
   - **Decision:** Replace 2nd gear synchronizer assembly

2. **Blocker Ring Teeth Wear:**
   - Visual inspection for rounded teeth
   - Measure tooth height if specification available
   - Reject if teeth are rounded or shortened

**Bearing Inspection:**

**Types in Manual Transmission:**
- Tapered roller bearings (input shaft, output shaft)
- Ball bearings (countershaft, some designs)
- Needle bearings (inside gear bores)

**Inspection Method:**

1. **Roughness Test:**
   - Clean bearing thoroughly (solvent + compressed air)
   - Rotate inner race by hand
   - Smooth rotation = acceptable
   - Grinding, clicking, rough spots = replace

2. **Visual Inspection:**
   - Pitting on races or rollers = replace
   - Discoloration (blue/brown) = overheating, replace
   - Rust or corrosion = replace

**I once reused a bearing that felt "slightly rough":**

Customer returned after 5,000 km—bearing failed completely, damaged case. I had to replace the entire transmission housing.

**Lesson: If in doubt, replace bearings. The ₹800 bearing cost is nothing compared to ₹25,000 housing replacement.**

**Gear Tooth Inspection:**

**Check for:**
- Pitting (small craters on tooth surface)
- Spalling (chunks missing from tooth)
- Excessive wear on one side (indicates misalignment)

**Measurement:**
- Gear backlash (0.10-0.25mm typical)
- Use dial indicator, measure at multiple teeth

**Shift Fork Wear:**

**Measurement Points:**
- Fork pad thickness (wears from contact with sliding sleeve)
- Fork shaft wear (wears from repeated movement)
- **Spec Example (Maruti):** Fork pad thickness minimum 5.5mm, replace if < 5.0mm

## Assessment Content

### Quiz Questions (5 Questions - PowerPoint Slide Format)

**Question 1: Diagnostic Analysis (Analyze Level)**

A customer complains: "Second gear grinds during upshifts when cold, but improves when warm."

Based on this symptom, what is the MOST likely cause?

A) Clutch hydraulic system failure
B) Second gear synchronizer cone wear
C) Shift linkage misadjustment
D) Input shaft bearing failure

**Answer:** B) Second gear synchronizer cone wear

**Explanation:** Cold transmission oil (higher viscosity) makes worn synchronizer work harder, exposing wear through grinding. Warm oil (lower viscosity) temporarily helps worn synchronizer function better. This temperature-dependent symptom is classic synchronizer wear. Clutch issues affect all gears equally. Shift linkage doesn't change with temperature. Bearing failure creates noise in neutral, not during shifts.

---

**Question 2: Component Measurement (Apply Level)**

You measure the synchronizer ring-to-gear cone clearance on a 5-speed transmission and get the following readings:

- 1st gear: 1.1mm
- 2nd gear: 0.4mm
- 3rd gear: 1.0mm
- 4th gear: 0.9mm
- 5th gear: 1.2mm

Specification: 0.8-1.5mm (new), Reject if < 0.5mm

Which synchronizer requires replacement?

A) 1st gear only
B) 2nd gear only
C) 5th gear only
D) All synchronizers

**Answer:** B) 2nd gear only

**Explanation:** 2nd gear clearance (0.4mm) is below reject limit (0.5mm), indicating excessive wear. All other gears are within acceptable range (0.8-1.5mm). Only the worn synchronizer requires replacement—replacing functional components wastes customer money.

---

**Question 3: Oil Analysis Interpretation (Analyze Level)**

During transmission disassembly, you drain the oil and find bronze-colored particles (non-magnetic) throughout the fluid. Oil level is normal.

What does this finding indicate?

A) Gear tooth damage (steel particles)
B) Bearing failure (steel particles)
C) Synchronizer wear (brass particles)
D) Case cracking (aluminum particles)

**Answer:** C) Synchronizer wear (brass particles)

**Explanation:** Bronze/brass particles (non-magnetic) come from synchronizer rings, which are made of brass/bronze alloy. Steel particles (magnetic) indicate gear or bearing wear. This finding confirms internal synchronizer wear before disassembly, helping focus inspection efforts.

---

**Question 4: Disassembly Procedure (Apply Level)**

What is the FIRST step when separating manual transmission case halves after removing all external components and shift rails?

A) Use pry bar to separate case halves
B) Remove all case bolts and use soft mallet to tap case halves apart
C) Heat case with torch to expand metal
D) Remove input shaft first, then separate cases

**Answer:** B) Remove all case bolts and use soft mallet to tap case halves apart

**Explanation:** Correct procedure: Remove all case bolts (note different lengths), then use soft mallet to gently tap case halves apart. NEVER use pry bar (damages sealing surface causing oil leaks). Heating is unnecessary and dangerous. Input shaft is removed AFTER case separation.

---

**Question 5: Safety and Best Practice (Understand Level)**

When removing a transmission from a vehicle, why must the driveshafts be supported and NOT allowed to hang unsupported?

A) Driveshaft weight will damage transmission output shaft
B) Unsupported driveshaft damages CV joints (₹3,000+ repair per side)
C) Driveshaft will leak transmission fluid
D) Hanging driveshaft prevents transmission removal

**Answer:** B) Unsupported driveshaft damages CV joints (₹3,000+ repair per side)

**Explanation:** CV joints (constant velocity joints) on driveshafts are precision components designed for specific angles. Allowing driveshaft to hang under its own weight hyperextends CV joint, damaging internal bearings and races. This creates expensive additional repair. Always support driveshafts with wire or hooks during transmission removal.

23. Install output shaft assembly (verify gear engagement)
24. Install input shaft with bearing

26. Install case halves and torque bolts in correct sequence
27. Install shift rails and forks (verify correct orientation using marks)
28. Install detent springs and balls (careful—spring tension)
29. Install rear extension housing and front bell housing

**Phase 6 - Bench Testing and Verification (45 min):**
31. Rotate input and output shafts by hand (verify smooth rotation, no binding)
32. Manually operate shift mechanism through all gears
33. Verify each gear engages smoothly without force
34. Check for any unusual noise or resistance
35. Verify shift pattern matches original operation

**Success Criteria:**
- **Disassembly:** Completed systematically with all components organized and labeled; no damaged parts
- **Measurement Accuracy:** All measurements within ±0.02mm of instructor's calibrated measurements
- **Diagnosis Accuracy:** Worn component identification matches instructor's assessment (minimum 90% accuracy)
- **Reassembly Quality:** All gears engage smoothly on bench test; no binding or unusual noise
- **Torque Compliance:** All fasteners torqued to specification (instructor spot-checks 5 critical bolts)
- **Customer Estimate:** Parts list and labor estimate completed with ±15% accuracy vs. actual market prices

**Common Challenges (Instructor Will Address):**

1. **Shift Rail Installation:** Incorrect sequence causes detent misalignment—gears jump out
   - **Solution:** Follow removal sequence in reverse; verify detent ball alignment before tightening

2. **Bearing Installation Damage:** Wrong driver size mushrooms bearing race
   - **Solution:** Use proper bearing driver that contacts inner race only; press evenly

3. **Case Bolt Confusion:** Different length bolts in wrong positions
   - **Solution:** Photograph bolt positions during disassembly; organize by length in labeled containers

4. **Synchro Ring Orientation:** Installing backward causes poor shift quality
   - **Solution:** Note chamfer direction during disassembly; match to gear cone taper

5. **Excessive Gasket Sealer:** Sealer squeezes into case, blocks oil passages
   - **Solution:** Thin, even bead on mating surface; wipe excess before curing

**Safety Reminders:**
- Wear safety glasses (detent springs under tension can launch)
- Use proper lifting technique (transmissions weigh 35-50 kg)
- Secure transmission on bench (prevent rolling or falling)
- Use snap ring pliers correctly (pliers can slip and cause hand injury)
- Keep workspace organized (scattered parts cause tripping hazards)
- Use bearing pullers and drivers correctly (improper use damages components and causes injury)

*Example for Slide 14 (Real Scenario: Swift 2nd Gear Grinding):*
"This slide shows the exact symptom analysis I performed on that Swift transmission I mentioned. Notice the temperature correlation: 80% grinding when cold, only 30% when warm. This temperature dependency is the key diagnostic clue. Cold oil has higher viscosity—think of honey versus water. The thicker cold oil makes the worn synchronizer work harder to match speeds, exposing the wear through grinding. As the oil warms and thins, it helps the worn synchro function better temporarily. This is classic synchronizer wear. If it were a clutch problem, temperature wouldn't matter—it would be consistent. If it were a linkage problem, it might be worse when warm due to thermal expansion. Always pay attention to when the symptom occurs—it guides your diagnosis."

*Example for Slide 39 (Synchro Ring-to-Gear Cone Clearance Measurement):*
"This photo shows the exact measurement technique you'll use this afternoon. Install the synchro ring on the gear cone, then use a feeler gauge to measure the gap between the ring and the gear face. Insert gauges starting from small (0.5mm) and work up until you find the largest gauge that fits. That's your clearance. For this Swift transmission, specification is 0.8-1.5mm when new. Reject limit is 0.5mm. Why? Below 0.5mm, the friction surface is so worn that it can't generate enough friction to match speeds—you get grinding. This is a precision measurement—your Week 1 feeler gauge skills come back here. Clean both surfaces before measuring, ensure ring is seated squarely, and measure at 3 points around the cone. Take the smallest reading—that's the most worn point."
