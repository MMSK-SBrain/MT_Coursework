**Phase 2: Repair Execution - Boost Hose Replacement (90 min)**

Back in Bay 3, you organize your workspace:
- Parts laid out on clean bench
- Tools organized: Screwdrivers, pliers, torque wrench, hose clamp pliers
- Service manual open to "Turbocharger Boost Hose Replacement" section
- Torque specifications written on whiteboard for easy reference

**Step 1: Remove Old Boost Hoses**

You start systematically:

1. **Disconnect battery negative terminal** (safety: prevents accidental short circuits)
2. **Remove engine cover** (4 clips, lift off)
3. **Identify hose routing** (take photos before removal for reassembly reference—smart move from Week 1 training)
4. **Loosen hose clamps:**
   - Inlet hose from turbo compressor: 2 worm-drive clamps
   - Outlet hose from turbo to intercooler: 2 spring clamps
   - Intercooler to throttle body: 2 worm-drive clamps

**Challenge Encountered:**

One clamp is seized (corrosion from heat cycles). You try to loosen it, but the screw head starts to strip.

**Your Thought Process:**

*Don't force it. Forcing it will strip the head completely, then I'll have to cut it off and waste time.*

You remember Week 2 training: **Apply penetrating oil, wait 10 minutes, try again.**

You spray WD-40, move to another task while waiting (EGR removal prep), return 10 minutes later. Clamp loosens easily.

*Patience. Systematic approach. This is professional work.*

5. **Remove all three old hoses**
6. **Inspect turbo connection points:** Clean surfaces, no carbon buildup, threads undamaged

**Step 2: Install New Boost Hoses**

You reference the photos you took during removal:

1. **Install inlet hose (turbo compressor to air filter outlet):**
   - Route hose correctly (avoid rubbing against engine parts)
   - Install worm-drive clamps (don't fully tighten yet—allow adjustment)
   - Position clamps away from heat sources (exhaust manifold)

2. **Install outlet hose (turbo compressor outlet to intercooler):**
   - This is the hose that had the split—NEW hose has no cracks or damage
   - Use spring clamps provided in kit (proper OE design)
   - Ensure full seating on both turbo and intercooler connections

3. **Install intercooler-to-throttle-body hose:**
   - Route to avoid interference with engine movement
   - Install worm-drive clamps

**Step 3: Torque Clamps to Specification**

Service manual specification: **Worm-drive clamps: 3.5 Nm (30 in-lbs)**

You use a small torque screwdriver (inch-pound range):
- Torque each worm-drive clamp to 30 in-lbs
- Check for even pressure around circumference (hose not deformed)

**Step 4: Leak Test**

Before moving on, you test your work:

1. **Visual inspection:** All hoses seated fully, no twisted sections
2. **Pressure test:**
   - Reconnect battery (needed for turbo operation)
   - Start engine
   - Increase RPM to 2,500 (creates boost pressure)
   - Listen for air leaks (none)
   - Feel connections for escaping air (none)
   - **Result:** No leaks, repair successful

**Boost Hose Replacement Complete: 90 minutes**

*Right on schedule. Now the EGR valve cleaning—slightly more complex.*

**Phase 3: EGR Valve Cleaning (120 min)**

**Step 1: Remove EGR Valve**

The EGR valve is located on the intake manifold, connected to the exhaust manifold via an EGR pipe.

**Removal Procedure:**

1. **Disconnect electrical connector** (EGR position sensor)
2. **Remove vacuum line** (EGR control vacuum)
3. **Disconnect EGR pipe from exhaust manifold:**
   - 2 bolts, 12mm
   - **Challenge:** Bolts are in hot section, very tight from heat cycles
   - Apply penetrating oil, wait, use breaker bar for leverage
   - Bolts come out successfully (no breakage—you applied proper technique)
4. **Remove EGR valve from intake manifold:**
   - 3 bolts, 10mm
   - Remove gasket (will replace with new)
5. **EGR valve removed**

**Step 2: Inspect Valve and Clean**

You bring the EGR valve to your workbench.

**Visual Inspection:**

The valve is heavily carboned—black buildup covers the valve seat and pintle. This confirms your diagnosis: carbon prevents valve from fully closing.

**Cleaning Procedure:**

1. **Soak valve in EGR cleaner:**
   - Spray EGR cleaner into valve passages
   - Let sit for 15 minutes (cleaner dissolves carbon)

2. **Mechanical cleaning:**
   - Use wire brush to scrub valve seat
   - Use pick tool to carefully remove carbon from pintle (don't damage precision surfaces)
   - Use compressed air to blow out loosened carbon

3. **Second soak and rinse:**
   - Spray again with EGR cleaner
   - Let sit 10 minutes
   - Rinse with brake cleaner (removes residue)
   - Dry with compressed air

**Inspection After Cleaning:**

Valve seat is now clean—no carbon buildup. Pintle moves freely. This valve will now fully close.

**Step 3: Clean Intake Manifold EGR Port**

While valve is out, you clean the intake manifold EGR port (where valve mounts):
- Scrape carbon from port opening with gasket scraper
- Clean sealing surface with brake cleaner
- Ensure gasket surface is flat and clean

**Step 4: Reinstall EGR Valve**

1. **Install new gasket** on intake manifold EGR port
2. **Position EGR valve** on gasket
3. **Install 3 mounting bolts** finger-tight
4. **Torque bolts in sequence:** (Service manual specification: 12 Nm)
   - Torque in star pattern to ensure even gasket compression
   - Verify with torque wrench: 12 Nm (all three bolts)
5. **Reconnect EGR pipe to exhaust manifold:**
   - New gasket on EGR pipe (prevent exhaust leak)
   - Install 2 bolts, torque to 25 Nm
6. **Reconnect vacuum line**
7. **Reconnect electrical connector** (EGR position sensor)

**EGR Valve Cleaning and Installation Complete: 120 minutes**

**Presentation Section 1: What We Found (3 min)**

**Ravi (showing tablet with photos):**

"Mr. Patel, based on your complaint of loss of power and black smoke, I performed a comprehensive diagnosis yesterday. Here's what I found:

**First**, the primary issue: your turbocharger boost hose had a split (shows photo on tablet—close-up of split hose with visible crack). This hose carries pressurized air from the turbocharger to the engine. When it splits, boost pressure leaks out. Let me show you the data (shows scan tool screenshot):

- Your turbo was trying to produce 150 kPa of boost pressure
- But because of the leak, only 90 kPa was reaching the engine
- That's 40% loss of boost pressure, which directly caused your loss of power

**Second**, I found your EGR valve was stuck partially open due to carbon buildup (shows photo of carbon-clogged EGR valve). The EGR valve should CLOSE during acceleration to allow maximum oxygen for combustion. When it's stuck partially open, exhaust gas dilutes the intake charge, reducing combustion efficiency. This caused two problems:
1. **Loss of power** (less oxygen = less power)
2. **Black smoke** (insufficient oxygen + diesel fuel = unburned fuel = black smoke)

These two faults together explain all your symptoms."

**Instructor (as Mr. Patel, engaged):** "Oh, I can see the crack in the hose in that photo. And that valve is really dirty with carbon."

---

**Presentation Section 2: Why It Failed - Root Cause (2 min)**

**Ravi:**

"You asked a great question when you first brought the vehicle in: 'Why did this happen?' Let me explain:

**The boost hose split** because rubber degrades over time, especially under heat and pressure. Your Creta has 82,000 km—the hoses are original from 2018. Rubber hardens and cracks after 6-8 years of heat cycles. This is age-related wear.

**The EGR valve stuck** because diesel engines produce carbon deposits from exhaust. Over time, carbon builds up inside the EGR system. Your service history shows the EGR has never been cleaned in 82,000 km. Hyundai recommends EGR cleaning every 40,000 km for diesel engines to prevent this exact problem.

**Bottom line:** These were wear-related issues. Normal maintenance could have prevented the EGR problem, and periodic inspection could have caught the hose before it split."

**Instructor (as Mr. Patel):** "So this is normal wear for the age and kilometers?"

**Ravi:** "Exactly. Your vehicle wasn't abused—this is expected maintenance for a diesel at this mileage."

---

**Presentation Section 3: What We Repaired (4 min)**

**Ravi (showing photos of repair work):**

"Here's what we did to fix your Creta:

**Repair #1: Replaced all turbocharger boost hoses**

I didn't just replace the split hose—I replaced the complete hose kit (shows photo of new hoses installed). Why all of them? Because if one hose deteriorated, the others are the same age and will fail soon. Replacing all three prevents you from coming back in 2 months with another split hose. This is the complete fix.

- Parts: Hyundai OE boost hose kit (₹2,800)
- Labor: 1.5 hours
- Result: No more boost pressure leaks

**Repair #2: Cleaned and serviced EGR valve**

I removed the EGR valve, cleaned all carbon deposits using professional EGR cleaner, inspected the valve pintle and seat, and reinstalled with new gaskets (shows photos: before carbon buildup, after cleaning—valve clean and shiny).

- Parts: Gaskets and cleaner (₹800)
- Labor: 2 hours
- Result: EGR valve now closes fully during acceleration

**Additional work performed:**
- Replaced intake manifold gasket (prevents vacuum leak)
- Cleaned EGR port on intake manifold
- Torqued all fasteners to Hyundai specifications
- Total labor time: 4.5 hours (including diagnosis and verification)"

**Instructor (as Mr. Patel):** "So the valve is reused, not replaced? Is that okay?"

**Ravi:** "Excellent question. I inspected the valve carefully. The valve itself—the metal housing and solenoid—is in perfect condition. The only issue was carbon buildup, which is 100% cleanable. Replacing the valve would cost ₹8,500 more with no performance benefit. Cleaning is the professional solution and saves you money while fully restoring function."
