#### Rising Action (Guided Journey) - 90 minutes

**Part 1: STEP 1 - VERIFY THE COMPLAINT (20 min)**

"The first rule of diagnostics: Never trust the customer's diagnosis. Trust their description, verify the symptom."

**Customer Statement:** "My engine misfires and check engine light is on."

**What NOT to do:**
- Read the code and start replacing parts
- Assume the customer is correct about the symptom

**What PROFESSIONALS do:**
1. **Interview the customer with specific questions:**
   - When does the misfire occur? (Cold start? Highway? Idle? Acceleration?)
   - How long has it been happening?
   - Any recent repairs or services?
   - Any other symptoms? (Noise, smoke, poor fuel economy?)

2. **Reproduce the symptom:**
   - Perform road test under conditions customer described
   - Feel the misfire yourself
   - Note engine behavior (stumble, rough idle, power loss)

**For the Maruti Ciaz Case:**
- Customer said: "Misfires during acceleration, especially when cold"
- Road test confirmed: Noticeable hesitation and rough running during acceleration from stop
- Check engine light: Solid (not flashing = not severe misfire)

**Why This Step Matters:**
If you can't reproduce the symptom, your chance of fixing it drops to near zero. Many technicians skip this step because "the customer already told us the problem." Wrong. The customer tells you the symptom. YOU diagnose the problem.

**Key Tools:** Road test checklist, customer interview form, symptom reproduction documentation

---

**Part 2: STEP 2 - GATHER INFORMATION (25 min)**

"This is where your scan tool becomes your diagnostic partner, not just a code reader."

**Information Sources:**

**A) Scan Tool Data:**
- Retrieve DTCs (Diagnostic Trouble Codes)
- Check freeze frame data (engine conditions when code set)
- Review live data (sensor values, fuel trims, misfire counters)
- Check readiness monitors

**For the Maruti Ciaz:**
- DTC: P0301 (Cylinder 1 Misfire Detected)
- Freeze frame: Occurred at 2,500 RPM, coolant temp 65°C, load 45%
- Live data: Cylinder 1 misfire counter climbing rapidly
- Fuel trim: Short-term +12% (slightly rich compensation)
- O2 sensor: Cycling normally
- MAF sensor: 4.2 g/sec at idle (normal for 1.5L engine)

**B) Service History:**
Check service records:
- Last service: 60,000 km (oil change, air filter)
- No timing belt service yet (due at 80,000 km per Maruti spec)
- No previous engine repairs

**C) Visual Inspection:**
- Check for obvious issues: vacuum leaks, loose connections, damaged wiring
- Inspect spark plug wires, coil boots, injector connectors
- Look for oil leaks, coolant leaks

**D) Technical Service Bulletins (TSBs):**
- Check if manufacturer has known issues for this model/symptom
- Look for recall campaigns

**For the Maruti Ciaz:**
Visual inspection revealed: All connectors secure, no vacuum leaks visible, no oil leaks

**Common Mistake I See:**
Technicians scan the code, see P0301, and immediately think "bad spark plug or coil." They skip the rest of the information gathering. They don't look at live data, don't check service history, don't do compression test.

**Professional Approach:**
Gather ALL available information before forming hypothesis. Information is free. Parts are expensive.

**Key Tools:** Scan tool with live data capability, service history records, TSB database, inspection checklist

---

**Part 3: STEP 3 - ANALYZE DATA AND FORM HYPOTHESIS (15 min)**

"This is where diagnostic thinking happens. We analyze all the information and form a testable hypothesis."

**The Thinking Process:**

**What We Know:**
- Cylinder 1 misfire (confirmed by scan tool and road test)
- Occurs during acceleration, especially when cold
- Fuel trim slightly rich (engine compensating)
- No obvious visual issues
- No recent repairs

**What Causes Misfire?**

A cylinder misfires when it lacks one of three things:
1. **Air/Fuel mixture** (fuel injector issue, vacuum leak)
2. **Compression** (worn valve, blown head gasket, worn piston rings)
3. **Spark** (spark plug, ignition coil, wiring)

**Analyzing the Clues:**

**Clue 1:** Misfire is specific to ONE cylinder (not random)
- This rules out: General fuel delivery issues, MAF sensor, throttle body
- This points to: Component specific to cylinder 1

**Clue 2:** Worse when cold, improves slightly when warm
- This suggests: Mechanical issue (oil viscosity, valve clearance, compression-related)
- Less likely: Ignition (doesn't typically change with temperature)

**Clue 3:** Fuel trim is slightly rich, not lean
- This suggests: Engine is compensating for loss of power
- If injector was clogged, fuel trim would be LEAN

**Hypothesis Formation:**

Based on analysis, most likely causes (in order of probability):
1. **Compression loss in cylinder 1** (worn valve, valve not sealing)
2. Ignition coil failure (but less likely given temperature relationship)
3. Fuel injector partially clogged (but fuel trim doesn't support this)

**This is Critical Thinking:**
We're not guessing. We're using evidence to form a ranked list of possibilities. Now we test the most likely hypothesis first.

**Key Skill:** Pattern recognition, logical deduction, probability ranking

---

**Part 4: STEP 4 - ISOLATE THE FAULT (20 min)**

"Now we test our hypothesis with targeted tests. We don't replace parts until we have proof."

**Testing Hypothesis 1: Compression Loss**

**Test:** Compression test on all cylinders

**Procedure:**
1. Remove all spark plugs
2. Disable fuel and ignition
3. Install compression gauge in cylinder 1
4. Crank engine for 5 compression strokes
5. Record reading
6. Repeat for cylinders 2, 3, 4

**Maruti Ciaz Results:**
- Cylinder 1: 105 psi
- Cylinder 2: 175 psi
- Cylinder 3: 180 psi
- Cylinder 4: 178 psi

**Specification:** 170-190 psi (Maruti 1.5L K-series engine)
**Maximum variation:** 10% between cylinders

**Analysis:**
Cylinder 1 is 70 psi BELOW specification and 40% lower than other cylinders. **FAULT ISOLATED: Compression loss in cylinder 1.**

**Follow-Up Test: Wet Compression Test**

To determine if compression loss is from valves or piston rings:
1. Add small amount of oil through spark plug hole (coats piston rings)
2. Retest compression

**Result:**
- Cylinder 1 wet test: 110 psi (barely increased)

**Conclusion:** Compression loss is from **valve not sealing**, not worn piston rings (if rings were worn, oil would seal them temporarily and compression would increase significantly).

**Root Cause Identification:**

Visual inspection of camshaft (remove valve cover):
- Cylinder 1 intake cam lobe shows significant wear
- Lobe height measured with micrometer: 8.2mm (specification: 9.5mm)
- Valve barely opening due to worn cam lobe

**FAULT CONFIRMED: Worn camshaft lobe on cylinder 1 intake valve**

**This is Professional Diagnostics:**
- Total diagnostic time: 30 minutes
- Cost of testing: ₹0 (compression gauge is shop equipment)
- Confidence level: 100% (tested and confirmed)
- Parts replaced: 0 (until fault is confirmed)

**Compare to Part-Swapper:**
- Total diagnostic time: 6 hours across multiple visits
- Cost of wrong parts: ₹16,300
- Confidence level: 0% (guessing)
- Customer satisfaction: 0%

**Key Tools:** Compression gauge, inspection mirror, valve cover removal tools, micrometer

**Part 5: STEP 5 - REPAIR AND VERIFY (10 min)**

"Only after confirming the fault do we repair. And we ALWAYS verify the fix."

**Repair Plan for Maruti Ciaz:**
- Replace camshaft (₹8,500 part + ₹3,000 labor = ₹11,500 total)
- Inspect all cam lobes during installation
- Set valve clearances to specification
- Replace timing chain and tensioner as preventive maintenance (recommended at this mileage)

**Repair Execution:**
1. Remove timing cover and timing chain
2. Remove camshaft
3. Inspect journals for wear
4. Install new camshaft with assembly lube
5. Install new timing chain
6. Set valve clearances: 0.15mm intake, 0.20mm exhaust (per Maruti spec)
7. Reinstall valve cover with new gasket
8. Torque all fasteners per specification

**Verification Steps:**

**Immediate Verification:**
1. Rotate engine by hand 2 complete revolutions (check for interference)
2. Recheck valve clearances after rotation
3. Start engine and listen for abnormal sounds
4. Check for oil leaks

**Performance Verification:**
1. Clear DTCs from scan tool
2. Perform road test under same conditions as initial complaint
3. Monitor misfire counters in live data
4. Verify smooth acceleration from cold start
5. Check fuel trim returns to normal (±5%)

**Maruti Ciaz Result:**
- Smooth idle and acceleration
- Zero misfire counts after 20-minute drive
- Fuel trim: +2% (normal)
- Customer complaint: RESOLVED

**Why Verification Matters:**
Never hand a vehicle back to customer without personally verifying the complaint is resolved. Your reputation depends on it.

**Feature 2: Freeze Frame Analysis**

**What is Freeze Frame?**
When a DTC sets, the ECU captures a "snapshot" of engine conditions at that exact moment. It's like a crime scene photo—shows you what was happening when the fault occurred.

**Maruti Ciaz Freeze Frame Data (P0301):**
- RPM: 2,500
- Coolant temp: 65°C (engine warming up)
- Load: 45% (moderate acceleration)
- Fuel trim: +12% (slightly rich)
- Vehicle speed: 35 km/h
- Throttle position: 28%

**What This Tells Us:**
- Fault occurs during acceleration (not idle)
- Occurs when engine is warming up (not fully hot)
- Moderate load (not heavy load like highway passing)
- Fuel trim shows engine compensating

This matches customer complaint: "Misfire during acceleration, especially when cold."

**Professional Tip:**
Always check freeze frame BEFORE clearing codes. It's evidence that disappears if you clear DTCs first.

---

**Feature 3: Bidirectional Controls (Active Testing)**

**What are Bidirectional Controls?**
Ability to command the ECU to activate components ON/OFF while vehicle is stationary. Tests if actuators physically work.

**Common Applications:**
1. **Fuel injector pulse test:** Activate injector, listen for clicking (proves electrical circuit and injector physically work)
2. **Ignition coil test:** Fire coil, look for spark (proves coil and wiring functional)
3. **Cooling fan relay:** Command fan ON/OFF (tests relay, wiring, fan motor)
4. **VVT solenoid test:** Actuate solenoid, listen for operation
5. **ABS pump motor:** Run pump, verify hydraulic pressure builds
6. **Turbo wastegate test:** Open/close wastegate (diesel applications)

**Example: Diagnosing Cooling Fan Not Working**

**Traditional Method (time-consuming):**
1. Check fuse (5 min)
2. Check relay (5 min)
3. Check wiring to fan (10 min)
4. Check fan motor (5 min)
**Total: 25 minutes**

**Professional Method (bidirectional control):**
1. Connect scan tool
2. Select "Cooling fan relay" bidirectional control
3. Command fan ON
   - If fan runs: Electrical system works, problem is sensor or ECU logic
   - If fan doesn't run: Electrical problem, now test relay/wiring/motor
**Total: 2 minutes to isolate electrical vs. control problem**

**Safety Note:**
Some bidirectional tests can be dangerous:
- Fuel pump (will spray fuel if lines disconnected)
- Injectors (will flood engine if left on too long)
- Throttle actuator (can create sudden throttle movement)

Always ensure vehicle is safe before using active tests.

---

**Feature 4: Readiness Monitors**

**What are Readiness Monitors?**
Self-tests the ECU runs to verify emission systems are functioning. Required for emission testing compliance (BS4/BS6 in India).

**The 11 Monitors:**
1. Misfire detection
2. Fuel system
3. Comprehensive component
4. Catalyst efficiency
5. Heated catalyst
6. Evaporative system
7. Secondary air system
8. A/C system refrigerant
9. Oxygen sensor
10. Oxygen sensor heater
11. EGR system

**Status:**
- **Ready:** Monitor has run and passed
- **Not Ready:** Monitor hasn't completed self-test yet
- **Not Supported:** Vehicle doesn't have this system

**Why This Matters:**

**After Clearing Codes:**
All monitors reset to "Not Ready." If customer immediately goes for emission test, vehicle will FAIL (not because emissions are bad, but because monitors haven't completed).

**Professional Responsibility:**
After any emission-related repair:
1. Clear codes
2. Perform drive cycle to set monitors to "Ready"
3. Verify all monitors ready before returning vehicle
4. Explain to customer: Vehicle is ready for emission test

**Maruti Ciaz Example:**
After camshaft replacement, I performed drive cycle:
- Idle 2 minutes (coolant temp monitor)
- Drive 15 minutes city (catalyst, O2 sensor monitors)
- Drive 10 minutes highway steady speed (EVAP monitor)
- Result: All monitors "Ready"

---

**Feature 5: Data Graphing for Intermittent Faults**

**The Hardest Diagnostic Challenge:**
Intermittent faults—problems that come and go unpredictably.

**Example Case: 2018 Hyundai Creta - Intermittent Stalling**

Customer complaint: "Engine stalls randomly, but restarts immediately. No pattern. Happens once a week."

**Challenge:**
Can't reproduce during shop visit. No DTCs stored. Customer frustrated.

**Professional Solution: Data Graphing**

1. Connect scan tool
2. Set up custom PID list: RPM, Fuel pressure, Crank sensor, Cam sensor, Throttle position
3. Set trigger: Record when RPM drops below 500 (indicates stall)
4. Give scan tool to customer for 1 week
5. Scan tool records data automatically when trigger occurs

**Result:**
Customer experienced stall 3 times during week. Scan tool captured all 3 events.

**Graph Analysis:**
- RPM dropped suddenly to zero
- Crank position sensor signal dropped to zero simultaneously
- Cam sensor continued normally
- Fuel pressure remained steady

**Diagnosis:** Failing crankshaft position sensor (intermittent internal short)

**Verification:**
Replaced crank sensor. Customer drove 2 weeks. No stalling. Problem solved.

**Without Data Graphing:**
This would have been impossible to diagnose. We would have guessed: fuel pump, ignition coil, ECU, wiring. Customer would have lost faith.

**With Data Graphing:**
One week of recording captured the evidence. Precise diagnosis. ₹1,500 repair. Customer thrilled.

### Story Integration (Additional 20 minutes - embedded in Part 4)

**Root Cause Analysis: The "5 Whys" Technique**

"Fixing the symptom is temporary. Finding the root cause prevents recurrence."

**Real Case: 2017 Tata Nexon - Repeated Turbo Failures**

A customer brought in a Tata Nexon diesel with failed turbocharger. This was the THIRD turbo failure in 18 months. Customer was furious: "Your workshop keeps replacing turbos but they keep failing!"

**Symptom-Level Repair (What Previous Technicians Did):**
1. Turbo failed (shaft play excessive, oil leaking)
2. Replaced turbo
3. Three months later: Turbo failed again
4. Replaced turbo again
5. Three months later: Turbo failed again

**Cost to Customer:** ₹85,000 (3 turbos × ₹25,000 + labor)
**Customer Satisfaction:** 0% (ready to sue workshop)

**The Problem:**
Previous technicians fixed the SYMPTOM (failed turbo) but never identified the ROOT CAUSE (why turbo keeps failing).

---

**Root Cause Analysis Using "5 Whys"**

**The 5 Whys Technique:**
Keep asking "why" until you reach the true root cause. Typically takes 5 iterations.

**Tata Nexon Case:**

**Problem:** Turbocharger failed

**Why #1:** Why did the turbocharger fail?
**Answer:** Turbo bearings wore out prematurely, causing shaft play and oil leaking.

**Why #2:** Why did the bearings wear out prematurely?
**Answer:** Lack of lubrication to turbo bearings.

**Why #3:** Why was there lack of lubrication?
**Answer:** Oil contamination with metal particles blocked turbo oil feed line.

**Why #4:** Why was oil contaminated with metal particles?
**Answer:** Engine had worn main bearings creating metal debris in oil.

**Why #5:** Why did engine have worn main bearings?
**Answer:** Customer was using wrong oil viscosity (20W-50 instead of specified 5W-30) and extending oil change intervals to 15,000 km instead of 10,000 km.

**ROOT CAUSE IDENTIFIED:**
Wrong oil specification + extended oil change intervals → engine bearing wear → metal contamination in oil → blocked turbo oil feed → turbo bearing failure

---

**The Complete Solution:**

**Repair Actions:**
1. Replace turbocharger (₹25,000)
2. Replace engine main bearings (₹15,000)
3. Flush entire oil system (₹2,000)
4. Install correct oil viscosity 5W-30 (specified by Tata)
5. Reset oil change interval reminder to 10,000 km

**Customer Education:**
- Explained why wrong oil caused turbo failures
- Showed customer worn bearings and metal contamination in oil
- Provided oil specification chart from owner's manual
- Set service reminder in phone app

**Total Cost:** ₹42,000 (comprehensive repair)

**Result:**
18 months later, turbo still functioning perfectly. Customer now follows oil change schedule religiously. Customer became loyal, referred 5 friends to our workshop.

**Comparison:**

**Symptom Repair:** ₹85,000 (3 failed repairs), angry customer, potential lawsuit
**Root Cause Repair:** ₹42,000 (one comprehensive repair), satisfied customer, referrals

---

**Root Cause Categories to Always Consider:**

When component fails, investigate these categories:

**1. Installation Error:**
- Wrong torque applied
- Gasket missing or incorrect
- Component not aligned properly
- Wiring connected incorrectly

**Example:** Cylinder head gasket keeps blowing → Root cause: Technician didn't follow torque sequence or used angle torque incorrectly

---

**2. Contamination:**
- Dirt in system
- Wrong fluid used
- Metal particles from wear
- Coolant in oil / oil in coolant

**Example:** Fuel injectors clogging repeatedly → Root cause: Fuel tank contaminated with dirt, fuel filter never replaced

---

**3. Normal Wear:**
- Component reached end of service life
- Preventive replacement needed for related components

**Example:** Timing belt broke → Root cause: Belt exceeded 80,000 km service life, tensioner also worn

---

**4. Abnormal Wear (Requires Investigation):**
- Component failed earlier than expected
- Find out WHY premature wear occurred

**Example:** Brake pads worn out at 15,000 km (should last 40,000 km) → Root cause: Caliper piston seized, brake dragging constantly

---

**5. Design Flaw (Rare but Important):**
- Known manufacturer defect
- Check Technical Service Bulletins (TSBs)
- May be covered under warranty extension

**Example:** 2016-2017 Maruti Baleno CVT judder → Root cause: Design flaw in CVT belt material, Maruti issued TSB for CVT fluid replacement

---

**6. Customer Usage Pattern:**
- Driving style causing premature wear
- Environmental conditions
- Lack of maintenance

**Example:** Clutch failure at 25,000 km → Root cause: Customer drives in heavy traffic daily, rides clutch constantly

---

**Documentation Template for Root Cause Analysis:**

Every diagnosis should include:

**1. Symptoms Observed:**
- Customer complaint (exact words)
- Symptoms verified during road test
- DTCs present

**2. Tests Performed:**
- Compression test: Cylinder 1 = 105 psi (spec 170-190)
- Visual inspection: Worn camshaft lobe on cylinder 1 intake
- Measurement: Cam lobe height 8.2mm (spec 9.5mm)

**3. Root Cause:**
- Worn camshaft lobe preventing proper valve operation

**4. Why Did It Fail? (5 Whys if needed)**
- Lobe wear due to lack of lubrication during cold starts (customer frequently started engine and drove immediately without warm-up)

**5. Corrective Action:**
- Replace camshaft
- Replace timing chain (preventive)
- Set valve clearances to spec
- Educate customer on proper warm-up procedure

**6. Verification:**
- Compression test after repair: All cylinders 175-180 psi
- Road test: No misfire, smooth acceleration
- Customer followed up 2 weeks later: Problem resolved

**7. Recommendations to Prevent Recurrence:**
- Use synthetic oil for better cold-start protection
- Allow 30-second idle before driving when cold
- Follow 10,000 km oil change intervals

---

**The Professional Mindset:**

**Symptom-Level Thinking:** "Turbo failed, replace turbo."
**Root Cause Thinking:** "Turbo failed. Why? What caused it to fail? What can we fix to prevent it from failing again?"

**This afternoon's diagnostic scenarios:**

When you identify the failed component, I will ask you: "Why did it fail?"

If you can't answer, you haven't finished the diagnosis. Don't just identify what's broken. Identify WHY it broke.

This is what separates master technicians from parts replacers.

### Quiz Questions (5 Questions - PowerPoint Slide Format)

**Question 1: Diagnostic Methodology (Apply Level)**

A customer reports "engine shaking at idle." What is the FIRST step in the 6-step diagnostic methodology?

A) Scan for DTCs using scan tool
B) Check spark plugs and ignition coils
C) Verify the complaint through road test or operation check
D) Replace engine mounts

**Answer:** C) Verify the complaint through road test or operation check
**Explanation:** Step 1 is always VERIFY the complaint. Reproduce the symptom yourself. Never skip this step and assume customer description is accurate.

---

**Question 2: Scan Tool Advanced Functions (Apply Level)**

You're diagnosing an intermittent stalling issue that occurs randomly once per week. Which scan tool function is MOST useful for capturing this fault?

A) Read DTCs
B) Clear DTCs
C) Data graphing with trigger recording
D) Bidirectional controls

**Answer:** C) Data graphing with trigger recording
**Explanation:** For intermittent faults that can't be reproduced in the shop, data graphing with trigger recording allows the scan tool to capture data automatically when the fault occurs. Set trigger (e.g., RPM drops below 500) and leave scan tool with customer.

---

**Question 3: Diagnostic Logic (Analyze Level)**

A Maruti Swift shows DTC P0301 (Cylinder 1 Misfire). Compression test results:
- Cyl 1: 110 psi
- Cyl 2: 175 psi
- Cyl 3: 178 psi
- Cyl 4: 180 psi

Wet compression test on Cyl 1: 115 psi (barely increased)

What is the MOST LIKELY root cause?

A) Worn piston rings
B) Blown head gasket
C) Valve not sealing properly
D) Faulty spark plug

**Answer:** C) Valve not sealing properly
**Explanation:** Low compression (110 vs 175-180 psi) with minimal increase during wet test indicates valve sealing issue. If piston rings were worn, wet test would show significant increase. Spark plug doesn't affect compression.

---

**Question 4: Root Cause Analysis (Evaluate Level)**

A customer's turbocharger failed twice in 6 months. Using the "5 Whys" technique, you discover:
- Turbo bearings failed due to lack of lubrication
- Oil feed line was blocked with metal particles
- Engine has worn bearings creating metal debris

What is the COMPLETE repair solution?

A) Replace turbocharger only
B) Replace turbocharger and flush oil system
C) Replace turbocharger, engine bearings, and flush oil system
D) Replace engine

**Answer:** C) Replace turbocharger, engine bearings, and flush oil system
**Explanation:** Root cause is worn engine bearings creating contamination. Must fix root cause (bearings) and symptom (turbo) and remove contamination (flush) to prevent recurrence. Replacing turbo alone will result in third failure.

---

**Question 5: Professional Documentation (Apply Level)**

Which of the following is NOT required in professional diagnostic documentation?

A) Customer complaint in their exact words
B) Tests performed with results and measurements
C) Technician's personal opinion about customer's driving habits
D) Root cause and corrective action

**Answer:** C) Technician's personal opinion about customer's driving habits
**Explanation:** Documentation must include facts: complaint, symptoms verified, tests performed, measurements, diagnosis, repair, verification. Personal opinions are not professional and can create legal issues.

### Hands-On Exercise Preview (Afternoon Practical - 3 Hours)

**Exercise Title:** Systematic Diagnostic Challenge

**Objective:**
Apply the 6-step diagnostic methodology to diagnose a real vehicle fault using scan tool, testing equipment, and systematic approach.

**Step-by-Step Activity:**

**Setup (15 min):**
1. Each student assigned one vehicle with known fault (instructor pre-diagnosed)
2. Vehicles provided:
   - Maruti Swift - Check engine light, poor fuel economy
   - Hyundai i20 - Rough idle, misfire at idle only
   - Tata Nexon - Turbo underboost, loss of power
   - Mahindra Scorpio diesel - Hard starting when cold
   - Honda City - Intermittent stalling after warm-up
3. Each student receives:
   - Scan tool with live data capability
   - Repair order documentation template
   - Access to all shop tools and testing equipment
   - Service history for the vehicle
4. Time limit: 3 hours total

**Diagnostic Phase (2.5 hours):**

**STEP 1: VERIFY COMPLAINT (20 min)**
- Interview instructor (acting as customer) about symptoms
- Perform road test to reproduce symptom
- Document symptoms on repair order

**STEP 2: GATHER INFORMATION (30 min)**
- Scan for DTCs and record all codes
- Review freeze frame data
- Monitor live data (create custom PID list)
- Perform visual inspection
- Review service history
- Check for TSBs (provided by instructor)

**STEP 3: ANALYZE DATA (15 min)**
- Write hypothesis based on information gathered
- List most likely causes in order of probability
- Explain reasoning (show critical thinking)
- Present hypothesis to instructor BEFORE testing

**STEP 4: ISOLATE FAULT (45 min)**
- Perform targeted tests to confirm hypothesis:
  - Compression test (if engine mechanical suspected)
  - Voltage drop test (if electrical suspected)
  - Pressure test (if fuel/cooling system suspected)
  - Component measurement (if wear suspected)
- Use bidirectional controls if applicable
- Document all test results with measurements
- Identify root cause using 5 Whys if needed

**STEP 5: REPAIR (30 min)**
- Present diagnosis and repair plan to instructor for approval
- Perform repair following proper procedures
- Torque fasteners per specification
- Verify repair through road test or operation check
- Clear DTCs and verify no codes return

**STEP 6: DOCUMENT (10 min)**
- Complete repair order with all required elements:
  - Customer complaint (from Step 1)
  - Symptoms verified
  - Tests performed with results
  - Root cause identified
  - Repairs performed
  - Parts used
  - Verification of fix
  - Recommendations

**Final Presentation (30 min total, 6 min per student):**
- Present diagnosis to class as if presenting to customer
- Explain: What was wrong, why it failed, what you repaired, cost estimate
- Show evidence: Test results, measurements, photos if applicable
- Answer questions from instructor and classmates

**Success Criteria:**
- **Correct diagnosis:** Identified actual fault (not guessed)
- **Systematic methodology:** Followed all 6 steps in order
- **Critical thinking:** Hypothesis showed logical reasoning
- **Professional documentation:** Repair order complete and professional
- **Verification:** Confirmed repair resolves customer complaint
- **Time management:** Completed within 3 hours
- **Safety:** 100% compliance throughout

**Common Challenges (Instructor Will Address):**
1. **Jumping to conclusions:** Seeing DTC and immediately replacing parts without testing
2. **Skipping verification:** Not reproducing symptom before starting diagnosis
3. **Incomplete documentation:** Missing key elements in repair order
4. **Root cause neglect:** Identifying failed component but not WHY it failed

**Safety Reminders:**
- Chock wheels before road test
- Ensure proper ventilation when running engines in shop
- Disconnect battery when working on electrical systems
- Follow lockout/tagout procedures for safety
- Wear safety glasses during all testing
- Never work under unsupported vehicle
