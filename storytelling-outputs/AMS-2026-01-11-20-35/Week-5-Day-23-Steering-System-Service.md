#### Scenario 1: Hydraulic Power Steering Service (Honda City) - 75 minutes

**Part 1: Customer Consultation and Initial Inspection (10 min)**

Mrs. Patel explained, "Two months ago, steering started feeling slightly heavy when parking. I thought maybe it was just the car getting older. But last week, it became very difficult to park—I have to use both hands and put all my strength. And when I turn at low speed, there's a loud groaning noise. It's embarrassing when parking in my apartment complex."

Suresh asked diagnostic questions:
- "When does the heavy steering occur?" → "Mostly at parking speeds, less noticeable at highway speeds"
- "Any fluid leaks under the vehicle?" → "I see some oily spots on my parking space"
- "How long since power steering fluid was checked?" → "Never checked it, didn't know I had to"

**Initial Inspection Findings:**

1. **Fluid Level Check:**
   - Opened hood, checked power steering reservoir
   - Fluid level below "MIN" line (critically low)
   - Fluid color: Dark brown, contaminated (should be reddish)
   - **Diagnosis: Low and contaminated fluid**

2. **Visual Leak Inspection:**
   - Traced fluid leak to power steering pump area
   - Pump output hose has wetness at connection (leaking)
   - Rack boots intact, no visible rack leakage
   - **Diagnosis: High-pressure hose leaking at pump connection**

3. **Steering Play Check:**
   - Suresh rocked steering wheel back and forth with wheels on ground
   - No excessive play (normal: less than 1 inch at wheel rim)
   - **Diagnosis: No worn steering components**

4. **Test Drive (Suresh with Mrs. Patel):**
   - Low-speed turns: Heavy steering, loud groaning noise from pump area
   - Highway driving: Steering slightly heavy but manageable
   - **Diagnosis: Power steering system not providing adequate assist**

**Suresh's Conclusion:**

"Mrs. Patel, your power steering pump has been leaking fluid from the high-pressure hose connection. Over time, fluid level dropped below minimum, and the remaining fluid became contaminated with wear particles and lost its lubricating properties. The pump is now running without adequate fluid—that's why it groans and can't provide full assist. The good news: the rack and steering components are fine. We need to replace the leaking hose, flush the contaminated fluid, and refill with fresh fluid. The pump itself may still be OK if we caught it before internal damage."

**Part 2: Power Steering Hose Replacement and Fluid Service (30 min)**

**Step-by-Step Procedure (Suresh demonstrates to Rahul):**

**Step 1: Drain Old Fluid**
- Disconnect return hose from reservoir
- Place drain pan under return hose
- Start engine briefly (3-5 seconds) to pump fluid out
- Turn steering lock-to-lock to expel fluid from rack
- **Safety:** Don't run pump dry for more than 5 seconds—can damage pump

**Step 2: Remove Leaking High-Pressure Hose**
- Loosen high-pressure hose connection at pump (flare nut, 17mm)
- Fluid sprays slightly—wear safety glasses
- Remove hose connection at steering rack (19mm flare nut)
- Remove hose from vehicle
- Inspect pump and rack fittings: No damage, threads OK

**Step 3: Install New High-Pressure Hose**
- New Honda OEM hose: ₹2,800 (includes fittings)
- Install at pump end first, hand-tighten flare nut
- Route hose along factory path (avoid contact with hot exhaust)
- Install at rack end, hand-tighten flare nut
- Torque pump end: 30 Nm (don't over-torque—can crack fitting)
- Torque rack end: 35 Nm

**Step 4: Fill System with Fresh Fluid**
- Use Honda genuine power steering fluid (important: wrong fluid causes seal damage)
- Fill reservoir to MAX line
- Turn steering wheel lock-to-lock 10 times (engine OFF) to push fluid through system
- Refill reservoir (fluid level will drop as system fills)
- Repeat until fluid level stays at MAX

**Step 5: Bleeding Air from System (Critical Procedure)**

Suresh explained to Rahul, "Air in the power steering system causes noise, reduced assist, and pump damage. We must bleed all air out."

**Bleeding Procedure:**
1. Start engine, let idle
2. Turn steering wheel slowly lock-to-lock (left full to right full)
3. Pause 2-3 seconds at each lock position
4. Watch fluid in reservoir: Look for air bubbles rising
5. Repeat lock-to-lock turns 15-20 times
6. Listen for noise: Groaning or whining indicates air still present
7. Check fluid level frequently, top up as needed
8. Continue until:
   - No more air bubbles in reservoir
   - No noise during steering
   - Fluid level stable

**Time:** 10-15 minutes of bleeding

**Step 6: Test Power Steering Pump Pressure (Verification)**

Suresh installed a power steering pressure gauge between pump and rack:
- Gauge connection: Install in high-pressure hose line
- Start engine, let idle (800 RPM)
- **Idle pressure (no steering input):** 80-100 psi (normal)
- Turn steering to full lock and hold (briefly—max 5 seconds)
- **Maximum pressure at lock:** 1,200 psi (spec: 1,150-1,300 psi) ✓
- **Diagnosis:** Pump pressure within specification, pump not damaged

**Step 7: Test Drive and Final Verification**

Test drive with Mrs. Patel:
- Parking lot turns: Steering light and easy, no noise ✓
- Highway driving: Smooth steering assist, responsive ✓
- No leaks visible under vehicle after test drive ✓

**Mrs. Patel's Reaction:**

"It's like a new car! I forgot how light the steering should be. Thank you for explaining everything so clearly."

**Cost Breakdown:**
- High-pressure hose (OEM): ₹2,800
- Power steering fluid (1 liter): ₹400
- Labor (2 hours): ₹1,200
- **Total: ₹4,400**

**Part 3: Teaching Points for Students (15 min)**

Suresh explained to Rahul (and students):

**Common Power Steering Issues in India:**

1. **Fluid Leaks (Most Common):**
   - Hoses deteriorate from heat and age (Mumbai heat accelerates degradation)
   - Rack seals leak due to contaminated fluid
   - Pump seals leak from wear or wrong fluid type
   - **Prevention:** Check fluid level every 10,000 km, replace fluid every 60,000 km

2. **Contaminated Fluid:**
   - Fluid turns black/brown from wear particles
   - Loses lubricating and hydraulic properties
   - Causes pump and rack wear, eventual failure
   - **Prevention:** Use correct fluid type (don't mix different brands/types)

3. **Air in System:**
   - Enters through low fluid level or loose connections
   - Causes noise, reduced assist, pump cavitation
   - **Solution:** Proper bleeding procedure (takes patience)

4. **Worn Steering Components (Create Play):**
   - Tie rod ends: Check for play by rocking wheel
   - Steering rack bushings: Causes clunking over bumps
   - Steering column U-joints: Causes clunking when turning
   - **Diagnosis:** Physical inspection, feel for play

**Steering Play Measurement:**

Suresh demonstrated: "With wheels on ground, engine off, rock steering wheel back and forth. Measure free play before wheels start to move. Maximum acceptable: 1 inch (25mm) at wheel rim. More than that indicates worn components."

**Power Steering Fluid Types:**

- **ATF Dexron III:** Used in many older vehicles (Honda, Toyota)
- **Genuine OEM Fluid:** Required for newer vehicles (specific formulations)
- **DO NOT MIX:** Different fluids have different additives, can damage seals
- **Indian Market:** Many fake fluids—use genuine brands (Castrol, Mobil, Shell, OEM)

#### Scenario 2: Electric Power Steering (EPS) Diagnosis (Maruti Baleno) - 65 minutes

**Part 4: EPS System Overview and Customer Consultation (15 min)**

Mr. Singh approached Suresh. "My Baleno's EPS warning light came on 3 days ago. The steering still works fine, but I'm worried. Is it safe to drive?"

Suresh explained, "The Maruti Baleno uses Electric Power Steering—there's no hydraulic pump, hoses, or fluid. Instead, an electric motor mounted on the steering column provides assistance. When the EPS warning light comes on, it means the system has detected a fault and may have disabled power assist. Let me diagnose it using the scan tool."

**How EPS Works (Simplified Explanation for Customer):**

"In your Baleno, when you turn the steering wheel, a torque sensor measures how much force you're applying. The ECU reads this sensor and commands the EPS motor to assist you. The harder you turn, the more assist the motor provides. At highway speeds, the system reduces assist for better feel. At parking speeds, it provides maximum assist."

**Advantages of EPS:**
- More fuel-efficient (no engine-driven pump)
- Variable assist (computer-controlled)
- Easier packaging (no hydraulic lines)
- Less maintenance (no fluid to change)

**Common EPS Issues:**
- Torque sensor failure (most common)
- EPS motor failure (brushes wear out)
- ECU faults (software or electrical)
- Wiring/connector issues (corrosion, loose connections)

**Part 5: EPS Diagnostic Procedure Using Scan Tool (30 min)**

**Step 1: Connect Scan Tool and Retrieve DTCs**

Suresh connected his scan tool to the OBD-II port under the dashboard.

**Scan Tool Steps:**
1. Select vehicle: Maruti Baleno 2020
2. Navigate to: Chassis → Electric Power Steering (EPS) module
3. Read Diagnostic Trouble Codes (DTCs)

**DTCs Retrieved:**
- **C1513:** Torque Sensor Circuit Malfunction
- **Status:** Current (fault is active)

**Step 2: Check EPS Live Data**

Suresh navigated to live data and monitored key parameters while Rahul turned the steering wheel:

**Live Data Monitoring:**

| Parameter | Value (Engine OFF) | Value (Engine ON, No Steering Input) | Value (Turning Left) | Value (Turning Right) | Specification |
|-----------|-------------------|--------------------------------------|---------------------|----------------------|---------------|
| **Torque Sensor Voltage** | 2.5V | 2.5V | 2.5V (no change) | 2.5V (no change) | 0.5V-4.5V (varies with steering input) |
| **Steering Angle** | 0° | 0° | -180° | +180° | Varies -540° to +540° |
| **EPS Motor Current** | 0A | 0A | 0A | 0A | 0-40A (varies with load) |
| **EPS Status** | Fault | Fault | Fault | Fault | Normal |

**Critical Finding:**

"Look at the torque sensor voltage, Rahul," Suresh pointed. "It's stuck at 2.5V—the center voltage. When we turn the steering wheel, it should vary between 0.5V and 4.5V depending on how much torque is applied. The fact that it stays at 2.5V regardless of steering input means the torque sensor isn't working. The ECU can't tell how much force the driver is applying, so it disables power assist for safety."

**Step 3: Torque Sensor Circuit Testing**

**Option 1: Wiring Inspection**
- Check EPS harness connector at base of steering column
- Inspect for corrosion, damaged pins, loose connection
- Wiggle connector while watching scan tool: Voltage should change if loose connection
- **Result:** Connector looks clean, no corrosion, voltage stays at 2.5V

**Option 2: Torque Sensor Resistance Test**
- Disconnect EPS connector
- Use multimeter to measure torque sensor resistance
- **Specification:** 1,000-1,500 ohms (Maruti EPS torque sensor)
- **Measured:** Open circuit (infinite resistance)
- **Diagnosis:** Torque sensor internal failure

Suresh explained to Mr. Singh: "Your torque sensor has failed internally—it's open circuit, so the ECU can't read the signal. **Part 6: EPS Installation and Calibration (Overview - 20 min)**

Suresh explained the replacement procedure to Rahul (for when the part arrives):

**EPS Column Assembly Replacement Procedure:**

**Step 1: Remove Steering Wheel**
- Disconnect battery negative terminal (wait 2 minutes for airbag discharge)
- Remove airbag module from steering wheel (2 bolts behind wheel)
- Disconnect airbag connector (yellow connector—handle carefully)
- Remove steering wheel center nut (19mm, 50 Nm original torque)
- Use steering wheel puller to remove wheel

**Step 2: Remove Lower Dashboard Panel and Column Covers**
- Remove dashboard lower panel (4 screws)
- Remove steering column upper and lower covers (plastic clips)
- Expose EPS assembly and connectors

**Step 3: Disconnect EPS Assembly**
- Disconnect EPS electrical connector (large multi-pin connector)
- Disconnect steering shaft from steering rack using pinch bolt (14mm, at firewall)
- Mark shaft orientation before removal (important for alignment)

**Step 4: Remove EPS Column Assembly**
- Remove column mounting bolts (3 bolts securing to dashboard frame)
- Carefully remove entire EPS assembly from vehicle

**Step 5: Install New EPS Assembly**
- Install new assembly in reverse order
- Align steering shaft with rack coupling (match alignment marks)
- Torque pinch bolt: 25 Nm (critical—loose bolt causes play, tight bolt damages splines)
- Torque column mounting bolts: 20 Nm
- Reconnect EPS connector
- Reinstall steering wheel, torque center nut: 50 Nm

**Step 6: EPS Calibration Using Scan Tool (Critical)**

Suresh emphasized, "After replacing the EPS assembly, you MUST perform steering angle sensor calibration. Otherwise, the system won't know where the center position is."

**Calibration Procedure (Using Scan Tool):**
1. Connect scan tool, navigate to EPS module
2. Select: Special Functions → Steering Angle Sensor Calibration
3. Follow on-screen prompts:
   - Ensure wheels are pointed straight ahead
   - Start calibration procedure
   - Turn steering lock-to-lock as instructed (left full, right full, return to center)
   - System learns center position and steering angle range
4. Complete calibration
5. Clear DTCs (old codes from failed sensor)
6. Test drive to verify:
   - Power assist functions correctly
   - No warning lights
   - Steering feels natural (not too light or heavy)

**Step 7: Final Verification**
- Check scan tool live data: Torque sensor voltage varies correctly
- Steering angle reads 0° at center
- EPS motor current responds to steering input
- Test drive: Full power assist, no warnings, smooth operation

**Common EPS Calibration Mistakes:**

1. **Skipping calibration after replacement:**
   - Warning light may stay on
   - Assist may be incorrect (too much or too little)
   - Vehicle pull or wander

2. **Calibrating with wheels not straight:**
   - System learns wrong center position
   - Steering wheel off-center during driving
   - Must recalibrate with correct wheel position

3. **Not test-driving after calibration:**
   - Issue may not be apparent until driving
   - Always verify real-world operation

## Technical Content Delivery (Supporting the Scenario)

### Part 1: Steering System Types and Components (15 minutes)

**Three Types of Steering Systems in India:**

**1. Manual Steering (Rare in Modern Vehicles)**
- No power assist, pure mechanical linkage
- Requires significant effort to turn, especially at low speeds
- Found only in very old or budget vehicles (pre-2000s commercial vehicles)
- **Not covered in detail—nearly obsolete**

**2. Hydraulic Power Steering (Common in Older and Larger Vehicles)**

**Components:**
- **Power Steering Pump:** Belt-driven by engine, generates hydraulic pressure
- **Fluid Reservoir:** Stores power steering fluid, has level markings
- **High-Pressure Hose:** Carries pressurized fluid from pump to rack
- **Return Hose:** Returns fluid from rack to reservoir
- **Steering Rack:** Converts rotary motion to linear motion, hydraulic assist reduces effort
- **Tie Rods:** Connect rack to steering knuckles

**Fluid Flow:**
1. Pump pressurizes fluid (1,200+ psi)
2. Fluid flows through high-pressure hose to rack
3. When steering wheel is turned, fluid pressure assists rack movement
4. Fluid returns to reservoir through return hose
5. Cycle repeats continuously while engine runs

**Advantages:**
- Simple, proven technology
- Strong assist (good for heavy vehicles)
- No complex electronics

**Disadvantages:**
- Consumes engine power constantly (reduces fuel efficiency)
- Requires regular fluid changes
- Hoses and pump can leak

**3. Electric Power Steering (EPS) (Standard in Modern Vehicles)**

**Components:**
- **EPS Motor:** Electric motor provides steering assist
- **Torque Sensor:** Measures driver's steering input force
- **Steering Angle Sensor:** Measures steering wheel position
- **EPS ECU:** Computer controls motor based on sensor inputs and vehicle speed
- **Wiring and Connectors:** Electrical connections (no hydraulic lines)

**Operation:**
1. Driver turns steering wheel, applying torque
2. Torque sensor measures applied force (voltage signal to ECU)
3. ECU calculates required assist based on:
   - Steering torque (how hard driver is turning)
   - Vehicle speed (less assist at highway speeds, more at parking speeds)
   - Steering angle (how far wheels are turned)
4. ECU commands EPS motor to provide calculated assist
5. Motor assists steering column or rack directly

**Advantages:**
- Fuel-efficient (only uses power when steering)
- Variable assist (computer-controlled, adapts to speed)
- No fluid to leak or change
- Compact packaging (no pump, hoses, reservoir)

**Disadvantages:**
- More expensive to replace when failed
- Requires scan tool for diagnosis and calibration
- Less feedback feel (some drivers prefer hydraulic feel)

**Indian Market Trend:**
- Pre-2015: Mostly hydraulic power steering
- 2015-2020: Transition period (mix of hydraulic and EPS)
- Post-2020: Majority EPS (especially small/midsize vehicles)

### Part 2: Common Steering Problems and Diagnosis (20 minutes)

**Problem 1: Heavy Steering**

**Symptoms:** Increased effort required to turn steering wheel, especially at parking speeds

**Possible Causes:**

**Hydraulic Power Steering:**
- Low fluid level (most common—check first!)
- Contaminated fluid (turns dark brown/black)
- Failed power steering pump (no pressure generation)
- Air in system (from low fluid or leaks)
- Internal rack wear (high mileage)

**Electric Power Steering:**
- EPS system disabled due to fault (warning light ON)
- Low battery voltage (EPS motor requires 12V+)
- Failed EPS motor (worn brushes, burned winding)
- Failed torque sensor (ECU can't detect steering force)

**Diagnosis Flow:**
1. Check for warning lights (EPS light)
2. Check fluid level (hydraulic systems)
3. Test drive to confirm symptom
4. Test power steering pump pressure (hydraulic)
5. Scan tool diagnosis (EPS)

**Problem 2: Steering Noise (Groaning, Whining, Clicking)**

**Groaning/Whining (Hydraulic Systems):**
- Low fluid level (pump cavitation)
- Air in system (bubbles in fluid)
- Failed pump bearings (worn pump)
- Tight steering rack (internal wear)

**Clicking/Clunking (Both Systems):**
- Worn tie rod ends (click when turning)
- Worn steering rack bushings (clunk over bumps)
- Worn steering column U-joints (clunk when turning)

**Diagnosis:**
1. Identify noise location (pump area vs. rack area vs. column)
2. Check fluid level and condition
3. Physical inspection: Rock tie rods, check for play
4. Isolate noise during test drive

**Problem 3: Steering Play (Loose Steering)**

**Symptoms:** Excessive free movement of steering wheel before wheels respond

**Causes:**
- Worn tie rod ends (most common)
- Worn steering rack bushings
- Loose steering gear mounting
- Worn steering column U-joints
- Loose wheel bearings (can feel like steering play)

**Diagnosis Procedure:**
1. Measure steering wheel free play: Max 1 inch (25mm) at rim
2. Rock steering wheel with wheels on ground: Feel for play
3. Inspect tie rod ends: Grasp tire at 3 and 9 o'clock positions, rock back and forth
4. Inspect ball joints: Grasp tire at 12 and 6 o'clock, push/pull (separate test from steering play)

**Problem 4: Vehicle Wanders or Pulls to One Side**

**Causes:**
- Incorrect wheel alignment (most common—covered Day 22)
- Uneven tire pressure
- Worn suspension components
- Binding steering rack (causes pull)
- Cross-caster (covered yesterday)

**Diagnosis:**
1. Check tire pressures (rule out simple cause)
2. Perform wheel alignment check
3. Test drive: Feel for consistent pull vs. wandering
4. Inspect suspension components

**Problem 5: EPS Warning Light (Electric Systems Only)**

**Symptoms:** EPS warning light on dashboard, may or may not have heavy steering

**Common Causes:**
- Torque sensor failure (most common)
- EPS motor failure
- Low battery voltage (poor battery or alternator)
- Wiring/connector issues (corrosion)
- ECU software issue (rare)

**Diagnosis Flow:**
1. Check battery voltage: Minimum 12.5V (weak battery causes EPS faults)
2. Connect scan tool, retrieve DTCs
3. Monitor live data: Torque sensor, motor current, steering angle
4. Interpret DTCs and data to identify failed component

**DTC Examples (Maruti/Suzuki EPS):**
- C1513: Torque sensor circuit malfunction
- C1521: EPS motor circuit malfunction
- C1531: Steering angle sensor malfunction
- C1541: EPS ECU communication error

### Part 3: Service Procedures (25 minutes)

**Procedure 1: Power Steering Fluid Service (Hydraulic Systems)**

**When to Service:**
- Manufacturer recommendation: Every 60,000 km or 3 years
- If fluid is contaminated (dark brown/black)
- After any component replacement (pump, rack, hoses)
- If air is present in system (whining noise)

**Fluid Replacement Procedure:**
1. Drain old fluid (disconnect return hose, run engine briefly)
2. Remove reservoir and clean (sediment accumulates at bottom)
3. Replace high-pressure hose if leaking or cracked
4. Fill with correct fluid type (check owner's manual)
5. Bleed air from system (lock-to-lock steering, 15-20 times)
6. Check for leaks after service

**Critical: Use Correct Fluid Type**
- ATF Dexron III (older Honda, Toyota vehicles)
- CHF 11S (Volkswagen, Audi, Skoda)
- Genuine OEM fluid (newer vehicles—specific formulations)
- **DO NOT mix different types—causes seal damage**

**Procedure 2: Tie Rod End Replacement**

**When to Replace:**
- Visible wear or torn dust boot
- Excessive play when rocked
- Clicking noise during turning

**Replacement Procedure:**
1. Lift vehicle, remove wheel
2. Loosen tie rod end lock nut (17mm)
3. Count threads exposed (for approximate alignment)
4. Remove tie rod end from steering knuckle:
   - Remove castle nut (17mm)
   - Remove cotter pin
   - Use pickle fork or tie rod puller to separate taper
5. Unscrew tie rod end (count rotations for reference)
6. Install new tie rod end:
   - Screw on same number of rotations (approximate alignment)
   - Install into steering knuckle taper
   - Torque castle nut: 45-55 Nm
   - Install new cotter pin (never reuse)
7. Tighten lock nut: 65 Nm
8. **Perform wheel alignment** (toe will have changed slightly)

**Important:** Always replace cotter pins. Never skip alignment after tie rod replacement.

**Procedure 3: Power Steering Pump Pressure Test**

**When to Test:**
- Heavy steering complaint
- Verify pump operation after fluid service
- Before replacing steering rack (ensure pump is working)

**Test Procedure:**
1. Install pressure gauge in high-pressure line (between pump and rack)
2. Start engine, let idle (800-900 RPM)
3. Measure idle pressure: 80-150 psi (normal)
4. Turn steering to full lock, hold briefly (max 5 seconds)
5. Measure maximum pressure: 1,150-1,300 psi typical (varies by vehicle)
6. Release steering immediately (holding at lock damages pump)
7. **Interpretation:**
   - Low idle pressure: Pump worn internally
   - Low max pressure: Pump failing, can't generate full pressure
   - Normal pressure but heavy steering: Rack internal wear or binding

**Procedure 4: EPS System Calibration (After Component Replacement)**

**When to Calibrate:**
- After EPS motor/column replacement
- After steering rack replacement
- After ECU replacement
- After battery disconnection (on some vehicles)

**Calibration Steps (Using Scan Tool):**
1. Ensure wheels are straight ahead (critical!)
2. Connect scan tool, navigate to EPS module
3. Select: Special Functions → Steering Angle Sensor Calibration
4. Follow prompts: Lock-to-lock steering, return to center
5. System learns center position and steering range
6. Clear DTCs (old fault codes from removed components)
7. Test drive to verify correct operation

**Verification After Calibration:**
- Steering angle sensor reads 0° when wheels straight
- Power assist feels natural (not too light or heavy)
- No EPS warning light
- Vehicle tracks straight (no pull)

### Quiz Questions (5 Questions - PowerPoint Slide Format)

**Question 1: Hydraulic Power Steering Diagnosis (Analyze Level)**
A customer complains that their Honda Civic's steering has become progressively heavier over the past month, especially when parking. During inspection, you find the power steering fluid level is low and the fluid is dark brown (contaminated). What is the most likely diagnosis?

A) Power steering pump has failed completely
B) Fluid leak has caused low level and contamination has reduced pump efficiency
C) Steering rack has failed internally
D) EPS system has disabled power assist

**Answer:** B) Fluid leak has caused low level and contamination has reduced pump efficiency
**Explanation:** Progressive heavy steering combined with low, contaminated fluid indicates a leak that has been present for some time. As fluid level drops, air enters the system and remaining fluid becomes contaminated with wear particles, reducing lubrication and hydraulic efficiency. The pump struggles to generate adequate pressure. This was the exact issue in Suresh's Honda City scenario. Repair requires finding and fixing the leak, flushing contaminated fluid, and refilling with fresh fluid.

---

**Question 2: Power Steering Bleeding Procedure (Apply Level)**
After replacing a power steering hose and refilling the system with fresh fluid, you start the engine and hear a groaning noise when turning the steering wheel. What is the cause and solution?

A) Wrong fluid type was used; drain and refill with correct fluid
B) Air is still present in the system; continue bleeding by turning steering lock-to-lock
C) Power steering pump has failed; replace pump
D) Steering rack is damaged; replace rack

**Answer:** B) Air is still present in the system; continue bleeding by turning steering lock-to-lock
**Explanation:** Groaning noise after fluid service indicates air trapped in the hydraulic system. Air causes pump cavitation (air bubbles collapsing) which creates noise and reduces assist. Solution: Continue bleeding by turning steering wheel lock-to-lock 15-20 times while watching for air bubbles rising in the reservoir. Refill fluid as level drops. Bleeding can take 10-15 minutes—patience is required. The noise will disappear once all air is expelled.

---

**Question 3: EPS Torque Sensor Failure (Analyze Level)**
A Maruti Baleno has the EPS warning light illuminated. Using a scan tool, you retrieve DTC C1513 (Torque Sensor Circuit Malfunction). In live data, the torque sensor voltage remains at 2.5V regardless of steering input. What does this indicate?

A) Torque sensor is functioning correctly (2.5V is normal center position)
B) Torque sensor has failed (voltage should vary with steering input)
C) EPS motor has failed (not generating assist)
D) Battery voltage is too low (causing sensor reading error)

**Answer:** B) Torque sensor has failed (voltage should vary with steering input)
**Explanation:** Torque sensor voltage should vary between 0.5V and 4.5V depending on steering input force. A reading stuck at 2.5V (center position) indicates the sensor is not responding to steering torque. This was the exact failure in Suresh's Baleno scenario—torque sensor internal open circuit. The ECU cannot detect driver's steering effort, so it disables power assist for safety. Repair requires replacing the EPS column assembly (torque sensor integrated, not sold separately on most vehicles).

**Question 4: Steering Play Diagnosis (Apply Level)**
During inspection of a Tata Nexon with a steering play complaint, you rock the steering wheel back and forth with the wheels on the ground. You measure 2 inches (50mm) of free play at the steering wheel rim before the wheels start to move. What is the diagnosis and action?

A) Steering play is within specification (max 1 inch), no repair needed
B) Steering play exceeds specification (max 1 inch), inspect for worn components
C) Steering play is normal for vehicles with 50,000+ km, customer education needed
D) Heavy steering issue, check power steering fluid level

**Answer:** B) Steering play exceeds specification (max 1 inch), inspect for worn components
**Explanation:** Maximum acceptable steering play is 1 inch (25mm) measured at the steering wheel rim. This vehicle has 2 inches (50mm)—double the specification—indicating worn steering components. Most likely causes: worn tie rod ends, worn steering rack bushings, or loose steering gear mounting. Next step: Physical inspection—rock tie rods by hand to check for play, inspect rack bushing condition, check steering gear mounting bolts. Worn components must be replaced, followed by wheel alignment.

**Question 5: EPS Calibration After Replacement (Apply Level)**
After replacing the EPS column assembly on a Maruti Baleno, what is the required next step before returning the vehicle to the customer?

A) Test drive to verify power assist operation
B) Perform steering angle sensor calibration using scan tool
C) Clear all DTCs and warning lights
D) Check fluid level in EPS system

**Answer:** B) Perform steering angle sensor calibration using scan tool
**Explanation:** After replacing any EPS component (motor, column assembly, ECU) or disconnecting the battery, steering angle sensor calibration MUST be performed. The calibration procedure teaches the ECU where the steering center position is and the full range of steering angle. Without calibration, the system doesn't know the correct center position, which can cause incorrect power assist levels, pulling, or wandering. Calibration uses scan tool special functions: turn steering lock-to-lock as prompted, system learns range. After calibration, then test drive and verify. (Note: EPS systems have no fluid—they're electric.)
