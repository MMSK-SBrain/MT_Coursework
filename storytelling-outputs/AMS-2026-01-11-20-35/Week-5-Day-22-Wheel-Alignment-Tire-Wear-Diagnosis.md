**Fourth Clue (The Critical Discovery):**

Priya noticed something Vikram had missed: **Cross-caster condition.**

- Left front caster: +3.2°
- Right front caster: +2.1°
- **Difference: 1.1°** (specification: maximum 0.5° difference)

Even though the right front caster was technically within the allowable range (barely), the **difference between left and right** exceeded the specification.

**What Is Cross-Caster?**

Caster is the angle of the steering axis when viewed from the side:
- **Positive caster:** Steering axis tilts toward rear (like motorcycle fork)
- **Negative caster:** Steering axis tilts toward front

**Effects of Caster:**
- More positive caster = more straight-line stability, heavier steering
- Less positive caster = lighter steering, less stability

**Effects of Cross-Caster (Unequal Side-to-Side):**
- Vehicle pulls toward the side with LESS caster
- In this case: Right side has less caster (+2.1°) → pulls RIGHT ✓ (matches symptom!)
- Also explains outside edge tire wear: Vehicle constantly pulling right causes right tire to scrub during straight-line driving

**Why Did Vikram Miss This?**

Vikram had focused on toe (which he adjusted) and camber (which was within spec). He saw caster values within individual specifications and assumed they were fine. He didn't check the **cross-caster difference.**

**Priya's Revelation:**

"This is it! Cross-caster is causing the pull. But now I need to figure out WHY the caster is different on each side. Caster isn't adjustable on Hyundai Creta—it's set by the control arm and frame geometry. If caster is out, something is bent or shifted."

Caster angle is determined by:
1. **Control arm position:** Forward or backward shift affects caster

3. **Frame damage:** Accident damage can alter steering axis

1. **Control arms:** No visible bending, bushings intact

**Priya's Conclusion:**

## Technical Content Delivery (Supporting the Story)

### Part 1: Understanding Tire Wear Patterns (20 minutes)

**The Five Common Tire Wear Patterns:**

**1. Inside Edge Wear (Inner Shoulder Worn)**
- **Appearance:** Inside 25% of tread worn significantly more than center and outside
- **Causes:**
  - Excessive negative camber (top of tire tilts inward)
  - Toe-out (front of tire points outward)
- **Common in India:** Vehicles with worn suspension bushings, lowered vehicles

**2. Outside Edge Wear (Outer Shoulder Worn)**
- **Appearance:** Outside 25% of tread worn significantly more than center and inside
- **Causes:**
  - Excessive positive camber (top of tire tilts outward)
  - Aggressive cornering (performance driving)
  - Cross-caster pulling vehicle into turns
- **Common in India:** Overloaded vehicles, pothole-damaged suspension (like Priya's case)

**3. Center Wear**
- **Appearance:** Center of tread worn, shoulders have more tread remaining
- **Causes:**
  - Over-inflation (tire pressure too high)
  - Too narrow tire for wheel width
- **Common in India:** Drivers who over-inflate thinking it improves fuel economy

**4. Both Edge Wear (Shoulders Worn, Center OK)**
- **Appearance:** Both inside and outside shoulders worn, center has good tread
- **Causes:**
  - Under-inflation (tire pressure too low)
  - Tire too wide for wheel
- **Common in India:** Very common due to poor tire pressure maintenance

**5. Cupping/Scalloping Wear**
- **Appearance:** High and low spots around tire circumference, wavy pattern
- **Causes:**
  - Worn shock absorbers (tire bounces, doesn't maintain road contact)
  - Unbalanced wheels
  - Worn suspension components (ball joints, control arm bushings)
- **Common in India:** Very common due to pothole damage and delayed shock absorber replacement

**6. Feathering Wear (Bonus Pattern)**
- **Appearance:** Tread ribs worn smooth on one side, sharp edge on other side (feels like saw teeth)
- **Causes:**
  - Incorrect toe (usually excessive toe-in or toe-out)
- **Common in India:** Vehicles that have never had alignment check after suspension repairs

**Diagnostic Flow Chart:**

```
Tire Wear Observed
↓
Where is the wear?
├─ Center → Check tire pressure (over-inflation)
├─ Both edges → Check tire pressure (under-inflation)
├─ Inside edge → Check camber (negative) or toe (toe-out)
├─ Outside edge → Check camber (positive), cross-caster, or cornering habits
├─ Cupping → Check shock absorbers, wheel balance, suspension wear
└─ Feathering → Check toe alignment
```

### Part 2: Wheel Alignment Angles Explained (25 minutes)

**The Three Critical Alignment Angles:**

**1. CAMBER (Side View of Wheel)**

**Definition:** The angle of the wheel relative to vertical when viewed from front of vehicle

- **Negative Camber:** Top of tire tilts inward (toward engine) \
- **Positive Camber:** Top of tire tilts outward (away from engine) /
- **Zero Camber:** Tire perfectly vertical |

**Effects:**
- **Slight negative camber (-0.5° to -1.0°):** Improves cornering grip (race cars use more)
- **Excessive negative camber:** Inside edge tire wear
- **Positive camber:** Outside edge tire wear, poor cornering
- **Zero camber:** Maximum tire contact on straight roads

**Typical Specifications (India Market):**
- Maruti Swift: -0.3° ± 0.5° (range: +0.2° to -0.8°)
- Hyundai Creta: -0.3° ± 0.5°
- Mahindra Scorpio: +0.2° ± 0.5° (SUVs often use slight positive)

**Adjustability:**
- Most Indian vehicles: Non-adjustable (set by control arm design)
- Adjustment method (if available): Eccentric bolts at strut-to-knuckle, shims at control arm
- **If camber is out of spec and non-adjustable → suspension damage present (bent component)**

**2. CASTER (Top View of Steering Axis)**

**Definition:** The angle of the steering axis relative to vertical when viewed from side of vehicle

- **Positive Caster:** Steering axis tilts toward rear (like motorcycle fork)
- **Negative Caster:** Steering axis tilts toward front
- **Zero Caster:** Steering axis perfectly vertical

**Effects:**
- **Positive caster (+3° to +5° typical):**
  - Improves straight-line stability (highway driving)
  - Self-centering steering (wheel returns to center after turn)
  - Slightly heavier steering effort
  - Modern vehicles use more positive caster for safety
- **Negative caster:** Light steering but vehicle wanders, unstable at speed
- **Cross-caster (side-to-side difference):** Vehicle pulls toward side with LESS caster

**Typical Specifications:**
- Maruti Swift: +3.0° ± 0.5° (range: +2.5° to +3.5°)
- Hyundai Creta: +3.0° ± 0.5°
- **Maximum cross-caster (left-to-right difference): 0.5°**

**Adjustability:**
- Most Indian vehicles: Non-adjustable
- Adjustment method (if needed): Eccentric bolts, shims, control arm position
- **If caster is out of spec → bent components or frame damage (common after pothole impacts)**

**Why Priya's Case Was Cross-Caster:**
- Right caster: +2.1° (low, but technically within ±0.5° range)
- Left caster: +3.2° (normal)
- Difference: 1.1° (exceeds maximum 0.5° cross-caster spec)
- Result: Vehicle pulls right (toward lower caster side)

**3. TOE (Viewed from Above Vehicle)**

**Definition:** The angle of the wheels relative to vehicle centerline when viewed from above

- **Toe-in:** Front of tires point toward each other (like pigeon-toed)
- **Toe-out:** Front of tires point away from each other
- **Zero Toe:** Tires point straight ahead, parallel to centerline

**Effects:**
- **Slight toe-in (+0.10° to +0.20° typical):**
  - Compensates for suspension deflection during driving (wheels straighten under load)
  - Improves straight-line stability
  - Most common specification for front-wheel-drive Indian vehicles
- **Excessive toe-in:** Feathering wear (outside edge sharp), steering feels heavy
- **Toe-out:** Feathering wear (inside edge sharp), darting/wandering, unstable
- **Zero toe:** Ideal for rear wheels (most specs)

**Typical Specifications:**
- Maruti Swift (front): +0.10° ± 0.10° per wheel (total: +0.20°)
- Hyundai Creta (front): +0.10° ± 0.10° per wheel
- Rear wheels: Usually 0° ± 0.10° (closer to zero)

**Adjustability:**
- **Front wheels:** ALWAYS adjustable using tie rod length
- **Rear wheels:** Adjustable on independent suspension (using toe link), non-adjustable on beam axles

**This is the most frequently adjusted angle in wheel alignment.**

### Part 3: Using Computerized Alignment Machine (15 minutes)

**Alignment Machine Components:**

1. **Wheel Sensors (4 units):** Attach to each wheel, measure wheel angle relative to machine
2. **Alignment Rack:** Level surface with turntables (front) and slip plates (rear) to allow suspension to settle
3. **Computer Display:** Shows live alignment angles, specifications, adjustment instructions
4. **Camera System (3D machines):** Tracks reflective targets on wheel sensors

**Alignment Procedure (8-Step Method):**

**Step 1: Pre-Alignment Inspection**
- Check tire pressures (must be correct before alignment)
- Check tire tread depth and wear patterns (document for customer)
- Check suspension components for wear (ball joints, tie rods, control arm bushings)
- Check for frame/suspension damage
- **If any component is worn or damaged, repair BEFORE alignment**

**Step 2: Position Vehicle on Alignment Rack**
- Drive vehicle onto rack, center on turntables/slip plates
- Set parking brake OFF (allows suspension to settle)
- Ensure vehicle is unloaded (no heavy cargo)

**Step 3: Attach Wheel Sensors**
- Attach sensors to all four wheels using clamps
- Ensure sensors are level and secure
- Enter vehicle information into computer (make, model, year)

**Step 4: Perform Wheel Compensation (Rolling Compensation)**
- Roll vehicle forward and back to calibrate sensors
- This eliminates wheel/tire runout from readings
- Computer calculates true wheel centerline

**Step 5: Measure Current Alignment (Before Readings)**
- Computer displays camber, caster, toe for all wheels
- Green = within specification
- Yellow = marginal (close to limit)
- Red = out of specification
- **Document "before" readings for customer (print report)**

**Step 6: Adjust Toe (Front and Rear if Adjustable)**
- Loosen tie rod lock nuts
- Rotate tie rod to lengthen or shorten (changes toe)
- Watch computer screen for live toe reading
- Adjust left and right equally to keep steering wheel centered
- **Target: Center of specification range, not just "green"**
- Torque lock nuts: 45-55 Nm (verify with torque wrench)

**Step 7: Adjust Camber and Caster (If Adjustable and Out of Spec)**
- If vehicle has adjustable camber/caster (eccentric bolts, shims):
  - Loosen bolts, rotate eccentric, or add/remove shims
  - Bounce suspension to settle after adjustment
  - Recheck reading
- If non-adjustable but out of spec:
  - Diagnose cause (bent components, frame damage)
  - Recommend repair before alignment can be completed

**Step 8: Final Verification and Documentation**
- Print "after" alignment report showing all angles in green
- Verify steering wheel is centered
- Test drive vehicle (straight-line stability, no pull)
- Give customer before/after report

**Common Mistakes to Avoid:**

1. **Aligning a vehicle with worn suspension components:**
   - Worn ball joints or tie rods cause alignment to drift immediately
   - Always inspect and repair first

2. **Adjusting only toe and ignoring camber/caster:**
   - Toe can temporarily mask a pull, but root cause remains
   - Check ALL angles and side-to-side differences

3. **Not bouncing suspension after adjustments:**
   - Suspension must settle into ride height
   - Bounce all four corners before taking final readings

4. **Incorrect tire pressure during alignment:**
   - Pressure affects ride height and suspension geometry
   - Always set to specification before starting

5. **Not test-driving after alignment:**
   - Alignment machine can show green, but vehicle may still pull
   - Always verify with test drive and check steering wheel centering

## Assessment Content

### Quiz Questions (5 Questions - PowerPoint Slide Format)

**Question 1: Tire Wear Diagnosis (Analyze Level)**
A customer's Maruti Swift has 35,000 km on the odometer. You inspect the front tires and observe that both inside edges (inner shoulders) are worn down to 2mm tread depth, while the centers and outside edges have 6mm tread remaining. What is the most likely cause?

A) Tire pressure too low (under-inflation)
B) Excessive negative camber on both front wheels
C) Excessive toe-out
D) Worn shock absorbers

**Answer:** B) Excessive negative camber on both front wheels
**Explanation:** Inside edge wear on both front tires indicates excessive negative camber (tops of tires tilt inward). This is common after suspension repairs where camber was not checked, or due to worn control arm bushings that allow camber to shift negative. Under-inflation causes BOTH edge wear, not just inside edges. Toe-out would cause feathering wear pattern, not edge wear.

---

**Question 2: Cross-Caster Diagnosis (Analyze Level)**
During wheel alignment on a Hyundai Creta, you observe the following readings:
- Front left caster: +3.5° (spec: +3.0° ± 0.5°)
- Front right caster: +2.2° (spec: +3.0° ± 0.5°)

The customer complains the vehicle pulls to the right. What is the diagnosis?

A) Both caster angles are within specification, alignment is correct
B) Right caster is out of specification, requires adjustment
C) Cross-caster exceeds maximum 0.5° difference, causing right pull
D) Left caster is out of specification, causing right pull

**Answer:** C) Cross-caster exceeds maximum 0.5° difference, causing right pull
**Explanation:** While left caster (+3.5°) is within spec and right caster (+2.2°) is barely within spec, the difference between them is 1.3° which exceeds the maximum cross-caster specification of 0.5°. Vehicle pulls toward the side with LESS caster (right side: +2.2°). This was the exact issue in Priya's diagnostic case. Requires structural inspection for bent components or shifted subframe.

---

**Question 3: Toe Adjustment (Apply Level)**
During toe adjustment on a Maruti Swift, the alignment machine shows:
- Left front toe: +0.20°
- Right front toe: +0.05°
- Specification: +0.10° ± 0.10° per wheel (total toe: +0.20° ± 0.20°)

The steering wheel is off-center, rotated 15° to the left. What adjustment should you make?

A) Shorten left tie rod, lengthen right tie rod equally
B) Lengthen left tie rod, shorten right tie rod equally
C) Adjust only the left tie rod to reduce toe
D) No adjustment needed, both wheels within specification

**Answer:** B) Lengthen left tie rod, shorten right tie rod equally
**Explanation:** Left toe is too much toe-in (+0.20°, above max +0.20°), right toe is correct (+0.05°). Steering wheel off-center to left indicates more toe-in on left side. To correct: Lengthen left tie rod (reduces toe-in) and shorten right tie rod (increases toe-in) by EQUAL amounts to center steering wheel and balance toe. Adjusting only one side will not center steering wheel.

---

**Question 4: Pothole Impact Effects (Understand Level)**
A customer reports hitting a large pothole at high speed. After the impact, the vehicle pulls to one side and has rapid tire wear. What alignment angle is MOST LIKELY affected by pothole impact damage?

A) Toe (easily knocked out by impact)
B) Camber (bent control arm changes camber)
C) Caster (shifted subframe or bent control arm mounting changes caster)
D) All three angles equally

**Answer:** C) Caster (shifted subframe or bent control arm mounting changes caster)
**Explanation:** Severe pothole impacts commonly shift the subframe or bend control arm mounting points, which primarily affects caster angle (steering axis tilt). This causes cross-caster and vehicle pull, as seen in Priya's case. Camber can also be affected by bent control arms. Toe is easily adjusted and less likely to be the primary issue from structural damage. Caster issues are often missed because they're non-adjustable on most vehicles.

---

**Question 5: Alignment Procedure (Apply Level)**
You are performing wheel alignment on a Hyundai i20. After adjusting the tie rods to correct toe, what is the NEXT step before taking final alignment readings?

A) Test drive the vehicle to verify alignment
B) Torque the tie rod lock nuts to specification
C) Bounce the suspension at all four corners to settle the vehicle
D) Print the alignment report for the customer

**Answer:** C) Bounce the suspension at all four corners to settle the vehicle
**Explanation:** After any alignment adjustment, you must bounce the suspension (push down on each corner and release) to allow the suspension to settle into its normal ride height position. Suspension components have friction and can "stick" in an adjusted position. Bouncing ensures accurate final readings. After bouncing, recheck alignment, THEN torque lock nuts, THEN test drive, THEN print report. This is a critical step often skipped by inexperienced technicians.

### Hands-On Exercise Preview (Afternoon Practical - 3 Hours)

**Exercise Title:** Tire Wear Detective Challenge & Wheel Alignment Mastery

**Objective:**
Analyze tire wear patterns to diagnose suspension/alignment issues, perform complete wheel alignment using computerized alignment machine, adjust toe to specification, and verify results with test drive.

**Step-by-Step Activity:**

**Setup (15 min):**
1. Students divided into groups of 4 (working in pairs)
2. Each pair assigned 1 vehicle with known alignment issues:
   - Vehicle options: Maruti Swift, Hyundai i20, Honda City, Tata Nexon (all with documented tire wear and alignment problems)
3. Each group receives:
   - Tire wear inspection checklist
   - Alignment specification sheet for their vehicle
   - Tread depth gauge
   - Tie rod adjustment tools (wrenches, lock nut sockets)
   - Torque wrench

**Task 1: Tire Wear Pattern Analysis (30 min):**

**Phase 1: Inspection (15 min)**
1. Inspect all four tires on assigned vehicle
2. Measure tread depth at three points per tire:
   - Inside edge (inner 25%)
   - Center
   - Outside edge (outer 25%)
3. Record measurements on worksheet
4. Photograph tire wear patterns
5. Check and record tire pressures

**Phase 2: Diagnosis (15 min)**
1. Identify wear pattern type:
   - Inside edge wear, outside edge wear, center wear, edge wear (both), cupping, feathering
2. Form hypothesis:
   - "I believe the cause is [camber/toe/caster/pressure/shock] because [evidence]"
3. Predict what alignment readings will show:
   - "I expect to see [negative camber / toe-out / cross-caster] on alignment machine"
4. Document on diagnostic worksheet
5. **Instructor reviews and discusses each group's hypothesis before proceeding**

**Task 2: Wheel Alignment Measurement (45 min):**

**Phase 1: Setup and Compensation (20 min)**
1. Drive vehicle onto alignment rack
2. Attach wheel sensors to all four wheels (instructor supervises)
3. Enter vehicle information into alignment computer
4. Perform rolling compensation (calibrate sensors)
5. **Call instructor to verify proper sensor installation**

**Phase 2: Initial Alignment Reading (25 min)**
1. Computer displays current alignment angles
2. Record "before" readings on worksheet:
   - Camber: Left front, right front
   - Caster: Left front, right front
   - Toe: Left front, right front, left rear, right rear
3. Identify out-of-spec angles (red on screen)
4. Calculate cross-camber and cross-caster differences
5. Compare actual readings to your earlier hypothesis:
   - "My hypothesis was [X], actual cause is [Y], I was correct/incorrect because..."
6. Print "before" alignment report

**Task 3: Toe Adjustment (60 min):**

**Instructor Demonstration (15 min):**
- Instructor demonstrates on one vehicle:
  - Loosening tie rod lock nut
  - Rotating tie rod to adjust toe
  - Watching live alignment reading on screen
  - Balancing left and right adjustments
  - Torquing lock nut

**Student Practice (45 min):**
1. **Front Toe Adjustment:**
   - Loosen lock nuts on both tie rods
   - Rotate left tie rod while watching computer screen
   - Rotate right tie rod to match left (keep steering wheel centered)
   - Target: Center of specification range
   - Bounce suspension to settle
   - Recheck reading
   - Fine-tune if needed
   - Torque lock nuts: 50 Nm (±5%)

2. **Rear Toe Adjustment (if adjustable on vehicle):**
   - Same process using rear toe links (if vehicle has independent rear suspension)
   - Non-adjustable beam axles: Measure only, document reading

3. **Final Verification:**
   - All angles now displayed on screen
   - All should be green (within spec)
   - Print "after" alignment report
   - Compare before/after reports

**Task 4: Test Drive and Documentation (30 min):**

**Test Drive (15 min per pair):**
1. Student driver and instructor drive vehicle
2. Test on straight road:
   - Hands-off test (briefly): Vehicle should track straight
   - Steering wheel centered (within 5° of vertical)
3. Test in turns:
   - Smooth steering, no pulling or darting
4. Document findings:
   - Pass: No pull, centered wheel, smooth handling
   - Fail: Still pulls or steers poorly (recheck alignment)

**Documentation (15 min):**
1. Complete customer service form:
   - "Before" tire wear description
   - "Before" alignment readings (out-of-spec angles highlighted)
   - Diagnosis: Root cause of wear/pull
   - "After" alignment readings (all within spec)
   - Test drive results
   - Recommendations (e.g., "Replace worn shocks," "Monitor tire wear for 1,000 km")
2. Attach before/after alignment reports
3. Submit to instructor for grading

**Challenges Expected (Instructor Will Address):**

1. **Difficulty reading tire wear patterns:**
   - Some wear is subtle or mixed (multiple causes)
   - Instructor provides reference tire samples with clear wear patterns

2. **Tie rod adjustment direction confusion:**
   - Students may rotate tie rod wrong direction, making toe worse
   - Instructor demonstrates: "Righty-tighty increases toe-in on that side"

3. **Steering wheel not centered after adjustment:**
   - Adjusted one side more than the other
   - Instructor teaches: "Equal rotation on both sides keeps wheel centered"

4. **Camber or caster out of spec but non-adjustable:**
   - Students may try to adjust something not designed to be adjusted
   - Instructor explains: "If non-adjustable angle is out, find bent component—don't force it"

**Pass Criteria:**
- ✅ Tire wear pattern correctly identified (5/5 tires)
- ✅ Diagnostic hypothesis documented before alignment check
- ✅ Alignment measurement procedure completed correctly
- ✅ Toe adjusted to within specification (green on alignment machine)
- ✅ Tie rod lock nuts torqued correctly (±5%)
- ✅ Test drive confirms no pull and centered steering wheel
- ✅ Documentation complete with before/after reports

**Safety Reminders:**
- Test drives conducted with instructor only
- Torque wrenches calibrated and used correctly
- Vehicle must be centered on alignment rack (prevents roll-off)
