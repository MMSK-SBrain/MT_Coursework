### Setup: The Assignment (10 minutes)

**Monday Morning, 9:00 AM - Kumar Auto Services, Nagpur**

Arjun arrived at the workshop early, as he did every day. Today felt different. His senior mechanic, Patil sir, had told him on Saturday: "Monday morning, you'll lead your first engine overhaul. Solo."

Arjun had spent Sunday watching YouTube videos, reviewing his foundation course notes, and barely sleeping.

**The Vehicle:**
A silver 2014 Maruti Swift Dzire 1.3L diesel sat in Bay 3. The owner, Mr. Deshmukh, a taxi driver, needed the engine overhauled quickly. "180,000 km, lots of city driving," Patil sir explained. "Oil consumption, blue smoke on startup. Classic wear. Straightforward job."

Straightforward for Patil sir, maybe. For Arjun, this was Mount Everest.

**Patil Sir's Brief:**
"Arjun, you know the theory. You've assisted me on five engine overhauls. Now you do one from start to finish. I'll check your work at key milestones, but you're leading. Remember the three principles we taught you:

2. **Systematic approach:** Follow the sequence, no shortcuts
3. **Document everything:** Photos, labels, notes—your reassembly depends on it

You have the service manual, the tools, and the knowledge. Go."

Arjun felt his heart race. Excitement? Nervousness? Both.

**His First Decision: Where to Start**

Arjun opened his notebook—the one he'd been keeping since foundation course. On the first page, he'd written:

**"Engine Disassembly Checklist - 3 Phases"**
- **Phase 1:** Removal from vehicle
- **Phase 2:** Systematic disassembly
- **Phase 3:** Cleaning and preparation

"Phase 1 first," he muttered to himself. "One step at a time."

### Rising Action: Phase 1 - Engine Removal (35 minutes)
**"Disconnect everything that connects the engine to the vehicle. Everything."**

**Step 1: Safety and Preparation (9:15 AM)**

Arjun's checklist:
- ✓ Safety glasses on
- ✓ Gloves ready
- ✓ Fire extinguisher location noted (fuel system work ahead)
- ✓ Workshop ventilation running (diesel fuel vapors)
- ✓ Engine hoist inspected (load capacity 500 kg, Swift engine ~180 kg = safe)
- ✓ Service manual open to "Engine Removal" section

**Arjun's Thought Process:**
"Patil sir always says: 'Five minutes of preparation saves an hour of problems.' Let me walk around the vehicle first. What am I dealing with?"

**His Observation Walk:**
- Engine bay: Crowded (common in compact cars)
- Access: Limited from sides, better from top and bottom
- Systems to disconnect: Fuel, cooling, electrical, exhaust, mounts
- Special challenges: AC compressor lines, power steering hoses

**The First Critical Decision:**

Arjun remembered from his foundation course: **"Battery first, always."**

Why? Disconnecting electrical components with battery connected risks:
- Short circuits (sparks near fuel)
- ECU damage (sudden voltage spikes)
- Airbag deployment (if steering column disturbed)

Arjun disconnected the negative terminal first (prevents accidental ground), then positive. He wrapped the negative cable end in a rag. "No accidental reconnection," he said aloud.

---

**Step 2: Fluid Drainage (9:30 AM)**

**Coolant Drainage:**

Arjun positioned a drain pan under the radiator petcock. He opened it slowly—coolant gushed out. Green coolant, relatively clean. "Good sign," he noted. "No rust or oil contamination."

He drained approximately 5 liters (Swift Dzire 1.3L capacity: 5.5L).

**Safety Note:** Arjun wore gloves. Ethylene glycol coolant is toxic, absorbed through skin.

**Engine Oil Drainage:**

He moved the drain pan under the oil pan, removed the drain plug with 17mm wrench. Black oil flowed out—thick, burnt smell. "Definitely overdue for change," Arjun observed.

Approximately 4.5 liters drained (capacity: 4.8L with filter).

**Arjun's Documentation:**
He took a photo of the oil in the drain pan. "Dark color, no metal shavings visible. Good. No catastrophic bearing failure."

---

**Step 3: System Disconnections (10:00 AM - 11:15 AM)**

This was the tedious part. Arjun worked methodically, following his checklist:

**Fuel System:**
1. **Fuel line disconnect:** Pressed the quick-release tabs on fuel rail
2. **Fuel return line:** Disconnected at rail
3. **Challenge:** Fuel sprayed slightly when disconnected
   - **Solution:** Arjun had wrapped rags around connection before releasing (learned from watching Patil sir)
4. **Safety:** Placed fuel-soaked rags in metal container outside workshop

**Cooling System:**
1. **Upper radiator hose:** Loosened clamp, twisted hose to break seal, removed
2. **Lower radiator hose:** Same process
3. **Heater hoses:** Two small-diameter hoses at firewall, disconnected at engine side
4. **Challenge:** Hoses stuck to metal pipes (rubber aged)
   - **Solution:** Used hose pick tool to pry gently, avoided cutting hoses (reusable)

**Electrical System:**
1. **Main engine harness:** Large connector at ECU, released clip, disconnected
2. **Alternator:** Three wires (B+, field, ground)
3. **Starter motor:** Two wires (power, ground)
4. **Sensors:** Coolant temp, oil pressure, camshaft position, crankshaft position
5. **Challenge:** Couldn't reach crankshaft position sensor connector
   - **Solution:** Raised vehicle on lift for better access from below

**Arjun's Documentation Habit:**
Before disconnecting each electrical connector, he took a photo with his phone. "Reassembly will be easier," he thought.

**Exhaust System:**
1. **Downpipe from turbocharger:** Four nuts on flange (corroded, needed penetrating oil)
2. **Challenge:** One stud broke when removing nut
   - **Arjun's Reaction:** "Don't panic. Patil sir said this happens. Mark it for thread repair later."
3. **Exhaust hanger:** Rubber mount at rear of exhaust, pried off

**Accessories and Mounts:**
1. **AC compressor:** Four bolts, moved compressor aside (did NOT disconnect AC lines—requires refrigerant recovery)
2. **Power steering pump:** Three bolts, moved pump aside with hoses attached
3. **Engine mounts:**
   - Left mount: Three bolts (chassis side)
   - Right mount: Three bolts (chassis side)
   - Rear torque mount: Two bolts
   - **Important:** Did NOT remove mount-to-engine bolts yet (engine supported by hoist first)

---

**Step 4: Transmission Separation (11:30 AM)**

**Arjun's Decision Point:**
Remove engine with or without transmission?

**Service Manual Recommendation:** Engine alone (lighter, easier to handle on engine stand)

**Procedure:**
1. **Bell housing bolts:** Eight bolts connecting engine to transmission (two at top, two at sides, four at bottom)
2. **Access:** Top bolts easy, bottom bolts required working from under vehicle
3. **Torque converter bolts (automatic):** Not applicable (Swift Dzire diesel has manual transmission)
4. **Clutch:** Will separate with transmission (normal)

**Challenge Faced:**
One lower bell housing bolt was hidden behind wiring harness.

**Arjun's Solution:**
Used flexible socket extension with universal joint. "Takes five extra minutes, but I won't damage the harness."

---

**Step 5: Hoisting the Engine (12:00 PM)**

This was the moment Arjun had been nervous about. Lifting 180 kg of engine out of a tight engine bay.

**Preparation:**
1. **Engine hoist positioned:** Boom extended over engine
2. **Lifting chain attached:** Three points (front lift eye, rear lift eyes left and right)
3. **Balance check:** Lifted engine slightly (5 cm), checked if level
   - **Finding:** Engine tilted slightly forward (front heavier due to accessories)
   - **Adjustment:** Shortened front chain by one link

**Safety Verification:**
Arjun asked a colleague, Rajesh, to double-check the chain attachments. "Two pairs of eyes for safety-critical tasks," Patil sir had taught him.

**The Lift:**

Arjun pumped the hoist slowly. The engine rose 10 cm... 20 cm...

**Snag!**

The engine stopped. Something still connected.

Arjun's heart sank. "What did I miss?"

He looked underneath. **Ground strap!** A braided copper ground wire from engine block to chassis, hidden behind the starter motor location.

"Disconnected everything else, forgot the ground. Good catch before damaging it," Arjun said with relief.

He lowered the engine, disconnected the ground strap, and resumed lifting.

**Clearance Maneuvering:**

As the engine rose, Arjun guided it carefully:
- Cleared radiator support: 5 cm of clearance (tight)
- Cleared AC condenser: Tilted engine slightly forward
- Cleared firewall: Angled hoist boom

At 12:25 PM, the engine was free from the vehicle.

**Arjun's Emotional Moment:**
"First engine out. All by myself."

---

**Step 6: Engine Stand Mounting (12:30 PM)**

Arjun rolled the engine stand under the hoist. The stand had four arms with bolt holes matching engine bell housing mounting points.

**Mounting Process:**
1. Positioned engine vertically (flywheel facing stand)
2. Aligned stand arms with bell housing bolt holes
3. Installed four bolts (M12, Grade 10.9) with flat washers
4. Torqued bolts to 60 Nm (stand mounting specification)

**Safety Check:**
Arjun rotated the engine on the stand. Smooth rotation, no wobbling. Secure.

**Lunch Break (1:00 PM)**

Arjun stepped back and looked at his morning's work:
- Engine bay empty, no hanging wires or leaks
- Engine on stand, ready for disassembly
- All disconnected parts organized on the workbench

Patil sir walked by. "Inspection time after lunch. Looks good so far, Arjun."

Arjun smiled. Phase 1 complete.

### Climax: Phase 2 - Systematic Disassembly (45 minutes)
**"External components first, then internal. Never skip steps."**

**2:00 PM - The Disassembly Begins**

Arjun reviewed his notes:

**Disassembly Sequence (From Outside to Inside):**
1. Accessories and externals
2. Timing cover and timing components
3. Cylinder head
4. Oil pan and oil pump
5. Crankshaft and main bearings
6. Piston and connecting rod assemblies

**Why This Sequence?**
Patil sir's explanation: "Think of an engine like an onion. Peel layers from outside to inside. You can't reach the crankshaft until you remove the pistons. You can't remove pistons until you remove the head. Logical progression."

---

**Layer 1: Accessories and Externals (2:00 PM - 2:30 PM)**

**Components Removed:**
1. **Alternator:** Three bolts, disconnected earlier, now fully removed
2. **Starter motor:** Two bolts (accessible now that engine is out)
3. **Turbocharger:** Six nuts on exhaust manifold (saved turbo inspection for later)
4. **Exhaust manifold:** Eight studs (two broken from earlier—marked for repair)
5. **Intake manifold:** Four bolts, disconnected EGR pipe
6. **Fuel injection pump:** Four bolts (diesel high-pressure pump)
7. **Valve cover:** Ten bolts

**Arjun's Organization System:**

He had prepared six plastic trays, each labeled:
- "Timing Components"
- "Cylinder Head"
- "Oil System"
- "Crankshaft"
- "Pistons"
- "Accessories"

Every bolt, nut, and washer went into the correct tray.

**His Reasoning:** "I'm 22 years old. My memory isn't perfect. These trays are my insurance."

---

**Layer 2: Timing Components (2:30 PM - 3:00 PM)**

**The Critical Component:**

Timing belt on diesel engines synchronizes crankshaft and camshaft rotation. If timing is off by even one tooth, the engine won't run—or worse, valves hit pistons (interference engine).

**Arjun's Procedure:**

1. **Mark timing positions:**
   - Used white paint pen
   - Marked camshaft gear to cylinder head
   - Marked crankshaft gear to engine block
   - Marked belt to both gears
   - **Purpose:** Reassembly reference (although new belt will be installed)

2. **Photo documentation:**
   - Took three photos from different angles
   - "Before disassembly" shots

3. **Remove timing belt tensioner:**
   - Loosened tensioner bolt
   - Removed tensioner pulley

4. **Remove timing belt:**
   - Slid belt off gears carefully (no twisting)
   - Inspected: Cracked, glazed (due for replacement, good)

5. **Remove camshaft gear:**
   - One center bolt (M12, very tight—approximately 120 Nm)
   - Used impact wrench with camshaft locked (locking tool inserted in gear)

6. **Remove crankshaft gear:**
   - Three bolts (accessible with gear puller)

**Challenge Faced:**

Crankshaft gear was press-fitted. Arjun used a gear puller to remove it carefully.

**Lesson Learned:** "Some components need specialized tools. If I'd tried prying it, I would have damaged the gear."

---

**Layer 3: Cylinder Head Removal (3:00 PM - 3:45 PM)**

**The Big Moment:**

Removing the cylinder head is when you first see the internal engine condition.

**Arjun's Procedure:**

1. **Cylinder head bolt removal sequence:**
   - Service manual showed reverse of torque sequence (outside → inside)
   - Ten bolts total
   - Loosened each bolt 1/4 turn, then went back and loosened another 1/4 turn
   - **Why?** Prevents head warping (even pressure release)

2. **Torque-to-yield bolts:**
   - Arjun measured each bolt with caliper: 122mm length
   - Service manual max length: 123.5mm
   - **Decision:** Within spec, but Patil sir's policy is replace all head bolts
   - "New bolts: ₹2,800. Redo job if reused bolt fails: ₹15,000 labor. Easy decision," Arjun noted

3. **Breaking the gasket seal:**
   - Head stuck to block (gasket sealer and compression)
   - Used plastic pry bar at designated pry points (service manual diagram)
   - **Important:** NEVER pry on sealing surfaces (causes leaks)

4. **Lifting the head:**
   - Head weight: Approximately 25 kg (awkward to lift)
   - Used two-person lift with Rajesh's help
   - Placed head on clean workbench with wooden blocks

**The Reveal:**

Arjun looked into the cylinders. Carbon buildup at top of cylinders (expected). Pistons visible, no cracks or holes (good sign). Cross-hatch pattern on cylinder walls still visible (light wear).

"Not bad for 180,000 km," Arjun said.

Patil sir walked over. "Inspection time. Show me what you see."

**Arjun's Inspection:**
- "Cylinder walls: Minimal ridge at top, cross-hatch visible"
- "Pistons: Carbon buildup, but no damage"
- "Valves in head: Recessing normal, no burnt edges visible yet"
- "Head gasket: Failed between cylinders 2 and 3—see the burn-through?"

Patil sir nodded. "Good observation. That's why the customer had overheating. Combustion gases leaking into coolant. You found the smoking gun. Continue."

---

**Layer 4: Oil Pan and Oil Pump (3:45 PM - 4:15 PM)**

**Oil Pan Removal:**

1. **Seventeen bolts around perimeter (M8, 10 Nm specification)**
   - All aluminum threads (gentle torque on reinstall)
2. **Gasket stuck:** Used plastic scraper to break seal
3. **Inspection:**
   - Oil pan relatively clean (no sludge buildup—good maintenance)
   - Minor aluminum corrosion (normal)

**Oil Pump Removal:**

1. **Three bolts to engine block**
2. **Pickup tube:** Press-fitted into pump, removed carefully
3. **Inspection:**
   - Pump gears: Light wear, no scoring
   - Pickup screen: Clean (no blockage)

---

**Layer 5: Crankshaft Main Bearing Caps (4:15 PM - 4:45 PM)**

**The Foundation of the Engine:**

The crankshaft rides on five main bearings (Swift 1.3L diesel has 5-bearing crankshaft).

**Arjun's Procedure:**

1. **Mark main bearing caps:**
   - Stamped numbers 1-5 from front to rear
   - Verified orientation (arrow points toward front)
   - **Critical:** Caps MUST return to exact original position

2. **Remove main bearing cap bolts:**
   - Two bolts per cap (ten bolts total)
   - Service manual torque spec: 60 Nm + 60° angle (torque-to-yield)
   - **Arjun's Note:** "These bolts will be replaced, not reused"

3. **Remove main bearing caps:**
   - Tapped gently with soft mallet (breaks seal)
   - Laid caps on bench in order (1, 2, 3, 4, 5)

4. **Inspect main bearings:**
   - Bearing shells: Copper-colored (normal wear)
   - No scoring or metal flaking (good)
   - Even wear pattern (proper oil pressure)

**Crankshaft Removal:**

With caps removed, Arjun lifted the crankshaft straight up. Heavy—approximately 20 kg.

He placed it in a padded cradle on the bench.

**First Close Look:**

Five main bearing journals (where crankshaft rides on bearings). Arjun ran his fingernail across them. Smooth, no ridges. "Good sign. Might not need regrinding."

---

**Layer 6: Piston and Connecting Rod Assemblies (4:45 PM - 5:15 PM)**

**The Final Internal Components:**

Four pistons, four connecting rods, sixteen piston rings.

**Arjun's Procedure:**

1. **Mark connecting rods:**
   - Already stamped 1-4 from factory
   - Verified orientation (markings toward exhaust side)
   - **Purpose:** Rods MUST return to original cylinder

2. **Remove rod bearing cap bolts:**
   - Two bolts per rod (eight bolts total)
   - Torque spec: 35 Nm + 60° angle (TTY bolts)

3. **Push pistons out through top:**
   - Used wooden dowel (never metal—damages piston)
   - Pushed gently from crankshaft journal side
   - Each piston/rod assembly slid out smoothly

4. **Organize pistons:**
   - Placed in numbered plastic bags (1, 2, 3, 4)
   - Labeled bags with cylinder number

**Arjun's Observation:**

Piston #2 had slightly more carbon buildup than others. "This was the cylinder with the head gasket leak. Makes sense—combustion gases escaping."

---

**5:30 PM - Disassembly Complete**

Arjun stood in front of the workbench. The engine was now fully disassembled:

- **Accessories tray:** Alternator, starter, manifolds, turbo
- **Timing tray:** Belt, gears, tensioner
- **Head tray:** Cylinder head, valves still installed
- **Oil system tray:** Oil pan, pump, pickup tube
- **Crankshaft tray:** Crankshaft, main bearing caps, bearing shells
- **Piston tray:** Four pistons in numbered bags, connecting rods

**Patil Sir's Final Inspection:**

"Arjun, let me see your documentation."

Arjun pulled out his phone: 47 photos taken during disassembly. His notebook: Six pages of notes, torque specs, observations.

Patil sir smiled. "This is professional-level work. You're ready for Phase 3 tomorrow—cleaning and measurement. Get some rest. You've earned it."

### Resolution: Phase 3 - Cleaning and Preparation (20 minutes)
**"Measurement is only accurate on clean components."**

**Tuesday Morning, 9:00 AM - Day 2**

Arjun arrived refreshed. Today was cleaning and preparation—the foundation for accurate measurement (Day 4 topic).

---

**Step 1: Component Cleaning (9:00 AM - 11:00 AM)**

**Cleaning Methods by Component Type:**

**Cylinder Block:**
- **Method:** Solvent tank soak (30 minutes) + pressure washer
- **Focus areas:** Oil passages, coolant passages, cylinder walls
- **Tool:** Long-handled brush for oil galleries
- **Challenge:** Carbon ridge at top of cylinders
  - **Solution:** Used ridge reamer tool (cuts ridge without damaging cylinder wall)

**Cylinder Head:**
- **Method:** Parts washer with degreaser (1 hour soak)
- **Focus areas:** Combustion chambers, valve guides, coolant passages
- **Challenge:** Carbon buildup in combustion chambers
  - **Solution:** Wire brush on bench grinder (wore safety glasses)
  - **Caution:** Did NOT wire-brush valve sealing surfaces

**Crankshaft:**
- **Method:** Solvent spray + clean rags
- **Focus areas:** Oil passages in journals (critical for bearing lubrication)
- **Tool:** Rifle brush inserted into oil passages
- **Verification:** Compressed air blown through passages (came out clean = good)

**Pistons:**
- **Method:** Ring groove cleaner tool (scrapes carbon from ring grooves)
- **Challenge:** Carbon packed in ring grooves (prevents proper ring movement)
- **Solution:** 15 minutes per piston, three ring grooves per piston
- **Result:** Ring grooves clean, silver aluminum visible

**Connecting Rods:**
- **Method:** Solvent spray
- **Focus:** Bearing surfaces (must be perfectly clean for accurate measurement)

**Small Components:**
- **Method:** Ultrasonic cleaner (20-minute cycle)
- **Components:** Bolts, sensors, oil pump parts

---

**Step 2: Gasket Surface Preparation (11:00 AM - 12:00 PM)**

**The Critical Surfaces:**

**Cylinder Block Deck Surface:**
- Old head gasket stuck to surface
- **Arjun's Approach:**
  - Plastic scraper (NOT metal—aluminum surface scratches easily)
  - Scraped at shallow angle
  - Used gasket remover spray for stubborn areas
  - **Verification:** Ran fingertip across surface—smooth, no gasket material felt

**Cylinder Head Gasket Surface:**
- Same process as block deck
- **Extra care:** Head is aluminum, softer than block

**Oil Pan Surface:**
- Old gasket material and RTV silicone sealant
- Used plastic scraper + brake cleaner

**Timing Cover Surface:**
- Minimal gasket material (used gasket, not RTV)

---

**Arjun's Final Checks:**

**Visual Inspection:**

- Cylinder block: On engine stand, ready for bore measurement
- Crankshaft: In padded cradle, journals accessible
- Pistons: Laid out in order (1, 2, 3, 4)

## Technical Deep Dive (Integrated Learning)

### Engine Removal Safety (SO-1-3-1 Details)

**Load Capacity Verification:**

**Engine Weight Reference (Indian Vehicles):**
- Maruti Swift 1.2L petrol: ~95 kg
- Maruti Swift 1.3L diesel: ~180 kg
- Tata Innova 2.2L diesel: ~220 kg
- Mahindra Scorpio 2.5L diesel: ~250 kg

**Hoist Capacity:**
- Standard workshop hoist: 500 kg (1,100 lbs)
- Safety factor: 2.5x minimum (for 200 kg engine, 500 kg hoist = 2.5x safety factor)

**Balance Point:**
Engine center of gravity typically forward of block center (due to accessories, timing components). Adjust lifting chain to achieve level hoist.

- ✓ Coolant drained

**Cooling System:**
- ✓ Upper radiator hose

- ✓ Heater inlet hose
- ✓ Heater outlet hose

**Electrical:**
- ✓ Battery (negative first, then positive)
- ✓ Main engine harness connector
- ✓ Alternator wiring
- ✓ Starter motor wiring
- ✓ All sensor connectors (coolant temp, oil pressure, cam/crank position, O2 sensors)

**Exhaust:**
- ✓ Downpipe/exhaust flange bolts

### Systematic Disassembly Sequence (SO-1-3-2 Details)

**The Universal Sequence (Applies to Most Engines):**

**External → Internal Progression:**

1. **Accessories:** Alternator, starter, AC compressor, power steering pump
2. **Intake/Exhaust:** Manifolds, turbocharger (if equipped)
3. **Valve Train Cover:** Valve cover (exposes camshaft)
4. **Timing Components:** Timing cover, belt/chain, gears
5. **Cylinder Head:** Head bolts, cylinder head removal
6. **Oil System:** Oil pan, oil pump
7. **Rotating Assembly:** Crankshaft main bearing caps, crankshaft
8. **Pistons:** Connecting rod caps, piston/rod assemblies

**Why This Sequence?**
- Each layer exposes the next
- Cannot access internal components until external removed
- Prevents damage (e.g., can't remove crankshaft with pistons still attached)

---

**Organization Best Practices:**

**Tray System:**
- Minimum 6 trays, labeled by system
- Prevents mixing fasteners (different torque specs)

**Photo Documentation:**
- Take photos BEFORE disconnecting complex components
- Electrical connectors: Photo shows routing and connection
- Timing marks: Photo preserves alignment
- Bracket locations: Photo shows bolt hole positions

**Labeling:**
- Connecting rods: Already stamped 1-4, verify orientation
- Main bearing caps: Stamped 1-5, note front-facing arrow
- Bolt locations: If bolts vary in length, label (e.g., "head bolt - front left")

### Component Cleaning Methods (SO-1-3-3 Details)

**Cleaning Method Selection Guide:**

**Solvent Tank:**
- **Best for:** Engine blocks, heavy components with oil and grease
- **Solution:** Mineral spirits or commercial degreaser
- **Time:** 30-60 minute soak
- **Safety:** Well-ventilated area, no open flames

**Parts Washer:**
- **Best for:** Cylinder heads, manifolds, large parts
- **Solution:** Water-based degreaser (environmentally friendly)
- **Method:** Recirculating pump, scrub with brush
- **Time:** 1-2 hours

**Ultrasonic Cleaner:**
- **Best for:** Small precision parts (injectors, sensors, bolts)
- **Solution:** Ultrasonic cleaning solution
- **Method:** High-frequency sound waves create cavitation (micro-bubbles clean)
- **Time:** 20-30 minute cycle

**Manual Cleaning:**
- **Best for:** Bearing surfaces, machined surfaces, delicate areas
- **Tools:** Clean rags, brake cleaner spray, plastic brushes
- **Caution:** NO wire brush on soft metals (aluminum), bearing surfaces, or machined sealing surfaces

---

**Gasket Removal Techniques:**

**Paper Gaskets:**
- **Tool:** Plastic scraper at 30° angle
- **Solvent:** Gasket remover spray (let soak 10 minutes)
- **Verify:** Run fingertip across surface—no material felt = clean

**RTV Silicone Gaskets:**
- **Tool:** Plastic scraper + razor blade (carefully)
- **Solvent:** Brake cleaner (dissolves silicone)
- **Caution:** DO NOT gouge aluminum surfaces

**Metal Gaskets (MLS - Multi-Layer Steel):**
- **Tool:** Plastic scraper only (metal layers peel off cleanly)
- **Verify:** Surface must be perfectly flat (any damage = leaks)

---

**Surface Inspection After Cleaning:**

**Cylinder Block Deck:**
- Check for warping (use precision straight edge + feeler gauge)
- Max warpage: 0.05mm (most engines)

**Cylinder Head Surface:**
- Check for warping (straight edge + feeler gauge)
- Check for cracks (especially between valves, exhaust side)

**Cylinder Walls:**
- Check for scoring (vertical scratches)
- Check for ridge at top (wear indicator)

## Assessment Content

### Quiz Questions (5 Questions - PowerPoint Slide Format)

**Question 1: Engine Removal Safety (Apply Level)**

Before lifting an engine with a hoist, you notice the engine tilts to one side. What is the CORRECT action?

A) Continue lifting slowly and adjust angle while suspended
B) Lower the engine, adjust chain lengths, and verify balance before lifting
C) Add a second chain to the low side to level it
D) Proceed with lift; engines naturally tilt during removal

**Answer:** B) Lower the engine, adjust chain lengths, and verify balance before lifting
**Explanation:** Unbalanced load creates unsafe hoisting condition. Lower engine, adjust chain attachment points or shorten chain on heavy side, and verify balance at low height before lifting to full clearance. Adjusting while fully suspended risks dropping engine or hoist tip-over.

---

**Question 2: Disassembly Sequence (Understand Level)**

You are disassembling a Maruti Swift 1.3L diesel engine. What is the CORRECT disassembly sequence?

A) Cylinder head → Timing components → Accessories → Oil pan → Crankshaft
B) Oil pan → Crankshaft → Pistons → Cylinder head → Timing components
C) Accessories → Timing components → Cylinder head → Oil pan → Crankshaft
D) Crankshaft → Pistons → Cylinder head → Timing components → Accessories

**Answer:** C) Accessories → Timing components → Cylinder head → Oil pan → Crankshaft
**Explanation:** Disassembly follows external-to-internal progression. Accessories removed first (outside layer), then timing components (cover and belt), then cylinder head (exposes pistons), then oil pan (exposes crankshaft bottom), then crankshaft. Cannot remove crankshaft while pistons are still attached.

---

**Question 3: Component Organization (Apply Level)**

During engine disassembly, you remove 10 cylinder head bolts of varying lengths. What is the BEST practice?

A) Place all bolts in one container; they'll be easy to identify during reassembly
B) Lay bolts on bench in the order they were removed and take a photo
C) Measure each bolt length and write it on the service manual diagram
D) Thread each bolt back into its original hole finger-tight until reassembly

**Answer:** B) Lay bolts on bench in the order they were removed and take a photo
**Explanation:** Visual documentation preserves exact bolt positions. Different-length head bolts MUST return to original locations (short bolt in long hole = insufficient clamping; long bolt in short hole = bottoms out). Threading bolts back in risks cross-threading and wastes time. Photo + organized layout = foolproof reassembly.

---

**Question 4: Gasket Surface Cleaning (Apply Level)**

You are removing old head gasket material from an aluminum cylinder head surface. Which tool is SAFEST?

A) Wire brush on bench grinder
B) Metal scraper at 45° angle
C) Plastic scraper at 30° angle
D) Sandpaper (120 grit)

**Answer:** C) Plastic scraper at 30° angle
**Explanation:** Aluminum is soft and scratches easily. Plastic scraper removes gasket material without damaging sealing surface. Wire brush, metal scraper, and sandpaper all gouge aluminum, creating leak paths. Use plastic scraper + gasket remover spray for stubborn areas.

---

**Question 5: Engine Cleaning Methods (Understand Level)**

You need to clean oil passages inside a crankshaft. Which method is MOST effective?

A) Soak crankshaft in solvent tank for 1 hour, then air dry
B) Spray brake cleaner on journals and wipe with rag
C) Use rifle brush to scrub passages, then blow out with compressed air
D) Ultrasonic cleaner for 30 minutes

**Answer:** C) Use rifle brush to scrub passages, then blow out with compressed air
**Explanation:** Oil passages inside crankshaft journals must be completely clear (blockage = bearing failure). Rifle brush mechanically removes deposits. Compressed air verifies passages are clear (air flows freely = clean). Soaking and spraying don't clean internal passages. Ultrasonic cleaner works but rifle brush + compressed air is faster and verifies cleanliness.
