### Act 2: The Investigation - Evidence Collection (50 minutes)
**"Let the measurements speak. Every number tells part of the story."**

**Investigation Scene 1: The Cylinder Bores (9:45 AM - 10:30 AM)**

Ravi started with the most critical component: The cylinder walls.

**The Setup:**
- Bore gauge calibrated to 71.00mm using micrometer (i20 1.2L standard bore: 71.00-71.02mm)
- Service manual specification sheet open
- Measurement worksheet printed (3 heights × 2 orientations × 4 cylinders = 24 measurements)

**The Technique:**

Mehta sir had taught him: "Cylinders wear unevenly. Three patterns to look for:"
1. **Taper:** More wear at top (piston reversal + combustion heat)
2. **Out-of-round:** More wear in thrust direction (piston side-loading)
3. **Scoring:** Vertical scratches (contamination or lack of lubrication)

**Cylinder #1 - Intake Side:**

Ravi inserted the bore gauge at the top (10mm below deck), rocked it gently to find the smallest reading (true diameter), and noted the measurement.

**Top (10mm below deck):** 71.04mm
**Middle (mid-stroke):** 71.02mm
**Bottom (bottom of ring travel):** 71.01mm

He rotated 90° to thrust direction:

**Top (thrust direction):** 71.05mm

Ravi frowned. "That's above specification. Maximum spec is 71.02mm."

He calculated:
- **Taper:** 71.04 - 71.01 = 0.03mm (spec max: 0.02mm = OVER LIMIT)
- **Out-of-round (top):** 71.05 - 71.04 = 0.01mm (spec max: 0.02mm = OK)

**First Clue:** Cylinder #1 has excessive taper.

**Cylinders #2, #3, #4:**

Ravi methodically measured all cylinders. After 45 minutes, his worksheet looked like this:

| Cylinder | Top (Intake) | Top (Thrust) | Middle | Bottom | Taper | Out-of-Round | Status |
|----------|--------------|--------------|--------|--------|-------|--------------|--------|
| 1 | 71.04 | 71.05 | 71.02 | 71.01 | 0.03mm | 0.01mm | WORN |
| 2 | 71.05 | 71.06 | 71.02 | 71.01 | 0.04mm | 0.01mm | WORN |
| 3 | 71.03 | 71.04 | 71.02 | 71.01 | 0.02mm | 0.01mm | BORDERLINE |
| 4 | 71.04 | 71.04 | 71.02 | 71.01 | 0.03mm | 0.00mm | WORN |

**Ravi's Analysis:**

"All four cylinders show taper wear exceeding specification. Cylinder #2 is worst. But here's what's interesting: Out-of-round is minimal. That rules out bearing problems (bearing wear causes severe out-of-round). This is classic top-end wear."

**The Question:** What causes top-end cylinder wear?

**Possible Causes:**
1. Lack of lubrication at top of cylinder (oil ring not functioning)
2. Excessive combustion heat (lean mixture, overheating)
3. Contamination (dirt ingestion, poor air filter)
4. High mileage (normal wear)

"I need more clues," Ravi said.

**Investigation Scene 2: The Crankshaft Journals (10:30 AM - 11:15 AM)**

If the cylinder wear was severe, the crankshaft should also show wear (they're part of the same engine, same mileage).

**The Setup:**
- Crankshaft on padded V-blocks
- Outside micrometer (25-50mm range)
- Dial indicator on magnetic stand
- Service manual crankshaft specs:
  - Main journal diameter: 48.00-48.01mm
  - Rod journal diameter: 42.00-42.01mm
  - Maximum runout: 0.02mm

**Main Journal #1 (Front):**

Ravi measured at three points along the journal length (front, center, rear) and at two orientations (0°, 90°).

**Measurements:**
- Front: 47.99mm
- Center: 47.99mm
- Rear: 47.99mm
- 90° orientation: 47.99mm

**Analysis:** 0.01mm undersize (within spec), perfectly round, no taper.

**All Five Main Journals:**

Ravi measured all five main journals. Every single one: 47.99mm across all measurements.

"Incredible. This crankshaft is barely worn. At 85,000 km, it's basically new."

**Rod Journals (4 Total):**

Same pattern. All measured 42.00mm ± 0.005mm. Perfect.

**Crankshaft Runout Test:**

Ravi set up the dial indicator on main journal #3 (center), zeroed it, and rotated the crankshaft one complete revolution.

**Runout:** 0.008mm (spec max: 0.02mm = EXCELLENT)

**Second Major Clue:** Crankshaft is in excellent condition. Minimal wear.

**Ravi's Thoughts:**

"This doesn't make sense. The cylinders show significant wear, but the crankshaft is perfect. If the engine was neglected or abused, both would show wear. What's going on?"

---

**Investigation Scene 3: The Pistons and Rings (11:15 AM - 12:00 PM)**

**Piston Diameter Measurement:**

Service manual spec for Hyundai i20 1.2L piston: 70.97-70.98mm (measured at skirt, 10mm below ring grooves)

**Piston #1:**

Ravi measured perpendicular to piston pin axis: **70.96mm**

"Below specification. This piston has worn 0.01-0.02mm."

**All Four Pistons:**

| Piston | Diameter | Spec | Status |
|--------|----------|------|--------|
| 1 | 70.96mm | 70.97-70.98mm | WORN |
| 2 | 70.95mm | 70.97-70.98mm | WORN |
| 3 | 70.96mm | 70.97-70.98mm | WORN |
| 4 | 70.96mm | 70.97-70.98mm | WORN |

**Piston-to-Bore Clearance Calculation:**

- **Cylinder #2 bore (worst):** 71.05mm (top)
- **Piston #2 diameter:** 70.95mm
- **Clearance:** 71.05 - 70.95 = **0.10mm**

**Specification:** 0.02-0.04mm

**Ravi's Reaction:** "0.10mm clearance! That's 2.5 times specification. No wonder there's oil consumption."

**The Smoking Gun (Literally):**

Ravi inspected the piston rings. The **oil control ring (bottom ring)** on all four pistons was **completely clogged with carbon deposits**.

The oil ring has small holes (drain holes) that allow scraped oil to return to the crankcase. When clogged, oil stays on cylinder walls, burns in combustion, creates blue smoke, and causes more carbon buildup (cycle repeats).

**Third Major Clue:** Oil rings clogged with carbon = oil burning = cylinder wear accelerated.

---

**Investigation Scene 4: The Valves (12:00 PM - 12:30 PM)**

**Valve Stem Diameter Measurement:**

Service manual spec: 5.48-5.50mm

Ravi measured all eight intake valves and eight exhaust valves using micrometer.

**Results:** All valves between 5.49-5.50mm. Within specification.

**Valve Guide Wear Test:**

Ravi inserted a valve into its guide and set up a dial indicator to measure side-to-side play.

**Maximum play:** 0.3mm (spec max: 0.5mm = OK)

**Valve Face Inspection:**

Using a magnifying glass, Ravi inspected valve seating surfaces. All valves showed normal contact patterns (1.5mm wide band). No burning, no cracks.

**Fourth Clue:** Valve train in good condition. Not contributing to oil consumption or power loss.

Ravi's eyes widened. "Oil pump sends oil to crankshaft bearings under pressure. The crankshaft is bathed in oil—that's why it's perfect. But cylinder walls are lubricated by **splash and the oil rings scraping oil up from the crankcase**."

"Then the cylinder walls don't get enough oil! They wear faster!"

Ravi stood up. "I know what happened. Let me verify one more thing."

The carbon deposits in the oil ring grooves weren't just carbon—they were **sludge**. Thick, black, tar-like sludge.

"She followed the manual's normal interval, but she does city driving—short trips, traffic jams. That's **severe duty**. The oil degraded faster, formed sludge, and clogged the oil rings."

**The Chain of Events (Root Cause Analysis):**

1. **Extended oil change intervals** (10,000 km in severe city driving)
2. → Oil degraded, formed sludge
3. → Sludge clogged oil control rings
4. → Oil rings couldn't scrape oil off cylinder walls
5. → Excess oil remained on cylinder walls
6. → Oil burned during combustion (blue smoke)
7. → Carbon deposits formed, accelerating ring clogging (vicious cycle)
8. → Cylinder walls lacked proper lubrication
9. → Accelerated cylinder bore wear (taper formation)
10. → Excessive piston-to-bore clearance (0.10mm)
11. → Oil consumption, power loss, rough idle

"The previous mechanic saw a worn engine and said 'replace it.' But **he didn't understand why it wore out.** If Mrs. Verma replaces the engine without fixing the root cause—extended oil changes—the new engine will do the same thing."

## Technical Deep Dive (Integrated Learning)

### Cylinder Bore Measurement (SO-1-4-1 Details)

**The 3-Point, 2-Orientation Method:**

**Why 3 Heights?**
- **Top (10mm below deck):** Maximum wear (piston reversal, combustion heat, minimal lubrication)
- **Middle (mid-stroke):** Moderate wear (piston speed highest here)
- **Bottom (bottom of ring travel):** Minimal wear (less heat, better lubrication)

**Why 2 Orientations?**
- **0° (intake/exhaust side):** Wear from piston side loading during power stroke
- **90° (thrust direction):** Maximum side load (piston pushed against wall)

**Taper Calculation:**
- Taper = Top measurement - Bottom measurement
- Acceptable: < 0.02mm (most engines)
- Excessive: > 0.05mm (requires rebore)

**Out-of-Round Calculation:**
- Out-of-Round = Measurement at 0° - Measurement at 90° (at same height)
- Acceptable: < 0.02mm
- Excessive: > 0.05mm (indicates bearing or piston pin wear)

**Decision Matrix:**

| Taper/Out-of-Round | Bore Diameter | Action Required |
|--------------------|---------------|-----------------|
| < 0.02mm | Within spec | Hone + new rings (if needed) |
| 0.02-0.05mm | Slightly over spec | Rebore 0.25mm oversize |
| 0.05-0.10mm | Significantly over | Rebore 0.50mm oversize |
| > 0.10mm | Severely worn | Rebore 0.75mm or replace block |

**Indian Vehicle Specs (Standard Bore Diameters):**
- Maruti Swift 1.2L: 73.00-73.02mm
- Hyundai i20 1.2L: 71.00-71.02mm
- Tata Innova 2.2L: 85.00-85.02mm
- Honda City 1.5L: 73.00-73.02mm

### Crankshaft Measurement (SO-1-4-2 Details)

**Main Journal Measurement:**

**3-Point, 2-Orientation Method:**
- Measure front, center, rear of journal (detects taper)
- Measure at 0° and 90° (detects out-of-round)

**Interpretation:**
- Even wear = good oil pressure and lubrication
- Uneven wear = blocked oil passage or bearing misalignment
- Severe out-of-round = bearing failure (metal-to-metal contact)

**Crankshaft Runout Test:**

**Procedure:**
1. Place crankshaft on V-blocks at journals #1 and #5
2. Position dial indicator on journal #3 (center)
3. Zero dial indicator
4. Rotate crankshaft one full revolution
5. Note maximum and minimum readings
6. Runout = Max reading - Min reading

**Specification:** Most engines: 0.02mm maximum runout

**Interpretation:**
- Runout > 0.02mm = Crankshaft bent (usually from overheating or bearing failure)
- Bent crankshaft causes vibration, uneven bearing wear
- Correction: Straightening (specialized machine shop) or replacement

**Bearing Clearance Measurement (Plastigage Method):**

**Procedure:**
1. Clean journal and bearing shell completely (oil film adds thickness)
2. Place Plastigage strand on journal (parallel to crankshaft axis)
3. Install bearing cap and torque to specification
4. Remove bearing cap (do NOT rotate crankshaft)
5. Measure flattened Plastigage width using scale on package
6. Width correlates to clearance (wider = smaller clearance)

**Specification:** 0.025-0.075mm typical

**Interpretation:**
- < 0.025mm = Too tight (bearing will overheat)
- 0.025-0.075mm = Correct
- > 0.075mm = Too loose (low oil pressure, noise)

---

### Piston and Ring Measurement (SO-1-4-3 Details)

**Piston Diameter Measurement:**

**Location:** Measure at skirt, 10mm below bottom ring groove, perpendicular to piston pin axis

**Why this location?**
- Piston is tapered (smaller at top, larger at skirt)
- Skirt is the bearing surface (largest diameter, contacts cylinder wall)

**Piston-to-Bore Clearance:**

**Calculation:** Cylinder bore diameter - Piston diameter = Clearance

**Specification:** 0.02-0.04mm (typical)

**Effects:**
- Too tight (< 0.02mm): Piston seizes (expands with heat, no room)
- Correct (0.02-0.04mm): Smooth operation, minimal noise
- Too loose (> 0.05mm): Piston slap noise, oil consumption, power loss

---

**Piston Ring Measurement:**

**1. Ring End Gap (Installed in Cylinder):**

**Procedure:**
1. Insert ring into cylinder bore
2. Use piston to push ring square (perpendicular to bore)
3. Position ring at unworn area (bottom of cylinder)
4. Measure gap with feeler gauge

**Specification:** 0.25-0.40mm (typical top ring), 0.30-0.50mm (oil ring)

**Interpretation:**
- Too tight (< 0.25mm): Ring ends contact when hot, causes scuffing
- Correct: Allows for thermal expansion
- Too loose (> 0.60mm): Blowby (compression loss), oil consumption

**2. Ring Side Clearance (In Piston Groove):**

**Procedure:**
1. Install ring in piston groove
2. Insert feeler gauge between ring and groove
3. Measure clearance

**Specification:** 0.03-0.08mm (typical)

**Interpretation:**
- Too tight: Ring sticks, doesn't seal properly
- Correct: Ring moves freely, maintains tension
- Too loose: Ring flutter, poor sealing

---

**Valve Stem and Guide Wear:**

**Valve Stem Diameter:** Measure with micrometer at three points along stem

**Valve Guide Wear Test:**
1. Insert valve into guide
2. Position dial indicator perpendicular to valve stem
3. Push valve side-to-side
4. Note total indicator movement

**Specification:** 0.5mm maximum play

**Interpretation:**
- > 0.5mm: Guide worn, needs replacement or machining
- Worn guides = oil consumption (oil pulled down stem into combustion chamber)

## Assessment Content

### Quiz Questions (5 Questions - PowerPoint Slide Format)

**Question 1: Cylinder Bore Analysis (Analyze Level)**

You measure a Maruti Swift 1.2L cylinder bore and get these results:
- Top: 73.04mm
- Middle: 73.02mm
- Bottom: 73.01mm
- Specification: 73.00-73.02mm, Max taper: 0.02mm

What is the CORRECT diagnosis and action?

A) Hone cylinder and install standard pistons
B) Rebore to 0.25mm oversize
C) No action needed, within specification
D) Replace engine block

**Answer:** B) Rebore to 0.25mm oversize
**Explanation:** Taper = 73.04 - 73.01 = 0.03mm (exceeds 0.02mm maximum). Top measurement 73.04mm also exceeds maximum specification 73.02mm. Honing removes glaze but not taper. Must rebore to 0.25mm oversize (73.25mm) and install oversize pistons.

---

**Question 2: Crankshaft Runout Interpretation (Analyze Level)**

You check crankshaft runout using V-blocks and dial indicator. The dial indicator shows 0.05mm total movement during one revolution. Specification: 0.02mm maximum runout.

What does this indicate?

A) Crankshaft is acceptable, 0.05mm is within tolerance
B) Crankshaft is bent and needs straightening or replacement
C) Main bearings are worn
D) Measurement error, re-check calibration

**Answer:** B) Crankshaft is bent and needs straightening or replacement
**Explanation:** 0.05mm runout exceeds 0.02mm specification by 2.5x. Excessive runout indicates bent crankshaft (usually from overheating or bearing failure). Bent crankshaft causes vibration and uneven bearing wear. Requires professional straightening or replacement. This is not measurement error—runout exceeding spec indicates mechanical damage.

---

**Question 3: Piston-to-Bore Clearance (Apply Level)**

You measure:
- Cylinder bore: 71.05mm
- Piston diameter: 70.96mm
- Specification: 0.02-0.04mm clearance

What is the clearance, and what is the consequence?

A) 0.09mm clearance; acceptable, within specification
B) 0.09mm clearance; excessive, will cause oil consumption
C) 0.04mm clearance; acceptable
D) Cannot calculate without ring gap measurement

**Answer:** B) 0.09mm clearance; excessive, will cause oil consumption
**Explanation:** Clearance = 71.05 - 70.96 = 0.09mm. Specification is 0.02-0.04mm. Clearance of 0.09mm is 2x specification. Excessive clearance allows oil to pass between piston and cylinder wall, causing oil consumption and blue smoke. Also causes piston slap noise and power loss.

**Question 4: Ring End Gap Interpretation (Understand Level)**

You install a new piston ring in an unworn area of the cylinder and measure the end gap with feeler gauges. The gap is 0.15mm. Specification: 0.25-0.40mm.

What action should you take?

A) Install the ring; 0.15mm is acceptable
B) Do not install; gap is too small, ring needs filing to increase gap
C) Install ring and check clearance after engine heat cycle
D) Replace cylinder; bore is too small

**Answer:** B) Do not install; gap is too small, ring needs filing to increase gap
**Explanation:** 0.15mm gap is below minimum specification (0.25mm). When engine heats up, ring expands. If gap is too small, ring ends will contact (butt together), causing ring to lose tension and score the cylinder. File ring ends carefully to achieve 0.25-0.30mm gap before installation.

**Question 5: Root Cause Analysis (Analyze Level)**

You find these conditions in a Hyundai i20:
- Cylinder bores: Excessive taper wear (0.04mm)
- Crankshaft: Minimal wear, within spec
- Oil control rings: Clogged with carbon sludge
- Service records: Oil changes every 10,000 km (city driving)

What is the ROOT CAUSE of cylinder wear?

A) Normal high-mileage wear
B) Extended oil change intervals caused sludge, clogging oil rings, reducing cylinder wall lubrication
C) Manufacturing defect in cylinder material
D) Crankshaft bearing failure causing contamination

**Answer:** B) Extended oil change intervals caused sludge, clogging oil rings, reducing cylinder wall lubrication
**Explanation:** Pattern indicates top-end wear (cylinders) with bottom-end preservation (crankshaft). Clogged oil rings couldn't scrape oil properly, starving cylinder walls of lubrication. Extended intervals in severe duty (city driving) caused oil degradation and sludge formation. This is root cause. Normal wear would affect both top and bottom end. Manufacturing defect would show from early mileage. Bearing failure would show crankshaft damage.

### Hands-On Exercise Preview (Afternoon Practical - 3 Hours)

**Exercise Title:** Detective's Workshop - Engine Component Measurement Challenge

**Objective:**
Measure engine components using precision tools, record data systematically, analyze wear patterns, and diagnose root cause of engine failure.

**Setup (20 min):**
1. Students divided into groups of 4
2. Each group receives:
   - Training engine with known wear (instructor pre-measured)
   - Bore gauge + micrometer for calibration
   - Outside micrometers (25-50mm, 0-25mm)
   - Dial indicator + magnetic base
   - V-blocks for crankshaft
   - Plastigage (for bearing clearance)
   - Feeler gauges (for ring gaps)
   - Measurement worksheet (structured data collection)
   - Service manual with specifications

**Activity 1: Cylinder Bore Investigation (60 min)**

**Task:**
Measure all four cylinders using 3-point, 2-orientation method:
1. Calibrate bore gauge to nominal diameter using micrometer
2. Measure Top, Middle, Bottom at 0° orientation
3. Measure Top at 90° orientation
4. Record all measurements on worksheet
5. Calculate taper and out-of-round for each cylinder
6. Compare to specifications
7. Recommend action (hone, rebore 0.25mm, rebore 0.50mm)

**Success Criteria:**
- All 24 measurements (4 cylinders × 6 measurements) within ±0.01mm of instructor measurements
- Correct calculation of taper and out-of-round
- Accurate diagnosis (hone vs. rebore decision)

**Instructor Verification:**
- Spot-check 3 random measurements per group
- Review calculation methodology
- Verify bore gauge technique (rocking motion, finding smallest reading)

---

**Activity 2: Crankshaft Analysis (50 min)**

**Part A: Journal Measurement (30 min)**

**Task:**
1. Measure all five main journals (front, center, rear × 2 orientations = 6 measurements each)
2. Measure all four rod journals (same method)
3. Record on worksheet
4. Identify maximum wear and location
5. Calculate taper and out-of-round for worst journal
6. Determine if regrinding required

**Part B: Runout Check (20 min)**

**Task:**
1. Set up crankshaft on V-blocks
2. Position dial indicator on center main journal
3. Zero indicator
4. Rotate crankshaft one revolution
5. Note total indicator movement
6. Compare to specification (0.02mm max)

**Success Criteria:**
- Journal measurements within ±0.01mm of instructor
- Correct runout measurement technique
- Accurate regrinding decision

---

**Activity 3: Piston and Ring Measurement (50 min)**

**Part A: Piston Measurement (20 min)**

**Task:**
1. Measure all four pistons at skirt (perpendicular to pin axis)
2. Calculate piston-to-bore clearance for each cylinder
3. Compare to specification (0.02-0.04mm)
4. Identify which pistons need replacement

**Part B: Ring End Gap (20 min)**

**Task:**
1. Insert new ring into cylinder bore
2. Use piston to square ring in bore
3. Measure end gap with feeler gauge
4. Compare to specification
5. Determine if gap adjustment needed

**Part C: Ring Side Clearance (10 min)**

**Task:**
1. Install ring in piston groove
2. Measure side clearance with feeler gauge
3. Verify ring moves freely

**Success Criteria:**
- Piston measurements within ±0.01mm
- Clearance calculations correct
- Ring gap measurements within ±0.02mm

---

**Activity 4: Root Cause Analysis (20 min)**

**Task:**
Based on all measurements collected, answer these questions:
1. What components are within specification?
2. What components exceed wear limits?
3. What wear pattern exists (even, uneven, top-end, bottom-end)?
4. What is the most likely root cause?
5. What repair action do you recommend?

**Group Discussion:**
Each group presents findings (5 minutes per group)

**Instructor Feedback:**
Reveal actual root cause of training engine failure; discuss diagnostic reasoning
