## 📚 COURSE STRUCTURE

### Module 1: Engine Foundations - Build Your Engine (2 sessions)
**Gamified approach:** Students make design decisions for a workplace engine application

- **Session 1:** Architecture, Power, Thermodynamics
  - Configuration choices, displacement, compression ratio
  - Torque vs power, BMEP, thermal efficiency
  - Otto and Diesel cycles

- **Session 2:** Breathing & Control Systems
  - 4-stroke cycle, volumetric efficiency
  - Variable valve timing
  - ECU, sensors, actuators, closed-loop control

**Situated Learning Problem:** Design and Justify Your Workplace Engine

---

### Module 2: Performance & Calibration Excellence (2 sessions)

- **Session 3:** Performance Analysis & Operating Characteristics
  - Thermal efficiency, BSFC, specific outputs
  - Torque and power curves interpretation
  - Effects of spark timing, injection timing, compression ratio

- **Session 4:** Engine Calibration Strategies
  - SI calibration: Spark timing, fuel metering, VVT
  - CI calibration: Injection timing/pressure, EGR, boost control
  - Multi-objective optimization and trade-offs

**Situated Learning Problem:** Optimize Performance in Your Fleet

---

### Module 3: Air & Fuel Systems - Foundation of Good Combustion (3 sessions)

- **Session 5:** Air Intake, Exhaust & Boosting Systems
  - Volumetric efficiency factors
  - Turbocharging fundamentals and matching
  - Advanced boosting: VGT, twin-scroll, supercharging

- **Session 6:** Fuel Injection Systems & Mixture Formation
  - SI systems: PFI vs GDI, stratified operation
  - CI systems: Common rail, multiple injections
  - Lambda, AFR, fuel properties

- **Session 7:** Charge Motion & In-Cylinder Flow Dynamics
  - Swirl, tumble, squish, turbulence
  - SI strategy (tumble-based) vs CI strategy (swirl-based)
  - Unwanted flows: Crevices, blow-by

**Situated Learning Problem:** Air & Fuel System Optimization (choice of turbo matching, fuel diagnosis, or VE improvement)

---

### Module 4: Combustion Processes - Where the Magic Happens (3 sessions)

- **Session 8:** Combustion in SI Engines
  - Flame propagation process
  - Engine knock mechanism and mitigation
  - Octane rating, advanced SI strategies

- **Session 9:** Combustion in CI Engines
  - Four phases of diesel combustion
  - Fuel spray behavior and air-fuel mixing
  - NOx-PM trade-off, injection effects

- **Session 10:** Advanced Engine Cycles & Combustion Modes
  - Thermodynamic cycles comparison
  - Atkinson/Miller cycles (VVT implementation)
  - HCCI, RCCI, PCCI - benefits and challenges
  - Variable compression ratio

**Situated Learning Problem:** Combustion Optimization & Diagnostics (choice of SI knock mitigation, diesel NOx-PM optimization, or advanced mode feasibility)

---

### Module 5: Emissions - The Constraint That Defines Modern Design (3 sessions)

- **Session 11:** Emission Formation Mechanisms
  - NOx: Thermal Zeldovich mechanism
  - CO: Incomplete combustion
  - HC: Crevice volumes, quench
  - PM: Fuel-rich zones, soot formation/oxidation

- **Session 12:** Exhaust Gas After-Treatment Systems
  - SI: Three-way catalyst (TWC), GPF
  - CI: DOC, DPF (regeneration strategies), SCR (urea/ammonia)
  - System integration and thermal management

- **Session 13:** Regulations, Compliance, OBD & Diagnostics
  - Global emission standards (Euro, EPA, BS-VI, China)
  - Real Driving Emissions (RDE) testing
  - OBD-II architecture, MIL, DTCs, diagnostic workflow

**Situated Learning Problem:** Emissions Compliance Strategy (choice of meeting new standard, emission diagnostics, or after-treatment optimization)

---

### Module 6: Advanced Technologies & Future Directions (2 sessions)

- **Session 14:** Alternate Fuels & Energy Transition
  - CNG, hydrogen, ethanol, biodiesel, e-fuels
  - Well-to-wheel emissions and lifecycle analysis
  - IC engines in future mobility (where they persist)

- **Session 15:** Thermal Management, Friction Reduction & System Integration
  - Heat transfer and thermal management
  - Friction sources and reduction strategies
  - Waste heat recovery (turbocompound, ORC, TEG)
  - Engine downsizing, hybridization, system integration

**Situated Learning Problem:** Future Powertrain Technology Assessment (comprehensive capstone project)

## 🎓 PEDAGOGICAL APPROACH

### Instructional Design Principles

1. **Backward Design:**
   - Started with "What should students DO?" (outcomes)
   - Then "How will we know?" (assessments)
   - Finally "What experiences will get them there?" (instruction)

2. **Situated Learning:**
   - Authentic workplace problems
   - Complex challenges requiring concept integration
   - Real-world constraints (cost, regulations, trade-offs)
   - Peer learning through presentations

3. **Active Learning:**
   - Minimal passive lecture
   - MCQs embedded throughout for engagement
   - Think-pair-share discussions
   - Interactive calculations and design exercises
   - Real-time formative assessment

4. **Cognitive Load Management:**
   - Content chunked into 10-15 minute parts
   - Build on prior knowledge systematically
   - Use worked examples before practice
   - Scaffold complex concepts

5. **Differentiation:**
   - For experienced students: Challenge questions, leadership roles
   - For less experienced: Glossary, foundational resources, scaffolding
   - For different industries: Contextualized examples

## 📊 ASSESSMENT STRATEGY

### Formative Assessment (Ongoing Feedback)
- **MCQs during lectures (45 total):** 2-3 per session
  - Immediate feedback
  - Identify misconceptions
  - Adjust teaching in real-time
  - Not graded (or low-stakes points for participation)

- **In-class exercises:** Design scenarios, calculations, diagnostic challenges
  - Active practice
  - Peer discussion
  - Debrief and correct errors

- **Minute papers / Exit tickets:** Optional reflection
  - "What did you learn today?"
  - "What question remains?"
  - Inform next session planning

### Summative Assessment (Graded)
- **Situated Learning Problems (6 total, ~70-80% of grade):**
  - One per module
  - Major projects (3-12 pages each)
  - Apply concepts to authentic problems
  - Assessed using detailed rubrics
  - Selected students present to class

- **Final Exam (Optional, ~20-30% of grade if required):**
  - Comprehensive exam drawing from MCQ bank
  - Include calculations and short-answer problems
  - Or replace with final presentation of Module 6 SLP

**Assessment Alignment:**
- Every session outcome is assessed (through MCQs or SLPs)
- Bloom levels matched: Understand→MCQs, Apply→Calculations, Analyze/Evaluate/Create→SLPs
- Frequent low-stakes + fewer high-stakes assessments

## 🔧 CUSTOMIZATION OPTIONS

### For Different Industries

**Automotive (Passenger Vehicles):**
- Use WLTP/RDE testing, Euro 6d regulations
- Examples: Turbocharged GDI engines, hybrid systems
- Emphasize fuel economy, CO2 reduction, customer drivability

**Heavy-Duty Trucking:**
- Use EPA regulations, long-haul diesel examples
- Emphasize TCO (total cost of ownership), durability
- Focus on SCR urea consumption, DPF regeneration, fuel efficiency

**Power Generation:**
- Stationary engine examples, continuous operation
- Emphasize thermal efficiency, waste heat recovery
- Emissions compliance (EPA stationary source rules)

**Off-Highway (Construction, Agriculture):**
- Rugged applications, variable duty cycles
- Fuel flexibility, durability, ease of maintenance
- EPA Tier 4 Final regulations

**Marine Engines:**
- Large diesel engines, IMO regulations
- Fuel oil properties, slow-speed vs medium-speed
- SCR for NOx, fuel economy for operating cost

### For Different Time Allocations

**If More Time (e.g., 3 hours per session):**
- Add hands-on labs (engine dyno, teardown, OBD-II diagnostics)
- Include guest speakers from industry
- Extend exercises to full-period workshops
- Add software simulations (GT-Power, WAVE, AVL BOOST)

**If Less Time (e.g., 60 minutes per session):**
- Streamline content - focus on key concepts
- Move some content to flipped classroom (videos before class)
- Reduce number of MCQs (1-2 per session)
- Shorten or combine some sessions
- Make exercises homework instead of in-class

### For Different Student Backgrounds

**If Students Have Minimal Engine Knowledge:**
- Add prerequisite module (online, pre-semester)
- Spend more time on Module 1 fundamentals
- Provide glossary and supplementary readings
- Use more analogies and less jargon

**If Students Are Experienced Engineers:**
- Accelerate through Module 1
- Emphasize advanced topics (Module 4-6)
- Challenge with deeper case studies
- Invite students to present their workplace experiences

## 🏆 EXPECTED OUTCOMES

Upon successful completion of this course using this framework, students will be able to:

1. **Design** IC engine systems with appropriate architecture, displacement, compression ratio, and breathing strategies for specific applications

2. **Analyze** engine performance using metrics like BMEP, thermal efficiency, BSFC, and torque/power curves to identify optimization opportunities

3. **Optimize** air intake, fuel injection, and charge motion systems to maximize volumetric efficiency, mixture quality, and combustion performance

4. **Diagnose** combustion-related issues (knock, emissions, efficiency) using thermodynamic principles and diagnostic data, and propose evidence-based solutions

5. **Evaluate** emission control strategies integrating in-cylinder measures (EGR, injection timing) with after-treatment systems (TWC, DPF, SCR) to achieve regulatory compliance

6. **Assess** the technical and economic feasibility of advanced technologies (alternate fuels, HCCI, waste heat recovery, hybridization) for real-world implementation

**Most importantly:** Students will think like IC engine engineers, making data-driven decisions that balance performance, efficiency, emissions, cost, and durability.
