**Duration:** 90 minutes lecture + 30 minutes Q&A

## 🎯 Session Objectives (For Instructor)

By the end of this session, students will be able to:
- Understand tire construction and specifications
- Calculate normal forces and load distribution
- Apply friction principles to tire force generation
- Analyze friction circle for combined forces

### **TRIGGER: The Fundamental Limit** (Slides 5-6, ~5 minutes)

#### Slide 5: The Maximum Force Limit
**Visual:** Car sliding vs gripping
**Instructor Script:**
> "Here's a question that will drive everything today: **Why can't tires provide unlimited force?**
>
> If I asked you to accelerate at 5 m/s², you might do it easily. But if I asked for 15 m/s², your tires would just spin. Why? What's the limit?
>
> The answer is in the physics of friction. Let me show you."

#### Slide 6: Friction - The Only Force Source
**Visual:** Free body diagram of tire contact patch
**Instructor Script:**
> "Every force a tire generates – whether pushing the car forward, slowing it down, or turning – comes from **one source**: friction between rubber and road.
>
> No friction = no force = no motion control.
>
> This is why:
> - Ice is terrifying (low friction)
> - Racing slicks exist (maximize friction)
> - Worn tires are dangerous (reduced friction)
>
> Today, we'll calculate exactly how much force friction can provide."

### **RISING ACTION: Building Understanding** (Slides 7-22, ~50 minutes)

#### **Part 1: Tire Construction & Specifications** (Slides 7-10, ~12 minutes)

##### Slide 7: What's Inside a Tire?
**Visual:** Cross-section diagram of tire
**Instructor Script:**
> "Before we talk friction, let's understand what we're working with. A modern tire is engineering marvel. Let me show you what's inside.
>
> [Point to diagram layers]:
> - **Tread**: Rubber that contacts road
> - **Belt**: Steel or fabric reinforcement
> - **Carcass**: Fabric plies that give structure
> - **Bead**: Steel wire that seals to rim
>
> Two main types: **Radial** (modern standard) vs **Bias-ply** (older, some specialty uses)."

##### Slide 8: Radial vs Bias-Ply
**Visual:** Side-by-side comparison
**Instructor Script:**
> "**Radial tires** (what you drive on):
> - Cords run radially (perpendicular to direction of travel)
> - Better heat dissipation
> - Longer life, better fuel economy
> - Less rolling resistance
>
> **Bias-ply tires** (rare today):
> - Cords run at angles
> - Stiffer sidewalls
> - Still used in some agricultural and aircraft applications
>
> For this course, assume radial unless stated otherwise."

##### Slide 9: Tire Nomenclature - Reading the Sidewall
**Visual:** Tire sidewall with callouts
**Instructor Script:**
> "When you see **205/55 R16 91V**:
> - **205**: Width in mm (205 mm)
> - **55**: Aspect ratio (height is 55% of width)
> - **R**: Radial construction
> - **16**: Wheel diameter in inches
> - **91**: Load index (615 kg capacity)
> - **V**: Speed rating (up to 240 km/h)
>
> Engineers use this to select appropriate tires for vehicle loads and speeds."

##### Slide 10: Quick Exercise
**Visual:** Different tire codes
**Instructor Script:**
> "Decode this: **245/40 R18 97Y**
>
> [Pause for students]
>
> - 245 mm wide
> - Height = 40% of 245 = 98 mm
> - Radial, 18-inch wheel
> - 730 kg load capacity per tire
> - Speed rated to 300 km/h (performance tire)
>
> Moving on to forces."

#### **Part 2: Normal Force & Load Distribution** (Slides 11-14, ~12 minutes)

##### Slide 11: Normal Force - The Foundation of Friction
**Visual:** Free body diagram showing weight and normal force
**Instructor Script:**
> "Here's a critical concept from physics: **The maximum friction force depends on the normal force.**
>
> Normal force (N) is the force pressing the tire into the road. For a stationary car on flat ground:
>
> **N = Weight on that tire**
>
> But it's not always equal on all four tires. Load distribution matters."

##### Slide 12: Load Distribution Basics
**Visual:** Top view of car with weight percentages
**Instructor Script:**
> "Most cars are front-heavy (engine in front). Typical distribution:
> - **Front axle**: 60% of weight
> - **Rear axle**: 40% of weight
>
> For our 1,300 kg Honda Civic:
> - Total weight (W) = 1,300 × 9.81 = 12,753 N
> - Front axle: 0.60 × 12,753 = **7,652 N** (3,826 N per tire)
> - Rear axle: 0.40 × 12,753 = **5,101 N** (2,551 N per tire)
>
> This asymmetry affects braking, acceleration, and handling. We'll see why in later sessions."

##### Slide 13: Calculation Example
**Visual:** Step-by-step calculation
**Instructor Script:**
> "Let's calculate normal force for each tire:
>
> **Front tires** (assuming equal left/right):
> N_front = 7,652 N / 2 = **3,826 N per tire**
>
> **Rear tires**:
> N_rear = 5,101 N / 2 = **2,551 N per tire**
>
> Remember these numbers. They determine maximum friction force at each tire."

##### Slide 14: Load Transfer Teaser
**Visual:** Car braking with weight shift forward
**Instructor Script:**
> "Quick note: This is for static conditions. When the car accelerates, brakes, or turns, weight transfers between tires. We'll dive deep into that in Session 9 (Vehicle Dynamics).
>
> For today, assume static loading. But keep in mind: real-world normal forces change dynamically."

#### **Part 3: Friction Fundamentals** (Slides 15-18, ~15 minutes)

##### Slide 15: The Friction Equation
**Visual:** F_friction = μ × N (emphasized)
**Instructor Script:**
> "Here it is. The equation that governs every force a tire can generate:
>
> **F_friction = μ × N**
>
> Where:
> - F_friction = maximum friction force (N)
> - μ (mu) = coefficient of friction (dimensionless)
> - N = normal force (N)
>
> Simple, powerful, fundamental. Let me break down each part."

##### Slide 16: Coefficient of Friction (μ)
**Visual:** Table of μ values for different surfaces
**Instructor Script:**
> "The coefficient of friction depends on **two** surfaces in contact:
>
> **Typical Values:**
> - Dry asphalt + rubber: **μ ≈ 0.8 - 1.0**
> - Wet asphalt + rubber: **μ ≈ 0.5 - 0.7**
> - Ice + rubber: **μ ≈ 0.1 - 0.2**
> - Racing slicks on track: **μ ≈ 1.2 - 1.5** (hot)
>
> Higher μ = more grip = more force available.
>
> Note: These are simplified. Reality is complex (temperature, slip angle, etc.). But for our calculations, these work."

##### Slide 17: Maximum Tire Force Calculation
**Visual:** Worked example with Honda Civic
**Instructor Script:**
> "Let's calculate maximum force from one front tire on dry asphalt:
>
> Given:
> - N_front = 3,826 N
> - μ_dry = 0.9 (good tire, dry road)
>
> **F_max = μ × N**
> **F_max = 0.9 × 3,826**
> **F_max = 3,443 N**
>
> This is the absolute maximum force this tire can provide in **any direction** – braking, accelerating, or cornering.
>
> Exceed this, and the tire breaks traction (slides)."

##### Slide 18: All Four Tires Combined
**Visual:** Sum of all tire forces
**Instructor Script:**
> "Maximum total force from all four tires:
>
> **Front**: 2 × 3,443 N = 6,886 N
> **Rear** (using N_rear = 2,551 N): 2 × (0.9 × 2,551) = 4,592 N
>
> **Total available friction force = 11,478 N**
>
> Compare this to Session 1: At 100 km/h on flat road, we only needed ~465 N to maintain speed (rolling + drag). We have **massive margin**.
>
> But what about acceleration? Braking? Cornering at the limit? That's where it gets interesting."

---

#### **Part 4: Tire Slip & Force Generation** (Slides 19-20, ~6 minutes)

##### Slide 19: Slip - The Surprising Truth
**Visual:** Graph showing force vs slip percentage
**Instructor Script:**
> "Here's something that surprises most people: **Tires must slip slightly to generate force.**
>
> [Show graph of friction force vs slip %]
>
> - 0% slip = no force (tire rolling freely)
> - 5-15% slip = maximum force (optimal)
> - 100% slip = wheel locked or spinning (lower force, no control)
>
> This is why:
> - ABS prevents wheel lock (keeps slip in optimal range)
> - Traction control prevents wheelspin
> - Skilled drivers 'feel' the limit
>
> We'll use this in future sessions."

##### Slide 20: Longitudinal vs Lateral Slip
**Visual:** Diagram showing both slip types
**Instructor Script:**
> "Two types of slip:
>
> **Longitudinal slip**: Forward/backward (acceleration/braking)
> - Wheel rotating faster or slower than vehicle speed
>
> **Lateral slip (slip angle)**: Sideways (cornering)
> - Direction tire points vs direction it travels
>
> Both generate force. Both can't be maximized simultaneously. That leads us to the friction circle."

---

#### **Part 5: The Friction Circle** (Slides 21-22, ~5 minutes)

##### Slide 21: The Friction Circle Concept
**Visual:** Circle diagram with axes (longitudinal vs lateral force)
**Instructor Script:**
> "This is one of the most important concepts in vehicle dynamics. Let me show you why.
>
> [Show friction circle]:
> - **Radius** = maximum friction force (μN)
> - **X-axis** = longitudinal force (braking/accelerating)
> - **Y-axis** = lateral force (cornering)
>
> **Rule**: The resultant force vector must stay inside the circle.
>
> What does this mean?"

##### Slide 22: Combined Forces - The Trade-Off
**Visual:** Scenarios on friction circle
**Instructor Script:**
> "**Scenario 1**: Pure braking (straight line)
> - Use 100% of available friction for braking
> - Maximum deceleration
>
> **Scenario 2**: Pure cornering (constant speed)
> - Use 100% of available friction for turning
> - Maximum cornering force
>
> **Scenario 3**: Braking WHILE cornering
> - Must split available friction between both
> - If you brake too hard while turning, you exceed the circle → slide
>
> This is why:
> - Race drivers 'trail brake' (gradually release brakes in turn)
> - Emergency maneuvers are dangerous (hard brake + swerve = exceed limit)
> - ESC systems work (manage forces to stay in circle)
>
> Professional driving is about staying at the edge of this circle without exceeding it."

### **CLIMAX: Real-World Application** (Slides 23-24, ~8 minutes)

#### Slide 23: Complete Example - Emergency Braking While Turning
**Visual:** Scenario diagram with calculations
**Instructor Script:**
> "Let's apply everything. Emergency scenario:
>
> **Situation**: Front tire, dry road, turning + braking simultaneously
> - N = 3,826 N (we calculated this)
> - μ = 0.9
> - F_max = 3,443 N (circle radius)
> - Currently using 2,000 N for cornering (lateral)
>
> **Question**: How much braking force can you add without losing grip?
>
> **Solution**:
> Using Pythagorean theorem (forces are perpendicular):
> F_lateral² + F_braking² ≤ F_max²
> (2,000)² + F_braking² ≤ (3,443)²
> F_braking² ≤ 11,854,249 - 4,000,000
> F_braking ≤ **2,801 N**
>
> **Answer**: Maximum 2,801 N of braking force available. Beyond that, tire slides."

#### Slide 24: Factors Affecting Grip
**Visual:** List with visual icons
**Instructor Script:**
> "Before we finish, factors that change μ:
>
> **Tire-related**:
> - Tire pressure (under-inflated = less contact pressure = lower μ)
> - Tire temperature (cold tires = less grip)
> - Tread depth (worn = lower μ, especially wet)
> - Rubber compound (soft = more grip but wears faster)
>
> **Road-related**:
> - Surface type (asphalt > concrete > gravel)
> - Wetness (water film reduces contact)
> - Temperature (cold asphalt = lower μ)
> - Contaminants (oil, leaves, ice)
>
> Good engineers account for all of these."

>
> **Weight** (mg) →
> **Normal Force** (N = portion of weight) →
> **Friction** (F_max = μN) →

>
> This chain determines every performance limit:
> - How fast you can accelerate
> - How quickly you can stop
> - How fast you can corner
>
> Master this, and you understand vehicle limits."
