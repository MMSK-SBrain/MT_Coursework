### SLIDE 2: Crash Physics - Kinetic Energy & Deceleration
**Visual:** Diagram showing vehicle collision with energy equation and deceleration forces
**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"Let's start with physics. When you're driving at 50 km/h in a 1,500 kg vehicle, you have kinetic energy. Remember the equation from Session 1: KE = ½mv². At 50 km/h, that's about 145 kilojoules of energy - equivalent to dropping the car from a 10-meter building!

When you crash into a solid barrier, this energy must be dissipated - converted from kinetic energy to other forms, primarily deformation energy and heat. The vehicle structure crumples, absorbing energy. The question is: over what distance and time does this deceleration happen?

Here's the critical insight: the same change in velocity - stopping from 50 km/h to zero - can produce vastly different forces depending on the stopping distance. If you stop over 0.7 meters of crumple zone deformation, the deceleration might be 30g. If you hit a rigid wall and stop in just 5 cm, the deceleration could be 200g. At 200g, even for a fraction of a second, occupants suffer fatal injuries.

This is why crumple zones exist. By designing the front and rear of the vehicle to crush progressively over a longer distance, we reduce peak deceleration forces to survivable levels. Instead of 200g, we target 20-40g sustained deceleration, which humans can survive when properly restrained."

**PPT Content:**
```
CRASH PHYSICS: KINETIC ENERGY & DECELERATION

Kinetic Energy in Motion:
KE = ½ m v²

Example: 1,500 kg vehicle at 50 km/h (13.9 m/s)
KE = ½ × 1,500 × (13.9)² = 145,000 J = 145 kJ

This energy must be dissipated during a crash!

Deceleration Force:
F = m × a
Where: a = Δv / Δt (deceleration)

Also: v² = v₀² + 2as → a = v₀² / (2s)
Where:
• v₀ = Initial velocity
• s = Stopping distance
• a = Deceleration

Critical Relationship:
Longer stopping distance → Lower deceleration → Lower force

EXAMPLE 1: Crash with 0.7 m crumple zone
v₀ = 13.9 m/s, s = 0.7 m
a = (13.9)² / (2 × 0.7) = 138 m/s² ≈ 14g
Force on 1,500 kg vehicle: F = 1,500 × 138 = 207 kN

EXAMPLE 2: Crash into rigid wall (s = 0.05 m)
a = (13.9)² / (2 × 0.05) = 1,932 m/s² ≈ 197g
Force: F = 1,500 × 1,932 = 2,898 kN (FATAL!)

Human Tolerance:
• <20g sustained: Generally survivable (with restraints)
• 20-50g sustained: Injury likely, survivable
• 50-80g sustained: Severe injury, often fatal
• >100g sustained: Almost always fatal
• >200g even briefly: Fatal

Key Insight:
Crumple zones extend stopping distance → Reduce deceleration → Reduce forces → Save lives
```

**Teaching Note:**
Emphasize the ½mv² squared relationship - doubling speed quadruples energy! 100 km/h crash has 4× energy of 50 km/h crash.

**Outcomes Addressed:** SO-6-18-1 (crash safety principles)

### SLIDE 3: Crash Safety Strategy - Crumple Zones & Safety Cage
**Visual:** Side-view cutaway showing crumple zones (front/rear) and rigid safety cage (cabin)
**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"So how do we achieve this longer stopping distance? The answer is a two-part strategy that has defined automotive safety since the 1950s: crumple zones combined with a rigid safety cage.

The crumple zones are the front and rear sections of the vehicle - the areas ahead of the front wheels and behind the rear wheels. These structures are designed to deform progressively during a crash, absorbing energy through plastic deformation of metal. Think of them as controlled crush zones.

The key word is 'controlled.' We don't want random crumpling. Engineers design these zones with specific weak points and fold patterns so the structure collapses in a predictable way, maximizing energy absorption while maintaining a controlled deceleration rate.

In contrast, the passenger cabin - the area where you sit - is the safety cage. This is built like a strong cage using high-strength steel or ultra-high-strength steel, sometimes reinforced with boron steel pillars. The safety cage is designed NOT to deform. It must remain intact to preserve survival space for occupants.

So the philosophy is: sacrifice the vehicle structure outside the cabin to absorb energy, but protect the cabin integrity at all costs. The car is totaled, but you walk away.

You studied body construction in Session 16. Remember the materials discussion? This is why modern vehicles use a mix: high-strength steel in the safety cage for rigidity, and carefully engineered mild steel or aluminum in crumple zones for controlled deformation."

**PPT Content:**
```
CRASH SAFETY STRATEGY: CRUMPLE ZONES & SAFETY CAGE

Two-Part Design Philosophy:

1. CRUMPLE ZONES (Front & Rear)
   Location: Ahead of front wheels, behind rear wheels
   Purpose: Absorb crash energy through deformation
   Design: Progressive, controlled crushing
   Materials: Mild steel, aluminum, designed-to-crush structures

   Energy Absorption Mechanisms:
   • Plastic deformation (permanent bending/folding)
   • Buckling of structural members
   • Tearing and fracture (controlled)
   • Friction during deformation

   Typical crush distance: 0.5-0.8 m (frontal crash, 50 km/h barrier)
   Energy absorbed: ~100-150 kJ (most of kinetic energy)

2. SAFETY CAGE (Passenger Cabin)
   Location: Passenger compartment (A-pillar to C-pillar)
   Purpose: Maintain survival space, resist intrusion
   Design: Rigid, high-strength structure
   Materials: High-strength steel (HSS), ultra-high-strength steel (UHSS), boron steel

   Key Elements:
   • A-pillar, B-pillar, C-pillar: Vertical roof supports (very strong)
   • Roof rails: Longitudinal roof structure (rollover protection)
   • Floor pan: Strong floor structure
   • Door beams: Side impact protection bars
   • Firewall: Separates engine bay from cabin

   Requirement: Minimal deformation (<5 cm intrusion in severe crashes)

LOAD PATHS:
Crash forces transferred through designed load paths:
Front crash → Longitudinal rails → Cross-members → Safety cage
Forces routed AROUND cabin, not through it

Historical Context:
Invented by Béla Barényi at Mercedes-Benz (1959)
Before crumple zones: Rigid cars → Occupants absorb energy → Fatal
After crumple zones: Car absorbs energy → Occupants protected

Crash Energy Distribution (Typical 50 km/h frontal crash):
• Crumple zone deformation: ~70-80% of energy
• Seatbelt/occupant restraint: ~10-15% of energy
• Other (tires, suspension, debris): ~5-10% of energy
```

**Real-World Example:**

### SLIDE 4: Load Paths & Structural Reinforcements
**Visual:** Top-view diagram showing crash force load paths through vehicle structure
**Timing:** 2 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"Let me show you how forces flow through the vehicle structure. This concept is called load paths, and it's critical to crash safety.

In a frontal crash, the impact force starts at the bumper. But we don't want this force going straight back into the cabin where you're sitting. Instead, the structure channels forces along designed paths - typically through the front longitudinal rails, which are strong beams running along the length of the vehicle on each side.

These rails have strategically placed crumple sections that fold predictably. As they crush, they absorb energy. The rails connect to the firewall - the strong barrier between the engine compartment and cabin. The firewall redirects forces to the floor pan and the A-pillars, distributing the load.

For side impacts, the load path is different. Side door beams - strong steel tubes inside the door - catch the impact and transfer forces to the B-pillar, floor, and roof rail. This keeps the intrusion minimal.

In roof crush scenarios - like a rollover - the A, B, and C pillars act as the load path, supported by roof rails. These must be strong enough to support 3-4 times the vehicle's weight to maintain survival space.

The key is that all these load paths bypass the occupant seating area. Forces flow around you, not through you."

**PPT Content:**
```
LOAD PATHS & STRUCTURAL REINFORCEMENTS

FRONTAL CRASH Load Paths:
Bumper → Crash box (deformable) → Longitudinal rails (crush progressively)
→ Firewall (redirects forces) → A-pillars + Floor pan (distributes to safety cage)
→ B-pillars + Roof (final distribution)

Key Components:
• Crash box: Sacrificial energy absorber (bolted to rails, replaceable in minor crashes)
• Longitudinal rails: Main energy-absorbing members (controlled crush zones)
• Firewall: Strong barrier, prevents engine intrusion into cabin
• Cross-members: Connect left/right rails, distribute forces evenly

SIDE IMPACT Load Paths:
Door outer panel → Door beam (strong tube) → B-pillar
                                          → Floor sill
                                          → Roof rail
Forces distributed vertically (pillar) and horizontally (floor/roof)

Door Beam Construction:
• Material: Ultra-high-strength steel (UHSS) or boron steel
• Shape: Tubular or stamped beam
• Yield strength: >1,000 MPa
• Location: Inside door, at hip/torso height

REAR CRASH Load Paths:
Rear bumper → Rear rails → Rear cross-member → Floor pan → Seatbacks (must resist)

ROOF CRUSH (Rollover) Load Paths:
Roof → A/B/C pillars → Floor pan (pillars act as columns)
Requirement (FMVSS 216): Roof must withstand 3× vehicle weight

Structural Reinforcements:
• A-pillar: Extra reinforcement plates, boron steel (resists frontal & roof crush)
• B-pillar: Thickest pillar, often 3-4 layers of steel (side impact protection)
• Door sills: Strong lower structure (side impact, prevents floor intrusion)
• Rear seat cross-member: Prevents rear seat from collapsing forward

Material Selection by Location:
• Crumple zones: Mild steel, aluminum (designed to crush, absorb energy)
• Safety cage pillars: UHSS, boron steel (resist deformation, protect cabin)
• Floor pan: HSS (balance of strength and formability)
```

**Outcomes Addressed:** SO-6-18-1 (load paths in crash structures)

### SLIDE 5: Crash Types & Protection Strategies
**Visual:** Four scenarios - frontal, side, rear, rollover crashes with protection strategies
**Timing:** 2 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"Not all crashes are the same. Let me quickly outline the main crash types and how protection differs for each.

Frontal crashes are the most common and typically the most survivable because we have the most crumple zone distance. The front of the car can crush 50-70 cm before reaching the cabin. Protection strategy: long crumple zones, seatbelts, frontal airbags.

Side impacts are more dangerous because there's very little distance between the door and your body - maybe 20-30 cm. We don't have much crumple zone. Protection relies on strong door beams, B-pillar reinforcement, and side airbags to cushion the impact.

Rear crashes are similar to frontal but usually at lower speeds. The main concern is preventing fuel tank rupture and protecting rear passengers. Strategy: rear crumple zone and strong seatback structures.

Rollovers are complex. The roof must resist crushing to maintain survival space. Strong pillars and roof rails are critical. Seatbelts keep you in place - you don't want to be ejected. Side curtain airbags help protect against roof contact.

Each crash mode requires specific engineering solutions, which is why crash testing covers multiple scenarios."

**PPT Content:**
```
CRASH TYPES & PROTECTION STRATEGIES

1. FRONTAL CRASH
   Frequency: ~45% of fatal crashes
   Severity: Moderate (with good design)
   Crash distance: 0.5-0.8 m crumple zone available

   Protection Strategy:
   ✓ Long front crumple zones (maximize energy absorption)
   ✓ 3-point seatbelts (restrain occupants)
   ✓ Frontal airbags (driver & passenger)
   ✓ Knee airbags (prevent submarining)
   ✓ Steering column collapse (prevents chest impact)

   Peak deceleration: 20-40g (survivable with restraints)

2. SIDE IMPACT
   Frequency: ~25% of fatal crashes
   Severity: High (limited crumple distance)
   Crash distance: 0.2-0.3 m door/interior distance only!

   Protection Strategy:
   ✓ Strong door beams & B-pillar (resist intrusion)
   ✓ Side torso airbags (cushion impact)
   ✓ Side curtain airbags (head protection)
   ✓ Seatbelts (keep occupant away from door)
   ✓ Seat-mounted airbags (move with occupant)

   Peak intrusion: <15 cm (target, severe crash)

3. REAR IMPACT
   Frequency: ~20% of crashes (often low severity)
   Severity: Moderate
   Crash distance: 0.3-0.5 m rear crumple zone

   Protection Strategy:
   ✓ Rear crumple zone (energy absorption)
   ✓ Strong seatbacks (prevent rearward collapse)
   ✓ Head restraints (prevent whiplash)
   ✓ Fuel tank protection (prevent rupture/fire)

   Main injury: Whiplash (neck hyperextension)

4. ROLLOVER
   Frequency: ~5% of crashes, but high fatality rate
   Severity: Very high (especially if ejected)

   Protection Strategy:
   ✓ Strong roof structure (resist crush, 3× vehicle weight)
   ✓ A/B/C pillar reinforcement (maintain cabin)
   ✓ Seatbelts (prevent ejection - critical!)
   ✓ Side curtain airbags (protection during roll)
   ✓ Electronic Stability Control (prevention, not passive safety)

   Requirement: Roof withstands multiple rolls without collapse

Injury Mechanisms by Crash Type:
• Frontal: Chest, head, lower leg injuries (contact with steering wheel, dashboard)
• Side: Torso, pelvis, head injuries (door intrusion, B-pillar contact)
• Rear: Neck injuries (whiplash), head injuries (if head restraint inadequate)
• Rollover: Ejection (often fatal), roof crush injuries, multiple impacts

Multi-Event Crashes:
Many real-world crashes involve multiple impacts (e.g., frontal + rollover)
Protection systems must work in sequence
```

### SLIDE 7: The Seatbelt - Most Important Safety Device
**Visual:** Statistics comparison: crash survival rates with/without seatbelts
**Timing:** 2 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"Before we had airbags, traction control, or any electronic safety systems, we had seatbelts. And here's the remarkable fact: seatbelts alone reduce fatality risk by about 45% in frontal crashes and 50% in rollovers. No other single device comes close to this effectiveness.

Why are seatbelts so effective? Because they solve the fundamental problem in a crash: occupant restraint. Remember Newton's First Law - an object in motion stays in motion unless acted upon by a force. When your car crashes and decelerates from 50 km/h to zero in half a second, your body wants to keep moving at 50 km/h.

Without a seatbelt, you become a projectile inside the car. You'll hit the steering wheel, windshield, or dashboard at full impact speed. The deceleration happens in centimeters over hard surfaces - that's a 100g+ impact, often fatal.

With a seatbelt, your body is coupled to the car's deceleration. You decelerate at the same rate as the vehicle - 20-30g over the crumple zone distance. The seatbelt spreads this force over strong parts of your body - chest and pelvis - and extends the deceleration time. It's still violent, but survivable.

The modern 3-point seatbelt - lap belt plus diagonal shoulder belt - was invented by Volvo engineer Nils Bohlin in 1959. Volvo made the patent open to save lives. It's considered one of the most important safety innovations in history."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE SEATBELT: MOST IMPORTANT SAFETY DEVICE

Effectiveness Statistics:
• Frontal crash: 45% reduction in fatality risk
• Side crash: 45% reduction in fatality risk
• Rollover: 50% reduction in fatality risk (prevents ejection!)
• Overall: Saves ~15,000 lives per year in US alone

Problem Solved: Occupant Restraint

WITHOUT SEATBELT:
Crash scenario: 50 km/h → 0 in 0.5 seconds
Vehicle decelerates: 30g (with crumple zone)
Occupant: Continues at 50 km/h (Newton's 1st Law)
→ Strikes steering wheel/windshield at 50 km/h
→ Deceleration over ~5 cm contact: 100-200g
→ Fatal injuries

WITH SEATBELT:
Crash scenario: Same
Vehicle decelerates: 30g
Occupant: Restrained by seatbelt, decelerates with vehicle
→ Deceleration: ~30g (same as vehicle)
→ Force distributed over chest and pelvis (strong body parts)
→ Deceleration time: Same as vehicle (~0.5 s)
→ Survivable injuries or no injuries

Historical Development:
1885: First automotive seatbelts (2-point lap belt)
1959: 3-point seatbelt invented by Nils Bohlin (Volvo)
1968: US federal requirement for seatbelts in all new cars
1970s-1980s: Mandatory seatbelt laws enacted globally
1990s: Pretensioners and load limiters added

Why 3-Point Design?
• Lap belt alone: Risk of "submarining" (sliding under belt) and spinal injury
• Shoulder belt alone: No pelvic restraint, occupant rotates forward
• 3-point (lap + diagonal shoulder): Restrains pelvis AND torso, distributes force optimally

Force Distribution:
Chest belt: ~2/3 of restraint force (2-4 kN typical)
Lap belt: ~1/3 of restraint force (1-2 kN typical)
Total force: 3-6 kN (50 km/h crash, 75 kg occupant)
Pressure on body: Distributed over belt width (~50 mm) → 60-120 kPa (survivable)
```

**Real-World Note:**
"Even with all the advanced airbags in modern cars, the seatbelt does 70% of the restraint work. Airbags are supplemental - they need seatbelts to be effective."

### SLIDE 8: 3-Point Seatbelt Mechanism
**Visual:** Cutaway diagram of seatbelt retractor with locking mechanism
**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"Let's look at how a seatbelt actually works mechanically. The key component is the retractor - that spring-loaded spool inside the B-pillar that stores the belt webbing.

Under normal conditions, the retractor lets you pull the belt out smoothly and retracts it gently when you unbuckle. There's a constant slight tension from a spiral spring, which keeps the belt snug but comfortable.

But here's the clever part: the retractor has two locking mechanisms to stop the belt from extending during a crash.

The first is the webbing-sensitive lock, also called emergency locking. Inside the retractor is a weighted pendulum or inertia sensor. During sudden deceleration - like hard braking or a crash - this sensor swings forward and engages a pawl (a mechanical lock) that stops the spool from rotating. The belt can no longer extend. You're locked in place.

The second is vehicle-sensitive locking. A small ball or weighted mechanism inside the retractor responds to vehicle tilt or sudden motion. If the car tips beyond a certain angle - like in a rollover - or experiences sudden lateral acceleration - like in a side crash - this mechanism locks the belt. This prevents you from being thrown around during complex crash scenarios.

Some retractors also have belt-sensitive locking - if you pull the belt too quickly, it locks. That's why if you yank the seatbelt fast, it won't extend. This prevents the belt from pulling out too much if your body jerks forward slightly during normal braking.

So the seatbelt is smart - it allows free movement under normal conditions but locks instantly when it detects a crash or dangerous situation."

**PPT Content:**
```
3-POINT SEATBELT MECHANISM

Key Components:
1. Belt webbing (polyester, high tensile strength)
2. Retractor (spool with spring and locking mechanism)
3. Tongue (sliding buckle on belt)
4. Buckle (latching mechanism, fixed to seat/floor)
5. D-ring/shoulder anchor (upper attachment point on B-pillar)
6. Lower anchor (attachment to seat frame or floor)

Retractor Design:
Located in: B-pillar (lower section)
Contains:
• Spool: Stores belt webbing (~3-4 meters)
• Spiral spring: Retracts belt when unbuckled (~2-3 N tension)
• Locking mechanism: Prevents belt extension during crash

LOCKING MECHANISMS:

1. WEBBING-SENSITIVE LOCK (Emergency Locking Retractor - ELR)
   Sensor: Weighted pendulum or inertial mass inside retractor
   Trigger: Sudden belt pull (rapid deceleration)
   Threshold: ~0.3-0.4 g deceleration

   Operation:
   • Normal: Belt pulls out smoothly
   • Hard braking/crash: Occupant jerks forward → Belt pulls rapidly
   • Inertial mass swings → Engages pawl → Locks spool
   • Belt stops extending → Occupant restrained

   Response time: <50 milliseconds

2. VEHICLE-SENSITIVE LOCK
   Sensor: Tilt ball or weighted mechanism
   Trigger: Vehicle tilt beyond threshold (e.g., >20° from level)
            OR sudden vehicle deceleration

   Operation:
   • Level vehicle: Mechanism in neutral position
   • Rollover/severe side crash: Vehicle tilts
   • Ball rolls → Engages locking mechanism
   • Belt locks → Prevents occupant from being thrown

   Prevents: Ejection during rollover, being thrown across cabin in side impact

3. BELT-SENSITIVE LOCK (Some Systems)
   Trigger: Rapid belt pull (even without vehicle deceleration)
   Threshold: Pull speed >1-1.5 m/s

   Operation:
   • Centrifugal clutch or inertial sensor on spool
   • Rapid pull → High spool rotation speed → Clutch engages → Lock

   Purpose: Prevents belt from extending if occupant jerks forward during normal braking

Material & Strength:
Belt webbing: Polyester (high strength, UV resistant)
Width: 48-50 mm (distributes force over large area)
Tensile strength: >20 kN (can withstand forces from 75 kg occupant at 60g)
Stitching: Reinforced, designed to fail progressively if overloaded (energy absorption)

Force Limiting (Discussed later):
Modern belts include load limiters to prevent excessive chest compression
```

### SLIDE 9: Advanced Feature 1 - Pretensioners
**Visual:** Cross-section of pyrotechnic pretensioner with before/after deployment states
**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"Modern seatbelts have two advanced features that significantly improve protection. The first is the pretensioner.

Here's the problem pretensioners solve: even when buckled, there's always some slack in the seatbelt. Maybe a centimeter or two of webbing looseness, or the belt isn't pulled perfectly snug. In a crash, that slack means the occupant moves forward 5-10 cm before the belt engages fully. That's wasted crumple zone distance and allows the occupant to build up speed before being restrained.

The pretensioner eliminates this slack instantly when a crash is detected. Within the first 10-15 milliseconds of a crash - before the occupant has moved significantly - the pretensioner fires and yanks the belt tight, pulling the occupant back firmly into the seat.

How does it work? The most common type is pyrotechnic - a small explosive charge, similar to an airbag initiator. When the airbag control unit detects a crash, it sends an electrical signal to the pretensioner. The explosive detonates, driving a piston or pulling a cable that rotates the retractor spool backward, retracting the belt rapidly - typically 10-15 cm in under 10 milliseconds.

There are also mechanical pretensioners that use a pre-tensioned spring or a ball-and-tube mechanism, but pyrotechnic are most common because they're fast and powerful.

The result is that the occupant starts the crash sequence already firmly restrained, maximizing the effectiveness of both the belt and the airbag. This can reduce forward movement by 5-10 cm, which significantly reduces injury risk."

### SLIDE 10: Advanced Feature 2 - Load Limiters
**Visual:** Load limiter mechanism diagram with force-displacement curve
**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"The second advanced feature is the load limiter, and it seems to contradict what I just said about keeping you firmly restrained. Here's the paradox: we want the belt to hold you tightly, but not TOO tightly.

In a severe crash, the seatbelt can exert huge forces on your chest - potentially 6-8 kN or more. That's 600-800 kg of force concentrated across a 5 cm wide belt on your ribcage. This can cause rib fractures, internal organ damage, or in extreme cases, chest crush injuries.

The load limiter solves this by allowing controlled belt extension when forces exceed a safe threshold. Think of it like a safety valve. Once the belt tension exceeds about 4-5 kN - which is the maximum force a human chest can safely tolerate - the load limiter mechanism activates and lets the belt pay out slightly.

The most common design is a torsion bar - a metal rod inside the retractor that twists under high torque. When belt tension exceeds the threshold, the torsion bar begins to deform plastically, allowing the spool to rotate slightly. The belt extends maybe 5-10 cm, absorbing energy through the torsion bar's deformation.

This does allow the occupant to move forward a bit more than with a rigid belt, but it prevents chest injury. It's a carefully calibrated trade-off: we limit peak forces to safe levels while still keeping the occupant restrained enough to avoid hitting hard surfaces.

Advanced systems have adaptive load limiters that adjust the threshold based on crash severity, occupant size (detected by seat sensors), and belt usage. For example, a lighter occupant might get a lower threshold (3 kN) while a heavier occupant tolerates a higher threshold (5 kN)."

**PPT Content:**
```
ADVANCED FEATURE 2: LOAD LIMITERS

Problem:
High belt forces in severe crashes → Chest injury
Peak belt force without limiting: 6-10 kN (severe frontal crash)
Human tolerance: ~4-5 kN maximum (chest compression limit)
Risk: Rib fractures, sternum fracture, internal organ damage

Solution: Load Limiter
Allows controlled belt extension when force exceeds safe threshold
Limits peak force to ~4-5 kN (calibrated for safety)

• Torsion bar: Metal rod inside retractor, designed to twist under high load
• Connected to spool: Spool rotation → Torsion bar twists
• Calibrated yield point: Begins to deform at target force (e.g., 4 kN)

Operation:
1. Crash begins, belt restrains occupant
2. Belt tension rises as occupant loads belt (2 kN... 3 kN... 4 kN...)
3. At threshold (e.g., 4 kN): Torsion bar begins plastic deformation (permanent twist)
4. Belt pays out slightly as spool rotates (torsion bar twisting)
5. Force plateaus at threshold level (doesn't exceed 4-5 kN)
6. Occupant continues moving forward slowly, but force limited
7. Energy absorbed by torsion bar deformation

Performance:
Force threshold: 3-5 kN (varies by vehicle, occupant detection)
Extension distance: 5-15 cm (depending on crash severity)
Energy absorbed: 200-600 J (force × distance)

Force-Displacement Curve:

ALTERNATIVE DESIGNS:

• Reinforced stitching on belt webbing
• Stitches rip progressively at target force
• Belt extends as stitches fail
• Simple, low-cost, but less precise

Yielding Element:
• Deformable metal element in load path
• Bends/tears at threshold force
• Similar concept to torsion bar

Adjusts threshold based on:
• Crash severity (detected by ACU)
• Occupant weight (detected by seat sensor)
• Seat position (closer to dashboard → lower threshold)
• Belt usage (if unbuckled, no load limiting relevant)

Example:
Small occupant (50 kg): Threshold 3 kN
Average occupant (75 kg): Threshold 4 kN
Large occupant (100 kg): Threshold 5 kN

Effectiveness:
Reduces chest injury (AIS 3+) by ~20-30%
Trade-off: Slight increase in forward displacement (+5-10 cm)
Net benefit: Significant injury reduction (chest injuries more severe than head contact with airbag)

Integration with Pretensioners:
Sequence in crash:
1. Pretensioner fires (10 ms): Removes slack, pulls occupant back
2. Belt engages and loads (20-50 ms): Force rises
3. Load limiter activates (if force exceeds threshold): Limits peak force
4. Airbag deploys (30-50 ms): Cushions forward motion

System Working Together:
Pretensioner: Eliminates slack, optimizes initial restraint
Load limiter: Prevents excessive force, protects chest
Airbag: Cushions head, distributes contact forces
→ Complete occupant restraint system
```

### SLIDE 11: Seatbelt Effectiveness & Biomechanics
**Visual:** Biomechanical diagram showing belt forces distributed over torso and pelvis
**Timing:** 2 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"Let me summarize why seatbelts are so effective from a biomechanics perspective.

The human body can tolerate large forces if they're distributed over strong structures and applied over sufficient time. The seatbelt does both.

It distributes forces over the chest and pelvis - these are strong skeletal structures that can handle loads. The belt width (5 cm) spreads the force over a large area, reducing pressure. Compare this to hitting a steering wheel where the force is concentrated on a small area - that causes localized fractures and organ damage.

The belt also extends the deceleration time. By coupling you to the vehicle's crumple zone deceleration, you slow down over 0.5 seconds instead of the 0.01 seconds it would take to hit the dashboard. That 50× time increase reduces peak forces by a factor of 50.

With modern pretensioners and load limiters, we've optimized both timing and force levels. Pretensioners engage you early when forces are lower. Load limiters cap peak forces at safe levels. The result is that seatbelts can protect occupants in crashes up to 50-60 km/h with relatively minor injuries.

The statistics bear this out: in modern cars with advanced seatbelt systems, belted occupants have a >95% survival rate in frontal crashes up to 50 km/h."

**PPT Content:**
```
SEATBELT EFFECTIVENESS & BIOMECHANICS

Force Distribution:
3-point belt contacts:
• Shoulder/chest: Diagonal belt across clavicle, sternum, ribs (~60-70% of force)
• Pelvis/hips: Lap belt across iliac crest (pelvic bones) (~30-40% of force)

Both areas: Strong skeletal structures (can tolerate high loads)
Belt width: 48-50 mm (distributes force, reduces pressure)

Force: 4,000 N (with load limiter)
Belt width: 50 mm, contact length: 200 mm (chest), 150 mm (lap)
Chest pressure: 4,000 × 0.6 / (0.05 × 0.2) = 240 kPa (tolerable)

Compare: Point impact (steering wheel, 50 mm diameter)
Same force: 4,000 N / (π × 0.025²) = 2,037 kPa (10× higher! → Fractures)

Deceleration Time Extension:

With belt: Decelerate with vehicle over ~70 cm in ~0.5 s → 30g
→ 3× reduction in peak deceleration

Injury Risk by Speed (Modern 3-Point Belt with Pretensioner & Load Limiter):
• 30 km/h: Minor/no injury (>98% survival)
• 50 km/h: Moderate injury possible (95-97% survival)
• 70 km/h: Serious injury likely (80-85% survival)
• 90 km/h: Severe/fatal injury likely (50-60% survival)
• >100 km/h: Fatal injury very likely (<30% survival)

Survival rates assume: Frontal crash, modern vehicle, airbag deployment

Usage Statistics (Global):

Rear seat belt usage: ~60-75% (lower than front)
Unbelted occupants: 10× higher fatality risk

✗ Belt behind back: No restraint (fatal)

Special Populations:
• Pregnant: Lap belt BELOW abdomen, shoulder belt between breasts
• Children: Booster seat until belt fits properly (~12 years old)
• Obese: May need belt extender (proper fit critical)
```

### SLIDE 12: Seatbelt Reminder & Enforcement Systems
**Visual:** Seatbelt reminder light and buckle switch sensor diagram
**Timing:** 2 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"Despite overwhelming evidence of seatbelt effectiveness, about 10-15% of people still don't buckle up. That's why modern vehicles have seatbelt reminder systems.

The simplest version is a warning light on the dashboard and a chime that sounds if you start driving without buckling. This uses a buckle switch - a simple sensor in the belt buckle that detects when the tongue is inserted.

Advanced systems also have seat occupancy sensors - weight sensors in the seats that detect if someone is sitting there. This allows the system to remind passengers, not just the driver. Some systems escalate the warning - starting with a gentle chime, then a continuous beep, then even limiting vehicle speed.

In some markets, regulations require all seating positions to have belt reminder systems. Studies show these reminders increase belt usage by 5-10 percentage points, which translates to hundreds of lives saved annually.

There's also an integration with the airbag system: if the ACU detects an unbuckled occupant, it may adjust airbag deployment to compensate - though an airbag without a seatbelt is far less effective and can even cause injury."

### SLIDE 14: Airbag Fundamentals - Purpose & Physics
**Visual:** Crash sequence showing airbag deployment and occupant contact
**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"Now let's talk about airbags - the supplemental restraint system. The key word is 'supplemental' - airbags are designed to work WITH seatbelts, not replace them.

Here's what airbags do: they cushion the head and upper body contact that would otherwise occur with hard surfaces like the steering wheel, dashboard, or door panel. Even with a seatbelt, your head and upper torso move forward significantly in a crash. Without an airbag, your head would decelerate violently when it hits the steering wheel - maybe 100g or more over a few centimeters.

The airbag provides a cushion - a gas-filled bag that your head contacts instead. This contact happens over a larger area (the whole airbag surface) and the airbag deflates as you compress it, extending the deceleration time. The result is much lower peak forces - maybe 30-40g instead of 100g.

But here's the engineering challenge: the airbag must fully inflate BEFORE the occupant contacts it. In a 50 km/h crash, the occupant's head moves forward at about 14 m/s. It reaches the steering wheel in about 60-70 milliseconds. So the airbag must detect the crash, make a deployment decision, and fully inflate in less than 50 milliseconds!

This is one of the fastest mechanical systems in any vehicle. To achieve this, airbags use pyrotechnic gas generators - a chemical reaction that produces massive volumes of gas almost instantaneously. We'll look at the chemistry in detail in a moment."

**PPT Content:**
```
AIRBAG FUNDAMENTALS: PURPOSE & PHYSICS

Problem Solved: Head & Torso Contact with Hard Surfaces

Even with Seatbelt:
• Torso restrained, but head continues moving forward (neck flexes)
• In 50 km/h crash: Head moves forward ~30-40 cm before fully stopped
• Without airbag: Head impacts steering wheel/dashboard
  → Contact area: Small (~50-100 cm²)
  → Deceleration distance: 2-5 cm
  → Deceleration: 80-120g
  → Result: Severe head/facial injuries, often fatal

With Airbag:
• Head contacts inflated airbag (soft, gas-filled cushion)
  → Contact area: Large (~1,000-2,000 cm²)
  → Deceleration distance: 10-15 cm (airbag compresses and deflates)
  → Deceleration: 30-40g
  → Result: Minor injuries or no injury

Energy Absorption Mechanism:
Airbag is NOT a rigid pillow!
• Inflated with gas (~60-70 liters for driver airbag)
• Vents: Small holes allow controlled gas escape during compression
• As head compresses airbag: Gas vents out → Absorbs energy
• Progressive deflation: Extends deceleration time

Timing Challenge:
50 km/h crash:
• Occupant head velocity: ~14 m/s
• Distance to steering wheel: ~0.6 m (typical)
• Time to contact: 0.6 / 14 ≈ 0.043 s = 43 ms

Airbag deployment must complete BEFORE contact:
Required timeline:
• Crash detection: 0-5 ms

• Inflation: 10-40 ms

Frontal crashes: 20-30% additional fatality reduction (beyond seatbelt alone)
```
