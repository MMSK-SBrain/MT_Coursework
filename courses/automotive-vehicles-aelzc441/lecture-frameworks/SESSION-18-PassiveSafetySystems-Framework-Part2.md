### SLIDE 15: Frontal Airbag System - Driver & Passenger
**Visual:** Steering wheel and dashboard cutaway showing driver and passenger airbags
**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"Let's start with the most common airbags: frontal airbags for the driver and front passenger. These are mandatory in almost all markets since the 1990s.

The driver airbag is housed in the steering wheel center - you see it every time you drive, usually marked 'SRS' or 'AIRBAG'. It's a tightly folded fabric bag, typically about 60-70 liters when fully inflated, with a pyrotechnic inflator behind it.

The passenger airbag is much larger - 120-160 liters - because the passenger sits farther from the dashboard, so we need a bigger cushion to bridge that distance. It's hidden behind a tear seam in the dashboard, usually in front of the passenger seat.

When deployment is triggered, several things happen almost simultaneously. First, the inflator fires - we'll discuss the chemistry next. Gas rushes into the bag at extreme pressure. The bag bursts through its cover - the steering wheel hub splits open, or the dashboard tear seam rips apart. The bag inflates outward at speeds exceeding 100 km/h.

Within 30-40 milliseconds, the bag is fully inflated. It has small vent holes that are closed initially but open as the bag is compressed. When the occupant's head contacts the bag, these vents allow gas to escape in a controlled manner, cushioning the impact.

After the crash, the bag deflates completely within a few seconds. You want it to deflate quickly so it doesn't obstruct vision or trap the occupant if there's a fire or secondary impact.

Modern systems have dual-stage inflators - we can fire a smaller charge for low-severity crashes or both charges for high-severity crashes. This adaptive deployment reduces the risk of airbag-induced injuries in minor crashes."

**PPT Content:**
```
FRONTAL AIRBAG SYSTEM: DRIVER & PASSENGER

DRIVER AIRBAG:

Location: Steering wheel hub (center)
Size: 60-70 liters (fully inflated)
Shape: Circular/spherical
Distance to occupant: ~30-40 cm (typical)
Inflation time: 30-40 ms

Components:
• Airbag fabric: Nylon (heat resistant, flexible, high tensile strength)
• Inflator: Pyrotechnic gas generator (behind airbag)
• Cover: Steering wheel hub (tear seam, splits open during deployment)
• Vents: Small holes in bag (allow controlled deflation during contact)

Deployment Sequence:
1. ACU sends trigger signal (electrical)
2. Inflator initiator fires (pyrotechnic squib)
3. Gas generation begins (<5 ms)
4. Pressure breaks steering wheel cover seam
5. Bag inflates outward at ~200-300 km/h
6. Full inflation: 30-40 ms, 60-70 liters
7. Occupant contacts bag (50-70 ms)
8. Bag compresses, vents open, controlled deflation
9. Complete deflation: 2-3 seconds after deployment

PASSENGER AIRBAG:

Location: Dashboard (in front of passenger seat)
Size: 120-160 liters (fully inflated) - MUCH larger!
Shape: Elongated (bridges distance to passenger)
Distance to occupant: ~60-80 cm (farther than driver)
Inflation time: 35-45 ms (slightly slower, larger volume)

Why Larger?
• Greater distance to occupant requires larger cushion
• Passenger may not be in optimal position (no steering wheel reference)
• Must accommodate range of seating positions

Dashboard Integration:
• Hidden behind tear seam in dashboard top surface
• Invisible when not deployed (aesthetic requirement)
• Cover designed to split cleanly (prevent sharp fragments)

Occupant Position Detection:
• Some systems have passenger seat occupancy sensor
• If seat empty: Passenger airbag disabled (no deployment)
• If child seat detected: Airbag disabled (child safety)

DUAL-STAGE INFLATORS (Modern Systems):

Design:
• Two pyrotechnic charges (Stage 1: smaller, Stage 2: larger)
• Two initiator squibs (independently triggerable)

Deployment Strategies:
Low-severity crash (25-35 km/h):
→ Fire Stage 1 only → Partial inflation → Lower deployment force

High-severity crash (>40 km/h):
→ Fire Stage 1 + Stage 2 → Full inflation → Maximum protection

Sequential deployment:
→ Fire Stage 1 first, then Stage 2 (10-20 ms delay) → Softer initial inflation

Benefits:
• Reduces airbag injury risk in minor crashes
• Adaptive to crash severity
• Optimizes protection vs. injury trade-off

Airbag Injuries:
Despite protection, airbags can cause injuries:
• Facial abrasions (contact with deploying bag)
• Arm/hand fractures (hands on steering wheel during deployment)
• Hearing damage (loud deployment noise, ~160 dB)
• Burns (hot gas, though usually minor)

Risk factors:
• Out-of-position occupants (too close to airbag)
• Unbelted occupants (high-speed contact with bag)
• Children in front seat (should NEVER be in front with active airbag)

Mitigation:
• Dual-stage deployment reduces force in minor crashes
• Occupant position detection (some advanced systems)
• Proper belt use (keeps occupant at safe distance)

Regulations:
• US (FMVSS 208): Frontal airbags mandatory since 1998
• EU: Mandatory since early 2000s
• Most markets: Now standard equipment
```

"Never put a rear-facing child seat in the front passenger seat if the airbag is active. The airbag can deploy and strike the back of the child seat, causing severe injury to the infant."

### SLIDE 16: Airbag Chemistry - Pyrotechnic Gas Generation
**Visual:** Chemical reaction diagram showing sodium azide decomposition
**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"Let's look at the chemistry that makes this incredibly fast inflation possible. Remember, we need to generate 60-70 liters of gas in 30 milliseconds. That's insane speed!

The traditional airbag inflator uses sodium azide (NaN₃) as the primary propellant. When an electrical current heats the initiator squib, it ignites the sodium azide. The decomposition reaction is:

2 NaN₃ → 2 Na + 3 N₂

This produces sodium metal and nitrogen gas. The nitrogen is what inflates the bag - it's inert, non-toxic, and safe. But sodium metal is highly reactive and dangerous, so there's a secondary reaction with a metal oxide, typically potassium nitrate and silicon dioxide, to convert the sodium into harmless sodium silicate (essentially glass).

The complete reaction generates nitrogen gas at extremely high temperature - around 500-800°C - and pressure. About 50-100 grams of sodium azide can generate 60-70 liters of nitrogen in milliseconds.

Newer inflators use alternative chemistries because sodium azide is toxic and expensive. Modern systems often use guanidine nitrate or other nitrogen-rich compounds. Some use hybrid inflators - a combination of pyrotechnic charge and stored compressed gas (argon or nitrogen). The pyrotechnic charge heats the compressed gas, causing rapid expansion. This is cleaner and cooler but more complex.

The key point is that airbag inflation is a controlled explosion - a very precisely engineered chemical reaction that produces the exact volume of gas needed at the exact rate needed to inflate the bag safely and effectively."

**PPT Content:**
```
AIRBAG CHEMISTRY: PYROTECHNIC GAS GENERATION

SODIUM AZIDE INFLATOR (Traditional, Still Common):

Chemical Propellant: Sodium Azide (NaN₃)
Amount: ~50-100 grams (driver airbag)

Primary Reaction (Decomposition):
2 NaN₃ → 2 Na + 3 N₂

Triggered by: Electrical current heating initiator squib (~1 A, ~2 A)
Initiator: Small pyrotechnic charge (like a firework igniter)
Ignition: Squib heats to ~300°C → Ignites sodium azide
Decomposition: Exothermic (releases heat), extremely fast

Products:
• Nitrogen gas (N₂): 3 moles per 2 moles NaN₃ → ~67% of product gas
• Sodium metal (Na): 2 moles per 2 moles NaN₃ → Solid, reactive

Problem: Sodium metal is highly reactive (dangerous!)

Secondary Reaction (Neutralization):
10 Na + 2 KNO₃ → K₂O + 5 Na₂O + N₂
Na₂O + SiO₂ → Na₂SiO₃ (sodium silicate, stable glass-like solid)

Additives:
• Potassium nitrate (KNO₃): Oxidizer, reacts with sodium
• Silicon dioxide (SiO₂): Converts sodium oxide to silicate
• Result: All sodium converted to harmless sodium silicate slag

Gas Production:
50 grams NaN₃:
• Moles: 50 / 65 = 0.77 mol NaN₃
• N₂ produced: 0.77 × (3/2) = 1.15 mol N₂
• Volume at STP: 1.15 × 22.4 = 25.8 L
• Volume at high temp (500°C): ~25.8 × (773/273) = ~73 L ✓

Inflation Performance:
Temperature: 500-800°C (hot gas!)
Pressure: 200-300 kPa (during inflation, then vents)
Gas volume: 60-70 L (driver), 120-160 L (passenger)
Inflation time: 30-40 ms
Gas velocity: Exits inflator at ~200-300 km/h

ALTERNATIVE CHEMISTRIES (Modern Inflators):

Guanidine Nitrate Inflator:
Formula: CH₆N₄O₃ (guanidine nitrate)
Reaction: Produces N₂, H₂O, CO₂
Benefits: Non-toxic reactants, cleaner combustion
Drawback: Produces some moisture (not ideal)

Tetrazole-Based Inflator:
Nitrogen-rich organic compound
Similar gas production to sodium azide
Less toxic, more stable

HYBRID INFLATOR (Compressed Gas + Pyrotechnic):

Design:
• Compressed inert gas (argon or nitrogen) stored at high pressure (~300-400 bar)
• Small pyrotechnic charge (heater)

Operation:
1. Pyrotechnic heater fires (small explosive)
2. Rapid heat release (~2000°C briefly)
3. Compressed gas absorbs heat → Expands rapidly (PV=nRT)
4. Hot, high-pressure gas inflates airbag

Benefits:
• Cooler gas (less heating of bag, less burn risk)
• Cleaner (no combustion products in bag)
• Precise control (adjust stored gas amount)

Drawbacks:
• More complex (pressure vessel required)
• Heavier (gas storage cylinder)
• More expensive

COLD GAS INFLATOR (Future/Advanced):

Design:
• Compressed inert gas only (no pyrotechnic)
• Rapid valve release mechanism

Operation:
• Electrical or mechanical trigger opens valve
• Compressed gas (~400 bar) rushes into bag
• No heat, no combustion

Benefits:
• Safest (no explosives, no heat)
• Reusable (refillable gas cylinder)
• Environmentally friendly

Drawbacks:
• Very heavy (high-pressure cylinder)
• Complex valve mechanism
• Expensive
• Not yet mainstream

Safety Considerations:
• Deployed airbag is HOT (fabric can be 100-200°C briefly)
• White powder residue: Talc or cornstarch (keeps bag folded, harmless)
• Smell: Burnt chemicals (normal, not dangerous)
• Never disassemble an undeployed airbag (explosive hazard!)
```

**Teaching Note:**
"The white powder you see after airbag deployment isn't dangerous - it's talcum powder or cornstarch used to keep the folded bag from sticking to itself during storage."

### SLIDE 17: Side Impact Airbags - Torso & Pelvis Protection
**Visual:** Side-impact crash with side airbag deployment from seat
**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"Frontal airbags were just the beginning. Side impacts are particularly dangerous because there's very little structure between the door and your body - maybe 20-30 cm. So in the 1990s, automakers developed side airbags.

Side torso airbags are typically mounted in the outboard side of the front seat - the side closest to the door. When a side impact is detected, these bags deploy outward from the seat, creating a cushion between your torso and the door/B-pillar.

They're much smaller than frontal airbags - typically 10-15 liters - because the distance to cover is small. But they must deploy even faster - full inflation in 10-15 milliseconds - because there's so little time before door intrusion reaches the occupant.

The deployment strategy is different too. Frontal crashes provide some warning - the front crumple zone crushes first. But in a side impact, the door can be hit directly with almost no warning. So side airbag systems must be extremely sensitive and react to the first signs of lateral deceleration.

Advanced systems have thorax airbags and pelvis airbags as separate units. The thorax bag protects the ribcage, while the pelvis bag protects the hip and pelvic bones. By separating them, each can be optimally sized and positioned.

One challenge with side airbags is that they deploy between you and the door - the area where you might put your arm or lean against. So the system must be designed to minimize injury to occupants who are out of position, like resting against the door."

**PPT Content:**
```
SIDE IMPACT AIRBAGS: TORSO & PELVIS PROTECTION

Motivation:
Side crashes: ~25% of fatal crashes
Limited crumple zone distance: 20-30 cm (door to occupant)
High injury risk: Torso, pelvis, ribs, internal organs

SIDE TORSO AIRBAG:

Location: Outboard side of front seat (in seatback or cushion)
Size: 10-15 liters (smaller than frontal airbags)
Deployment direction: Laterally (out from seat toward door)
Inflation time: 10-15 ms (VERY fast! Faster than frontal)
Coverage area: Torso (ribcage, abdomen)

Deployment Trigger:
• Side impact crash detection (lateral acceleration sensors)
• Threshold: ~5-8g lateral deceleration
• Sensor location: B-pillar, door, sill, floor (side-mounted accelerometers)
• Deployment decision: 5-10 ms (very aggressive timing)

Seat-Mounted Design:
Benefits:
• Moves with seat position (always correct distance to occupant)
• Covers door intrusion zone regardless of seat adjustment
• Independent of door structure (door may crush, bag still works)

Challenges:
• Electrical connection through seat (wiring to seat)
• Must survive seat movement and adjustments
• Replacement cost (entire seat assembly may need replacement after deployment)

PELVIS AIRBAG (Advanced Systems):

Location: Lower seat cushion (outboard side)
Size: 5-8 liters (even smaller)
Coverage: Pelvis, hip bones
Purpose: Protect pelvic structure (fractures are severe, long-term disability)

THORAX + PELVIS COMBINATION (Two-Chamber Airbag):

Design:
• Single airbag with two chambers (upper and lower)
• Inflator feeds both chambers (may have separate vents)
• Upper chamber: Thorax protection (chest, ribs)
• Lower chamber: Pelvis protection (hips)

Benefits:
• Comprehensive side protection
• Optimal pressure distribution (different chamber sizes)
• Single deployment system (lower cost vs. separate bags)

Inflation Characteristics:
Pressure: Lower than frontal airbags (~50-100 kPa vs. 200-300 kPa)
Reason: Smaller volume, less compression needed
Venting: Minimal vents (bag stays inflated longer to resist door intrusion)

Side Airbag Challenges:

1. SPEED:
   • Side impact: Very short time to deployment (<15 ms required)
   • Lateral deceleration rises very quickly (no crumple zone dampening)
   • Must have extremely responsive sensors and fast inflators

2. OUT-OF-POSITION OCCUPANTS:
   • Occupant leaning against door (arm on windowsill, head on pillow)
   • Airbag deploys INTO occupant (potential injury)
   • Mitigation: Lower deployment force, pressure sensors (some systems)

3. SEAT INTEGRATION:
   • Electrical connection to moving seat (wear, reliability)
   • Aesthetic (visible seam in seat)
   • Cost (seat becomes part of safety system, expensive replacement)

Effectiveness:
Side torso airbags: 25-30% reduction in side impact fatalities (thorax injuries)
Pelvis airbags: 15-20% reduction in pelvis fractures
Combined with strong side structure: ~40% overall side impact fatality reduction

Deployment Conditions:
• Typically deploy only on impact side (left impact → left airbag only)
• Threshold: Moderate to severe side impacts (~25 km/h+ impact speed)
• Do NOT deploy in frontal or rear crashes (side sensors don't trigger)
• May deploy in rollovers (vehicle tilt detected by side sensors)
```

"Side airbags deploy so fast that in crash test videos, you often can't even see the deployment process - the bag goes from folded to fully inflated in a single frame."

### SLIDE 18: Curtain Airbags - Head Protection
**Visual:** Curtain airbag deployed along roof rail, covering side windows
**Timing:** 2 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"In addition to torso airbags, most modern vehicles have side curtain airbags - also called inflatable curtains or head airbags.

These are long, narrow airbags stored in the roof rail - the structure above the side windows. When a side impact or rollover is detected, the curtain deploys downward like a curtain, covering the side windows and providing a cushion between the occupant's head and the window/B-pillar.

Curtain airbags typically extend the full length of the vehicle - from the A-pillar to the C-pillar - protecting both front and rear seat occupants. They're about 50-80 liters in volume, shaped like a long tube or rectangular panel.

What's clever about curtain airbags is that they stay inflated for several seconds - much longer than frontal or side torso airbags. Why? Because in a rollover, the vehicle may roll multiple times, and you need continuous head protection throughout the sequence. Typical inflation duration is 5-10 seconds.

To achieve this, curtain airbags have minimal venting. They're designed to hold pressure, acting almost like an inflatable pillow that stays firm. The gas used may also include argon, which leaks through the fabric more slowly than nitrogen.

Curtain airbags are highly effective at reducing head injuries in side impacts and are critical for rollover protection. They also help keep the occupant inside the vehicle during a rollover - ejection is often fatal, so preventing it dramatically improves survival."

### SLIDE 19: Knee Airbags & Other Supplemental Airbags
**Visual:** Dashboard knee airbag deployment and other supplemental airbag locations
**Timing:** 2 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"Beyond the main airbags, modern vehicles have several supplemental airbag types for specific protection needs.

Knee airbags deploy from below the steering column or dashboard to cushion the driver's knees and lower legs. Why is this important? In a frontal crash, there's a risk of 'submarining' - the occupant sliding forward under the lap belt, which can cause severe pelvic and abdominal injuries. The knee airbag creates a barrier that prevents this forward-downward motion, keeping the occupant properly positioned relative to the seatbelt and frontal airbag.

Some vehicles also have rear seat airbags - either built into the front seatbacks or deploying from the rear cabin roof. These protect rear passengers, especially in markets where rear seat belts aren't consistently used.

Center airbags - a newer innovation - deploy between the driver and front passenger to prevent them from colliding with each other during side impacts. In a far-side impact (impact on the opposite side from the occupant), the occupant is thrown toward the center of the vehicle and could collide with the other front occupant.

High-end vehicles may even have seatbelt airbags - inflatable sections built into the seatbelt webbing that inflate during a crash to better distribute belt forces over the chest, reducing pressure.

The trend is toward more airbags in more locations, with increasingly sophisticated deployment strategies. Premium vehicles may have 10-12 airbags or more."

### SLIDE 20: Airbag Control Unit (ACU) - The Safety Brain
**Visual:** ACU block diagram showing inputs, processing, and outputs
**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"We've talked about all these airbags and seatbelt systems, but how does the vehicle know when to deploy them? That's the job of the Airbag Control Unit, or ACU - sometimes called the SRS (Supplemental Restraint System) ECU.

The ACU is a dedicated safety computer, and it's one of the most critical ECUs in the vehicle. It continuously monitors crash sensors throughout the vehicle - accelerometers that measure deceleration and lateral acceleration. It processes this sensor data in real-time and makes deployment decisions within 10 milliseconds of a crash starting.

The ACU runs sophisticated algorithms - crash detection algorithms - that distinguish between a real crash requiring airbag deployment and normal driving events like hitting a pothole or hard braking. It must be extremely sensitive to catch crashes quickly, but also selective to avoid false deployments, which are expensive and dangerous.

When the ACU determines a deployment is necessary, it sends high-current electrical pulses to the appropriate airbag initiators and seatbelt pretensioners. It selects which airbags to fire based on the crash type - frontal sensors trigger frontal airbags, side sensors trigger side and curtain airbags.

For dual-stage airbags, the ACU decides whether to fire one stage or both based on crash severity and occupant factors like belt usage and seat position.

The ACU also has a backup power supply - a capacitor that stores enough energy to deploy airbags even if the main battery is disconnected in the crash. And it stores crash data in non-volatile memory for post-crash analysis by investigators."

```

• SDM (Sensing and Diagnostic Module)

• Monitor crash sensors continuously
• Detect crash events in real-time

   • Airbag circuit resistance (continuity check for each airbag)
   • Pretensioner circuit resistance

Sampling Rate: 1,000-10,000 Hz (1-10 kHz) - very fast!
Every 0.1-1 ms, ACU reads all sensors and processes

Crash Detection Algorithm:
1. Read accelerometer values
2. Calculate velocity change (integrate acceleration):
   Δv(t) = ∫ a(t) dt

3. Apply threshold checks:
   • Is deceleration > threshold? (e.g., >8g frontal, >5g side)
   • Is Δv > threshold? (e.g., Δv > 15 km/h for deployment)
   • Duration check (sustained deceleration, not brief spike)

4. Pattern recognition:
   • Frontal crash: High X-axis deceleration, low Y-axis
   • Side crash: High Y-axis deceleration (left or right)
   • Rollover: Z-axis changes, sustained lateral acceleration

   • Low severity (Δv = 15-25 km/h): Stage 1 airbag only (if dual-stage)

7. Occupant adaptation:
   • If unbuckled: May suppress or reduce deployment
   • If seat empty (passenger): Suppress passenger airbag
   • If seat too close: May reduce deployment force (some systems)

Decision Time: 5-10 ms from crash start to deployment command

• Pre-crash: Speed, throttle, brake, belt status (last 5 seconds)

• Post-crash: Multiple events (if vehicle crashes again)

Uses:
• Accident investigation (determine crash severity, occupant behavior)
• Manufacturer analysis (improve airbag algorithms)
• Legal evidence (insurance claims, litigation)

Privacy Note: EDR data is vehicle property, but laws vary on access rights.

• Checks electrical resistance of each airbag circuit (every few seconds)
• Detects: Open circuit (broken wire), short circuit, incorrect resistance
• If fault detected → SRS warning light illuminates on dashboard
• Fault code stored for service diagnosis

Common faults:
• Clock spring failure (rotating electrical connector in steering wheel)
• Seat sensor malfunction
• Low battery voltage
• Airbag circuit open (connector loose)

Regulations:
• FMVSS 208 (US): Requires ACU, redundant sensors, crash data recording
• UN R94 (EU): Frontal crash detection requirements

### SLIDE 21: Crash Sensors - Accelerometers
**Visual:** MEMS accelerometer cross-section and sensor placement diagram
**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"The crash sensors that feed the ACU are accelerometers - devices that measure acceleration (or deceleration, which is negative acceleration).

Modern automotive accelerometers are MEMS devices - Micro-Electro-Mechanical Systems. These are tiny silicon chips with microscopic mechanical structures etched into them. Inside the chip, there's a tiny proof mass - essentially a microscopic weight suspended by flexible beams. When the vehicle decelerates suddenly, this proof mass moves relative to the sensor housing due to inertia.

The movement is detected capacitively or piezoelectrically and converted to an electrical signal - a voltage proportional to the acceleration. These sensors can measure accelerations from near-zero up to 100g or more with very fast response times - microseconds.

Vehicles have multiple accelerometer sensors positioned strategically. Frontal crash sensors are in the front bumper area or radiator support structure - these are the 'early warning' sensors that detect a crash first. Side crash sensors are in the doors, B-pillars, and floor sills. And there's almost always a central accelerometer inside the ACU itself.

The central sensor serves as a safing sensor - to deploy airbags, the ACU typically requires both a peripheral sensor (front or side) AND the central sensor to detect a crash. This redundancy prevents false deployments from isolated sensor malfunctions or localized impacts like hitting a shopping cart.

Some advanced vehicles also have pressure sensors in the doors that detect the rapid pressure increase when a door is struck. These can provide even earlier warning than accelerometers in side impacts."

**PPT Content:**
```
CRASH SENSORS: ACCELEROMETERS

Purpose: Measure vehicle deceleration (acceleration) during crashes
Output: Voltage signal proportional to acceleration

MEMS ACCELEROMETER (Modern Standard):

MEMS = Micro-Electro-Mechanical Systems
Construction:
• Silicon chip with microscopic mechanical structure
• Proof mass: Tiny mass (micrograms) suspended by flexible beams
• Capacitive plates: Detect proof mass displacement
• Size: ~5mm × 5mm chip (incredibly small!)

Operating Principle:
1. Vehicle decelerates suddenly in crash
2. Proof mass resists deceleration (inertia: F = ma)
3. Proof mass moves relative to sensor housing
4. Movement changes capacitance between proof mass and fixed plates
5. Capacitance change → Voltage change (measured by electronics)
6. Voltage proportional to acceleration: V = k × a

Performance:
• Measurement range: ±100g (some sensors ±250g for severe crashes)
• Accuracy: ±2-5% of full scale
• Response time: <1 ms (some <100 μs - extremely fast!)
• Sampling rate: 1-10 kHz (matches ACU processing rate)
• Axes: Single-axis, dual-axis, or tri-axial (X, Y, Z)

Advantages:
• Very small, lightweight
• Low cost (mass production)
• High reliability (no moving parts at macro scale)
• Low power consumption (~5-10 mA)

• Measures: X-axis deceleration
• Must agree with peripheral sensors for deployment

→ Prevents false deployment from single sensor fault

Location 1: Door-mounted (inside door, near latch)
• Purpose: Detect door intrusion
• Measures: Y-axis acceleration (lateral)
• Challenges: Must survive door crush

Location 2: B-pillar mounted (inside pillar structure)
• Purpose: Detect side impact at pillar
• Measures: Y-axis acceleration

Location 3: Floor sill / rocker panel (under door)
• Purpose: Detect under-vehicle side impacts (SUV-to-car, pole)
• Measures: Y-axis acceleration

Left side crash: Left sensors + Central → Deploy left side & curtain airbags
Right side crash: Right sensors + Central → Deploy right side & curtain airbags

ROLLOVER SENSORS:

Type: Roll rate sensor + Z-axis accelerometer
• Roll rate sensor: Gyroscope (measures angular velocity, degrees/second)
• Z-axis accelerometer: Detects vertical acceleration changes
• Combined: Determine vehicle roll angle and rate

Threshold:
• Roll angle > 45-60° (vehicle tipping)
• Roll rate > 100-150°/s (fast rolling motion)
→ Deploy curtain airbags (both sides, extended inflation)

PRESSURE SENSORS (Advanced Systems):

Location: Inside door cavity
Type: Barometric pressure sensor

Operating Principle:
• Door cavity is sealed
• Side impact: Door intrudes → Compresses air in cavity → Pressure spike
• Pressure sensor detects rapid pressure increase
• Trigger: Δp > 20-50 kPa in <10 ms

Advantage over accelerometer:
• Earlier detection (pressure wave travels at speed of sound in air)
• Responds to door deformation (even if door not accelerating yet)
• Gain ~5-10 ms earlier deployment

SENSOR POWER & COMMUNICATION:

Power: 5V or 12V from ACU (via wiring harness)
Signal: Analog voltage (0-5V) or digital (some use CAN or LIN bus)
Wiring: Dedicated wiring to ACU (critical safety circuit)
Redundancy: Dual wiring paths (some systems)

SENSOR FAULTS & DIAGNOSTICS:

ACU monitors sensor health:
• Open circuit: Sensor disconnected or wire broken
• Short circuit: Wire shorted to ground or power
• Out-of-range: Sensor reading impossible value (e.g., 150g at rest)
• Implausible: Sensor reading conflicts with other sensors

If fault detected:
→ SRS warning light illuminates
→ Fault code stored
→ That sensor may be ignored (system relies on other sensors)

SENSOR CALIBRATION:

Accelerometers calibrated at factory (zero-g offset, sensitivity)
Field calibration: Not typically required
Some systems: Self-calibration at startup (detect gravity vector → Determine orientation)

Gravity Compensation:
Accelerometers sense gravity (~1g downward) when vehicle at rest
ACU software subtracts gravity vector to determine crash acceleration
Example: Vehicle on flat ground, sensor reads ~1g down (gravity)
Crash starts, sensor reads 1g down + 20g back = Combined reading
ACU extracts crash component (20g back)
```

### SLIDE 22: Deployment Decision Algorithm
**Visual:** Flowchart showing crash detection logic from sensor input to deployment
**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"Let me walk you through how the ACU actually decides to deploy airbags, because it's more sophisticated than just 'if acceleration exceeds threshold, deploy.'

First, the ACU is continuously sampling accelerometers at very high speed - typically 1,000 to 10,000 times per second. It's looking for sudden changes in acceleration that indicate a crash.

When deceleration exceeds a preliminary threshold - say, 8g - the ACU enters 'crash detection mode.' It starts integrating the acceleration over time to calculate velocity change, or delta-v. Delta-v is actually a better indicator of crash severity than peak acceleration because it represents the total momentum change.

The ACU compares the acceleration pattern and delta-v against stored crash signatures - templates of what different crash types look like. A frontal barrier crash has a different signature than a pole crash or an oblique crash. This pattern matching helps distinguish real crashes from non-crash events.

The safing logic requires multiple sensors to agree. For frontal deployment, you need a front peripheral sensor AND the central sensor to both detect a crash. This prevents deployment from a single sensor fault or a localized low-speed impact.

Once the algorithm confirms a deployment-worthy crash, it selects which airbags to fire. Frontal crashes trigger frontal airbags and pretensioners. Side crashes trigger the appropriate side and curtain airbags.

For dual-stage systems, the ACU also decides deployment force. If it's a moderate crash - delta-v around 20-25 km/h - maybe fire only Stage 1. If it's severe - delta-v over 35 km/h - fire both stages for maximum protection.

All of this - from initial sensor trigger to deployment command - happens in 5 to 10 milliseconds. It's algorithmic decision-making at machine speed."

**PPT Content:**
```
DEPLOYMENT DECISION ALGORITHM

ACU PROCESSING LOOP (Continuous, 1-10 kHz):

STEP 1: READ SENSORS
• Read all accelerometers (frontal, side, central, roll rate)
• Read occupant sensors (belt status, seat occupancy, seat position)
• Read vehicle status (ignition, speed from CAN bus)

STEP 2: CRASH DETECTION
Check: Is any accelerometer reading > preliminary threshold?
• Frontal sensors: >5-8g (X-axis)
• Side sensors: >4-6g (Y-axis)
• Rollover: Roll rate >80°/s or tilt >30°

If YES → Enter crash analysis mode
If NO → Continue monitoring (loop)

STEP 3: CALCULATE DELTA-V (Velocity Change)
Integrate acceleration over time:
Δv(t) = ∫ a(t) dt

Example:
• Time 0 ms: a = 0g, Δv = 0 km/h
• Time 5 ms: a = 20g avg, Δv ≈ 3.5 km/h
• Time 10 ms: a = 25g avg, Δv ≈ 8 km/h
• Time 20 ms: a = 22g avg, Δv ≈ 17 km/h ← Deployment threshold!
• Time 30 ms: a = 18g avg, Δv ≈ 23 km/h

STEP 4: CRASH SEVERITY ASSESSMENT
Compare Δv to deployment thresholds:

No deployment: Δv < 12-15 km/h (minor impact, airbags not needed)
Low deployment: Δv = 15-25 km/h (Stage 1 only, if dual-stage)
Full deployment: Δv > 25-30 km/h (Stage 1 + 2, full protection)

STEP 5: PATTERN RECOGNITION (Crash Type)
Analyze sensor activation pattern:

FRONTAL CRASH:
• Front sensors: High X-axis deceleration
• Side sensors: Low Y-axis deceleration
• Pattern: Symmetric (left and right front sensors similar)
→ Classification: Frontal crash

SIDE CRASH (Left):
• Left side sensors: High Y-axis deceleration
• Right side sensors: Low Y-axis deceleration
• Front sensors: Moderate X-axis (may have some forward component)
→ Classification: Left side impact

POLE CRASH (Narrow Object):
• One front sensor: Very high X-axis deceleration
• Other front sensor: Lower X-axis deceleration
• Pattern: Asymmetric (localized impact)
→ Classification: Offset/pole crash

ROLLOVER:
• Roll rate sensor: High angular velocity (>100°/s)
• Z-axis accelerometer: Vertical acceleration changes
• X/Y sensors: Sustained lateral acceleration
→ Classification: Rollover

STEP 6: SAFING LOGIC (Redundancy Check)
Require multiple sensors to confirm:

Frontal deployment:
IF (Left_front_sensor > threshold OR Right_front_sensor > threshold)
   AND Central_sensor > threshold
   AND Δv > deployment_threshold
→ THEN Deploy frontal airbags

Side deployment:
IF (Left_side_sensor > threshold)
   AND (Central_sensor > lateral_threshold OR B_pillar_sensor > threshold)
   AND Δv_lateral > deployment_threshold
→ THEN Deploy left side & curtain airbags

Purpose of safing:
• Prevent single sensor fault from causing deployment
• Require central ACU sensor to independently confirm crash
• Reduce false deployment risk

STEP 7: OCCUPANT ADAPTATION
Adjust deployment based on occupant status:

Seatbelt unbuckled (driver):
→ May delay or reduce frontal airbag deployment (occupant farther forward)
→ Still deploy, but adapted

Passenger seat unoccupied (weight sensor <25 kg):
→ Suppress passenger frontal airbag (no deployment)
→ Save cost, prevent unnecessary deployment

Passenger seat: Child seat detected (special pattern):
→ Suppress passenger airbag (child safety)

Seat too close (<25 cm from airbag):
→ May reduce deployment force (less distance = less needed)

STEP 8: AIRBAG SELECTION
Based on crash type, select which airbags to deploy:

Frontal crash (>30 km/h):
→ Driver frontal, Passenger frontal (if occupied)
→ Driver pretensioner, Passenger pretensioner
→ Driver knee, Passenger knee (if equipped)

Side crash left (>25 km/h):
→ Driver side torso, Left curtain
→ Driver pretensioner (pulls occupant away from door)

Side crash right (>25 km/h):
→ Passenger side torso, Right curtain
→ Passenger pretensioner

Rollover:
→ Left curtain, Right curtain (both sides, extended inflation)
→ All pretensioners (keep occupants in seats)

Severe multi-axis crash:
→ May deploy all airbags (frontal + side + curtain + knee)

STEP 9: DUAL-STAGE CONTROL (If Equipped)
Determine deployment force:

Low severity (Δv = 15-25 km/h):
→ Fire Stage 1 only (lower gas charge, gentler deployment)

High severity (Δv > 30 km/h):
→ Fire Stage 1 immediately, Fire Stage 2 after 10-20 ms (full deployment)

Very high severity (Δv > 45 km/h):
→ Fire Stage 1 and Stage 2 simultaneously (maximum immediate protection)

STEP 10: SEND DEPLOYMENT COMMANDS
ACU outputs electrical trigger pulses:
• High current pulse (2-3 A) for 2-3 ms
• Sent to each airbag/pretensioner initiator squib
• Multiple devices may fire simultaneously (parallel triggering)

Timing Example (50 km/h Frontal Crash):
0 ms: Crash begins (vehicle contacts barrier)
5 ms: Front sensors detect >10g, ACU enters crash mode
8 ms: Δv reaches 15 km/h, pattern confirms frontal crash, safing logic satisfied
10 ms: ACU sends trigger → Pretensioners fire
12 ms: ACU sends trigger → Frontal airbags fire (Stage 1)
25 ms: Δv reaches 35 km/h (severe crash confirmed)
30 ms: ACU sends trigger → Frontal airbags fire (Stage 2)
40 ms: Airbags fully inflated
60 ms: Occupant contacts airbag

ALGORITHM TUNING:
Manufacturers tune thresholds based on:
• Vehicle mass and structure (heavier vehicles: higher thresholds)
• Crumple zone design (longer crumple zone: different signature)
• Crash test data (hundreds of tests to refine algorithm)
• Real-world crash data (EDR analysis from actual crashes)

Trade-offs:
• Too sensitive: False deployments (expensive, dangerous)
• Too conservative: Miss low-speed crashes where deployment would help
→ Balance: Deploy when needed, suppress when not needed
```

### SLIDE 23: Pedestrian Protection Systems
**Visual:** Pedestrian impact scenario with active hood lift deployment
**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"So far we've focused on protecting occupants inside the vehicle. But modern vehicles also have systems to protect pedestrians who are struck by the vehicle - pedestrian protection systems.

When a vehicle hits a pedestrian, the most dangerous injuries come from the pedestrian's head striking hard structures under the hood - the engine block, suspension towers, or the base of the windshield. These rigid structures cause severe head trauma.

Active hood lift systems address this by using pyrotechnic actuators to lift the rear edge of the hood - the part near the windshield - by about 5-10 cm within 30-50 milliseconds of detecting a pedestrian impact. This creates clearance space between the hood and the hard structures underneath, so when the pedestrian's head contacts the hood, the hood can deform and absorb energy instead of transmitting force directly to rigid components.

The detection system uses sensors in the front bumper - either pressure sensors or accelerometers - that detect the specific impact signature of a pedestrian. A pedestrian impact is different from a vehicle-to-vehicle crash - it's a lower-force, more localized impact at bumper height.

Some advanced vehicles also have external pedestrian airbags that deploy from the base of the windshield to cushion the pedestrian's head and cover the hard A-pillars.

These systems are becoming mandatory in European markets and are increasingly common worldwide. They represent the expanding scope of passive safety - protecting not just the occupants but also vulnerable road users outside the vehicle."

### SLIDE 24: Post-Crash Systems & Occupant Safety
**Visual:** Post-crash vehicle showing fuel pump shutoff, door unlock, battery disconnect
**Timing:** 2 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"Passive safety doesn't end when the airbags deploy. There are several post-crash systems that activate automatically to improve occupant safety after the crash.

First, the fuel pump shuts off immediately when a crash is detected. This prevents fuel from being pumped from the tank if there are fuel line ruptures, reducing fire risk dramatically. The fuel pump relay is controlled by the ACU or engine ECU, and it's cut within milliseconds of airbag deployment.

Second, the battery disconnect system. Some vehicles have a pyrotechnic device that physically disconnects the main battery cable after a severe crash. This prevents electrical shorts that could cause fires and also de-energizes high-voltage systems in hybrid/electric vehicles.

Third, automatic door unlocking. The body control module unlocks all doors after a crash so that occupants can exit and emergency responders can access the vehicle. This is critical if the crash damages the locking mechanism.

Fourth, the hazard lights automatically turn on after a crash to warn approaching traffic and mark the vehicle's position, especially important at night.

Some vehicles also have automatic emergency calls - eCall in Europe or similar systems elsewhere. After a crash, the vehicle automatically dials emergency services via the built-in cellular modem, transmits GPS coordinates, and opens a voice channel. This can significantly reduce response time, especially in crashes where occupants are incapacitated.

These systems show that passive safety is really a comprehensive approach to crash protection - before, during, and after the impact."

**PPT Content:**
```

5. Opens voice channel (occupant can speak to dispatcher)
6. If no response from occupant → Dispatcher assumes incapacitation, sends help

Benefits:
• Faster emergency response (especially rural areas, unconscious occupants)
• Accurate location (GPS precise to ~10 meters)
• Crash severity info (helps dispatch appropriate resources)

Effectiveness:
Response time reduction: ~30-40% (Europe, where eCall is mandatory)
Fatality reduction: Estimated 2-4% (from faster medical response)

Regulations:
• EU: eCall mandatory in all new vehicles (since 2018)
• Russia: ERA-GLONASS mandatory
• US: Not mandatory, but many vehicles have it (OnStar, etc.)

Manual activation: Button in cabin to manually trigger eCall (if crash sensor doesn't trigger)

6. INTERIOR LIGHTING:

Trigger: Airbag deployment
Action: Cabin lights turn on automatically
Duration: 10-15 minutes (or until manually turned off)

Purpose:
• Visibility for occupants to assess injuries, find belongings
• Visibility for emergency responders entering vehicle
• Signal vehicle location at night

7. ENGINE SHUTOFF (Beyond Fuel Pump):

Trigger: Severe crash
Action: Engine ignition disabled (ECU command)
Timing: Within 1 second of crash

Purpose:
• Prevent engine from running if cooling system damaged (prevent overheating)
• Reduce electrical load
• Safety (prevent vehicle movement if transmission damaged)

8. HIGH-VOLTAGE DISCONNECT (Hybrid/Electric Vehicles):

Trigger: Crash detected
Action:
• High-voltage contactors open (disconnect HV battery from vehicle systems)
• HV battery isolated (no current flow)
• HV cables de-energized

Purpose:
• Prevent electric shock to occupants or rescuers
• Prevent HV arc flash or fire
• Critical safety feature for EVs/hybrids (HV system: 200-400V, lethal!)

Timing: <100 ms (very fast, for safety)

Visual indicator: HV warning light stays illuminated (shows HV system disconnected)

INTEGRATION:

All post-crash systems coordinated by ACU and other ECUs:
ACU → Triggers airbags, sends crash signal to other ECUs
Engine ECU → Shuts off fuel pump, stops engine
Body Control Module → Unlocks doors, turns on lights, activates hazards
eCall module → Dials emergency services
Battery disconnect → Isolates battery (if severe crash)

Sequence (typical severe crash):
0 ms: Crash begins
10 ms: Airbags deploy
50 ms: Fuel pump relay opens (pump stops)
100 ms: HV disconnect (if EV/hybrid)
200 ms: Battery disconnect (if equipped, severe crash)
500 ms: Doors unlock
1 sec: Hazard lights activate, cabin lights on
2 sec: eCall initiated (dialing emergency services)
5 sec: eCall connected, data transmitted

All automatic, no occupant action required

### SLIDE 25: Maintenance & Service Considerations
**Visual:** Service technician replacing airbag components with warning symbols
**Timing:** 2 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"Before we move to safety testing, let me briefly cover maintenance and service of passive safety systems - this is important for anyone who might work on vehicles.

First, passive safety components are generally maintenance-free. Airbags don't require periodic replacement under normal conditions - they can last the life of the vehicle. Seatbelts don't require routine maintenance either.

Service technicians must take special precautions when working on or around airbag systems. The electrical connectors have safety interlocks, but there's still a risk of accidental deployment if procedures aren't followed. Disconnecting the battery and waiting for the backup capacitor to discharge is standard practice before working on SRS components.

Never attempt to disassemble or modify airbag components - they contain explosive materials and improper handling can cause injury or accidental deployment."

### SLIDE 26: Safety Testing Overview - NCAP Programs
**Visual:** NCAP star rating system and crash test dummy
**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"We've covered all the passive safety systems. Now the question is: how do we know they work? How are vehicles tested to ensure they protect occupants?

The answer is crash testing, conducted by New Car Assessment Programs - NCAP organizations. The most prominent are Euro NCAP in Europe, NHTSA in the United States, Latin NCAP, ASEAN NCAP, and Global NCAP (which includes Bharat NCAP in India).

These organizations purchase new vehicles off dealer lots and subject them to standardized crash tests. They measure injury risk using crash test dummies equipped with sensors - accelerometers in the head, chest, and pelvis, load sensors in the neck and femurs, and more.

The tests cover multiple crash scenarios: frontal barrier crash, side impact, pole crash, and rollover resistance. Each test has specific speed and configuration requirements.

After testing, vehicles receive star ratings - typically from 1 to 5 stars, with 5 stars being the best. These ratings are based on measured injury metrics, structural performance, and the presence of safety technologies.

For consumers, these ratings are incredibly valuable in making safety-informed vehicle choices. For manufacturers, achieving 5-star ratings has become a competitive priority and a marketing advantage.

Let me walk you through the main crash test types."

### SLIDE 27: Frontal Crash Tests
**Visual:** Frontal offset and full-width barrier crash test setups
**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"The first major test is the frontal crash test, and there are actually several variants.

The full-width frontal barrier test involves crashing the vehicle straight into a rigid, flat barrier at a specific speed - typically 50 km/h in Euro NCAP. The entire front of the vehicle contacts the barrier simultaneously. This test evaluates the crumple zone, seatbelt, and frontal airbag performance under a symmetric frontal load.

More challenging is the frontal offset test - only 40% of the vehicle's width hits the barrier, either on the driver side or passenger side. This is meant to simulate a real-world crash where vehicles don't hit perfectly head-on. The offset crash is more severe because the crash energy is concentrated on one side of the structure, and the load paths are different.

Euro NCAP uses a 50 km/h offset crash, while IIHS in the US has an even more severe test - the small overlap test at 64 km/h where only 25% of the vehicle's width hits the barrier. This is one of the hardest tests because very little structure is available to absorb energy, and there's a risk of the wheel and suspension intruding into the cabin.

During these tests, dummies are placed in the driver and passenger seats, and sometimes rear seats. After the crash, engineers download data from the dummy sensors and examine the vehicle structure. They measure:
- Head injury criterion
- Chest compression and deceleration
- Femur loads
- Neck loads
- Knee and lower leg forces

They also visually inspect the cabin - did the doors stay closed? Can the doors still be opened? Is there excessive intrusion of the dashboard, footwell, or firewall?

Vehicles that achieve low injury metrics across all body regions and maintain good structural integrity score highly in frontal crash tests."

**PPT Content:**
```
FRONTAL CRASH TESTS

Test Types:

1. FULL-WIDTH FRONTAL BARRIER (Euro NCAP, NHTSA):

Setup:
• Rigid, flat barrier (fixed, does not deform)
• Vehicle impacts at 50 km/h (Euro NCAP) or 56 km/h (NHTSA)
• 100% of vehicle width contacts barrier (symmetric crash)
• Vehicle perpendicular to barrier (0° angle)

• Driver: 50th percentile male
• Passenger: 50th percentile male (Euro NCAP) or 5th percentile female (NHTSA)
• Rear seats: Child dummies (Euro NCAP, 6-year and 10-year in child seats)

• Cabin integrity (minimal intrusion into survival space)

• Seatbelt performance (proper restraint, load limiting)
• Dummy injury metrics (HIC, chest, femur, neck loads)

• Head and neck: Max XX points

• Pelvis and legs: Max XX points
• Child occupants: Separate scoring

Setup:
• Rigid barrier
• Vehicle impacts with only 40% width overlap (driver or passenger side)
• Speed: 50 km/h (Euro NCAP), 64 km/h (IIHS moderate overlap)
• Offset to driver side (primary) or passenger side (some programs)

• Simulate more realistic crash (vehicles rarely hit perfectly head-on)
• More severe than full-width (energy concentrated on one side)

• One side of crumple zone absorbs most energy
• Risk of greater intrusion on impact side

• Structural performance on impact side vs. non-impact side

• Structural integrity (cabin collapse?)

### SLIDE 28: Side Impact & Other Crash Tests
**Visual:** Side impact test setup and pole test configuration
**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Instructor Narrative:**
"Side impact testing is equally important because side crashes have limited crumple zone distance - there's very little between you and the intruding object.

The side barrier test uses a moving deformable barrier - essentially a large cart weighing 950 kg (representing another vehicle) that strikes the side of the stationary test vehicle. The barrier is angled and covered with a deformable honeycomb face to simulate a vehicle front end. It strikes at 50-60 km/h, impacting the driver's door and B-pillar area.

Dummies are placed in both front seats, and the side facing the barrier is the critical measurement area. The test evaluates:
- Door intrusion - how far does the door move inward?
- Side airbag deployment effectiveness
- Torso and pelvis protection - are chest and pelvis loads within safe limits?
- Head protection - does the curtain airbag prevent head contact with the intruding door or B-pillar?

The pole test is even more severe. The vehicle is pushed sideways at 32 km/h into a rigid pole positioned at the driver's door. This simulates sliding into a tree or pole. Because the pole is narrow, it concentrates forces on a small area - much higher intrusion risk. This test especially evaluates curtain airbag and door beam performance.

There's also the rollover resistance test, though this isn't a full rollover crash. Instead, NHTSA uses a calculation-based test: they measure the vehicle's center of gravity height and track width to determine rollover propensity - the Static Stability Factor. Vehicles with high centers of gravity (SUVs) score lower. Some programs also test roof strength - the roof must withstand a load of 3-4 times the vehicle's weight without excessive crush.

Finally, there are whiplash tests for rear impacts, and pedestrian protection tests we already discussed. Each test contributes to the overall safety rating."

**PPT Content:**
```
SIDE IMPACT & OTHER CRASH TESTS

1. SIDE MOBILE BARRIER TEST (Euro NCAP, NHTSA):

Setup:
• Moving Deformable Barrier (MDB): Cart with honeycomb face, mass 950 kg
• Barrier impacts side of stationary vehicle
• Speed: 60 km/h (Euro NCAP), 62 km/h (NHTSA)
• Impact point: Driver's door and B-pillar (represents vehicle-to-vehicle crash)
• Barrier height: Aligned to simulate sedan hitting sedan

Dummies:
• Driver seat: 50th percentile male (impact side - critical measurements)
• Passenger seat: 50th percentile male (non-impact side - lesser concern)

  - Target: <15 cm

  - Head: Contact with door/pillar (should be prevented by curtain airbag)
  - Chest: Rib deflection from door intrusion (should be limited by torso airbag)
  - Abdomen: Compression from door beam or sill intrusion
  - Pelvis: Pelvic loading (pelvis airbag effectiveness)

• Chest: Highest injury risk (direct door contact → rib fractures, organ damage)
• Head: Second highest (B-pillar contact → severe head injury)
• Abdomen and pelvis: Moderate risk

Setup:
• Rigid pole (diameter: 25.4 cm, represents tree or utility pole)

• Impact point: Driver's door, aligned with driver's torso/head
• Vehicle slides laterally (simulates loss of control, sliding off road)

Purpose:
• More severe than barrier test (narrow pole → concentrated forces)
• Tests door beam and B-pillar strength
• Tests curtain airbag effectiveness (critical for head protection)

• Driver seat only: 50th percentile male or World SID dummy

Evaluated:

  - Curtain airbag must prevent/cushion contact

Pole test is very challenging - many vehicles show higher injury risk than barrier test

• Mobile barrier strikes passenger side (right side in LHD vehicles)
• Evaluates driver protection (driver is on far side, away from impact)
• Driver thrown laterally toward passenger (unrestrained lateral motion)

Evaluated:
• Driver head: Does driver contact passenger or center console?
• Center airbag: Does it deploy and prevent driver-passenger contact?
• Lateral restraint: Seatbelt and seat effectiveness in lateral loading

Purpose:
• Far-side crashes often neglected (no airbag on that side historically)
• Center airbag needed for good rating

4. ROLLOVER RESISTANCE (NHTSA):

Test Method: Calculation-Based (Static Stability Factor)
SSF = Track Width / (2 × Center of Gravity Height)

Higher SSF: More stable, lower rollover risk
• SSF > 1.45: Low rollover risk (5-star)
• SSF 1.20-1.45: Moderate rollover risk (3-4 star)
• SSF < 1.20: High rollover risk (1-2 star)

Typical Values:
• Sedan (low CG, wide track): SSF ~1.4-1.5 → 5 stars
• SUV (high CG, narrower track): SSF ~1.1-1.3 → 2-4 stars

Note: ESC (Electronic Stability Control) dramatically reduces real-world rollover risk
→ NHTSA gives bonus points for ESC

5. ROOF STRENGTH TEST (NHTSA, IIHS):

Purpose: Ensure roof can withstand rollover without crushing cabin

Setup:
• Stationary vehicle, tilted 25° (simulating rollover orientation)
• Rigid plate (25 cm × 65 cm) presses down on roof edge (above A-pillar or B-pillar)
• Force applied until roof crushes 12.7 cm (5 inches) or maximum force reached

Requirement:
• Roof must withstand force ≥ 3 × vehicle weight before 12.7 cm crush
• IIHS higher standard: ≥ 4 × vehicle weight (for "Good" rating)

Example:
Vehicle weight: 1,500 kg
Required roof strength: 1,500 × 3 × 9.81 = 44,145 N ≈ 44 kN
IIHS "Good": >59 kN

Evaluated:
• Peak force before failure
• Roof intrusion into headroom (survival space)

Effectiveness:
Strong roof → Reduces rollover fatality risk by 40-50% (maintains cabin integrity)

6. WHIPLASH PROTECTION (Euro NCAP):

Test: Rear Impact Sled Test

Setup:
• Seat mounted on sled (simulates vehicle)
• Sled accelerates rearward at 16-24 km/h delta-v (simulates rear-end collision)
• BioRID dummy (specialized rear impact dummy with detailed neck sensors)

Evaluated:
• Head restraint geometry: Distance from head to restraint (closer is better, <5 cm optimal)
• Head restraint height: Should align with center of head
• Neck loading: Extension, shear, tension
• Torso motion: Seat deformation and rebound

Rating:
• Good: Head restraint close, well-aligned, low neck loads
• Poor: Head restraint far back or low, high neck extension (whiplash risk)

Whiplash Injury:
• Most common injury in low-speed rear crashes (<25 km/h)
• Neck hyperextension (head whips backward)
• Not life-threatening, but causes chronic pain, disability
• Proper head restraint reduces whiplash risk by 30-40%

7. PEDESTRIAN PROTECTION TEST (Euro NCAP):

Tests:
A) Head Impact:
• Adult headform (mass 4.5 kg) fired at 40 km/h at hood surface and windshield base
• Child headform (smaller, 3.5 kg) fired at 35 km/h at hood
• Measure HIC (must be <1000, ideally <650)
• Tests multiple impact points across hood

B) Leg Impact:
• Legform impactor (simulates pedestrian leg) fired at bumper at 40 km/h
• Measure knee and tibia bending moments, ligament forces
• Bumper should absorb energy, prevent leg fracture

Evaluated:
• Hood deformation: Does hood absorb energy (soft impact) or bottom out on engine (hard impact)?
• Active hood lift: Does it deploy and increase clearance?
• External airbag: Cushion for windshield base impact
• Bumper design: Energy absorption

Rating Impact:
Pedestrian protection is 20% of overall Euro NCAP rating
Poor pedestrian rating → Can drop from 5-star to 4-star overall

OVERALL SAFETY RATING CALCULATION (Euro NCAP Example):

Four Categories:
1. Adult Occupant Protection (40%):
   - Frontal crash: 40% of category
   - Side crash: 40% of category
   - Pole test: 20% of category

2. Child Occupant Protection (20%):
   - Child dummy injuries in frontal and side crashes
   - Child seat installation ease

3. Vulnerable Road Users - Pedestrian Protection (20%):
   - Hood impact tests
   - Bumper impact tests
   - Active hood lift bonus

4. Safety Assist - Active Safety (20%):
   - AEB (Automatic Emergency Braking): Major component
   - Lane Keep Assist, Speed Assist, Seatbelt Reminders
   - (Not passive safety, but affects overall rating)

Final Star Rating:
• 5 stars: >80% overall + each category meets minimum threshold
• 4 stars: 70-80%
• 3 stars: 60-70%
• 2 stars: 50-60%
• 1 star: <50%

If ANY category scores very poorly (<40%), vehicle cannot achieve 5 stars even if other categories are excellent

   - Full system replacement: ₹1-3 lakhs. Often totals older vehicles. Insurance usually covers.

5. **"Can I sell a car with deployed airbags?"**
   - Legal but must disclose. Value significantly reduced. Buyer should verify proper replacement.
