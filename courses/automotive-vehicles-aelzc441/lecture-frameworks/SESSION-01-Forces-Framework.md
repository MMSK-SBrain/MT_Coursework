# Session 1: Forces Acting on a Moving Vehicle
## Instructor-Led Story Framework for PPT Preparation

**Duration:** 90 minutes lecture + 30 minutes Q&A
**Approach:** Teacher-Led Narrative with Technical Depth
**PPT Target:** 25-30 slides

---

## 🎯 Session Objectives (For Instructor)

By the end of this session, students will be able to:
- Apply Newton's laws to vehicle motion
- Calculate rolling resistance, aerodynamic drag, and gradient forces
- Determine total resistance for different driving scenarios
- Understand vehicle performance metrics

### **RISING ACTION: Building Understanding** (Slides 9-22, ~50 minutes)

#### **Part 1: Rolling Resistance** (Slides 9-13, ~15 minutes)

##### Slide 9: What is Rolling Resistance?
**Visual:** Diagram showing tire deformation
**Instructor Script:**
> "Imagine pushing a shopping cart. Even on flat ground, you need force to keep it moving. Why? The wheels resist motion. Same with car tires.
>
> When a tire rolls, it deforms slightly – it's not perfectly rigid. This deformation eats energy. That's rolling resistance."

##### Slide 10: The Formula (Simplified)
**Visual:** F_rolling = C_rr × W (with annotations)
**Instructor Script:**
> "The formula is beautifully simple:
>
> **F_rolling = C_rr × W**
>
> Where:
> - F_rolling = rolling resistance force (N)
> - C_rr = coefficient of rolling resistance (dimensionless, ~0.01 for cars)
> - W = vehicle weight (N) = mg
>
> That's it. Weight times a small coefficient."

##### Slide 11: Example Calculation (Live)
**Visual:** Step-by-step calculation on slide
**Instructor Script:**
> "Let me show you with our Honda Civic:
> - Mass (m) = 1,300 kg
> - Weight (W) = mg = 1,300 × 9.81 = 12,753 N
> - C_rr = 0.012 (typical for car tires on asphalt)
>
> F_rolling = 0.012 × 12,753 = **153 N**
>
> That's the force just to overcome tire resistance. Write that number down – we'll use it later."

##### Slide 12: Factors Affecting Rolling Resistance
**Visual:** Table or icons showing factors
**Instructor Script:**
> "Quick note on what affects C_rr:
> - Tire pressure (lower = higher resistance)
> - Tire material (harder rubber = lower resistance)
> - Road surface (smooth asphalt vs rough gravel)
> - Temperature
>
> For most calculations, we use 0.01 to 0.015 for cars."

##### Slide 13: Practice Problem
**Visual:** Different vehicle specs
**Instructor Script:**
> "Your turn. Calculate rolling resistance for:
> - Truck: 3,500 kg, C_rr = 0.008
> - SUV: 2,200 kg, C_rr = 0.012
>
> Take 2 minutes. I'll show the solution after."

#### **Part 2: Aerodynamic Drag** (Slides 14-18, ~20 minutes)

##### Slide 14: Air Resistance - The Hidden Force
**Visual:** Car with airflow lines
**Instructor Script:**
> "Rolling resistance is constant – same force at 10 km/h or 100 km/h. But there's another resistance that grows dramatically with speed. You've felt it if you've ever stuck your hand out of a car window at highway speeds.
>
> Air resistance. And it follows a fascinating relationship with velocity."

##### Slide 15: The Drag Equation
**Visual:** F_drag = ½ ρ C_d A v² (with annotations)
**Instructor Script:**
> "This equation looks more complex, but let me break it down for you:
>
> **F_drag = ½ ρ C_d A v²**
>
> - F_drag = aerodynamic drag force (N)
> - ρ = air density (1.225 kg/m³ at sea level)
> - C_d = drag coefficient (0.25-0.35 for modern cars)
> - A = frontal area (m²)
> - v = velocity (m/s)
>
> Notice that **v²** at the end? That's the key. Double your speed, quadruple the drag force."

##### Slide 16: Understanding Each Term
**Visual:** Visual breakdown of each variable
**Instructor Script:**
> "Let me explain each term:
>
> **ρ (rho) - Air Density:** 1.225 kg/m³ at sea level, 20°C. Lower at high altitudes.
>
> **C_d - Drag Coefficient:** How 'slippery' the car is. Sports cars: 0.25-0.30. SUVs: 0.35-0.40. It's all about shape.
>
> **A - Frontal Area:** How much air the car pushes. Measure the face of the car. Sedans: ~2-2.5 m². SUVs: ~3 m².
>
> **v - Velocity:** Speed in m/s. Remember: 100 km/h = 27.8 m/s."

##### Slide 17: Example Calculation (The Power of v²)
**Visual:** Table showing drag at different speeds
**Instructor Script:**
> "Back to our Honda Civic:
> - C_d = 0.30 (pretty aerodynamic)
> - A = 2.2 m²
> - ρ = 1.225 kg/m³
> - v = 100 km/h = 27.8 m/s
>
> F_drag = 0.5 × 1.225 × 0.30 × 2.2 × (27.8)²
> F_drag = 0.5 × 1.225 × 0.30 × 2.2 × 772
> F_drag = **312 N** at 100 km/h
>
> But watch what happens at 50 km/h (13.9 m/s):
> F_drag = 0.5 × 1.225 × 0.30 × 2.2 × (13.9)²
> F_drag = **78 N** at 50 km/h
>
> Half the speed = quarter the drag. This is why highway fuel economy is so different from city driving."

##### Slide 18: Practice Problem + Discussion
**Visual:** Comparison table
**Instructor Script:**
> "Calculate drag force for the same car at:
> - 60 km/h
> - 120 km/h
>
> Then think: At what speed does aerodynamic drag equal rolling resistance (153 N)?
>
> This is practical engineering thinking."

#### **Part 3: Gradient Resistance** (Slides 19-22, ~15 minutes)

##### Slide 19: Climbing Hills - Gravity Fights Back
**Visual:** Car on inclined surface with force diagram
**Instructor Script:**
> "Flat roads are the easy case. But what about climbing a hill? Now we're fighting gravity directly.
>
> This is where your trigonometry becomes useful."

##### Slide 20: The Gradient Force Equation
**Visual:** Free body diagram with angle θ
**Instructor Script:**
> "When a vehicle climbs a slope at angle θ:
>
> **F_gradient = mg sin(θ)**
>
> Or, if you know the grade percentage:
>
> **F_gradient = mg × (grade/100)**
>
> For small angles (which most roads are), grade percentage is approximately the angle in degrees × 1.75."

##### Slide 21: Example - Climbing a 5% Grade
**Visual:** Calculation on slide
**Instructor Script:**
> "Our Honda Civic climbing a 5% grade (about 3 degrees):
>
> Method 1 (using percentage):
> F_gradient = 1,300 × 9.81 × (5/100)
> F_gradient = **627 N**
>
> Method 2 (using angle):
> θ = 2.86° (from arctan(0.05))
> F_gradient = 12,753 × sin(2.86°)
> F_gradient = **636 N** (slightly more accurate)
>
> That's more than rolling resistance and drag combined at moderate speeds!"

##### Slide 22: Gradient Scenarios
**Visual:** Table of common gradients
**Instructor Script:**
> "For reference:
> - 2-3%: Gentle highway grade
> - 5-7%: Noticeable hill
> - 10%: Steep hill (some mountain roads)
> - 15%+: Very steep (challenges even powerful vehicles)
>
> Now you can calculate the force needed for any slope."

### **CLIMAX: Putting It All Together** (Slides 23-25, ~10 minutes)

#### Slide 23: Total Resistance Force
**Visual:** Equation showing all three forces added
**Instructor Script:**
> "Here's the moment it all comes together. The **total resistance force** a vehicle faces is:
>
> **F_total = F_rolling + F_drag + F_gradient**
>
> Three independent forces, one total force the engine must overcome.
>
> Let me show you a complete scenario."

#### Slide 24: Complete Example - The Full Picture
**Visual:** Comprehensive calculation table
**Instructor Script:**
> "Honda Civic, 100 km/h, climbing 3% grade:
>
> **F_rolling** = 0.012 × 12,753 = 153 N
> **F_drag** = 0.5 × 1.225 × 0.30 × 2.2 × (27.8)² = 312 N
> **F_gradient** = 12,753 × (3/100) = 383 N
>
> **F_total = 153 + 312 + 383 = 848 N**
>
> **That's the answer.** 848 Newtons of force, continuously, just to maintain 100 km/h up a 3% grade.
>
> This is the force the powertrain must provide through the tires. Everything else in the car – the engine, transmission, differential – exists to deliver this force to the road."

#### Slide 25: From Force to Performance
**Visual:** Link to next concepts
**Instructor Script:**
> "Now you understand the 'why.' Next session, we'll explore 'how' – how tires actually generate this force through friction. And in Session 3, we'll convert this force to power requirements.
>
> But you've already taken the biggest step. You can now calculate the fundamental challenge every vehicle faces: resistance to motion."

## 📊 PPT DESIGN GUIDANCE

### Visual Style:
- **Professional engineering aesthetic**: Clean layouts, technical diagrams
- **Color scheme**: Blues and grays (professional), highlight key equations in orange
- **Consistent**: Same template throughout

### Key Slides to Emphasize:
1. **Slide 2** (The Question) - Hook students immediately
2. **Slide 10, 15, 20** (Formulas) - Make these crystal clear
3. **Slide 24** (Complete Example) - The "aha" moment
4. **Slide 27** (Summary) - Students will photograph this

### Animations:
- Minimal and professional
- Use build animations for multi-step calculations
- Highlight key terms as you mention them
- Reveal solutions to practice problems after pause

### Technical Diagrams:
- Use clean, labeled free-body diagrams
- Show force vectors clearly
- Include units on all calculations

## 🎯 INSTRUCTOR NOTES

### Pedagogical Strategy:
This session uses **Teacher-Led Narrative** to:
1. **Build confidence**: Start with "you already know this"
2. **Demystify complexity**: Break down into three simple forces
3. **Provide tools**: Every formula is immediately applied
4. **Connect to experience**: Highway driving, hill climbing are relatable

### Common Student Challenges:
- **Unit conversion** (km/h to m/s): Emphasize early, practice often
- **When to use sin vs percentage**: Show both methods
- **Understanding v²**: Use the comparison table (Slide 17)

### Timing Flexibility:
- If running short: Skip one practice problem
- If running long: Move detailed gradient discussion to Q&A
- Core content: Slides 1-25 (must cover)

### Engagement Points:
- **Slide 6**: Pause for guesses before revealing challenge
- **Slide 13, 18**: Student calculation time (active learning)
- **Slide 24**: "Let's calculate together" - work through live
- **Slide 26**: Can be homework if time is tight
