## 3. Learning Outcomes

### **Course Objectives (CO):**

**CO1:** Study main components/systems of an automobile (engine, transmission, drive-axle system, suspension system, brake system, etc.)

**CO2:** Understand fundamental working principles of different systems

**CO3:** Learn performance analysis along with working and important aspects of various components of automotive vehicles

**CO4:** Get acquainted with advanced concepts through projects and assignments

### **What Learners Will DO (After Course Completion):**

**LO1:** Apply physics and mathematics to calculate vehicle performance (forces, torque, power, acceleration, braking)

**LO2:** Recognize and identify different vehicle systems and components and their interactions

**LO3:** Analyze the functions and evaluate the performance of vehicle systems

**LO4:** Understand importance of each system and how it affects safety, reliability, and performance of vehicle

**LO5:** Perform engineering calculations for powertrain, dynamics, and chassis systems

### **Critical Skills:**

1. **Mathematical/Analytical Skills (PRIORITY):**
   - Calculate resistance forces (rolling, aerodynamic, gradient)
   - Calculate torque and power requirements
   - Analyze tire-road friction and forces
   - Perform vehicle dynamics calculations (acceleration, braking, load transfer)
   - Calculate gear ratios and transmission requirements
   - Analyze suspension geometry and steering angles

2. **Technical Understanding:**
   - Understand IC engine operation (simplified, functional view)
   - Understand transmission systems and gear ratios
   - Understand vehicle dynamics and handling
   - Understand electrical and electronic control systems
   - Understand vehicle communication networks (CAN/LIN)

3. **Integration Skills:**
   - Understand how different subsystems interact
   - Analyze system-level vehicle behavior
   - Understand control architecture and sensor-actuator integration

4. **Problem-Solving:**
   - Apply physics principles to automotive problems
   - Diagnose system issues using engineering principles
   - Perform numerical analysis and calculations

### **Nice-to-Have:**

- Exposure to automotive industry standards and practices
- Understanding of emerging technologies (EV, ADAS - introductory level)
- Basic familiarity with automotive terminology

## 4. Content Structure (REVISED)

### **PEDAGOGICAL APPROACH:**

**Build confidence with familiar physics and mathematics BEFORE introducing complex automotive systems**

**Progression:** WHY (forces/power needed) → HOW (systems that provide it) → CONTROL (managing motion) → INTEGRATION (complete vehicle)

---

### **Main Modules:**

**Module 1: Vehicle Motion Fundamentals (Sessions 1-3)** ⭐ **NEW FOUNDATION MODULE**
- **Philosophy:** Answer "Why do we need power?" before "How do engines work?"
- Session 1: Forces Acting on a Moving Vehicle
  - Newton's laws applied to vehicles
  - Resistance forces (rolling, aerodynamic, gradient)
  - Force calculations and performance metrics
- Session 2: Tires & Friction Fundamentals
  - Tire construction and types
  - Friction principles and normal force
  - Friction circle concept
  - Tire force generation and slip
- Session 3: From Forces to Power Requirements
  - Force → Torque → Power relationships
  - Vehicle performance calculations (acceleration, top speed, gradeability)
  - **Conclusion:** "Now we know HOW MUCH power we need. Next: HOW to generate it?"

**Module 2: Powertrain Systems (Sessions 4-8)**
- Session 4: Introduction to IC Engines (Simplified)
  - Engine as torque-generating device (NO thermodynamics initially)
  - Basic mechanical components and 4-stroke cycle
  - Engine torque and power curves
- Session 5: Engine Support Systems - Cooling & Lubrication
  - Why cooling and lubrication are needed
  - Cooling systems (air vs liquid)
  - Lubrication systems and oil specifications
- Session 6: Engine Performance & Fuel Systems
  - Performance maps and BSFC
  - Fuel delivery systems (carburetor, MPFI, GDI)
  - Basic emissions overview
- Session 7: Transmission Systems - Part 1
  - Why transmission is needed
  - Gear ratios and torque multiplication
  - Manual transmission and clutch
- Session 8: Transmission Systems - Part 2 & Alternatives
  - Automatic, CVT, DCT
  - Differential and final drive
  - Brief introduction to electric powertrains

**Module 3: Chassis Dynamics & Control (Sessions 9-12)** ⭐ **MATH-HEAVY MODULE**
- Session 9: Vehicle Dynamics Fundamentals
  - Longitudinal dynamics (acceleration, braking)
  - Load transfer calculations
  - Weight distribution effects
- Session 10: Braking Systems
  - Braking physics and friction calculations
  - Hydraulic brake systems
  - ABS operation
- Session 11: Steering Systems
  - Ackermann geometry and steering angles
  - Turning radius calculations
  - Power steering systems
- Session 12: Suspension Systems
  - Spring-mass-damper systems (physics)
  - Suspension types and components
  - Ride vs handling trade-offs

**Module 4: Vehicle Electrical & Control Systems (Sessions 13-15)**
- Session 13: Electrical Architecture
  - 12V/24V systems
  - Battery, alternator, starter
  - Electrical loads and distribution
- Session 14: Vehicle Control Systems
  - ECU introduction
  - Sensors and actuators
  - Closed-loop control basics
- Session 15: Vehicle Communication Networks
  - CAN bus fundamentals
  - LIN bus basics
  - OBD-II diagnostics

**Module 5: Vehicle Architecture & Comfort (Sessions 16-18)**
- Session 16: Chassis & Body Architecture
  - Vehicle classification
  - Chassis types (monocoque, ladder frame)
  - Weight distribution and crash structures
- Session 17: HVAC Systems
  - HVAC fundamentals
  - Refrigeration cycle (thermodynamics application HERE)
  - Climate control systems
- Session 18: Passive Safety Systems
  - Crash safety principles
  - Seatbelt and airbag systems
  - Safety testing standards

**Module 6: Active Safety & Emerging Technologies (Sessions 19-20)**
- Session 19: Active Safety & Vehicle Stability
  - ESC and TCS operation
  - Understeer/oversteer correction
  - Integration with ABS
- Session 20: Introduction to ADAS & Future Mobility
  - ADAS overview (ACC, LKA, AEB - brief only)
  - Sensor technologies
  - SAE autonomy levels
  - Future mobility trends

---

### **Sequencing Rationale (REVISED):**

#### **Phase 1: Foundation (Sessions 1-3) - Build Confidence**
- Start with **familiar physics** (forces, friction, power)
- **Heavy math focus** - calculations students can do
- Answer fundamental question: "How much power does a vehicle need?"
- **Build confidence** before complex systems

#### **Phase 2: Power Generation (Sessions 4-8) - Answer the HOW**
- Now they understand **WHY** engines exist
- Simplified engine view (no thermodynamics)
- Cooling/lubrication **immediately after engines** (logical grouping)
- Transmission systems (matching engine to wheels)

#### **Phase 3: Motion Control (Sessions 9-12) - Physics Application**
- Apply Session 1-3 foundation to **chassis systems**
- Math-based dynamics, braking, steering, suspension
- Use physics they already know (springs, friction, geometry)

#### **Phase 4: Control & Electronics (Sessions 13-15)**
- Build on mechanical understanding
- Add electronic control layer
- Sensors, actuators, networks

#### **Phase 5: Integration (Sessions 16-20)**
- Complete vehicle architecture
- Comfort systems (HVAC - thermodynamics introduced here)
- Safety systems (passive and active)
- Future technologies

---

### **Logical Progression:**

```
Sessions 1-3:   FOUNDATION
                ↓ (Physics & Math - "Why do we need power?")

Sessions 4-8:   POWER GENERATION
                ↓ (How engines and transmissions work)

Sessions 9-12:  MOTION CONTROL
                ↓ (How to control vehicle motion - math-based)

Sessions 13-15: ELECTRONIC CONTROL
                ↓ (ECUs, sensors, networks)

Sessions 16-18: INTEGRATION
                ↓ (Complete vehicle architecture + comfort)

Sessions 19-20: ADVANCED & FUTURE
```

### **Key Differences from Traditional Approach:**

| Traditional | Revised (Physics First) |
|------------|------------------------|
| Start with vehicle architecture | Start with vehicle motion physics |
| Introduce engines with thermodynamics | Introduce engines as torque generators |
| Chassis systems late in course | Foundation includes tires/friction early |
| Math sprinkled throughout | Heavy math concentration in Sessions 1-3, 9-12 |
| Assumes automotive background | Assumes NO automotive background |

## 5. Assessment Strategy

### **Assessment Types:**

1. **Quiz (10%)**
   - Focus: Basic calculations (forces, torque, power, gear ratios)
   - Timing: After Module 1 or Module 2

2. **Assignment (20%)**
   - Vehicle performance calculations
   - System analysis problems
   - Numerical problem-solving

3. **Mid-term Examination (30%)**
   - Coverage: Modules 1-3 (Foundation + Powertrain + Dynamics)
   - Heavy emphasis on calculations

4. **Comprehensive Final Examination (40%)**
   - Coverage: All modules
   - Mix of calculations, conceptual understanding, system analysis

### **Grading Criteria:**

- **Minimum Passing:** 40% aggregate
- **Recommended for Strong Understanding:** 60%+

### **Focus Areas for Assessment:**

- **40% Numerical Problems** (calculations, problem-solving)
- **40% Conceptual Understanding** (explain systems, principles)
- **20% Analysis** (compare systems, analyze trade-offs)

## 7. Recommended Teaching Approach

### **Sessions 1-3 (Foundation):**
- **Show numerical examples** for every concept
- Provide sample problems with solutions
- Use real vehicle data (mass, Cd, tire specs)
- Emphasize: "You can calculate this!"

### **Sessions 4-8 (Powertrain):**
- Show engine operation through **mechanical perspective first**
- Use torque/power curves extensively
- Link back to Session 3 power requirements
- Cooling/lubrication: "Why do we need these for the engine we just learned?"

### **Sessions 9-12 (Chassis Dynamics):**
- **Calculations in every session**
- Use load transfer to explain suspension/tire importance
- Link to Session 2 (tire friction)
- Emphasize physics principles

### **Sessions 13-20:**
- Build on mechanical understanding
- Show how electronics enhance mechanical systems
- Integration focus: "How do all these systems work together?"
