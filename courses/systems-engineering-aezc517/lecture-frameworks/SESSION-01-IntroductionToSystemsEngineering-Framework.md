# Session 1: Introduction to Systems Engineering
## Instructor-Led Story Framework for PPT Preparation

**Duration:** 120 minutes (90 minutes lecture + 30 minutes discussion/Q&A)
**Approach:** Story-Based with Real-World Failure Case Studies
**PPT Target:** 28-30 slides

---

## 🎯 Session Objectives (For Instructor)

By the end of this session, students will be able to:
- Understand the critical importance of systems engineering through real-world failure examples
- Recognize the cost and consequences of poor systems engineering
- Define what a system is and identify key system characteristics
- Articulate the value proposition of systems engineering in automotive contexts
- Begin developing a systems thinking mindset

**Learning Outcomes Addressed:**
- **LO1:** Describe processes, methods, and practices of systems engineering (Understand)
- **LO3:** Recognize important systems engineering and systems thinking strategies and practices (Understand)

---

This is Session 1, so we're building from:
- General engineering education and principles
- Students' experiences with complex products and systems
- Basic understanding of engineering failures from news/media
- Undergraduate engineering fundamentals (requirements, design, testing)

**Bridge:** "You've learned to design components. Now we learn to design systems."

#### Slide 10: Apollo 13 - The Systems Engineering Failure
**Visual:** Timeline diagram showing the cascade of decisions

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Here's what happened. During pre-flight testing, ground crews couldn't remove oxygen from tank 2. So they ran the heater to boil it off. But here's the problem: the thermostatic switches were rated for 28 volts. The ground equipment used 65 volts.
>
> The switches welded shut. Nobody noticed. During the mission, the heater cycled on and off... but the switches couldn't open. The temperature soared. Teflon insulation ignited in the pure oxygen environment. Boom.
>
> Now, was this a design failure? Yes. But more importantly, it was a **systems engineering failure**. Let me show you how."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE SYSTEMS ENGINEERING FAILURES:

1. Interface Mismatch
   • Tank designed for 28V spacecraft systems
   • Ground equipment operated at 65V
   • No verification of compatibility

2. Design Change Propagation
   • Upgraded to 65V spacecraft systems
   • Did NOT upgrade tank components
   • Change impact analysis failed

3. Testing Limitations
   • Ground test anomaly (couldn't remove O2)
   • Workaround implemented (heat it out)
   • Nobody recognized danger signal

4. Requirements Gap
   • No requirement for ground equipment compatibility
   • No verification of thermostatic switch ratings
```

**Learning Outcomes:** LO1, LO3 - Understanding SE process failures

#### Slide 11: Apollo 13 - The Systems Lesson
**Visual:** Diagram showing interconnected system elements that failed

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Here's the systems engineering lesson: **Every component exists in a context**. The oxygen tank worked fine in isolation. But in the context of ground support equipment, in the context of the 65-volt upgrade, in the context of the testing procedures... it was a bomb waiting to go off.
>
> Good component engineering. Failed systems engineering. The tank designers focused on their component. Nobody looked at the whole system context. Nobody asked, 'What else changed? What are the interfaces? What are the hidden interactions?'
>
> That's systems engineering. And when you don't do it, this is what happens."

**PPT Content:**
```
SYSTEMS ENGINEERING LESSONS:

✗ Component worked in isolation
✗ Failed in system context
✗ Interface assumptions were wrong
✗ Design changes didn't propagate
✗ Testing didn't reveal system-level issue

CRITICAL SE PRACTICES MISSING:
• Interface control and verification
• Change impact analysis
• System-level requirements
• Context-aware testing
• Cross-functional communication

"Good parts do not guarantee a good system."
```

**Learning Outcomes:** LO1, LO3 - SE principles and practices

#### Slide 13: Therac-25 - The Systems Engineering Catastrophe
**Visual:** Diagram showing removed hardware interlocks, software dependency

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Previous versions—Therac-6 and Therac-20—had hardware interlocks. Physical safety mechanisms that prevented overdoses even if software failed. But Therac-25 removed those. Why? Cost savings. Space savings. And confidence in software.
>
> The engineers made the software responsible for safety. But the software had race conditions, inadequate error handling, and cryptic error messages. Operators saw 'Malfunction 54' and were told to continue treatment.
>
> Here's the systems engineering failure: **They removed redundant safety layers without understanding the system-level risks**. They optimized for cost, not safety. They trusted a single layer of defense."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE SYSTEMS ENGINEERING FAILURES:

1. Safety Architecture Degradation
   • Removed hardware safety interlocks
   • Relied solely on software for safety
   • No defense-in-depth strategy

2. Software Requirements Gap
   • No formal safety requirements for software
   • Race conditions in critical code
   • Inadequate error handling

3. Human-System Interface Failure
   • Cryptic error messages ("Malfunction 54")
   • Operators not trained on error meanings
   • No clear safety guidance

4. Inadequate Testing
   • Software tested in isolation
   • Not tested for race conditions under realistic timing
   • System-level safety scenarios not exercised

5. Lack of System Safety Analysis
   • No failure modes and effects analysis (FMEA)
   • No hazard analysis
   • Didn't understand system-level failure modes
```

**Learning Outcomes:** LO1, LO3 - SE safety practices

#### Slide 14: Therac-25 - The Systems Lesson
**Visual:** Layered safety model showing single point of failure vs. defense-in-depth

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The lesson here is about **system safety architecture**. In previous versions, if software failed, hardware caught it. There were layers of defense. Therac-25 had a single point of failure: the software. And it failed.
>

>
> Think about it: Your autonomous emergency braking system. Your lane-keeping assist. Your adaptive cruise control. All software-intensive. All safety-critical. Are you building Therac-25, or are you building defensible systems?"

✗ Inadequate hazard analysis

CRITICAL SE PRACTICES MISSING:
• System safety analysis (FMEA, hazard analysis)

AUTOMOTIVE RELEVANCE: Software-intensive safety systems today

#### Slide 16: Toyota - The Systems Engineering Breakdown
**Visual:** Complexity diagram showing ECU interactions, software interdependencies

**Instructor Narration:**

>
> **If you can't verify your system works, you don't have engineering—you have hope**. Toyota built a system so complex that even NASA couldn't fully analyze it. They violated fundamental systems engineering principles: simplicity, modularity, verifiability."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE SYSTEMS ENGINEERING FAILURES:

1. Unmanageable Complexity
   • 67 tasks on single CPU
   • Global variables shared across functions
   • No memory protection between tasks

2. Inadequate Software Architecture
   • Spaghetti code structure
   • Tight coupling between functions
   • Poor modularity
   • Stack overflow vulnerabilities

3. Insufficient Verification
   • System too complex to fully verify
   • NASA: 94% code coverage, but still couldn't guarantee safety
   • Unable to prove absence of failure modes

4. Requirements Traceability Gap
   • Difficult to trace code to requirements

#### Slide 23: Defining "System" - First Principles
**Visual:** Simple diagram showing elements interconnected

**Instructor Narration:**
> "We've talked about systems engineering without defining 'system.' Let's fix that. What *is* a system?
>
> Here's the formal definition from INCOSE—the International Council on Systems Engineering: **'A system is a combination of interacting elements organized to achieve one or more stated purposes.'**
>
> Let me break that down, because every word matters."

**PPT Content:**
```
WHAT IS A SYSTEM?

INCOSE DEFINITION:
"A system is a combination of interacting elements
organized to achieve one or more stated purposes."

KEY WORDS:
• COMBINATION → Multiple parts
• INTERACTING → Not just collection, but connections
• ELEMENTS → Components, subsystems, people, processes
• ORGANIZED → Structured, not random

#### Slide 24: System Characteristics - What Makes It a System?
**Visual:** Diagram illustrating each characteristic

**Instructor Narration:**
> "A system has specific characteristics that distinguish it from just a random collection of things. Let me give you six key characteristics:
>
> **1. Purpose**: Every system exists to achieve specific objectives. A vehicle's purpose is transportation.
>
> **2. Boundaries**: Systems have defined limits. What's inside the system? What's outside? For a vehicle, is the driver inside or outside the system boundary? It depends on your perspective.
>
> **3. Elements**: The components and subsystems. Engine, transmission, wheels, software, etc.
>
> **4. Interactions**: How elements work together. This is where complexity lives.
>
> **5. Emergent Properties**: The system has properties that individual elements don't have. A wheel can't transport you. An engine can't transport you. But a *vehicle system* can.
>
> **6. Environment**: Systems operate in a context. Weather, roads, regulations, user behavior—all external factors affecting the system.
>
> Understanding these characteristics is fundamental to systems thinking."

   • Reason for existence
   • Measurable outcomes

#### Slide 25: Emergent Properties - The Key Insight
**Visual:** Illustration showing individual parts vs. system behavior

**Instructor Narration:**

#### Slide 28: System Interfaces - Where Failure Lives

#### Slide 29: Introduction to Systems Thinking
**Visual:** Comparison table showing traditional vs systems thinking

**Instructor Narration:**
> "We've covered a lot today. Now I want to introduce you to *systems thinking*—the mindset that underlies all of systems engineering.
>
> **Traditional thinking** focuses on isolated components, linear cause-effect, optimization of parts, and reductionism—break it down to understand it.
>
> **Systems thinking** focuses on relationships and interactions, feedback loops and dynamics, optimization of the whole system, and emergence—understand the whole to understand behavior.
>
> Let me give you examples. Traditional thinking: 'The engine needs more power.' Systems thinking: 'What's the vehicle-level performance requirement? Is more engine power the right solution, or can we reduce weight, improve aerodynamics, or optimize the transmission?'
>
> Traditional: 'This software module crashed.' Systems: 'What interactions with other modules caused the crash? What's the system-level failure mode?'
>
> This is the mindset shift you need to make. From parts to wholes. From isolation to context. From components to systems."

**PPT Content:**
```
SYSTEMS THINKING: The Essential Mindset

TRADITIONAL THINKING vs SYSTEMS THINKING

Focus:
Traditional: Individual components
Systems: Relationships and interactions

Causality:
Traditional: Linear cause → effect
Systems: Feedback loops, circular causality

Optimization:
Traditional: Optimize each part
Systems: Optimize the whole (may sub-optimize parts)

Analysis:
Traditional: Reductionism (break down)
Systems: Holism (understand whole context)

Problem-Solving:
Traditional: Fix the failing component
Systems: Understand system dynamics causing failure

SYSTEMS THINKING PRINCIPLES:

1. The whole is greater than sum of parts
2. Understand relationships, not just elements
3. Consider context and environment
4. Recognize feedback loops and dynamics
5. Expect emergent behaviors
6. Think in hierarchies and levels
7. Anticipate unintended consequences
8. Balance short-term and long-term effects

AUTOMOTIVE EXAMPLE:
Traditional: "Add more airbags for safety"
Systems: "How does the entire safety system (structure, restraints,
ADAS, driver behavior) work together? Where's the optimal investment?"

→ Systems engineering applies systems thinking systematically
```

**Q: "How is SE different from project management?"**
A: PM focuses on resources, schedule, budget. SE focuses on technical content—requirements, architecture, interfaces, verification. They complement each other.

**Q: "Do we really need SE for smaller, simpler systems?"**
A: The rigor scales with complexity. Simple systems need less formal SE, but the thinking and key practices (requirements, interfaces, verification) always apply.
