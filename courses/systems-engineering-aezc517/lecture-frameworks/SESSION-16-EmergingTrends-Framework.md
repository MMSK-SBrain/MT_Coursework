### **RISING ACTION: Exploring the Emerging Trends** (Slides 8-24, ~52 minutes)

#### **Part 1: Digital Engineering and Digital Thread** (Slides 8-11, ~10 minutes)

##### Slide 8: What is Digital Engineering?
**Visual:** Concept diagram showing integrated digital environment
**Instructor Script:**
> "First major trend: **Digital Engineering (DE)**.
>
> **Traditional Systems Engineering:**
> - Information scattered across multiple tools and documents
> - Word specifications, Excel spreadsheets, PowerPoint presentations
> - Manual synchronization between disciplines
> - Data silos between teams
> - Static snapshots, not continuous updates
>
> **Digital Engineering:**
> - **Definition** (per DoD): 'An integrated digital approach that uses authoritative sources of system data and models as a continuum across disciplines to support lifecycle activities from concept through disposal.'
>
> **Key concept: AUTHORITATIVE SOURCE OF TRUTH**
> - Single, integrated digital model of the system
> - All stakeholders access the same data
> - Changes propagate automatically
> - Real-time consistency across all views
>
> **Think of it as:**
> - From document-based to data-based
> - From static to dynamic
> - From siloed to integrated
> - From manual to automated synchronization
>
> This is the infrastructure enabling all other trends we'll discuss."

##### Slide 9: The Digital Thread
**Visual:** Flow diagram showing digital thread connecting lifecycle stages
**Instructor Script:**
> "At the heart of digital engineering is the **Digital Thread**.
>
> **Definition:** A digital thread is the communication framework that connects data flows and integrates information throughout a system's lifecycle across traditionally siloed functional perspectives.
>
> **Automotive Example - Brake System Digital Thread:**
>
> **Requirements Phase:**
> - Stakeholder need: 'Vehicle must stop safely in all conditions'
> - Requirement: 'Braking distance from 100 km/h shall be ≤40m on dry asphalt'
> - *Stored in requirements management tool*
>
> **Design Phase:**
> - Brake system architecture (hydraulic + electronic)
> - CAD models of brake components
> - Control algorithm models
> - *Stored in PLM system, linked to requirements*
>
> **Simulation Phase:**
> - Virtual testing of braking performance
> - Results automatically traced to requirements
> - *Stored in simulation database*
>
> **Manufacturing Phase:**
> - Production specifications derived from design models
> - Quality control parameters
> - *MES system accesses design data*
>
> **Operations Phase:**
> - Brake wear sensors feed data back to digital twin
> - Predictive maintenance algorithms
> - *Fleet data informs future designs*
>
> **THE THREAD:** All of this data is CONNECTED. A change in requirements automatically flags impacted designs, tests, and production specs.
>
> **Benefit:** What took weeks of manual tracking now happens instantly."

##### Slide 10: Digital Twin Technology
**Visual:** Digital twin concept with physical-virtual connection
**Instructor Script:**
> "Digital twins are a key enabler of digital engineering.
>
> **Definition:** A digital twin is a virtual representation of a physical system that is updated with data from the physical system throughout its lifecycle.
>
> **Types of Digital Twins in Automotive:**
>
> **1. Component Digital Twin**
> - Example: Battery pack digital twin
> - Tracks cell degradation, temperature, charge cycles
> - Predicts remaining life, optimal charging strategy
>
> **2. System Digital Twin**
> - Example: Powertrain digital twin
> - Entire electric drivetrain modeled
> - Used for performance optimization, fault diagnosis
>
> **3. Vehicle Digital Twin**
> - Complete virtual representation of individual vehicle
> - Updated with real-time sensor data
> - Tesla creates digital twin of every vehicle produced
>
> **4. Fleet Digital Twin**
> - Aggregated model across entire fleet
> - Identifies patterns, informs future designs
> - Example: BMW analyzes millions of vehicles to improve next generation
>
> **Applications:**
>
> **Development:**
> - Test designs virtually before building prototypes
> - Explore thousands of scenarios in simulation
> - Example: Waymo drives 20+ billion virtual miles annually
>
> **Operations:**
> - Predictive maintenance (fix before failure)
> - Performance optimization (adapt to driving patterns)
> - Remote diagnostics
>
> **Service:**
> - Technicians can query digital twin before touching vehicle
> - Simulate repairs virtually
> - Order correct parts before visit
>
> **This is the future of systems engineering - physical and digital worlds continuously synchronized.**"

#### **Part 2: AI/ML in Systems Engineering** (Slides 12-14, ~8 minutes)

##### Slide 12: AI Augmenting Systems Engineers
**Visual:** Human-AI collaboration diagram
**Instructor Script:**
> "Second major trend: **Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning augmenting systems engineering.**
>
> **Let's be clear:** AI is NOT replacing systems engineers. It's AUGMENTING us, making us more capable.
>
> **Current Applications of AI/ML in SE:**
>
> **1. Requirements Analysis**
> - **Natural Language Processing (NLP)** analyzes requirements documents
> - Detects ambiguity, inconsistency, incompleteness
> - Example: IBM Watson analyzes 10,000 requirements in minutes, flags issues
> - Human SE reviews flagged items, makes decisions
>
> **2. Design Space Exploration**
> - **Generative design algorithms** explore thousands of design alternatives
> - Optimize for multiple objectives (weight, cost, performance, manufacturability)
> - Example: Airbus uses AI to generate aircraft partition designs - 45% lighter than human designs
> - Human SE sets constraints, selects final design
>
> **3. Test Case Generation**
> - **ML algorithms** analyze system models and generate test cases
> - Ensure coverage of edge cases humans might miss
> - Example: Autonomous vehicle testing - AI generates dangerous scenarios
> - Human SE validates test relevance
>
> **4. Fault Prediction and FMEA**
> - **ML models** predict failure modes based on historical data
> - Automate parts of FMEA process
> - Example: Predictive maintenance systems
> - Human SE interprets results, decides mitigations
>
> **5. Project Risk Prediction**
> - **ML analyzes** past project data to predict risks
> - Identifies patterns: 'Projects with X characteristic tend to overrun'
> - Example: NASA uses ML to predict project delays
> - Human SE takes preventive actions
>
> **Pattern:** AI handles data-intensive, pattern-recognition tasks. Humans handle judgment, creativity, ethics, stakeholder engagement.
>
> **We're entering the era of augmented systems engineering.**"

##### Slide 13: AI in Autonomous Systems Development
**Visual:** Workflow showing AI in AV development pipeline
**Instructor Script:**
> "The most intensive use of AI in SE today is in developing autonomous systems themselves.
>
> **Autonomous Vehicle Development - AI Throughout:**
>
> **Perception:**
> - Deep learning for object detection, classification
> - Training on millions of labeled images
> - Continuous learning from fleet data
>
> **Prediction:**
> - ML models predict behavior of other road users
> - 'Will that pedestrian cross? Will that car change lanes?'
>
> **Planning:**
> - Reinforcement learning for path planning
> - Optimize for safety, comfort, efficiency
>
> **Validation:**
> - Scenario generation using generative AI
> - Create edge cases for testing
> - Example: 'Generate 1000 near-collision scenarios'
>
> **Systems Engineering Challenge:**
>
> **Traditional SE:** We specify exact behavior
> - 'If sensor detects object at <5m, brake'
> - Deterministic, traceable, verifiable
>
> **AI/ML Systems:** Behavior is learned, not specified
> - Neural network trained on data
> - Non-deterministic, hard to explain
> - How do we verify? Validate? Certify?
>
> **New SE Methods Required:**
>
> **1. Data-Centric Requirements**
> - Requirements on training data quality, diversity
> - 'Training dataset shall include ≥10,000 pedestrian examples in varied lighting'
>
> **2. Performance Envelope Testing**
> - Define operational design domain (ODD)
> - Test at boundaries: 'Works in conditions A, B, C; not guaranteed in D'
>
> **3. Continuous Validation**
> - Monitor AI performance in field
> - Retrain when degradation detected
>
> **4. Explainable AI (XAI)**
> - Techniques to understand AI decisions
> - 'Why did the system brake there?'
>
> **This is frontier systems engineering. Standards are still being written.**"

##### Slide 14: Ethical Considerations of AI in SE
**Visual:** Ethical framework diagram
**Instructor Script:**
> "AI in systems engineering raises profound ethical questions you must be prepared to address.
>
> **Key Ethical Issues:**
>
> **1. Algorithmic Bias**
> - AI systems inherit biases from training data
> - Example: Facial recognition systems perform worse on darker skin tones
> - **SE Responsibility:** Ensure diverse, representative training data; test for bias
>
> **2. Autonomous Decision-Making in Critical Situations**
> - The 'trolley problem' in autonomous vehicles
> - If crash unavoidable, who should the AI protect?
> - **SE Responsibility:** Make value trade-offs explicit; involve stakeholders in ethical decisions
>
> **3. Accountability**
> - If AI-designed system fails, who is responsible?
> - The AI? The SE who deployed it? The organization?
> - **SE Responsibility:** Maintain human oversight; document AI use; ensure interpretability
>
> **4. Job Displacement**
> - AI automates some SE tasks
> - What happens to engineers whose skills become automated?
> - **SE Responsibility:** Continuous learning; focus on uniquely human skills (judgment, creativity, ethics)
>
> **5. Transparency and Explainability**
> - Stakeholders need to understand system behavior
> - 'Black box' AI is problematic for safety-critical systems
> - **SE Responsibility:** Use explainable AI techniques; maintain interpretability
>
> **Framework for Ethical AI Use in SE:**
> - **Beneficence:** AI should benefit humans
> - **Non-maleficence:** AI should not harm
> - **Autonomy:** Preserve human decision-making authority
> - **Justice:** AI benefits and risks should be fairly distributed
> - **Explicability:** AI decisions should be explainable
>
> **As systems engineers, you are the ethical gatekeepers of AI deployment.**"

#### **Part 3: DevOps for Systems Engineering** (Slides 15-16, ~6 minutes)

##### Slide 15: Extending DevOps Beyond Software
**Visual:** DevOps cycle adapted for systems engineering
**Instructor Script:**
> "Third trend: **DevOps principles applied to systems engineering.**
>
> **DevOps Background (Software Development):**
> - **Traditional:** Developers write code, 'throw over wall' to operations
> - **Result:** Conflicts, delays, finger-pointing
> - **DevOps Solution:** Integrate development and operations
>   - Continuous integration (CI): Merge code frequently, test automatically
>   - Continuous delivery (CD): Deploy frequently, reliably
>   - Automation, monitoring, feedback loops
>
> **Can we apply DevOps to SYSTEMS (hardware + software)?**
>
> **Yes - with adaptations. Emerging concept: DevSecOps for Systems Engineering.**
>
> **DevOps for SE Principles:**
>
> **1. Continuous Integration - System Level**
> - Integrate subsystems frequently (weekly, not at end)
> - Automated testing of integrations
> - Example: BMW integrates vehicle systems weekly in virtual environment
>
> **2. Continuous Verification**
> - Automated testing pipelines for system models
> - Every model change triggers verification suite
> - Example: MBSE models auto-tested against requirements
>
> **3. Continuous Deployment (for software-defined systems)**
> - OTA updates to vehicles in field
> - Tesla deploys software updates every 2-4 weeks
> - Requires robust rollback mechanisms
>
> **4. Shift-Left Security**
> - Cybersecurity testing early in development
> - Automated security scans of system architectures
>
> **5. Infrastructure as Code**
> - Test benches, HIL rigs defined in code
> - Reproducible test environments
>
> **6. Monitoring and Feedback**
> - Fleet data informs development
> - Closes the loop from operations back to engineering
>
> **Key Enabler:** Digital engineering and digital thread provide the data infrastructure for DevOps practices."

##### Slide 16: Case Study - Tesla's DevOps Approach
**Visual:** Tesla's development and deployment pipeline
**Instructor Script:**

>
> **Tesla's Approach:**
>

**Visual:** Comparison of waterfall, agile software, and agile SE

> "Fourth trend: **Agile methodologies extending from software to systems engineering.**
>
> **We discussed Agile SE in Session 9. Let's look at where it's heading.**
>
> **Agile Software Development (Recap):**
> - Iterative, incremental development
> - Short sprints (2-4 weeks)
> - Continuous stakeholder feedback
> - Embrace change
> - Works well for software (easy to iterate)
>
> **Challenge for Systems:**
> - Hardware is expensive to change
> - Long lead times for procurement
> - Safety certification requires upfront rigor
> - How do we apply agile principles?
>
> **Evolution of Agile SE:**
>
> **Phase 1 (2010-2015): Agile for Software in Systems**
> - Software components developed using Agile
> - Hardware still waterfall
> - Integration challenges at interfaces
>
> **Phase 2 (2015-2020): Hybrid Approaches**
> - 'Agile within a waterfall envelope'
> - High-level requirements stable (waterfall)
> - Detailed implementation agile
> - Example: SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework)
>
> **Phase 3 (2020-Present): Model-Based Agile SE**
> - MBSE enables agile for full systems
> - Iterate on models, not hardware
> - Virtual prototyping reduces physical build cycles
> - Hardware defined late, when requirements stable
>
> **Phase 4 (Emerging): Continuous Systems Engineering**
> - Enabled by digital twins, OTA updates
> - System continues evolving after deployment
> - Requirements emerge from field data
> - Lines blur between development and operations
>
> **This is the trajectory: toward continuous, adaptive systems engineering.**"

##### Slide 18: Scaling Agile - SAFe for Automotive
**Visual:** SAFe framework adapted for automotive
**Instructor Script:**
> "For large systems like vehicles, we need **Scaled Agile frameworks**.
>
> **SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework) in Automotive:**
>
> **Team Level (Agile Teams):**
> - 5-9 person teams (software, controls, testing)
> - 2-week sprints
> - Work on features for specific subsystems
> - Example: Adaptive Cruise Control feature team
>
> **Program Level (Agile Release Train - ART):**
> - 50-125 people
> - Develop major system (e.g., ADAS system)
> - Program Increments (PI): 10-12 weeks
> - All teams synchronize to PI cadence
>
> **Portfolio Level:**
> - Multiple ARTs working on vehicle platform
> - Coordinate dependencies
> - Align to strategic themes
>
> **Key Practices:**
>
> **PI Planning:**
> - Every 10-12 weeks, entire program plans together (in-person or virtual)
> - Align on objectives, identify dependencies
> - Builds commitment and coordination
>
> **System Demo:**
> - End of each iteration, integrated system demo
> - Stakeholders see working system (in simulation if hardware not ready)
> - Feedback informs next iteration
>
> **Inspect and Adapt:**
> - After each PI, retrospective
> - Quantitative: measure velocity, quality
> - Qualitative: what worked, what didn't
> - Continuous improvement
>
> **Automotive Companies Using SAFe:**
> - BMW, Ford, GM, Volvo, Continental, Bosch
> - Reporting 20-30% productivity improvements
> - Faster time-to-market, better quality
>
> **Challenges:**
>
> **Safety Certification:**
> - ISO 26262 requires documented evidence
> - How do we maintain traceability in agile?
> - Solution: Continuous documentation, automated traceability
>
> **Hardware Constraints:**
> - Can't iterate physical prototypes every 2 weeks
> - Solution: Shift to virtual prototypes (MBSE, digital twins)
>
> **Supplier Integration:**
> - Tier 1 suppliers may not be agile
> - Interface management critical
> - Solution: Clearly defined interface requirements (stable), internal implementation agile
>
> **Future Direction:** Agile SE is maturing. It's no longer experimental - it's becoming mainstream for automotive."

#### **Part 6: Autonomous Systems Engineering Challenges** (Slides 21-22, ~8 minutes)

##### Slide 21: Unique Challenges of Autonomous Systems
**Visual:** Complexity comparison: traditional vs autonomous systems
**Instructor Script:**
> "Sixth trend: **Engineering autonomous systems - unprecedented challenges.**
>
> **Why Autonomous Systems Are Different:**
>
> **Traditional Automotive System (e.g., ABS):**
> - Defined operating envelope (speed range, road conditions)
> - Deterministic behavior (if X, then Y)
> - Complete specification possible
> - Test all scenarios in controlled environment
> - Certification based on meeting specifications
>
> **Autonomous System (e.g., Self-Driving Car):**
> - Nearly infinite operating envelope (any road, any condition, any scenario)
> - Probabilistic behavior (ML-based perception and decision-making)
> - Complete specification impossible
> - Cannot test all scenarios (combinatorial explosion)
> - Certification framework still being developed
>
> **Specific SE Challenges:**
>
> **1. Requirements Engineering:**
> - **Traditional:** 'System shall brake if obstacle detected at <5m'
> - **Autonomous:** 'System shall safely navigate all plausible driving scenarios'
> - How do you specify 'all plausible scenarios'? (You can't.)
> - **New approach:** Operational Design Domain (ODD) requirements
>   - 'System shall operate safely in conditions: X, Y, Z'
>   - 'System shall detect when outside ODD and transition to safe state'
>
> **2. Verification & Validation:**
> - **Traditional:** Test against specifications
> - **Autonomous:** No complete specification exists
> - **New approach:**
>   - Scenario-based testing (millions of scenarios)
>   - Statistical validation (confidence intervals)
>   - Field testing (real-world miles)
>   - Continuous monitoring (after deployment)
> - Example: Waymo requires billions of virtual miles + millions of real miles
>
> **3. Safety Assurance:**
> - **Traditional:** Failure rate targets (e.g., <10 FIT)
> - **Autonomous:** How do we prove self-driving is safer than human?
> - **New approach:**
>   - Safety cases (structured argument)
>   - Dissimilar redundancy (different sensors, algorithms)
>   - Human oversight / takeover capability
> - ISO 21448 (SOTIF - Safety Of The Intended Functionality) addresses this
>
> **4. Ethical Decision-Making:**
> - **Traditional:** Engineer makes all decisions
> - **Autonomous:** System makes real-time ethical decisions
> - Example: Unavoidable collision - who should AI protect?
> - **New approach:** Make value trade-offs explicit, involve society
>
> **This is the frontier of systems engineering. Standards are being written NOW.**"

##### Slide 22: SAE Levels of Autonomy and SE Complexity
**Visual:** SAE J3016 levels with SE complexity overlay
**Instructor Script:**
> "Let's connect autonomous systems to the SAE levels framework.
>
> **SAE J3016 Levels of Driving Automation:**
>
> **Level 0 - No Automation**
> - Human does everything
> - SE Complexity: LOW
> - Example: 1990s vehicle
>
> **Level 1 - Driver Assistance**
> - System assists with EITHER steering OR acceleration/braking
> - Human monitors and controls
> - SE Complexity: MODERATE
> - Examples: Adaptive Cruise Control, Lane Keeping Assist
> - Challenges: Human-machine interface, mode confusion
>
> **Level 2 - Partial Automation**
> - System controls BOTH steering AND acceleration/braking simultaneously
> - Human monitors, ready to take over
> - SE Complexity: HIGH
> - Examples: Tesla Autopilot, GM Super Cruise
> - Challenges: Driver attention monitoring, safe handoff
>
> **Level 3 - Conditional Automation**
> - System drives in specific conditions
> - Human must be ready to take over when requested
> - SE Complexity: VERY HIGH
> - Example: Mercedes Drive Pilot (traffic jam pilot)
> - Challenges: Defining ODD precisely, safe takeover request mechanism
>
> **Level 4 - High Automation**
> - System drives in specific conditions
> - NO human intervention needed within ODD
> - System handles takeover (e.g., pull over safely)
> - SE Complexity: EXTREME
> - Examples: Waymo, Cruise robotaxis (limited geographic areas)
> - Challenges: Handle all scenarios within ODD, safe degradation
>
> **Level 5 - Full Automation**
> - System drives everywhere, all conditions
> - No human driver needed
> - SE Complexity: CURRENTLY IMPOSSIBLE?
> - Example: None in production
> - Challenges: 'All conditions' is unbounded, cannot verify
>
> **SE Complexity Drivers:**
> - **Perception:** L0-L2 use simple sensors. L3-L5 need sensor fusion (camera, radar, lidar), ML perception
> - **Decision-Making:** L0-L2 simple rules. L3-L5 complex planning, prediction of other agents
> - **Validation:** L0-L2 traditional testing. L3-L5 require billions of test miles (virtual + real)
> - **Safety:** L0-L2 fail to safe state (human takes over). L3-L5 must handle failures autonomously
>
> **Industry Reality:**
> - Most production systems: L2 (human in the loop)
> - Few L3 systems (Mercedes, Honda in limited conditions)
> - L4 robotaxis in limited areas (Waymo, Cruise)
> - L5 is research, not near-term production
>
> **Your role as future SE:** Engineering L3-L5 autonomy is the grand challenge of our generation."

## 🎯 INSTRUCTOR NOTES

### Pedagogical Strategy:
- **Future-oriented mindset**: Prepare students for rapid change
- **Practical focus**: These trends are happening NOW, not theoretical
- **Balanced optimism and realism**: Trends are exciting but have barriers
- **Empowerment**: Students can be ahead of curve by learning now
- **Ethical grounding**: With new capabilities come new responsibilities

### Common Student Challenges:

**Challenge 1: "These trends seem overwhelming - how can I learn all this?"**
**How to address:** You don't need to master everything. Develop T-shaped skills (deep in 1-2 areas, broad awareness of rest). Focus on fundamentals (SE principles don't change), then layer on emerging tools/methods. Show Slide 26 - actionable learning path.

**Challenge 2: "Will AI replace systems engineers?"**
**How to address:** AI augments, not replaces. Show Slide 12 - AI handles data-intensive tasks, humans handle judgment, creativity, ethics, stakeholder engagement. Emphasize uniquely human skills. Historical precedent: CAD didn't replace engineers, it made us more productive.

**Challenge 3: "My future employer might not use these new methods"**
**How to address:** True, adoption varies. But: (1) Knowledge helps you recognize when new methods are needed, (2) You can be change agent, (3) Over career, you'll likely work for multiple companies - pick those embracing innovation. Leaders adopting these trends are outperforming laggards.

**Challenge 4: "How do I stay current when technology changes so fast?"**
**How to address:** Develop continuous learning habits (Slide 26). Follow industry news, take online courses, join professional societies (INCOSE), attend conferences. Dedicate 2-3 hours/week to learning. Career is 40+ years - staying current is not optional.

**Challenge 5: "These trends seem to conflict - Agile vs. safety rigor, speed vs. thoroughness"**
**How to address:** This is the art of SE - balancing trade-offs. Agile and rigor can coexist (Slide 18 - SAFe framework). Speed comes from automation and virtual testing, not cutting corners. Show how trends complement each other (Slide 25).
