>
> **'Systems engineering is a transdisciplinary and integrative approach to enable the successful realization, use, and retirement of engineered systems, using systems principles and concepts, and scientific, technological, and management methods.'**

### **RISING ACTION: Building Understanding** (Slides 9-24, ~50 minutes)

#### **Part 1: Systems Thinking - The Mindset** (Slides 9-13, ~12 minutes)

##### Slide 9: Traditional vs Systems Thinking
**Visual:** Side-by-side comparison table
**Instructor Script:**
> "To practice systems engineering, you must first learn to THINK like a systems engineer. Let me show you the difference.
>
> **Traditional Engineering Thinking**:
> - Focus on components
> - Optimize individual parts
> - Linear cause-effect
> - Discipline-specific
>
> **Systems Thinking**:
> - Focus on relationships between components
> - Optimize the whole system
> - Recognize feedback loops and emergent behavior
> - Cross-disciplinary integration
>
> Example: Traditional engineer makes the best possible engine. Systems engineer asks: 'What engine characteristics serve the OVERALL vehicle performance, cost, and user needs?'"

##### Slide 10: The Seven Principles of Systems Thinking
**Visual:** Circular diagram with 7 principles
**Instructor Script:**
> "Systems thinking rests on seven core principles:
>
> 1. **Holistic thinking**: See the forest AND the trees
> 2. **Hierarchy**: Systems contain subsystems; systems exist in super-systems
> 3. **Emergence**: System behavior isn't just sum of parts (2+2=5)
> 4. **Boundaries**: Define what's IN the system vs the environment
> 5. **Interfaces**: Critical connections between elements
> 6. **Context**: Systems exist in specific operational environments
> 7. **Trade-offs**: Optimize for system goals, not local optima
>
> Let me give you real examples of each."

##### Slide 11: Example - Emergence
**Visual:** Diagram showing individual components vs emergent system behavior
**Instructor Script:**
> "**Emergence** is critical. Consider an automobile:
>
> - Individual component: Tire (provides friction)
> - Individual component: Engine (generates torque)
> - Individual component: Steering (changes direction)
> - Individual component: Brake (provides deceleration)
>
> **Emergent system behavior**: Vehicle dynamics, handling characteristics, driver experience
>
> You CANNOT predict handling characteristics by analyzing tires alone. It emerges from the INTERACTION of suspension, steering, tires, weight distribution, and more.
>
> This is why systems engineering matters. We manage these emergent properties."

##### Slide 12: Example - Boundaries and Context
**Visual:** System boundary diagram for automotive system
**Instructor Script:**
> "**Boundaries** define scope. Consider designing a car:
>
> **What's IN the system?**
> - Vehicle hardware and software
> - Onboard diagnostics
> - User interfaces
>
> **What's OUTSIDE but interfaces?**
> - Roads and infrastructure
> - Traffic regulations
> - Maintenance facilities
> - Fuel supply chain
> - Other vehicles
> - Weather conditions
>
> **Why does this matter?** Because you design INTERFACES for external elements, but you don't control them. You must account for their variability.
>
> Wrong boundary definition = missed requirements = failure."

##### Slide 13: Systems Thinking in Practice
**Visual:** Case study example
**Instructor Script:**
> "Quick example: You're designing an electric vehicle charging system.
>
> **Component thinker** says: 'Maximize charging speed.'
>
> **Systems thinker** asks:
> - How does fast charging affect battery life (system performance over time)?
> - What's the grid infrastructure impact (context)?
> - How does it affect user wait times and experience (stakeholder needs)?
> - What's the cost-performance trade-off for different use cases?
> - How does this interface with home charging, public charging, fleet charging?
>
> See the difference? Systems thinking expands the problem space to find better solutions."

#### **Part 2: ISO/IEC 15288 - The Standard Framework** (Slides 14-17, ~10 minutes)

##### Slide 14: Why Standards Matter
**Visual:** Global map showing ISO adoption
**Instructor Script:**
> "Before I introduce the framework, let me explain why standards matter in systems engineering.
>
> Imagine you're Boeing collaborating with suppliers in 50 countries. You need a COMMON LANGUAGE for:
> - What processes to follow
> - What artifacts to produce
> - How to verify requirements
> - How to manage interfaces
>
> **ISO/IEC 15288** is that common language. It's the international standard for system life cycle processes.
>
> Published jointly by ISO (International Organization for Standardization) and IEC (International Electrotechnical Commission), it's used worldwide in aerospace, automotive, defense, medical devices, and more."

##### Slide 15: ISO/IEC 15288 Process Groups
**Visual:** Four-quadrant diagram
**Instructor Script:**
> "ISO/IEC 15288 organizes all systems engineering activities into FOUR process groups:
>
> **1. Agreement Processes** (with customers, suppliers)
> - Acquisition
> - Supply
>
> **2. Organizational Project-Enabling Processes** (infrastructure, resources)
> - Life cycle model management
> - Infrastructure management
> - Portfolio management
> - Human resource management
> - Quality management
>
> **3. Technical Management Processes** (planning, controlling)
> - Project planning
> - Project assessment and control
> - Decision management
> - Risk management
> - Configuration management
> - Information management
>
> **4. Technical Processes** (the actual engineering)
> - Stakeholder needs and requirements definition
> - Requirements analysis
> - Architecture design
> - Implementation
> - Integration
> - Verification
> - Validation
> - Operation
> - Maintenance
> - Disposal
>
> Today, we'll focus on Technical Processes - the core engineering activities."

##### Slide 16: Technical Processes - The Engineering Core
**Visual:** Flow diagram of technical processes
**Instructor Script:**
> "Let me walk you through the technical process flow:
>
> **1. Stakeholder Needs → Requirements Definition**
> - Who are the stakeholders?
> - What do they need?
> - Convert needs to verifiable requirements
>
> **2. Requirements Analysis**
> - Analyze feasibility
> - Allocate to subsystems
> - Define performance criteria
>
> **3. Architecture Design**
> - Define system structure
> - Design interfaces
> - Allocate functions
>
> **4. Implementation**
> - Build/code/fabricate components
>
> **5. Integration**
> - Combine components into subsystems
> - Combine subsystems into system
>
> **6. Verification**
> - Did we build it right?
> - Test against requirements
>
> **7. Validation**
> - Did we build the right thing?
> - Test against stakeholder needs
>
> **8. Operation → Maintenance → Disposal**
> - Use in field
> - Support and maintain
> - Retire safely
>
> Notice: It's NOT linear. These processes iterate and interact."

##### Slide 17: Why This Framework Matters
**Visual:** Before/after comparison - chaos vs structured process
**Instructor Script:**
> "You might think: 'This seems bureaucratic. Why not just build it?'
>
> Here's why it matters:
>
> **Without framework**:
> - Ad-hoc processes
> - Missed requirements
> - Poor traceability
> - Integration nightmares
> - Late-stage rework
>
> **With ISO/IEC 15288**:
> - Systematic approach
> - Nothing falls through cracks
> - Clear accountability
> - Early issue detection
> - Predictable outcomes
>
> It's the difference between amateur and professional engineering.
>
> We'll dive deep into specific processes in upcoming sessions. Today is about seeing the big picture."

#### **Part 3: System Life Cycle Stages** (Slides 18-20, ~8 minutes)

##### Slide 18: The Life Cycle Concept
**Visual:** Timeline showing birth to retirement
**Instructor Script:**
> "Systems aren't just designed and built. They have a LIFE CYCLE - from conception to disposal.
>
> Think of it like a living organism: birth, growth, maturity, aging, death.
>
> ISO/IEC 15288 defines distinct stages. Understanding these stages helps you:
> - Plan activities appropriately
> - Allocate resources
> - Manage transitions
> - Anticipate needs
>
> Let me show you the stages."

##### Slide 19: The Six Life Cycle Stages
**Visual:** Timeline with 6 stages illustrated
**Instructor Script:**
> "**Stage 1: Concept**
> - Identify need or opportunity
> - Feasibility analysis
> - Business case development
> - Key question: 'Should we do this?'
>
> **Stage 2: Development**
> - Requirements definition
> - Design and architecture
> - Implementation
> - Verification and validation
> - Key question: 'Can we build this?'
>
> **Stage 3: Production**
> - Manufacturing/coding at scale
> - Quality assurance
> - Delivery preparation
> - Key question: 'Can we make many of these reliably?'
>
> **Stage 4: Utilization**
> - Deployment
> - Operation in field
> - User support
> - Performance monitoring
> - Key question: 'Does it deliver value?'
>
> **Stage 5: Support**
> - Maintenance
> - Upgrades and modifications
> - Spare parts management
> - Training
> - Key question: 'How do we keep it working?'
>
> **Stage 6: Retirement**
> - Decommissioning
> - Disposal or recycling
> - Data archival
> - Lessons learned
> - Key question: 'How do we end responsibly?'
>
> Different stages require different processes, skills, and resources."

##### Slide 20: Life Cycle Example - Commercial Aircraft
**Visual:** Boeing 787 life cycle timeline with real numbers
**Instructor Script:**
> "Real example: Boeing 787 Dreamliner
>
> - **Concept** (2003-2004): Market research, business case, airline input
> - **Development** (2004-2011): Design, prototyping, testing (7 years!)
> - **Production** (2011-present): Manufacturing ramp-up, continuous production
> - **Utilization** (2011-present): Airlines operating ~1,000 aircraft
> - **Support** (ongoing): Maintenance, software updates, fleet monitoring
> - **Retirement** (future, 2040s+): Will require decommissioning processes
>
> Notice:
> - Development took 7 years
> - Production and utilization overlap
> - Support spans 20-30+ years
> - Total life cycle: 40+ years
>
> **Life cycle cost**: Development (~$32B), but total cost of ownership for airlines is FAR higher when you include operation, maintenance, fuel, crew, etc.
>
> Systems engineers must think across ALL stages, not just development."

#### **Part 4: Stakeholder Engagement** (Slides 21-22, ~6 minutes)

##### Slide 21: Who Are Stakeholders?
**Visual:** Network diagram showing different stakeholder types
**Instructor Script:**
> "Here's a critical truth: **A system is only successful if it satisfies its stakeholders.**
>
> But who ARE the stakeholders? It's not just the customer.
>
> **Stakeholder categories**:
> - **Users**: People who operate the system
> - **Customers**: People who buy/commission the system
> - **Acquirers**: Organizations that fund the system
> - **Operators**: People who manage system operations
> - **Maintainers**: People who support the system
> - **Developers**: Engineers building the system
> - **Suppliers**: Provide components or services
> - **Regulators**: Ensure compliance with laws/standards
> - **Society**: Affected by system externalities (safety, environment)
>
> Each stakeholder group has DIFFERENT needs, often conflicting.
>
> Example: Car buyers want low price. Regulators want high safety. Users want performance. Maintainers want serviceability. Manufacturers want profitability.
>
> Systems engineering balances these competing needs."

##### Slide 22: Stakeholder Engagement Process
**Visual:** Process flow diagram
**Instructor Script:**
> "How do we engage stakeholders systematically?
>
> **Step 1: Identify stakeholders**
> - Who will be affected?
> - Who has influence?
> - Who has valuable input?
>
> **Step 2: Analyze stakeholder needs**
> - What are their goals?
> - What constraints do they impose?
> - What measures of success do they use?
>
> **Step 3: Prioritize and negotiate**
> - Which needs are essential vs desirable?
> - Where are the conflicts?
> - What trade-offs are acceptable?
>
> **Step 4: Document requirements**
> - Convert needs to verifiable requirements
> - Establish traceability
> - Get stakeholder agreement
>
> **Step 5: Maintain engagement**
> - Regular reviews
> - Change management
> - Validation throughout life cycle
>
> This is iterative. Stakeholder needs evolve, and so must the system.
>
> In Session 4, we'll deep-dive into requirements engineering. Today, just understand: stakeholders are at the HEART of systems engineering."

#### **Part 5: The V-Model** (Slides 23-24, ~14 minutes)

##### Slide 23: Introducing the V-Model
**Visual:** Classic V-model diagram
**Instructor Script:**
> "Now I want to show you the most widely used model for understanding system development: **The V-Model**.
>
> It's called the V-Model because... well, it looks like the letter V.
>
> But it's much more than a visual. It represents the fundamental relationship between:
> - **Left side**: Decomposition (breaking down requirements into design)
> - **Right side**: Integration and verification (building up and testing)
>
> The key insight: **Each decomposition step on the left has a corresponding verification step on the right.**
>
> Let me walk you through it."

##### Slide 24: The V-Model in Detail
**Visual:** Annotated V-model with all levels
**Instructor Script:**
> "**LEFT SIDE - Decomposition and Definition:**
>
> **Top**: Stakeholder Needs & Requirements
> - What do stakeholders actually need?
> - Operational concepts
>
> ↓
>
> **System Requirements**
> - System-level functional and performance requirements
> - System boundaries and interfaces
>
> ↓
>
> **System Architecture**
> - Allocation to subsystems
> - Interface definitions
> - Functional architecture
>
> ↓
>
> **Subsystem/Component Design**
> - Detailed design specifications
> - Component-level requirements
>
> ↓
>
> **BOTTOM - Implementation**
> - Build components
> - Write code
> - Fabricate hardware
>
> **RIGHT SIDE - Integration and Verification:**
>
> ↑
>
> **Component Verification**
> - Unit testing
> - Component-level testing
> - 'Does this component meet its specs?'
>
> ↑
>
> **Subsystem Verification**
> - Integration testing
> - Interface testing
> - 'Do components work together?'
>
> ↑
>
> **System Verification**
> - System-level testing
> - Performance testing
> - 'Does the system meet requirements?'
>
> ↑
>
> **TOP - System Validation**
> - Acceptance testing
> - Operational testing
> - 'Does it solve the stakeholder's problem?'
>
> **The magic of the V**: Notice the horizontal connections. Each level on the left defines WHAT to test on the right.
>
> - Stakeholder needs → Validation test cases
> - System requirements → System verification test cases
> - Architecture → Integration test cases
> - Component design → Unit test cases
>
> This ensures **traceability** from needs to tests."

##### Slide 25: Why the V-Model Works
**Visual:** Comparison of V-model vs ad-hoc approach
**Instructor Script:**
> "Why is the V-Model so powerful?
>
> **1. Traceability**: Every requirement is verified
>
> **2. Early test planning**: You define tests BEFORE implementation, not after
>
> **3. Systematic integration**: Build from components → subsystems → system
>
> **4. Risk reduction**: Catch errors early at component level, not late at system level
>
> **5. Clear milestones**: Each verification step is a measurable milestone
>
> **Real example**: Consider Toyota's unintended acceleration problem.
>
> A proper V-Model approach would have:
> - **Left**: Defined safety requirements for throttle control at system level
> - **Right**: Created test cases for those safety requirements
> - **Verification**: Tested failure modes, error handling, sensor validation
>
> They likely HAD requirements, but the verification on the right side was insufficient. The V-Model forces you to think about testing AS you define requirements."

##### Slide 26: V-Model Variations
**Visual:** Side-by-side comparison of different V-model variants
**Instructor Script:**
> "The V-Model has evolved. You'll see variations:
>
> **Classic V-Model**:
> - Sequential phases
> - Works for stable requirements
> - Used in safety-critical systems (aerospace, medical devices)
>
> **Iterative V-Model**:
> - Multiple V cycles
> - Each iteration adds functionality
> - Common in automotive (incremental feature delivery)
>
> **Agile V-Model**:
> - Rapid iterations
> - Continuous integration and testing
> - Popular in software-intensive systems
>
> **Key point**: The PRINCIPLE stays the same - decompose, implement, integrate, verify. The TIME SCALE changes.
>
> In Session 3 (Traditional vs MBSE), we'll explore how model-based approaches implement the V-Model.
> In Session 8 (Integration, Verification & Validation), we'll dive deep into the right side of the V."

## 🎯 INSTRUCTOR NOTES

### Pedagogical Strategy:
- **Conceptual scaffolding**: Build from definition → thinking → processes → execution
- **Concrete examples**: Every abstract concept gets a real-world example
- **Connection to Session 1**: Explicitly show how SE prevents the failures discussed
- **Forward-looking**: Preview upcoming sessions to show progression
- **Frameworks over details**: Focus on understanding the BIG PICTURE today

### Common Student Challenges:
- **Abstract concepts**: Systems thinking is counterintuitive for component-focused engineers
  - *Solution*: Use concrete examples (Slides 11-13)
- **Process overload**: ISO/IEC 15288 has many processes
  - *Solution*: Focus on technical processes today, others come later
- **V-Model confusion**: Students confuse verification with validation
  - *Solution*: Emphasize "built it right" vs "built right thing" (Slide 24)

### Timing Notes:
- **V-Model section (Slides 23-26)**: Don't rush - allocate full 14 minutes
