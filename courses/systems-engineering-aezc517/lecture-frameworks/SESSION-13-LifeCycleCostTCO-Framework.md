#### Slide 6: The Systems Engineering Cost Principle
**Visual:** Timeline showing cost commitment vs. cost incurred

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before we dive in, understand this fundamental principle:
>
> **70-80% of life cycle costs are COMMITTED during the first 20% of the design phase.**
>
> Look at this graph. Early in the project - requirements, conceptual design - you're spending very little money. But you're COMMITTING enormous costs downstream.
>
> When you choose an architecture, you lock in manufacturing complexity. When you select components, you commit to maintenance costs. When you define interfaces, you determine integration costs.
>
> **Once you pass preliminary design review, 80% of costs are locked in. But you've only spent 5-10% of the budget.**
>
> This is why systems engineers must think about costs from day one. By the time you're in production, it's too late to fundamentally change life cycle costs.
>
> **Early decisions have lifetime consequences.**"

**PPT Content:**
```
THE CRITICAL SE COST PRINCIPLE

[Graph showing two curves over project lifecycle]

COST COMMITTED (Blue curve - rises sharply early):
• Requirements: 20% committed
• Conceptual Design: 50% committed
• Preliminary Design: 80% committed
• Detailed Design: 95% committed
• Production: 100% committed (locked in)

COST INCURRED (Red curve - rises late):
• Requirements: 1% spent
• Conceptual Design: 5% spent
• Preliminary Design: 10% spent
• Detailed Design: 20% spent
• Production: 75% spent

KEY INSIGHT:
When you've spent 10% of budget, you've committed 80% of life cycle costs

IMPLICATION FOR SE:
Life cycle cost analysis must happen EARLY in design process,
when you still have freedom to make impactful changes

"The time to think about cost is when you can still do something about it."

#### Slide 7: Defining Life Cycle Cost (LCC)
**Visual:** Life cycle stages with cost elements

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's define our terms precisely.
>
> **Life Cycle Cost (LCC):** The total cost of a system throughout its entire life cycle, from conception to disposal.
>
> **Total Cost of Ownership (TCO):** The total cost to acquire, operate, and dispose of a system from the OWNER'S perspective.
>
> **LCC is broader - includes manufacturer costs. TCO focuses on customer/operator costs.**
>
> The system life cycle includes:
> 1. **Conception and Development:** Research, design, testing, certification
> 2. **Production and Construction:** Manufacturing, tooling, quality control
> 3. **Operation and Support:** Use, maintenance, repair, training
> 4. **Retirement and Disposal:** Decommissioning, recycling, waste management
>
> **Every stage has costs. Systems engineers must consider ALL of them.**"

**PPT Content:**

   • Prototyping and testing
   • Certification and compliance

2. PRODUCTION & CONSTRUCTION
   • Manufacturing tooling
   • Component production

3. OPERATION & SUPPORT
   • Energy/fuel consumption

#### Slide 9: LCC Formula and Modeling
**Visual:** Mathematical representation of LCC

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's formalize this mathematically. The basic LCC equation is:
>
> **LCC = C_RDT + C_Prod + C_Ops + C_Disposal**
>
> Where:
> - C_RDT = Research, Development, Test & Evaluation
> - C_Prod = Production and Manufacturing
> - C_Ops = Operation and Support
> - C_Disposal = End-of-Life and Disposal
>
> But we need to account for TIME. A dollar today is not worth the same as a dollar in 10 years. So we use Net Present Value (NPV):
>
> **LCC = Σ(C_t / (1+r)^t)**
>
> Where:
> - C_t = Cost in year t
> - r = Discount rate (typically 3-8% for automotive)
> - t = Time period
>
> **Example:**
> A maintenance cost of $1,000 in year 5, with 5% discount rate:
> NPV = $1,000 / (1.05)^5 = $784
>
> **This is critical:** Far-future costs are discounted. This can lead to undervaluing long-term consequences. As systems engineers, be aware of this bias."

**PPT Content:**
```
LCC MATHEMATICAL MODELING

BASIC LCC EQUATION:
LCC = C_RDT + C_Prod + C_Ops + C_Disposal

NET PRESENT VALUE FORMULATION:
LCC = Σ(C_t / (1+r)^t) for t = 0 to n

Where:
• C_t = Cost in time period t
• r = Discount rate (time value of money)
• t = Time period (years)
• n = System lifetime

DISCOUNT RATE SELECTION:
• Corporate: 8-12% (reflects opportunity cost of capital)
• Government: 3-7% (social discount rate)
• Customer: 5-10% (personal finance rate)

EXAMPLE CALCULATION:
System with 10-year life, 6% discount rate

Year 0 (Development): $1,000,000 → NPV = $1,000,000
Year 1 (Production): $500,000 → NPV = $471,698
Year 2 (Production): $500,000 → NPV = $445,094
Year 3-10 (Operation): $100,000/yr → NPV = $590,169
Year 10 (Disposal): $50,000 → NPV = $27,919

TOTAL LCC (NPV) = $2,534,880

KEY INSIGHT:
Higher discount rates favor low upfront costs
Lower discount rates favor low operating costs
```

#### Slide 11: Cost vs. Value - Important Distinction
**Visual:** Cost-value matrix

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before we go further, distinguish between COST and VALUE.
>
> **Cost:** Resources consumed (money, time, materials)
> **Value:** Benefit delivered to stakeholders
>
> **The goal is NOT minimum cost. The goal is MAXIMUM value.**
>
> Example: Safety features add cost but deliver enormous value (lives saved, liability avoided).
>
> **Value Engineering:** Systematic approach to improving value by:
> - Reducing costs without reducing performance
> - Increasing performance without increasing costs
> - Reducing costs WHILE increasing performance (ideal)
>
> **Four Scenarios:**
>
> | Cost | Performance | Value | Example |
> |------|-------------|-------|---------|
> | Low | Low | Poor | Cheap components that fail frequently |
> | High | High | Good | Premium features customers value |
> | Low | High | **Excellent** | **Engineering innovation** |
> | High | Low | Poor | Over-engineered, gold-plating |
>
> **As systems engineers, you seek the third quadrant: Low cost, high performance.**
>
> This requires creativity, systems thinking, and life cycle perspective."

**PPT Content:**
```
COST vs. VALUE

DEFINITIONS:
• Cost = Resources consumed (inputs)
• Value = Benefits delivered (outputs)
• Value = Performance / Cost

GOAL: Maximize Value (NOT minimize cost)

VALUE ENGINEERING APPROACHES:

1. REDUCE COST, MAINTAIN PERFORMANCE
   • Standardization (fewer unique parts)
   • Design for manufacturing
   • Material substitution

2. INCREASE PERFORMANCE, MAINTAIN COST
   • Innovation and technology
   • Systems integration
   • Better requirements

3. REDUCE COST, INCREASE PERFORMANCE
   • This is engineering excellence
   • Requires deep systems thinking
   • Example: Electric powertrains (fewer parts, better performance)

4. AVOID: INCREASE COST, REDUCE PERFORMANCE
   • Over-complexity
   • Gold-plating
   • Poor requirements

AUTOMOTIVE EXAMPLES:

LOW COST, HIGH VALUE:
• LED lighting (lower power, longer life, better visibility)
• Turbocharging (smaller engine, better efficiency and power)
• Over-the-air updates (eliminate recall costs, improve functionality)

HIGH COST, HIGH VALUE:
• Advanced driver assistance systems (safety value justifies cost)
• Battery thermal management (extends battery life significantly)

"The cheapest system is not always the best. Optimize for life cycle value."
```

#### Slide 13: TCO Model - Consumer Vehicle
**Visual:** TCO breakdown for typical passenger vehicle

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's build a TCO model for a typical consumer vehicle. Assume:
> - Mid-size sedan, $35,000 purchase price
> - 10-year ownership, 15,000 miles/year
> - Gas price $3.50/gallon, 30 mpg average
>
> **TCO Calculation:**
>
> **1. Depreciation:** $24,500 (vehicle worth ~$10,500 after 10 years)
> **2. Fuel:** $17,500 (150,000 miles ÷ 30 mpg × $3.50/gal)
> **3. Insurance:** $15,000 ($1,500/year average)
> **4. Maintenance & Repairs:** $10,000 (increases with age)
> **5. Financing:** $6,000 (5-year loan, 6% APR on $30,000)
> **6. Taxes & Fees:** $3,000 ($300/year registration + initial sales tax)
>
> **Total 10-Year TCO: $76,000**
>
> **Breakdown:**
> - Depreciation: 32% of TCO
> - Fuel: 23% of TCO
> - Insurance: 20% of TCO
> - Maintenance: 13% of TCO
> - Financing: 8% of TCO
> - Taxes/Fees: 4% of TCO
>
> **Key insight:** Fuel and depreciation together are 55% of TCO. Engineering can affect both significantly."

**PPT Content:**
```
TCO MODEL: CONSUMER VEHICLE

ASSUMPTIONS:
• Vehicle: Mid-size sedan
• Purchase Price: $35,000
• Ownership: 10 years, 150,000 miles
• Fuel: Gasoline, $3.50/gal, 30 mpg
• Insurance: $1,500/year
• Financing: 60-month loan, 6% APR, 20% down

TCO BREAKDOWN (10 years):

1. DEPRECIATION: $24,500 (32%)
   • Year 1: -20% ($7,000)
   • Years 2-5: -10% average/year
   • Years 6-10: -5% average/year
   • Residual value: $10,500

2. FUEL: $17,500 (23%)
   • 15,000 miles/year ÷ 30 mpg = 500 gal/year
   • 500 gal × $3.50 × 10 years = $17,500

3. INSURANCE: $15,000 (20%)
   • $1,500/year × 10 years

4. MAINTENANCE & REPAIRS: $10,000 (13%)
   • Years 1-3: $500/year (basic maintenance)
   • Years 4-7: $1,000/year (wear items)
   • Years 8-10: $2,000/year (major repairs)

5. FINANCING: $6,000 (8%)
   • $28,000 loan @ 6% APR, 60 months
   • Total interest paid

6. TAXES & FEES: $3,000 (4%)
   • Initial sales tax: $2,000
   • Registration: $200/year × 10 = $2,000

#### Slide 19: Engineering Decisions That Drive LCC
**Visual:** Decision tree showing design choices and cost impacts

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Every engineering decision you make affects life cycle costs. Let me show you the most impactful decisions:
>
> **1. Architecture Selection**

> - Number of components and complexity
> - Example: Tesla's battery pack architecture reduces manufacturing cost by 30%
>
> **2. Component Selection**
> - Cost vs. reliability trade-off
> - Cheap components have high failure rates → high warranty costs
> - Example: Choosing premium capacitors adds $5 but saves $500 in warranty
>

>
> **5. Maintenance Access**
> - Design for serviceability
> - Example: Easy access to battery pack reduces labor cost from $2,000 to $500
>
> **6. Failure Modes and Reliability**

>
> **7. Software vs. Hardware Solutions**
> - Software has near-zero marginal cost but high development cost
> - Example: OTA updates replace recall campaigns (saves millions)
>
> **8. Standardization and Commonality**

#### Slide 20: Design for Cost (DFC) Principles
**Visual:** DFC framework diagram

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Design for Cost is a systematic approach to minimizing LCC. Here are the key principles:
>
> **1. Simplicity**
> - Fewer parts = lower cost (design, manufacturing, inventory, service)
> - Example: Tesla Model Y rear casting replaces 70 parts with 1 → saves $600/vehicle
>
> **2. Standardization**
> - Use common components across products
> - Reduces tooling, inventory, training costs
> - Example: Volkswagen MQB platform shares 60% of parts across 10 models
>
> **3. Design for Manufacturing (DFM)**
> - Easy to manufacture = lower labor and scrap costs
> - Minimize unique processes and tooling
> - Example: Snap-fit vs. fasteners saves $5/assembly
>
> **4. Design for Assembly (DFA)**
> - Reduce assembly time and complexity
> - Mistake-proof designs (poka-yoke)
> - Example: Reducing assembly time by 1 minute saves $1/vehicle at scale
>
> **5. Design for Serviceability (DFS)**
> - Easy access to wear items
> - Modular replacement vs. complete assembly
> - Example: Replaceable vs. sealed battery modules
>
> **6. Design for Reliability**
> - Prevent failures at source (cheaper than fixing later)
> - Use proven components and de-rating
> - Example: 50% de-rating on voltage extends life 10x
>
> **7. Design for Testability**
> - Built-in diagnostics reduce troubleshooting time
> - Example: OBD-II codes cut diagnostic time from hours to minutes
>
> **8. Design for End-of-Life**
> - Easy disassembly for recycling
> - Valuable material recovery
> - Example: Aluminum recovery pays for disposal
>
> **All of these are systems engineering practices that drive down LCC.**"

**PPT Content:**
```
DESIGN FOR COST (DFC) PRINCIPLES

1. SIMPLICITY
   ✓ Minimize part count
   ✓ Reduce design complexity
   ✓ Eliminate non-value-added features
   Example: Reduce assembly from 200 parts to 50 → -40% assembly cost

2. STANDARDIZATION
   ✓ Common parts across product lines
   ✓ Use industry-standard components
   ✓ Platform strategies
   Example: VW MQB platform saves $1,500/vehicle through commonality

3. DESIGN FOR MANUFACTURING (DFM)
   ✓ Minimize manufacturing steps
   ✓ Use standard processes
   ✓ Reduce tight tolerances where possible
   ✓ Design for automated assembly
   Example: Redesign to eliminate welding step → saves $25/vehicle

4. DESIGN FOR ASSEMBLY (DFA)
   ✓ Reduce assembly time
   ✓ Top-down assembly
   ✓ Self-locating/aligning parts
   ✓ Minimize fasteners
   Example: Snap-fit vs. screws: -30 seconds/assembly = $0.50 savings

5. DESIGN FOR SERVICEABILITY (DFS)
   ✓ Easy access to serviceable parts
   ✓ Modular design for quick replacement
   ✓ Clear service procedures
   ✓ Minimize special tools
   Example: Quick-connect vs. clamps: -15 min service = $15 labor savings

6. DESIGN FOR RELIABILITY
   ✓ Use proven components
   ✓ De-rate components (operate below max ratings)
   ✓ Robust design (insensitive to variation)
   ✓ Eliminate failure modes at source
   Example: Premium bearings cost +$20, save $500 in warranty

7. DESIGN FOR TESTABILITY (DFT)
   ✓ Built-in self-test (BIST)
   ✓ Accessible test points
   ✓ Clear fault indicators
   ✓ Diagnostic-friendly architecture
   Example: Built-in diagnostics save 30 min troubleshooting = $30/repair

8. DESIGN FOR END-OF-LIFE (DFEOL)
   ✓ Easy disassembly
   ✓ Material labeling
   ✓ Recyclable materials
   ✓ Minimize hazardous materials
   Example: Modular battery design enables cell-level recycling

DFC METHODOLOGY:
1. Establish cost targets (top-down)
2. Allocate targets to subsystems
3. Design to cost targets
4. Estimate actual costs
5. Identify cost drivers
6. Iterate design to reduce costs
7. Verify through prototyping

TOOLS:
• Cost modeling software
• Should-cost analysis
• Value engineering workshops
• Teardown analysis (benchmark competitors)
```
