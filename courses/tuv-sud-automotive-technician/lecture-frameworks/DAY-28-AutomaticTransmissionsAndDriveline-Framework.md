#### Slide 4: The Torque Converter — Replacing the Clutch
**Visual:** Cross-section cutaway of a torque converter showing the three main elements: impeller (pump), turbine, and stator. Arrows show fluid flow direction. The engine crankshaft connection on one side, transmission input shaft on the other.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "In a manual car, the clutch physically connects and disconnects the engine from the gearbox — two friction surfaces clamped together. In an automatic, there's no physical connection at low speeds. Instead, you have the torque converter — a sealed, doughnut-shaped housing filled with Automatic Transmission Fluid. Inside are three key components.
>
> First: the impeller, also called the pump. It's bolted to the engine's crankshaft — when the engine spins, the impeller spins. It acts like a fan blade throwing fluid outward. Second: the turbine. It sits opposite the impeller, connected to the transmission input shaft. The fluid thrown by the impeller hits the turbine blades and makes it spin — that's what drives the gearbox. Third: the stator, which sits between them. This is the clever part. It redirects the fluid returning from the turbine back into the impeller at an angle that multiplies torque. At stall — when you're stopped with the engine running — the torque converter can multiply engine torque by a factor of two or more. That's how an automatic pulls away from standstill so smoothly."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE TORQUE CONVERTER — Replaces the Clutch

Three internal elements:
1. IMPELLER (PUMP)
   -- Bolted to engine crankshaft
   -- Spins whenever engine runs
   -- Flings ATF outward like a centrifugal fan

2. TURBINE
   -- Connected to transmission input shaft
   -- Spun by fluid force from impeller
   -- Drives the gearbox

3. STATOR
   -- Sits between impeller and turbine
   -- Redirects returning fluid for TORQUE MULTIPLICATION
   -- One-way clutch allows freewheel at coupling speed

Working fluid: ATF (Automatic Transmission Fluid)

At stall: torque multiplication up to 2:1 or higher
At cruise: impeller and turbine spin at nearly same speed
```

#### Slide 5: Torque Converter — Coupling, Multiplication, and Lock-Up
**Visual:** Three-phase diagram showing: (1) stall/launch phase with large speed difference and torque multiplication arrows, (2) coupling phase where speeds converge, (3) lock-up phase with lock-up clutch engaged shown as a mechanical connection. Efficiency curve plotted against speed ratio.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The torque converter works in three phases. Phase one: torque multiplication. The engine is spinning fast, the turbine is barely moving — like pulling away from a stop. The stator redirects fluid to multiply torque. This is your launch phase. Phase two: coupling. As vehicle speed increases, the turbine catches up to the impeller. The stator freewheels on its one-way clutch because it's no longer needed. The converter is now just transferring torque one-to-one, but there's always some slip — the turbine never quite matches the impeller speed. That slip wastes energy as heat.
>
> Phase three: lock-up. At cruise speed — usually above 50-60 km/h — the TCU commands a lock-up clutch inside the converter to engage. This clamps the turbine directly to the impeller housing. Now you have a direct mechanical connection, zero slip, maximum efficiency. It's essentially a clutch — the same concept from yesterday — but it only engages at cruise speed. This is why modern automatics have become so fuel-efficient. Without lock-up, you'd lose 5-10% of your fuel to converter slip."

**PPT Content:**
```
THREE PHASES OF TORQUE CONVERTER OPERATION

PHASE 1: TORQUE MULTIPLICATION (launch)
-- Large speed difference: impeller fast, turbine slow
-- Stator redirects fluid → multiplies torque (up to 2.5:1)
-- Smooth, progressive pull-away

PHASE 2: COUPLING (mid-speed)
-- Turbine approaches impeller speed
-- Stator freewheels on one-way clutch
-- 1:1 torque transfer with ~3-5% slip
-- Slip = energy lost as heat in ATF

PHASE 3: LOCK-UP (cruise)
-- Lock-up clutch engages mechanically
-- ZERO slip → direct connection
-- Maximum efficiency
-- Typically above 50-60 km/h
-- Controlled by TCU (Transmission Control Unit)

Lock-up = the reason modern autos match manual efficiency
```

#### Slide 6: ATF — The Working Fluid
**Visual:** Photo of ATF samples in three transparent containers: fresh (red/pink), used-but-OK (darker red/brown), and burnt (dark brown/black). Below, a checklist of ATF functions.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The ATF is not just a lubricant. It's the working fluid of the entire transmission. In the torque converter, it transmits power — it IS the connection between engine and gearbox. In the valve body, it acts as hydraulic fluid, actuating clutch packs and bands. It lubricates all the gears and bearings. It cools the transmission through the ATF cooler — usually a heat exchanger in the radiator or a separate unit. And it provides the friction characteristics for the clutch packs to grip correctly.
>
> That's why ATF specification matters enormously. You cannot just pour any red fluid in. Each manufacturer specifies a particular ATF type — Dexron VI, Mercon LV, ATF+4, whatever it may be — and using the wrong one can cause shudder, harsh shifts, or premature failure. We'll do the colour-and-smell test in the practical. Fresh ATF is red or pink. As it ages, it darkens. If it's brown and smells burnt — like burnt toast — the transmission has been overheating and internal damage may have started."

**PPT Content:**
```
ATF — AUTOMATIC TRANSMISSION FLUID

Not just a lubricant — ATF has FIVE jobs:
1. POWER TRANSFER — working fluid in torque converter
2. HYDRAULIC ACTUATION — operates clutch packs & bands via valve body
3. LUBRICATION — gears, bearings, bushings
4. COOLING — carries heat to ATF cooler
5. FRICTION MODIFICATION — correct clutch pack engagement feel

ATF CONDITION TELLS THE STORY:
Fresh: Red / Pink — healthy
Aged: Darker red / light brown — monitor
Burnt: Dark brown / black, smells burnt — damage likely

CRITICAL RULE:
Always use the manufacturer-specified ATF type
Wrong ATF = shudder, harsh shifting, premature failure
```

#### Slide 7: Planetary Gear Sets — How Automatics Select Gears
**Visual:** Exploded view of a simple planetary gear set showing the sun gear (centre), planet gears (orbiting on carrier), and ring gear (outer). Arrows indicate which element is held, which is input, and which is output for different gear ratios. Side comparison: "Manual = sliding dog rings select gears. Auto = holding/releasing planetary elements selects gears."

**Instructor Narration:**
> "In a manual gearbox, you select gears by sliding synchro sleeves to lock different gear pairs onto the output shaft. In an automatic, there are no sliding gears at all. Instead, you have planetary gear sets — also called epicyclic gear sets.
>
> A planetary set has three parts: a sun gear in the centre, planet gears that orbit around it on a carrier, and a ring gear around the outside. Here's the magic: by holding one element stationary and driving another, you get a different gear ratio out the third. Hold the ring gear, drive the sun — you get a reduction (low gear). Hold the sun, drive the ring — you get a different ratio. Drive two elements together — you get direct drive (1:1). Connect multiple planetary sets in series and you can get 6, 8, even 10 different ratios.
>
> What holds these elements? Clutch packs and bands — friction devices operated by hydraulic pressure. When the TCU decides to shift, it tells the valve body to apply pressure to one clutch pack and release another. The shift happens in milliseconds because nothing slides — elements are simply clamped or released."

**PPT Content:**
```
PLANETARY GEAR SETS — The Heart of the Automatic

THREE ELEMENTS:
1. SUN GEAR — small, centre
2. PLANET GEARS — orbit the sun on a CARRIER
3. RING GEAR — large, outer, teeth face inward

GEAR SELECTION by holding / releasing elements:
Hold RING, drive SUN → Forward reduction (low gear)
Hold SUN, drive RING → Forward overdrive (high gear)
Lock TWO elements together → Direct drive (1:1)
Hold CARRIER, drive SUN → Reverse

CLUTCH PACKS — multi-plate friction discs (hydraulically applied)
BANDS — wrap around a drum to hold an element stationary

Multiple planetary sets = multiple gear ratios
Modern 8-speed: 4 clutch packs, 0 bands, 3 planetary sets
```

#### Slide 8: The Valve Body — Hydraulic Brain
**Visual:** Photo of a valve body removed from a transmission, showing the maze of channels. Beside it, a simplified schematic showing solenoids, spool valves, and fluid passages. An arrow indicates "TCU commands --> Solenoids --> Spool valves --> Clutch packs."

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The valve body is the hydraulic control centre of the automatic transmission. Think of it as a maze of precision-machined channels and valves — it routes ATF pressure to the correct clutch packs and bands to achieve each gear ratio.
>
> In older transmissions, the valve body was purely hydraulic — governed by throttle pressure and governor pressure from the vehicle speed. The shift points were set by spring tension and fluid dynamics. In modern transmissions, electronic solenoids controlled by the TCU command the spool valves. The TCU reads engine RPM, vehicle speed, throttle position, transmission temperature, and even driving style — and it calculates the perfect shift point and applies exactly the right pressure for a smooth, fast shift.
>
> Some manufacturers now call this a mechatronic unit — the valve body with the TCU electronics integrated directly onto it. Volkswagen's DSG and ZF's 8-speed both use mechatronic units. If the mechatronic fails, it's a significant repair. But the TCU also stores fault codes, so diagnostics start with the scan tool."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE VALVE BODY — Hydraulic Brain of the Transmission

TRADITIONAL (hydraulic):
-- Spool valves directed by throttle pressure + governor pressure
-- Shift points set mechanically by springs and fluid pressure
-- No electronics required

MODERN (mechatronic):
-- Electronic solenoids controlled by TCU
-- TCU reads: engine RPM, vehicle speed, throttle, ATF temp
-- TCU commands: shift timing, line pressure, clutch apply rate
-- Mechatronic unit = valve body + TCU integrated

TCU INPUTS:                    TCU OUTPUTS:
Engine RPM                     Shift solenoids
Vehicle speed                  Pressure control solenoids
Throttle position              Lock-up clutch solenoid
ATF temperature                Line pressure regulator
Brake switch
Sport/Eco mode selection

Fault codes stored in TCU → scan tool diagnostics
```

#### Slide 9: Conventional Automatic — Complete Power Path
**Visual:** Full schematic of a conventional 6-speed or 8-speed automatic showing: engine --> torque converter --> input shaft --> planetary gear sets --> clutch packs --> output shaft --> driveline. Callout boxes label each major component. Oil pump location shown at the front.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's trace the complete power path through a conventional automatic. Engine turns the crankshaft. The torque converter impeller spins, throwing ATF against the turbine. The turbine drives the input shaft into the transmission. Inside, the ATF pressure generated by the oil pump — which is driven by the impeller — feeds the valve body. The valve body directs pressure to specific clutch packs. These clutch packs engage and release elements of the planetary gear sets to produce the desired gear ratio. The output shaft carries the result to the driveline.
>
> Notice the oil pump is driven by the engine via the torque converter housing. This means: if the engine is off, there's no oil pressure. No oil pressure means no clutch pack engagement. That's why you can't push-start an automatic — and why towing an automatic with the drive wheels on the ground can destroy the transmission if you exceed the distance or speed limit."

**PPT Content:**
```
CONVENTIONAL AUTOMATIC — COMPLETE POWER PATH

Engine crankshaft
  ↓
Torque converter (impeller → fluid → turbine)
  ↓
Input shaft
  ↓
Oil pump (driven by torque converter — provides hydraulic pressure)
  ↓
Valve body / mechatronic (directs pressure to clutch packs)
  ↓
Planetary gear sets + clutch packs + bands (produce gear ratio)
  ↓
Output shaft
  ↓
Driveline → differential → wheels

KEY POINT:
Engine off = no oil pump = no pressure = no gear engagement
-- Cannot push-start an automatic
-- Towing rules: observe max speed and distance (owner's manual)
```

#### Slide 10: DSG / DCT — Dual Clutch Transmission
**Visual:** Cutaway of a DSG/DCT showing the two concentric input shafts, the two clutch packs (one for odd gears, one for even gears), and the gear pairs. Callouts highlight: "Clutch 1 = Gears 1, 3, 5, 7" and "Clutch 2 = Gears 2, 4, 6, R." A timing diagram below shows the handover between clutches during an upshift.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The DSG — Direct Shift Gearbox — or DCT — Dual Clutch Transmission — is a fundamentally different approach. It's actually two manual gearboxes in one housing, each with its own clutch. One clutch serves odd gears — 1, 3, 5, 7. The other serves even gears — 2, 4, 6 and sometimes reverse. The two clutches are concentric — one inside the other — each driving its own input shaft that's also concentric.
>
> Here's where it gets clever. While you're driving in, say, 3rd gear with clutch 1 engaged, the mechatronic unit has already pre-selected 4th gear on shaft 2. When it's time to shift, clutch 1 releases and clutch 2 engages almost simultaneously. There's no torque interruption — power is always flowing to the wheels. The result is a shift so fast and smooth that you barely feel it. Under 200 milliseconds.
>
> There are two types of clutch: wet and dry. Wet clutches run in oil — they handle higher torque and last longer but have slightly more drag. Dry clutches are lighter and more efficient but wear faster, especially in stop-and-go traffic where the clutches slip constantly at low speed. If a customer complains of shudder at low speed in a dry DCT, that's often clutch wear."

**PPT Content:**
```
DSG / DCT — DUAL CLUTCH TRANSMISSION

CONCEPT: Two manual gearboxes + two clutches in one housing

SHAFT 1 (Clutch 1): Gears 1, 3, 5, 7
SHAFT 2 (Clutch 2): Gears 2, 4, 6, Reverse

HOW IT SHIFTS:
1. Driving in 3rd gear (Clutch 1 engaged)
2. Mechatronic pre-selects 4th on Shaft 2
3. Clutch 1 opens, Clutch 2 closes simultaneously
4. Shift complete in < 200 ms — no torque interruption

CLUTCH TYPES:
Wet clutch (oil-bathed): higher torque capacity, longer life
Dry clutch (no oil): lighter, more efficient, wears faster in traffic

COMMON ISSUES:
-- Dry DCT: Low-speed shudder from clutch wear
-- Mechatronic unit failure (expensive)
-- Requires specific DCT fluid (NOT conventional ATF)

Used by: VW/Audi (DSG), BMW, Ford (PowerShift), Hyundai/Kia
```

#### Slide 11: CVT — Continuously Variable Transmission (Awareness)
**Visual:** Simplified cutaway of a CVT showing two variable-diameter pulleys connected by a steel push-belt or chain. Arrows show one pulley opening while the other closes to change the effective ratio. A graph shows the smooth RPM curve of a CVT vs the stepped RPM curve of a conventional auto.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "I want you to be aware of CVTs — Continuously Variable Transmissions — even though we won't go deep. Instead of fixed gears, a CVT uses two pulleys connected by a steel belt or chain. Each pulley is made of two cone-shaped halves that move together or apart. When one pulley opens wider, the belt rides lower — effectively making it a smaller gear. The other pulley closes, making it a larger gear. The ratio changes smoothly and continuously — there are no steps.
>
> The advantage: the engine can always run at its most efficient RPM while the car accelerates. The disadvantage: the rubber-band feel. The engine revs high and stays there while the car gradually accelerates. Many drivers find this unnatural. Some CVTs now simulate fixed gear steps to address this.
>
> CVTs are common in Japanese vehicles — Nissan, Subaru, Toyota hybrids use them. They're generally reliable but the belt and pulleys are precision components. If a CVT fails, it's usually a full replacement rather than a rebuild. Key service point: CVT fluid is specific — never use conventional ATF."

**PPT Content:**
```
CVT — CONTINUOUSLY VARIABLE TRANSMISSION (Awareness)

CONCEPT: No fixed gears — infinite ratios between limits

TWO VARIABLE PULLEYS + STEEL BELT (or chain)
-- Pulleys open/close to change effective diameter
-- Belt rides higher or lower → changes ratio
-- Smooth, stepless ratio change

ADVANTAGES:
-- Engine always at optimal RPM → best fuel economy
-- Smooth acceleration with no shift shock
-- Simple concept, fewer moving parts than planetary auto

DISADVANTAGES:
-- "Rubber band" feel (engine revs, car catches up)
-- Limited torque capacity (not for high-performance)
-- Belt/pulley failure = expensive replacement

SERVICE NOTES:
-- CVT fluid is SPECIFIC — never use standard ATF
-- Belt/chain inspection not typically possible without disassembly
-- Some CVTs have a conventional 1st gear + CVT range (Toyota)

Common in: Nissan, Subaru, Toyota (hybrid), Honda
```

**Instructor Narration:**

#### Slide 15: Driveline Overview — FWD vs RWD vs AWD
**Visual:** Three simplified drivetrain layouts: (1) Front-Wheel Drive showing transaxle with short half shafts, (2) Rear-Wheel Drive showing propshaft to rear differential, (3) All-Wheel Drive showing transfer case splitting to front and rear. Power flow arrows on each.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The transmission's output shaft doesn't connect directly to the wheels. There's always a driveline — a series of shafts and joints — between the gearbox and the tyres. The layout depends on the drivetrain configuration.
>
> Front-wheel drive — the most common today — has the engine and transmission combined into a transaxle at the front. Two short half shafts, also called drive shafts, connect the transaxle to the front wheels. Simple and compact. Rear-wheel drive uses a long propshaft running from the gearbox at the front to a differential at the rear axle. From the differential, two half shafts connect to the rear wheels. All-wheel drive combines both — a transfer case splits torque between a front and rear differential, each with its own half shafts.
>
> Regardless of layout, the same fundamental components appear: shafts to carry torque, joints to allow movement, and a differential to split power between left and right wheels."

**PPT Content:**
```
DRIVELINE OVERVIEW — THREE LAYOUTS

FRONT-WHEEL DRIVE (FWD) — most common
Engine + Transaxle → Short half shafts → Front wheels
-- Compact, lightweight, good traction (weight over driven wheels)
-- CV joints handle steering angle + suspension travel

REAR-WHEEL DRIVE (RWD)
Engine → Gearbox → Propshaft → Rear differential → Half shafts → Rear wheels
-- Better weight distribution, higher torque capacity
-- U-joints on propshaft, CV joints at rear axle (some)

ALL-WHEEL DRIVE (AWD)
Engine → Gearbox → Transfer case → Front diff + Rear diff
-- Torque split to all four wheels
-- Most complex: transfer case + front and rear driveline components

COMMON TO ALL:
Shafts (carry torque) + Joints (allow movement) + Differential (split power)
```

#### Slide 16: CV Joints — Constant Velocity at Every Angle
**Visual:** Cutaway of a Rzeppa (outer) CV joint and a tripod (inner) CV joint. The Rzeppa shows the cage, balls, inner and outer races, and the angular range. The tripod shows the three rollers on a spider and the tulip housing. A photo of a CV boot — intact and torn — sits below.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Every front-wheel-drive car uses CV joints — Constant Velocity joints. Why 'constant velocity'? Because the front wheels have to steer AND receive drive power simultaneously. The wheel moves up and down with the suspension AND turns left and right for steering. A simple universal joint would speed up and slow down as it rotated through an angle — that creates vibration. A CV joint delivers smooth, constant rotational speed regardless of the angle. That's what makes front-wheel drive work.
>
> There are two types on each half shaft. The outer CV joint — closest to the wheel — is typically a Rzeppa type. It uses six balls in a cage between inner and outer races. It can operate at high angles — up to 45-50 degrees — which is what allows the front wheels to steer through full lock. The inner CV joint — closest to the gearbox — is typically a tripod type. It has three rollers on a spider that slide in and out of a tulip housing. This allows plunge — the ability to extend and compress as the suspension moves up and down and the distance between gearbox and wheel changes.
>
> Both joints are packed with special CV grease and sealed inside a rubber or thermoplastic boot. The boot is the single most important component for joint longevity. If the boot splits or tears, grease leaks out and dirt gets in. A CV joint with a torn boot will destroy itself within weeks. Boot inspection is a standard part of every service."

**PPT Content:**
```
CV JOINTS — Constant Velocity at Steering + Suspension Angles

OUTER CV JOINT (wheel side) — RZEPPA TYPE
-- 6 balls in a cage between inner and outer races
-- Allows HIGH angles (up to 45-50°) for steering
-- Primarily angular movement
-- Common failure: clicking noise on full-lock turns

INNER CV JOINT (gearbox side) — TRIPOD TYPE
-- 3 rollers on a spider in a tulip housing
-- Allows PLUNGE (in-out sliding) for suspension travel
-- Lower angular range than Rzeppa
-- Common failure: vibration on acceleration (worn rollers)

CV BOOT — THE CRITICAL PROTECTOR
-- Rubber or thermoplastic bellows
-- Packed with special CV grease
-- Sealed with clamps at both ends

TORN BOOT = death sentence for the joint
-- Grease escapes, dirt/water enters
-- Joint destruction within weeks
-- Boot inspection: EVERY service
```

#### Slide 17: U-Joints — Universal Joints on Propshafts
**Visual:** Diagram of a propshaft (driveshaft) with two U-joints at each end and a centre bearing. Exploded view of a U-joint showing the cross (spider), needle bearing caps, and yokes. Below, a phasing diagram showing two U-joints cancelling out each other's speed variation.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Rear-wheel-drive and all-wheel-drive vehicles use a propshaft — a long shaft running from the gearbox to the rear differential. This shaft needs to cope with angle changes as the rear axle moves with the suspension, and it spans a significant distance. Universal joints — U-joints — handle this.
>
> A U-joint is simpler than a CV joint. It's a cross-shaped spider with four needle-bearing caps that fit into yokes at each end. The problem with a single U-joint is that it doesn't deliver constant velocity — the output shaft speeds up and slows down twice per revolution when it's at an angle. The solution is phasing: use two U-joints, one at each end of the propshaft, angled equally. The speed variation from the first joint is cancelled by the second. This is called the phasing rule — both joint angles must be equal and the yokes must be aligned.
>
> U-joints wear over time. The needle bearings lose lubrication, the caps develop play. You check for U-joint wear by grabbing the propshaft near the joint and trying to rotate it back and forth — any clunk or free play means the joint is worn. Vibration at road speed is the classic symptom of a worn U-joint or an out-of-balance propshaft."

**PPT Content:**
```
U-JOINTS — UNIVERSAL JOINTS (Propshafts)

CONSTRUCTION:
-- Cross-shaped spider (4 arms)
-- Needle bearing cap on each arm
-- Pressed into yokes on shaft and flange

PHASING RULE:
-- Single U-joint: output speed varies (not constant velocity)
-- Two U-joints cancel each other's variation
-- REQUIREMENT: Equal angles + aligned yokes

PROPSHAFT COMPONENTS:
-- Front U-joint (at gearbox flange)
-- Shaft tube (balanced to high RPM)
-- Centre bearing (long propshafts — 2-piece)
-- Rear U-joint (at differential flange)

WEAR CHECK:
-- Grab propshaft near joint, twist back and forth
-- ANY free play = joint worn → replace
-- Vibration at speed = worn joint or unbalanced shaft

U-joints are NOT sealed on all designs — some have grease nipples
```

#### Slide 18: The Differential — Splitting Power Left and Right
**Visual:** Cutaway of an open differential showing the ring gear, pinion gear, differential case, side gears, and spider (planet) gears. An animation-style sequence of three images: (1) driving straight — both wheels same speed, (2) turning — outer wheel faster, inner wheel slower, (3) one wheel on ice — spinning freely. Below, the final drive ratio formula.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The differential has two jobs. First: it contains the final drive ratio — the ring and pinion gear set that provides the last stage of gear reduction between the gearbox and the wheels. A typical final drive ratio might be 3.5:1 — meaning the propshaft or transaxle output turns 3.5 times for every one turn of the wheels. This multiplies torque at the expense of speed, and it also turns the power path 90 degrees in a rear-wheel-drive layout.
>
> Second: it allows the left and right wheels to spin at different speeds when cornering. When you turn a corner, the outer wheel travels a longer distance than the inner wheel — it needs to spin faster. The spider gears — also called planet gears — inside the differential allow this speed difference while still splitting the torque equally.
>
> The problem? An open differential always sends torque to the wheel with the LEAST resistance. Put one wheel on ice and the other on dry tarmac — the wheel on ice gets all the torque and spins uselessly. The dry wheel gets nothing. That's the limitation of an open differential, and it's why limited-slip differentials exist."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE DIFFERENTIAL — Two Critical Jobs

JOB 1: FINAL DRIVE RATIO (ring & pinion)
-- Last gear reduction before the wheels
-- Typical ratio: 3.0:1 to 4.5:1
-- Multiplies torque, reduces speed
-- Turns power 90° in RWD layout

JOB 2: SPEED DIFFERENTIATION (spider gears)
-- Allows left/right wheels to spin at different speeds
-- Essential for cornering without tyre scrub
-- Spider gears (planet gears) on a cross pin
-- Side gears connected to each half shaft

HOW IT WORKS:
Straight: Both wheels same speed, spider gears don't rotate
Cornering: Outer faster, inner slower, spiders rotate on pin
On ice: Open diff sends ALL torque to spinning wheel (problem!)

OPEN DIFFERENTIAL LIMITATION:
Torque goes to the wheel with LEAST resistance
One wheel on ice = that wheel gets everything = no forward motion
```

#### Slide 19: Limited-Slip Differentials (LSD)
**Visual:** Three LSD types shown side by side: (1) Clutch-pack LSD with friction plates visible, (2) Torsen (worm gear) LSD showing the helical gears, (3) Electronic LSD concept showing ABS sensor inputs and brake application to the spinning wheel. Below each, a one-line operating principle.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Limited-slip differentials solve the open diff's weakness by restricting the speed difference between the two wheels. There are several approaches.
>
> The clutch-pack LSD uses friction plates — like a small clutch pack — between the differential case and the side gears. When one wheel tries to spin faster than the other, the clutch packs resist. Torque is biased toward the slower wheel. These work well but the clutch packs wear over time and need specific limited-slip additive in the gear oil.
>
> The Torsen — short for torque-sensing — uses worm gears that inherently resist speed differences. It's a purely mechanical device with no friction materials to wear. The torque bias is determined by the gear geometry. Very robust, used in many Audi Quattro systems.
>
> The electronic LSD isn't really a differential at all — it uses the standard open differential but applies the brake to the spinning wheel via the ABS system. When the stability control detects one wheel spinning, it brakes that wheel, which forces torque to the other side. This is called brake-based torque vectoring. It's a software solution using existing hardware, which is why it's become very popular on modern cars. No special fluid, no wearing components — just clever use of the ABS system."

**PPT Content:**
```
LIMITED-SLIP DIFFERENTIALS (LSD)

TYPE 1: CLUTCH-PACK LSD
-- Friction plates between case and side gears
-- Resists speed difference through friction
-- Requires LSD additive in gear oil
-- Clutch packs wear over time
-- Used in: many performance and off-road vehicles

TYPE 2: TORSEN (Torque-Sensing)
-- Worm gear geometry resists speed difference
-- Purely mechanical — no friction surfaces to wear
-- Torque bias ratio set by gear design
-- Used in: Audi Quattro, some Mazda, Toyota

TYPE 3: ELECTRONIC LSD (eLSD / brake-based)
-- Standard open differential
-- ABS brakes the spinning wheel
-- Forces torque to the gripping wheel
-- Software solution using existing ABS hardware
-- No special fluid or wear components
-- Used in: most modern vehicles with ESC

TECHNICIAN NOTE:
Always check which LSD type before servicing — wrong oil = chatter
```

#### Slide 22: Station 1 — Torque Converter Examination
**Visual:** Annotated photo of a cutaway torque converter on a workbench with numbered callouts. Beside it, a hand demonstrating the one-way clutch direction on the stator.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "At this station, you have a cutaway torque converter. First, identify the impeller — it's the outer shell, the part that bolts to the flexplate on the engine. See the curved blades inside? Those throw the fluid outward. Now find the turbine — it's the element connected to the splined hub that goes into the transmission. Feel how the blades curve in the opposite direction to the impeller — they catch the fluid and convert its motion into rotation.
>
> Now the stator — it's the smaller element between them, mounted on a one-way clutch on the stator shaft. Try to turn it by hand. It should turn freely one way and lock the other way. That lock direction is what redirects fluid for torque multiplication. If this one-way clutch fails, you lose torque multiplication at launch — the car feels sluggish from standstill but fine at cruise.
>
> Finally, look at the lock-up clutch — the friction surface on the front of the turbine that presses against the inside of the converter housing. This is what eliminates slip at cruise speed."

**PPT Content:**
```
STATION 1: TORQUE CONVERTER — HANDS-ON IDENTIFICATION

TASK 1: Identify the IMPELLER
-- Outer housing, bolts to engine flexplate
-- Curved blades throw ATF outward

TASK 2: Identify the TURBINE
-- Splined hub for transmission input shaft
-- Blades curve opposite to impeller

TASK 3: Feel the STATOR one-way clutch
-- Should freewheel one direction, lock the other
-- Lock direction = torque multiplication
-- Failed one-way clutch = poor launch, OK at cruise

TASK 4: Find the LOCK-UP CLUTCH
-- Friction surface on turbine face
-- Clamps against inside of converter housing
-- Engages at cruise for zero-slip operation

RECORD in your notebook:
-- Which direction does the stator lock?
-- Sketch the fluid flow path through the converter
```

#### Slide 23: Station 2 — CV Joint and Boot Inspection

**Instructor Narration:**
> "At this station, the vehicle is on the lift with the wheels hanging free. Your job is to inspect all four CV boots — two per side on a front-wheel-drive car, inner and outer. #### Slide 24: Station 3 — U-Joint and Propshaft Check
**Visual:** Photo of a technician's hands gripping a propshaft near a U-joint, arrows showing the twist-test motion. Beside it, close-up photos of: (1) healthy U-joint with grease nipple, (2) worn U-joint with rust staining around the caps, (3) seized U-joint cap with visible play in the cross.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "At this station, you're checking the propshaft and its U-joints. The vehicle needs to be on the lift with the transmission in neutral and the parking brake off so the shaft can rotate freely.
>
> First, the play test. Grip the propshaft firmly near each U-joint. Try to twist it back and forth — any rotational free play is a sign of worn needle bearings in the U-joint caps. Even a small amount of play means replacement is needed. You should also try to move the shaft up and down and side to side at the joint — again, any movement means wear.
>
> Second, the visual inspection. Look at the U-joint caps — are they rusty? Is there discolouration from heat? Is there dried grease that's been forced out? These are all signs of wear. Some U-joints have grease nipples — if they do, check that they haven't been neglected. A joint that's never been greased won't last.
>
> Third, check the centre bearing if it's a two-piece propshaft. The rubber mounting should be firm, not cracked or collapsed. Push the shaft up and down at the centre bearing — excessive movement means the bearing or its mount is worn.
>
> Rotate the shaft slowly by hand and watch the U-joints — they should rotate smoothly without any notchy feeling or tight spots. A tight spot means the joint is seizing."

#### Slide 25: Station 4 — ATF Condition Check
**Visual:** Step-by-step photo sequence: (1) locating the dipstick/fill port, (2) wiping the dipstick on white paper, (3) colour comparison chart (fresh red to burnt black), (4) checking level marks at operating temperature. A smell indicator icon showing "neutral = OK" vs "burnt toast = concern."

**Instructor Narration:**
> "ATF condition is the fastest diagnostic window into an automatic transmission's health. At this station, you're going to check colour, smell, and level.
>
> Step one: locate the dipstick or fill port. Some vehicles have a conventional dipstick tube — check with the engine running, transmission in Park, at operating temperature. Others are sealed — you'll need to remove a fill plug and use a specific procedure, often at a specific temperature measured with the scan tool. Check the service manual.
>
> Step two: wipe a drop of ATF onto white paper or a white cloth. Fresh ATF is red or pink and translucent. As it ages, it darkens through dark red to brown. If it's dark brown or black and opaque, the fluid has been thermally degraded. The transmission has been running hot — possibly for a long time.
>
> Step three: smell it. Fresh ATF has a mild, slightly sweet smell. Burnt ATF smells unmistakably like burnt toast or burnt caramel. If you smell burning, clutch material is likely degrading inside the transmission. This is a serious warning sign.
>
> Step four: check the level. Under-filled transmissions overheat because there's insufficient fluid for cooling. Over-filled transmissions cause foaming — air bubbles in the ATF — which causes erratic shifting and poor lubrication. Both extremes cause damage. The level must be checked at the correct temperature — this is critical."

**PPT Content:**
```
STATION 4: ATF CONDITION CHECK

-- Sealed: fill plug access, specific temp procedure via scan tool
-- Always check service manual for correct procedure

STEP 2: COLOUR ASSESSMENT (drop on white paper)
Red / Pink: Fresh, healthy
Dark red: Aged but serviceable
Brown: Significant degradation — recommend fluid change
Dark brown / Black: Severe thermal damage — internal damage likely

STEP 3: SMELL TEST
Mild / Slightly sweet: Normal
Burnt toast / Burnt caramel: Clutch material degradation — SERIOUS

STEP 4: LEVEL CHECK
-- Too low: overheating (insufficient cooling capacity)
```

## Key Takeaways

1. The torque converter replaces the clutch using fluid dynamics — impeller, turbine, stator provide smooth power transfer and torque multiplication; lock-up clutch eliminates slip at cruise
2. Planetary gear sets select ratios by holding and releasing elements via clutch packs, controlled by the valve body or mechatronic unit under TCU command
3. DSG/DCT uses two clutches and pre-selected gears for instant shifts; CVT uses variable pulleys for infinite ratios — each type has distinct service requirements and fluid specifications
4. CV joints (Rzeppa outer, tripod inner) allow constant-velocity power delivery through steering and suspension angles — boot integrity is critical to joint survival
5. The differential provides final drive reduction and allows left/right wheel speed differentiation — LSD types (clutch, Torsen, electronic) solve the open diff's limitation on low-traction surfaces
6. ATF colour and smell are the fastest diagnostic indicators of automatic transmission health — always use the correct manufacturer-specified fluid type
