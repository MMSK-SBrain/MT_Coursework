#### Slide 6: Case Study 1 — Overheating (Diagnosis Path)
**Visual:** Diagnostic flowchart for overheating. Start: Check coolant level --> If low: pressure test for leaks --> If normal: check thermostat operation --> Check fan activation --> Combustion gas test in coolant

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Here's how you work through it systematically. Step one: check the coolant level. It's slightly low — about 200 ml below minimum. Top it up and pressure test the system. The pressure holds. No external leak. Step two: start the engine, connect the scan tool, and watch the coolant temperature PID. The temperature climbs steadily — 80, 85, 90, 95, 100, 105 degrees — and the upper radiator hose is still COLD. What does that tell you?"

*Pause for learner response.*

> "Exactly. The thermostat isn't opening. Hot coolant is trapped in the engine and never reaches the radiator. The thermostat is stuck closed. But wait — we're not done. WHY was the coolant 200 ml low? Possibly just evaporation over time, or the overheating event pushed some coolant out through the expansion tank overflow. We top up, replace the thermostat, run it to temperature, verify the upper hose gets hot at 87 degrees, and confirm the fans kick in at 100 degrees. Case closed."

**PPT Content:**
```
CASE 1: DIAGNOSTIC PATH

Step 1: Check coolant level → Slightly low (200 ml below min)
Step 2: Pressure test → Holds pressure — no external leak
Step 3: Scan tool — monitor coolant temp PID
        → Temp climbs past 100°C, upper hose COLD
Step 4: Conclusion — thermostat stuck CLOSED
        → Hot coolant never reaches radiator

ROOT CAUSE: Thermostat failure (wax element degraded)
ACTION: Replace thermostat + housing gasket
        Top up coolant to correct level
        Verify fan operation at activation temperature
        Re-test with road test

KEY LESSON: Cold upper hose + rising temp = thermostat stuck closed
           Hot upper hose + rising temp = radiator/fan problem
```

#### Slide 9: Case Study 2 — Oil Pressure Warning (Diagnosis Path)
**Visual:** Step-by-step diagnostic flow with oil system diagram highlighted at each stage. Mechanical oil pressure gauge shown alongside scan tool

**Instructor Narration:**
> "First things first: top up the oil to the correct level. Does the light go out? In this case, the light still appears at warm idle even with correct oil level. Next step: verify the complaint isn't a false alarm. Connect a mechanical oil pressure gauge to the engine — bypass the electronic sensor entirely. At warm idle, you read 0.8 bar. The spec says minimum 1.0 bar at idle. So the pressure IS genuinely low — it's not a sensor fault.
>
> Now, check the service history. When was the oil last changed? What grade was used? The customer says an independent shop did the last service 5,000 km ago. They used 5W-30, but the BMW spec calls for 5W-30 LL-04 — a specific long-life grade. Could be the wrong oil, but the viscosity grade matches.
>
> At 120,000 km on a diesel, bearing wear is plausible. But before condemning the engine, check if the oil pickup is clear. In this case, the low level over time suggests consumption. The root cause: the engine is consuming oil past worn valve stem seals, level drops, and with 120,000 km of bearing wear, the reduced level tips pressure below spec at idle. Action: replace valve stem seals, fresh oil and filter with correct spec, recheck pressure. If still low, further investigation into bearing clearances."

**PPT Content:**
```
CASE 2: DIAGNOSTIC PATH

Step 1: Top up oil to correct level → Light still ON at warm idle
Step 2: Connect mechanical oil pressure gauge (bypass sensor)
        → Reads 0.8 bar at warm idle (spec: min 1.0 bar)
        → Reads 2.5 bar at 3000 RPM (spec: min 2.0 bar)
        CONCLUSION: Genuinely low pressure at idle — not sensor fault
Step 3: Check service history → Correct viscosity grade used
Step 4: Check for oil consumption signs → Light blue smoke at startup
        → Valve stem seals suspected
Step 5: Inspect oil pickup strainer (if accessible) → Clear

ROOT CAUSE: Oil consumption (worn valve stem seals) + high mileage
            bearing clearance at limit
ACTION: Replace valve stem seals
        Fresh oil + filter (correct LL-04 spec)
        Recheck pressure after 1000 km
        MONITOR: If pressure still low, bearing inspection needed

KEY LESSON: Low pressure at idle that improves with RPM
            = Flow problem (level, clearance, pump wear)
            Low pressure at ALL RPMs = pump failure or severe damage
```

#### Slide 11: Case Study 3 — Boost Loss (Diagnosis Path)
**Visual:** Annotated engine bay showing turbo system with numbered test points: 1-air filter, 2-charge pipes, 3-intercooler, 4-VGT actuator, 5-wastegate, 6-exhaust backpressure tap

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Start simple, work complex. Check the air filter — it's dirty but not blocked. Not the cause. Next, physically inspect every charge pipe and hose between the turbo and the intake manifold. >

>

**PPT Content:**
```

Step 1: Check air filter → Dirty but not blocked — not the cause
Step 2: Visual inspection of charge pipes & intercooler hoses
        → Found: 3 cm split in silicone coupler (intercooler to throttle)
        → Boost air escaping before reaching engine
Step 3: Smoke test to confirm no other leaks → Clear
Step 4: Investigate WHY the hose split → Heat cycling, age, no secondary cause
Step 5: Replace coupler, clear P0299, road test
```

#### Slide 13: Case Study 4 — Emission Warning Lights (Diagnosis Path)

#### Slide 14: DPF Regeneration — Deep Dive
**Visual:** Diagram showing passive vs. active vs. forced regeneration with exhaust temperature thresholds. Timeline showing soot accumulation vs. regeneration events.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Since DPF issues are so common, let's take a moment to understand regeneration properly. Passive regeneration happens automatically at sustained motorway speeds — exhaust temperatures reach 350-500 degrees Celsius, and the soot burns off naturally. Active regeneration is triggered by the ECU when soot load exceeds about 60% — the ECU injects extra fuel to raise exhaust temperature, typically requiring sustained driving above 60 km/h for 15-20 minutes. Forced regeneration is what WE do with a scan tool in the workshop when active regen has failed and soot load exceeds 80-85%. If the DPF exceeds about 95% and shows high differential pressure, it may be clogged beyond chemical regeneration and need physical cleaning or replacement."

**PPT Content:**
```
DPF REGENERATION TYPES

PASSIVE REGEN (continuous, automatic)
• Exhaust temp naturally > 350°C (motorway cruising)
• Soot burns off gradually — driver never notices
• Requires sustained speed driving

ACTIVE REGEN (ECU-initiated, automatic)
• Triggered when soot load > ~60%
• ECU injects post-combustion fuel → raises exhaust temp to ~600°C
• Needs ~15-20 min sustained driving at >60 km/h
• Driver may notice: higher idle, fan running, hot exhaust smell

FORCED REGEN (technician-initiated via scan tool)
• Used when active regen fails or soot load > 80-85%
• Performed stationary with scan tool commanding regen
• Monitor exhaust temperature throughout
• FIRE RISK — ensure clear area behind vehicle

BEYOND REGEN (soot load > ~95%, differential pressure very high)
• Chemical regen may fail
• Options: off-car DPF cleaning or DPF replacement
• NEVER remove DPF — illegal and fails roadworthiness inspection
```

#### Slide 15: Condition Assessment — Service, Replace, or Monitor
**Visual:** Three-column decision matrix: "Service" (maintenance interval, fluid change, clean), "Replace" (failed, worn beyond spec, safety critical), "Monitor" (borderline, intermittent, not yet at limit)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "After every diagnosis, you face a decision: do we service it, replace it, or monitor it? This is where experience and specifications work together. If the thermostat is stuck — replace it. There's no 'servicing' a wax element thermostat. If the oil pressure is 0.1 bar below spec at idle but fine at all other RPMs, and the car has 190,000 km — you might recommend monitoring with more frequent oil changes while planning for future work. If the DPF soot load is at 70% — a forced regeneration is a service action, not a replacement.
>
> The key question is always: does this component still meet its specification? If yes, monitor. If no, can servicing restore it to spec? If yes, service. If no, replace."

**PPT Content:**
```
CONDITION ASSESSMENT FRAMEWORK

SERVICE (Restore to specification):
• DPF regeneration — soot load high but recoverable
• Coolant flush — contaminated but no component damage
• Oil and filter change — overdue interval
• Charge pipe replacement — worn but not failed
• AdBlue system flush — contaminated fluid

REPLACE (Cannot restore to specification):
• Thermostat stuck — no service option, replace
• Turbo with bearing play — not rebuildable in workshop
• Cracked intercooler — structural failure
• Catalyst below efficiency threshold after service — replace
• Water pump with shaft play/leak — replace

MONITOR (Borderline — not yet at action threshold):
• Oil pressure slightly below spec — short interval recheck
• DPF differential pressure slightly elevated — driving pattern advice
• Coolant temperature sensor reading 2°C off spec — recheck in 6 months
• Turbo actuator slow but functional — watch for worsening

ALWAYS DOCUMENT your recommendation and the customer's decision.
```

#### Slide 16: Cross-Referencing Scan Tool Data
**Visual:** Four scan tool PID screens shown side by side: oil pressure + temperature, coolant temperature + thermostat position, exhaust temperature pre/post DPF, boost pressure requested vs. actual

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Modern diagnostic thinking means looking at data from multiple systems simultaneously. Your scan tool can show you live data from every ECU on the car. The skill is knowing which PIDs to cross-reference.
>
> Example: the engine is overheating AND you see low oil pressure. Cross-reference coolant temperature with oil temperature and oil pressure. As coolant temp climbs, oil thins out, and pressure drops. The overheating caused the low oil pressure — fixing the cooling system fixes both symptoms.
>
> Example: boost is low AND exhaust backpressure is high. The DPF is blocked, creating backpressure that the turbo can't overcome. Regenerate the DPF, and boost returns to normal. One root cause, two symptoms in two different systems."

**PPT Content:**
```
CROSS-REFERENCING SCAN TOOL DATA

COOLING + OIL:
• Coolant temp vs. oil temp vs. oil pressure
• Overheating thins oil → pressure drops → secondary oil warning
• Fix cooling issue → oil pressure normalises

TURBO + EXHAUST:
• Boost requested vs. actual + exhaust backpressure
• High backpressure (blocked DPF) → turbo cannot build boost
• Regen DPF → boost restored

EXHAUST + COOLING:
• Exhaust temp sensors + coolant level trend
• Dropping coolant + high exhaust temps → head gasket leak
• Coolant entering combustion chamber → steam in exhaust

OIL + TURBO:
• Oil pressure + turbo shaft play
• Low oil pressure → starved turbo bearings → turbo failure
• Always check oil supply BEFORE replacing a turbo

THINK IN CONNECTIONS, NOT ISOLATED SYSTEMS
```

#### Slide 23: Week 4 — Quick Fire Review
**Visual:** Four-column summary — Day 16 (Oil), Day 17 (Cooling), Day 18 (Exhaust), Day 19 (Turbo) — with key concepts listed under each

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before the quiz, let's do a rapid review. Day 16, lubrication: oil protects, cools, cleans, and seals. Viscosity grades like 5W-30 tell you flow at cold and hot temperatures. Oil pumps are gear-type or rotor-type. The relief valve prevents over-pressure. Full-flow filtration means all oil passes through the filter.
>
> Day 17, cooling: the thermostat controls coolant flow to the radiator. It opens at around 82-95 degrees depending on the engine. Never mix OAT and IAT coolant — it gels and blocks passages. The water pump is driven by the timing belt or chain. The cooling fan activates when the A/C is on or when coolant temperature exceeds the threshold.
>
> Day 18, exhaust and emissions: the catalytic converter needs to reach light-off temperature to function. The DPF traps soot and needs regeneration. SCR uses AdBlue to reduce NOx. The EGR recirculates exhaust gas to lower combustion temperatures. Wideband O2 sensors measure air-fuel ratio precisely.
>
> Day 19, turbocharging: the turbo uses exhaust energy to compress intake air. Bearings are oil-fed and require proper pressure and flow. The wastegate limits boost pressure. VGT changes turbine geometry for better response. Intercoolers cool the compressed charge air."

**PPT Content:**
```
WEEK 4 — RAPID REVIEW

DAY 16: LUBRICATION
• Oil functions: lubricate, cool, clean, seal, protect
• Viscosity: 5W-30 (W = winter/cold, 30 = hot viscosity)
• Pump types: gear, gerotor (rotor)
• Full-flow filtration + bypass valve safety

DAY 17: COOLING
• Thermostat: wax element, opens 82-95°C
• Never mix coolant types (OAT + IAT = gel)
• Water pump: belt or chain driven
• Electric fan: temp-triggered + A/C activation

DAY 18: EXHAUST & EMISSIONS
• Catalytic converter: light-off temp ~250-300°C
• DPF: traps soot, needs passive/active/forced regen
• SCR: AdBlue (urea) injection reduces NOx
• EGR: recirculates exhaust to lower combustion temp
• O2 sensors: narrowband (switching) vs. wideband (linear)

DAY 19: TURBOCHARGING
• Exhaust-driven compressor — oil-fed bearings
• Wastegate: limits maximum boost
• VGT: variable geometry for broad RPM response
• Intercooler: cools charge air for density
```

## Key Takeaways

1. Real customer complaints span multiple systems — diagnostic technicians must think across system boundaries
2. The diagnostic thinking framework (Complaint > Symptoms > Systems > Data > Test > Interpret > Root Cause > Action) applies to every fault on every vehicle
3. Scan tool cross-referencing across systems reveals connections that single-system thinking misses
4. Condition assessment (service / replace / monitor) requires comparing measurements against specifications — not guessing
5. A $15 split hose can cause the same DTC as a $1500 turbo — always check simple causes first
6. DPF regeneration is a service action with three levels (passive, active, forced) — understanding when each applies prevents unnecessary part replacement
7. Week 4 content carries 16 marks on the final exam — mastery of oil, cooling, exhaust, and turbo systems is essential
