#### Slide 2: Car Audio System Architecture
**Visual:** System diagram showing signal and power flow: head unit (source) sends signal via RCA cables to amplifier, amplifier sends power via speaker wire to speakers (front component set, rear coaxial, subwoofer). Separate power cable from battery to amplifier with inline fuse.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "A car audio system follows the same electrical principles you learned in Week 2. You have a SOURCE — the head unit, which converts digital music files or radio signals into an audio signal. That signal travels through RCA cables — low-level signal cables — to an AMPLIFIER, which boosts the signal to a level that can drive speakers. Power cables connect the amplifier to the battery. And speaker wires connect the amplifier outputs to the individual speakers. Signal flows one direction: source to amp to speaker. Power comes from the battery. Ground returns to the chassis. It's just a circuit."

**PPT Content:**
```
CAR AUDIO SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE

SIGNAL PATH:
Source (head unit) → RCA cables → Amplifier → Speaker wire → Speakers

POWER PATH:
Battery (+) → Fused power cable → Amplifier → Battery (-) / Chassis ground

COMPONENTS:
1. HEAD UNIT (source): Radio, Bluetooth, USB, streaming
   - Outputs: RCA pre-outs (line level signal)
   - Typically 4 channels: front L, front R, rear L, rear R

2. AMPLIFIER (power): Boosts signal to drive speakers
   - Channels: 2-ch, 4-ch, 5-ch, mono (subwoofer)
   - Power rating: Watts RMS (continuous, NOT peak)

3. SPEAKERS (transducers): Convert electrical signal to sound
   - Component set (separate tweeter + midrange + crossover)
   - Coaxial (tweeter mounted on midrange — all-in-one)
   - Subwoofer (dedicated low-frequency driver)

4. WIRING:
   - Power cable: Battery to amplifier (fused at battery end)
   - RCA cables: Head unit to amplifier (shielded signal)
   - Speaker wire: Amplifier to speakers (matched gauge)
```

#### Slide 3: Head Unit — The Source
**Visual:** Annotated photo of a modern head unit showing: DIN size (single vs double), display, USB input, Bluetooth antenna, rear RCA pre-outs, antenna connection, power harness connector, CAN bus integration.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The head unit is the control centre. Modern units are double-DIN size — that's the standard mounting frame dimension used across the industry. On the back, you'll find the main power connector — which includes constant 12V (memory), switched 12V (ignition), ground, illumination, and speaker outputs. If the vehicle has a factory amplifier, you'll see RCA pre-out connections for line-level signal output. The antenna connection is typically a Fakra or ISO connector. And increasingly, the head unit integrates with the vehicle's CAN bus for steering wheel controls, vehicle speed signal, and reverse camera activation."

**PPT Content:**
```
HEAD UNIT — THE SOURCE

Mounting standard: Single DIN (50mm height) or Double DIN (100mm)

Rear connections:
- POWER HARNESS: constant 12V, switched 12V, ground, illumination
- SPEAKER OUTPUTS: 4 channels (if no separate amplifier)
- RCA PRE-OUTS: low-level signal for external amplifier
- ANTENNA INPUT: Fakra (European), ISO, or SMA connector
- USB / AUX: digital or analog source inputs
- CAN BUS: steering wheel controls, speed signal, vehicle integration
- CAMERA INPUT: reverse camera (RCA or LVDS)

Key specifications:
- Output power: typically 4 x 20-50W RMS (built-in amplifier)
- Pre-out voltage: 2V-5V (higher = better signal-to-noise ratio)
- Impedance: designed for 4-ohm speakers (standard automotive)

Installation note:
Many modern vehicles have CAN-integrated head units.
Replacing with aftermarket requires CAN interface adapter
to retain steering wheel controls, vehicle settings, and warnings.
```

#### Slide 4: Amplifiers — Power and Signal
**Visual:** Annotated photo of a 4-channel car amplifier showing: RCA signal inputs, speaker wire outputs, power terminal, ground terminal, remote turn-on wire, gain controls, crossover switches.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The amplifier takes the small signal from the head unit and makes it big enough to move speaker cones. Key settings: GAIN — this is NOT a volume control. It matches the amplifier's input sensitivity to the head unit's output voltage. Set too high, you get distortion and risk damaging speakers. Set too low, the system is quiet and has a poor signal-to-noise ratio. CROSSOVER — this filters which frequencies go to which speakers. You don't want bass going to your tweeters or treble going to your subwoofer. High-pass for front speakers, low-pass for subwoofer. The power cable from the battery must be fused within 300mm of the battery terminal — this protects against a short circuit in the power cable melting and causing a fire."

**PPT Content:**
```
AMPLIFIERS — KEY SETTINGS AND WIRING

SETTINGS:
- GAIN: Matches input sensitivity to head unit output level
  (NOT a volume control — set once during installation)
- CROSSOVER: Filters frequencies sent to each channel
  - High-pass (HPF): removes bass from front/rear speakers
  - Low-pass (LPF): removes treble from subwoofer channel
  - Typical HPF: 80 Hz    Typical LPF: 80 Hz

WIRING:
- POWER: Battery (+) to amplifier via fused cable
  * Fuse within 300mm of battery terminal
  * Cable gauge matched to amplifier current draw
  * Route AWAY from signal cables (prevent interference)
- GROUND: Amplifier to clean chassis point (bare metal, <450mm)
- REMOTE: Head unit remote turn-on wire to amplifier
  (turns amplifier on/off with head unit)
- SIGNAL: RCA cables from head unit to amplifier inputs
- OUTPUT: Speaker wire from amplifier to each speaker

POWER CABLE GAUGE GUIDE:
  Up to 400W RMS: 8 AWG (8 mm sq)
  400-800W RMS: 4 AWG (21 mm sq)
  800-1500W RMS: 1/0 AWG (50 mm sq)
```

#### Slide 5: Speakers — Component vs Coaxial vs Subwoofer
**Visual:** Three speaker types side by side — component set (separate tweeter + midrange + passive crossover), coaxial (all-in-one), subwoofer (large cone in enclosure).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Three types of speakers. Component sets separate the tweeter — the small driver that handles high frequencies — from the midrange driver. A passive crossover network divides the signal between them. Components give the best sound quality because the tweeter can be mounted at ear level on the A-pillar or dash while the midrange sits in the door. Coaxial speakers combine everything into one unit — tweeter mounted on a bridge above the midrange cone. Easier to install, lower cost, decent quality. Subwoofers handle bass below 80 Hz — they need a separate enclosure, either a sealed box for tight bass or a ported box for louder, boomy bass."

**PPT Content:**
```
SPEAKER TYPES

COMPONENT SET (best quality):
- Separate tweeter (high frequency: 3kHz - 20kHz)
- Separate midrange (mid frequency: 80Hz - 3kHz)
- Passive crossover network (divides signal by frequency)
- Tweeter: mount on A-pillar, dash, or door sail panel
- Midrange: mount in door or factory speaker location

COAXIAL (all-in-one):
- Tweeter mounted on bridge above midrange cone
- Single unit — drops into factory speaker location
- Good for rear fill and budget installations
- Less flexibility in tweeter positioning

SUBWOOFER (bass):
- Large driver (8", 10", 12", 15" diameter)
- Handles frequencies below 80-100 Hz
- Requires enclosure: sealed (tight bass) or ported (louder bass)
- Powered by dedicated mono amplifier channel

SPEAKER SPECIFICATIONS:
- Impedance: 4 ohm (standard automotive), 2 ohm (some aftermarket)
- Sensitivity: dB/W/m (higher = louder per watt)
- Power handling: RMS watts (continuous — match to amplifier output)
```

#### Slide 6: Audio System Wiring — Signal vs Power
**Visual:** Wiring routing diagram in a vehicle — power cable runs along one side (passenger), RCA signal cables run along the other side (driver), speaker wires route through doors and rear parcel shelf.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The number one wiring mistake in car audio: running power cables and signal cables together. A power cable carrying 20+ amps creates an electromagnetic field that induces noise into the low-level RCA signal cables — you hear this as a whine that changes pitch with engine speed. The rule: power cables on one side of the vehicle, signal cables on the other. If they must cross, they cross at 90 degrees — never parallel. Speaker wire gauge matters too: for short runs under 3 metres, 16 AWG is fine. For longer runs or higher power, use 14 or 12 AWG to avoid power loss."

**PPT Content:**
```
AUDIO WIRING RULES

POWER vs SIGNAL SEPARATION:
- Power cable: route along PASSENGER side of vehicle
- RCA signal cable: route along DRIVER side of vehicle
- If they must cross: 90-degree crossing only (NEVER parallel)
- Reason: electromagnetic interference causes alternator whine

SPEAKER WIRE GAUGE:
  Under 3m run, up to 50W RMS: 16 AWG (1.3 mm sq)
  3-5m run, up to 75W RMS: 14 AWG (2.0 mm sq)
  Over 5m run, or 100W+ RMS: 12 AWG (3.3 mm sq)
  Subwoofer (high current): 12 AWG minimum

RCA CABLES:
- Shielded twisted-pair construction
- Quality matters — cheap RCA cables pick up noise
- Keep as short as practical (don't coil excess)
- Blue stripe or marked end toward head unit (shield ground)

POWER CABLE:
- Fused within 300mm of battery terminal
- Route through firewall grommet (NEVER drill new holes near airbags)
- Secure with clips every 300mm (no hanging/rattling cables)
- Use rubber grommets at every panel pass-through
```

#### Slide 7: Common Audio System Faults
**Visual:** Troubleshooting table with symptoms, causes, and tests.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The most common audio complaint: alternator whine — a high-pitched whine that changes with engine RPM. Cause is almost always poor signal cable routing or a bad ground. Second most common: one speaker not working. Check the speaker wire continuity, the speaker itself (measure impedance — should read 3-5 ohms for a 4-ohm speaker), and the amplifier output. Third: distortion at volume. Usually the gain is set too high, or the speakers are being driven beyond their power handling. Audio troubleshooting is just electrical troubleshooting with a musical soundtrack."

**PPT Content:**
```
COMMON AUDIO FAULTS — DIAGNOSIS

ALTERNATOR WHINE (pitch changes with RPM):
- Cause: power/signal cable interference, bad ground
- Test: disconnect RCA from amp — whine stops? RCA routing issue
- Test: move ground point — whine changes? Ground issue
- Fix: re-route cables (separate power from signal), improve ground

ONE SPEAKER NOT WORKING:
- Test: swap known-good speaker into same channel
  → Speaker works? Original speaker is faulty
  → Still silent? Check wiring continuity (multimeter)
  → Wiring OK? Check amplifier output (test with different speaker)
- Common cause: broken wire at door hinge flex point

DISTORTION AT VOLUME:
- Cause: gain set too high, speaker overdriven, clipping
- Test: reduce head unit volume to 75%, adjust amp gain for clean output
- Fix: set gain correctly (match amp input to head unit output)

NO AUDIO FROM ANY SPEAKER:
- Check: fuse (in-line power fuse and head unit fuse)
- Check: remote turn-on wire (12V present when head unit is on?)
- Check: amplifier power LED (on? off? protect mode?)
- Check: head unit output (use RCA test cable to external speaker)
```

#### Slide 9: What Is NVH?
**Visual:** Three icons representing Noise (ear/sound wave), Vibration (steering wheel/seat shaking), Harshness (rough road surface transmitted to cabin). Definition bar beneath.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "NVH — Noise, Vibration, Harshness. It's the term engineers use for any unwanted sensory experience in the vehicle. Noise is anything you HEAR that shouldn't be there — a squeak, rattle, hum, or groan. Vibration is anything you FEEL — steering wheel shake, seat tremor, pedal pulsation. Harshness is the way road imperfections are transmitted to the cabin — how 'rough' the ride feels over bumps. NVH complaints are the most common customer issue you'll face, and also the most difficult to diagnose — because they're subjective. What one customer calls 'a terrible grinding noise,' another wouldn't even notice."

**PPT Content:**
```
NVH — NOISE, VIBRATION, HARSHNESS

NOISE (audible):
- Unwanted sounds heard by occupants
- Examples: squeak, rattle, hum, buzz, groan, whistle, clunk
- Sources: engine, exhaust, wind, road, suspension, trim, accessories

VIBRATION (tactile):
- Unwanted oscillation felt through steering wheel, seat, or pedals
- Examples: steering wheel shimmy, seat shake, pedal pulsation
- Sources: wheel balance, tyre defect, driveshaft, brake rotor

HARSHNESS (impact):
- Severity of impact transmitted from road surface to cabin
- Examples: harsh ride over bumps, thud from potholes
- Sources: suspension bushings, dampers, tyre pressure, tyre type

WHY NVH MATTERS:
- #1 customer complaint category in many workshops
- Subjective — difficult to reproduce and quantify
- Multiple possible sources — requires systematic diagnosis
- Can indicate safety-relevant problems (worn bearings, loose components)
```

#### Slide 10: NVH Classification — Speed, Load, and Road Surface
**Visual:** Decision tree — "When does it happen?" branching to speed-dependent, load-dependent, and road-surface-dependent, each with example sources.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The first diagnostic question for any NVH complaint is: WHEN does it happen? The answer falls into three categories. Speed-dependent: the noise or vibration changes with vehicle speed — this points to rotating components: wheels, tyres, hubs, driveshafts, wheel bearings. Load-dependent: it changes with engine load or throttle position — this points to engine, exhaust, transmission, or mounts. Road-surface-dependent: it only happens on rough roads — this points to suspension, bushings, or loose interior trim. Get the customer to tell you WHEN, and you've narrowed your search by 70 percent."

**PPT Content:**
```
NVH CLASSIFICATION — WHEN DOES IT HAPPEN?

SPEED-DEPENDENT:
- Changes with vehicle speed (regardless of engine RPM)
- Sources: wheel balance, tyre defect/wear pattern, wheel bearing,
  driveshaft, CV joint, brake rotor warp
- Test: coast in neutral at problem speed — still present?

LOAD-DEPENDENT:
- Changes with throttle position or engine RPM
- Sources: engine mounts, exhaust system, transmission,
  alternator bearing, A/C compressor, power steering pump
- Test: rev engine in neutral — present without driving?

ROAD-SURFACE-DEPENDENT:
- Only on rough/uneven surfaces
- Sources: worn suspension bushings, loose damper mounts,
  loose trim panels, exhaust heat shields, body panel buzz
- Test: smooth road vs rough road comparison

COMBINATION:
- Some faults produce NVH under specific combinations
- Example: vibration at 100 km/h under light throttle only
  (torque converter clutch engagement + out-of-balance driveshaft)

ASK THE CUSTOMER: "When? At what speed? Under what conditions?"
This is the most valuable diagnostic question.
```

#### Slide 12: NVH Diagnostic Tools
**Visual:** Photos of NVH diagnostic tools — chassis stethoscope (long metal probe with earpieces), electronic stethoscope with amplifier, vibration analyzer display, chassis ears (wireless transmitter clips).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The most useful NVH diagnostic tool costs about 10 euros — the chassis stethoscope. It's a metal probe with a diaphragm that you press against components while the engine is running or the vehicle is on a lift with wheels spinning. You can pinpoint a noisy bearing, a buzzing heat shield, or a clicking CV joint by touching the probe to different components and listening through the earpieces. For more advanced diagnosis, electronic stethoscopes amplify the sound, and chassis ears — wireless transmitters that clip to suspected components — let you listen inside the cabin while driving. Vibration analyzers are specialist tools that measure frequency and amplitude, but for most service work, your ears and the stethoscope are sufficient."

**PPT Content:**
```
NVH DIAGNOSTIC TOOLS

CHASSIS STETHOSCOPE (essential — every technician should own one):
- Metal probe transmits vibration to earpieces
- Touch probe to component — listen for the noise source
- Use on: bearings, mounts, heat shields, pumps, pulleys
- Cost: 10-20 EUR — best value diagnostic tool in the box

ELECTRONIC STETHOSCOPE:
- Battery-powered amplifier with headphones
- Higher sensitivity than mechanical stethoscope
- Some models record audio for comparison

CHASSIS EARS (wireless NVH clips):
- Small wireless transmitters clip to suspected components
- Receiver inside cabin with channel selector
- Drive the vehicle and switch between channels
- Identifies which component produces the noise

VIBRATION ANALYZER (specialist):
- Measures vibration frequency (Hz) and amplitude (mm/s)
- Used by: NVH specialists, driveline diagnostics
- Not required for general service but useful for awareness
- Many OEM scan tools have basic vibration analysis functions

YOUR BEST TOOL: Systematic methodology + experienced ears.
```

#### Slide 13: Road Test Methodology for NVH
**Visual:** Road test checklist — pre-test cabin preparation, test route with speed zones, data recording sheet.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The road test is where you confirm and characterise the NVH complaint. Before driving, prepare the cabin: turn off the radio and HVAC, close all windows, and brief your passenger — if you have one — to stay quiet during the test. Drive on a route that includes smooth road, rough road, and a section where you can safely reach motorway speeds. At each condition, note what you hear, feel, and where it's coming from — front, rear, left, right, floor, steering wheel, pedals. Change conditions systematically: coast vs power, straight vs cornering, braking vs free-rolling. Build a symptom profile. Then go back to the workshop and test each suspected source with your stethoscope."

**PPT Content:**
```
NVH ROAD TEST METHODOLOGY

PREPARATION:
1. Confirm customer complaint description (speed, conditions, location)
2. Turn off radio, HVAC, heated seats — minimise background noise
3. Close all windows
4. Note tyre pressures (incorrect pressure changes NVH dramatically)

TEST ROUTE:
- Smooth road surface section (isolate non-road-surface NVH)
- Rough road surface section (identify road-surface-dependent NVH)
- Speed range: 30, 50, 80, 100, 120 km/h (or as road allows)
- Include left and right turns (wheel bearing load change test)

SYSTEMATIC VARIATION:
- Coast in neutral vs power in gear (speed vs load dependent)
- Light throttle vs heavy throttle (engine/driveline NVH)
- Brake gently vs hard (brake-related NVH)
- A/C on vs off (compressor/belt NVH)
- Different gear positions (transmission NVH)

RECORD:
- Speed at onset
- Where felt/heard (steering wheel, seat, pedals, dash)
- Direction (front, rear, left, right)
- Constant vs intermittent
- Getting worse, stable, or improving

RETURN TO WORKSHOP: Target specific components with stethoscope.
```

#### Slide 15: Practical — Interior Trim Rattle Diagnosis
**Visual:** Photo of a technician pressing on dashboard trim sections while an assistant listens for a rattle — systematic elimination technique.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Second NVH exercise: finding a trim rattle. I've deliberately loosened one interior trim clip on the workshop vehicle. With the engine running and a source of vibration present, your job is to find it. The technique: press firmly on each trim panel section in turn. When you press on the section with the loose clip, the rattle stops. Release pressure — it starts again. That's your culprit. Mark the location, identify the clip type, and re-secure it. This technique works on every rattle complaint — systematic elimination by applying pressure to one section at a time."

**PPT Content:**
```
PRACTICAL: INTERIOR TRIM RATTLE DIAGNOSIS (10 min)

SCENARIO: One trim clip has been deliberately loosened.
Find it.

TECHNIQUE:
1. Engine running (vibration source)
2. Press firmly on each trim panel section
3. When pressing STOPS the rattle — that's the location
4. Release pressure — rattle returns (confirmed)
5. Identify clip type and secure it

SEARCH ORDER:
- Dashboard top → dashboard lower
- Door cards (driver → passenger)
- Centre console → glovebox
- A-pillar covers → B-pillar covers
- Parcel shelf / boot trim

WHEN FOUND:
- Identify the loose clip
- Re-secure (press home or replace)
- Verify rattle eliminated

This technique works for EVERY rattle complaint.
Systematic elimination > random guessing.
```

#### Slide 16: NVH Summary — The Diagnostic Approach
**Visual:** Flowchart summarizing the NVH diagnostic process from customer complaint to repair verification.

**Instructor Narration:**

NVH DIAGNOSTIC PROCESS

2. CLASSIFY: Speed-dependent / Load-dependent / Road-surface-dependent
3. ROAD TEST: Confirm complaint, characterise conditions
4. ISOLATE: Systematic variation (neutral/gear, A/C on/off, coast/power)
5. PINPOINT: Stethoscope on suspected components
6. DIAGNOSE: Identify root cause component
7. REPAIR: Replace/secure the faulty component
8. VERIFY: Second road test — complaint resolved

Key NVH lesson:
The customer's description is your starting point.
The road test is your confirmation.
The stethoscope is your pinpointing tool.
Systematic methodology is your advantage over guesswork.

## Key Takeaways

1. A car audio system is just an electrical circuit — source, amplifier, speakers, wiring, power, ground
2. Power cables and signal cables must be separated to prevent alternator whine
3. NVH stands for Noise, Vibration, Harshness — the most common customer complaint category
4. Classify NVH first: speed-dependent, load-dependent, or road-surface-dependent
5. The chassis stethoscope is the most valuable NVH tool — 10 euros, every technician should own one
6. Systematic road test methodology beats random guessing every time
7. Four career paths: dealership, independent, fleet, specialist — all are valid
8. Never stop learning — the next certification is the next step in your career
9. The technician's mindset — curious, honest, methodical, proud — is more valuable than any tool
