#### Slide 5: Routing Principle 1 — Follow the OEM Route
**Visual:** Engine bay photo with OEM harness routing highlighted in colour overlays — showing how the harness follows the inner wing, crosses behind the engine at specific points, uses factory clip locations.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Rule number one: follow the OEM route. The manufacturer spent thousands of engineering hours deciding where every wire goes. They ran thermal analysis, vibration simulations, and crash tests. The factory route avoids every hazard, uses every clip location, and allows for maintenance access. When you repair or replace a harness section, it goes back EXACTLY where the original was. Not close. Not approximately. Exactly."

**PPT Content:**
```
ROUTING PRINCIPLE 1: FOLLOW THE OEM ROUTE

Why:
- OEM route is thermally validated (away from heat sources)
- Vibration tested (secured at correct intervals)
- Crash tested (breakaway points designed in)
- Maintenance clearance verified (won't block access to service items)

How to find the OEM route:
- Workshop manual / service information system
- Photo documentation BEFORE removing damaged harness
- Follow existing clip locations and tie-down points
- Look for witness marks on insulation and body panels

NEVER invent your own route unless no other option exists.
```

#### Slide 6: Routing Principle 2 — Avoid Heat Sources
**Visual:** Engine bay thermal map overlay showing red zones (exhaust manifold 400-800 deg C, turbo 700-900 deg C, catalytic converter 300-600 deg C) and safe routing corridors in green.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The exhaust manifold runs at 400 to 800 degrees Celsius. The turbocharger gets even hotter. Standard automotive wire insulation starts to degrade at 105 degrees and melts at around 150. That means even being NEAR a heat source — not touching it — can damage insulation over time. The rule is simple: minimum 25mm clearance from any heat source. If you can't achieve that, use heat-resistant sleeving rated for the temperature zone."

**PPT Content:**
```
ROUTING PRINCIPLE 2: AVOID HEAT SOURCES

Temperature zones:
- Exhaust manifold: 400-800 deg C
- Turbocharger housing: 700-900 deg C
- Catalytic converter: 300-600 deg C
- EGR valve/pipe: 200-500 deg C
- Engine block surface: 80-120 deg C

Standard wire insulation limits:
- PVC: 80-105 deg C continuous
- Cross-linked PE: 125 deg C continuous
- Silicone: 200 deg C continuous

MINIMUM 25mm clearance from any component above 100 deg C.
When closer clearance is unavoidable: heat-resistant sleeving.
```

#### Slide 8: Loom Tape Types — PVC, Cloth, Fleece
**Visual:** Three tape samples shown side by side with close-up texture, plus application zone diagram on a vehicle.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Not all tape is the same. PVC tape is smooth, waterproof, and abrasion-resistant — use it in the engine bay and underbody where moisture and heat are concerns. Cloth tape — sometimes called friction tape or harness tape — has a fabric backing that doesn't unwind itself. It's used in interior areas, dashboard harnesses, and where the harness needs to be quiet. Fleece tape is the OEM favourite for interior work — it's soft, absorbs vibration, prevents rattles, and moulds itself to the harness shape. Never use standard electrical tape for harness work — it unwinds in heat, leaves adhesive residue, and looks unprofessional."

**PPT Content:**
```
LOOM TAPE SELECTION

PVC TAPE (e.g., Tesa 51036)
- Smooth, shiny surface
- Waterproof, chemical resistant
- Temperature range: -40 to +105 deg C
- USE: Engine bay, underbody, wheel arch areas
- Wrap method: Half-overlap spiral wrap

CLOTH TAPE (e.g., Tesa 51608)
- Woven fabric backing
- Stays put — does not unwind
- Good abrasion resistance
- USE: Transition areas, branch points, general purpose

FLEECE TAPE (e.g., Tesa 51612)
- Soft, conformable, non-woven
- Excellent NVH (noise/vibration) damping
- OEM standard for interior harnesses
- USE: Dashboard, door, headliner, any cabin harness
- Wrap method: Half-overlap spiral wrap

NEVER USE: Standard electrical insulation tape for harness work.
```

#### Slide 10: Splice Techniques — Solder, Crimp, Ultrasonic
**Visual:** Three-panel diagram showing each splice method step-by-step — solder splice with heat shrink, crimp splice with butt connector, ultrasonic weld cross-section.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "When you need to join wires — to repair a break, add a circuit, or replace a damaged section — there are three methods. Solder splice: strip, tin, join, heat shrink. It's the traditional craft method, produces a low-resistance joint, and is the standard for most independent workshop repairs. Crimp splice: strip, insert into a butt connector or splice clip, crimp with the correct tool. Faster than solder, and the OEM-preferred method when done with the right crimp tool — not pliers, not your teeth, the CORRECT ratcheting crimp tool. Ultrasonic welding: a factory process where vibration fuses the copper strands together at the molecular level. You won't do this in the workshop, but you'll see it on OEM harnesses — it looks like a small flattened blob on the wire."

**PPT Content:**
```
SPLICE TECHNIQUES

SOLDER SPLICE (Workshop standard)
1. Strip 10mm of insulation from each wire end
2. Tin each end with rosin-core solder (60/40 or lead-free)
3. Slide adhesive-lined heat shrink over one wire FIRST
4. Hook or overlap stripped ends, solder joint
5. Slide heat shrink over joint, shrink with heat gun
Result: Low resistance, waterproof, permanent

CRIMP SPLICE (OEM-preferred for production repairs)
1. Strip to correct length (per connector specification)
2. Insert into butt connector or splice clip
3. Crimp with RATCHETING crimp tool (ensures correct force)
4. Tug test — wire must not pull out
5. Seal with adhesive heat shrink if exposed to moisture
Result: Fast, repeatable, mechanically strong

ULTRASONIC WELD (Factory only)
- High-frequency vibration fuses copper strands
- Zero added material — pure copper-to-copper bond
- Seen on OEM harnesses as flattened junction points
- Cannot be replicated in workshop — awareness only
```

#### Slide 11: Connector Pinning and De-Pinning
**Visual:** Exploded view of a multi-pin automotive connector showing housing, terminal lock tab, terminal (male pin and female socket types), secondary lock, and seal. Plus photos of terminal release tools of various sizes.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "This is where most DIY repairs go wrong. Automotive connectors are precision components. Each terminal has a lock tab that clicks into the housing. To remove a terminal, you need a terminal release tool — a thin metal or plastic probe that depresses the lock tab while you slide the terminal out from the BACK of the connector. Never pull from the wire side without releasing the lock. Never pry with a screwdriver. Never force it. A damaged connector housing or bent terminal creates an intermittent connection that can take hours to diagnose."

**PPT Content:**
```
CONNECTOR PINNING & DE-PINNING

TERMINAL RELEASE (De-pinning):
1. Identify connector type and terminal size
2. Select correct terminal release tool (0.8mm, 1.5mm, 2.8mm typical)
3. Remove secondary lock (if present — usually a coloured slider)
4. Insert release tool from FRONT of connector alongside terminal
5. Depress lock tab while gently pulling wire from REAR
6. Terminal slides out intact

TERMINAL INSERTION (Re-pinning):
1. Verify terminal is correct type and size for housing
2. Ensure wire is crimped to terminal before insertion
3. Insert terminal from REAR of connector housing
4. Push until lock tab CLICKS into position
5. Tug test — terminal must not pull out
6. Re-engage secondary lock

CRITICAL RULES:
- Never use screwdrivers or picks to remove terminals
- Never pull wire without releasing lock tab
- Replace damaged housings — do not force bent terminals back in
- Match terminal material (tin, gold, silver) to original
```

#### Slide 12: Wire Gauge Selection for Repairs
**Visual:** Wire gauge chart showing AWG and metric mm-squared equivalents, with current capacity ratings. Colour-coded zones: green (safe), yellow (marginal), red (undersized).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "When you replace a wire, you match the gauge of the original — or go one size up. NEVER go smaller. A thinner wire has higher resistance and lower current capacity. Under load, it heats up. At best, it causes a voltage drop that makes the circuit underperform. At worst, it melts the insulation and starts a fire. Check the original wire gauge — it's usually printed on the insulation. If you can't read it, measure the conductor diameter with calipers and look it up. When in doubt, go one size up."

**PPT Content:**
```
WIRE GAUGE SELECTION FOR REPAIRS

RULE: Match original gauge OR go ONE size up. NEVER go down.

Common automotive wire gauges:
  0.35 mm sq (22 AWG) — Signal wires, sensors, low-current
  0.50 mm sq (20 AWG) — Interior lighting, switch circuits
  0.75 mm sq (18 AWG) — General lighting, solenoids
  1.00 mm sq (16 AWG) — Relays, motors, heavier loads
  1.50 mm sq (14 AWG) — Fuel pump, fan motors
  2.50 mm sq (12 AWG) — Headlights, high-current accessories
  4.00 mm sq (10 AWG) — Starter motor signal, high-power accessories
  6.00+ mm sq           — Battery cables, main power feeds

How to identify original gauge:
- Printed on insulation (e.g., "0.75" or "FLRY-B 1.0")
- Measure conductor diameter with calipers
- Reference wiring diagram for circuit amperage rating

Undersized wire = voltage drop + heat + fire risk.
```

#### Slide 14: Demonstration — Solder Splice Technique
**Visual:** Step-by-step photo sequence of a perfect solder splice — strip, tin, join, heat shrink.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Watch carefully. I'm going to show you a solder splice from start to finish. First, strip 10mm of insulation — use the correct slot on your stripper, don't nick the copper. Slide the heat shrink on BEFORE you solder — everyone forgets this at least once. Tin both wire ends separately — the solder should wick into the strands, not sit as a blob on top. Now hook the two tinned ends together and reflow the solder. Smooth, shiny joint — no cold solder balls. Slide the heat shrink over the joint and heat from the centre outward. You should see adhesive squeeze out at both ends. That's a waterproof, mechanically sound, low-resistance splice. Your turn."

**PPT Content:**
```
SOLDER SPLICE — STEP BY STEP

1. CUT wire ends clean and square
2. STRIP 10mm of insulation (correct stripper size)
3. SLIDE adhesive-lined heat shrink onto one wire (DO THIS FIRST)
4. TIN each wire end separately — solder wicks INTO strands
5. HOOK ends together (or overlap for inline splice)
6. REFLOW solder — smooth, shiny, wetted joint
7. INSPECT — no cold joints, no excessive solder blobs
8. SLIDE heat shrink over joint, centered
9. HEAT with heat gun — shrink from center outward
10. VERIFY adhesive squeeze-out at both ends

Quality checks:
- Shiny, smooth solder surface (not grainy = cold joint)
- Heat shrink fully sealed — adhesive visible at ends
- Tug test: joint does not separate under firm pull
- Continuity: 0 ohms across splice
```

#### Slide 15: Demonstration — Crimp Splice Technique
**Visual:** Step-by-step photo sequence of a crimp splice using a ratcheting crimp tool and insulated butt connector.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Crimp splice now. This is actually the OEM-preferred method because it's faster and more repeatable than solder. But it requires the right tool. See this ratcheting crimper? It won't open until it's applied the correct compression force. That's the key — consistent, correct crimp force every time. Strip the wire to the length marked on the butt connector. Insert until the conductor is visible through the inspection window. Crimp. The ratchet releases. Tug test — pull firmly. If it comes out, your strip length was wrong or the connector is the wrong size. Do it again."

**PPT Content:**
```
CRIMP SPLICE — STEP BY STEP

1. SELECT correct butt connector for wire gauge:
   - Red: 0.5-1.5 mm sq
   - Blue: 1.5-2.5 mm sq
   - Yellow: 4.0-6.0 mm sq

2. STRIP wire to correct length (usually 6-8mm)
3. INSERT wire into connector — conductor visible in window
4. CRIMP with RATCHETING tool — do not release until click
5. REPEAT for second wire on other side
6. TUG TEST both sides — firm pull, wire must not move
7. APPLY adhesive heat shrink over connector (for moisture areas)

PROHIBITED:
- Crimping with pliers (inconsistent force, fails tug test)
- Crimping with standard (non-ratcheting) crimper
- Using mismatched connector colour to wire gauge
- Skipping the tug test
```

#### Slide 16: Demonstration — Terminal Release and Re-Pinning
**Visual:** Close-up video still frames showing terminal release tool insertion, lock tab depression, terminal extraction, and reinsertion with click.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "This is the skill that takes the most finesse. I have a 6-pin Bosch connector here. First, remove the secondary lock — this small plastic slider. **PPT Content:**
```
TERMINAL RELEASE & RE-PINNING — DEMONSTRATION

DE-PINNING:
1. Remove secondary lock (if present)
2. Identify terminal type and select release tool size
3. Insert tool from CONNECTOR FACE (front)
4. Depress lock tab — feel it release
5. Pull wire gently from REAR — terminal slides out
6. Inspect terminal for damage (bent tabs, corrosion)

RE-PINNING:
1. Confirm correct cavity position (check pin-out diagram)
2. Orient terminal lock tab correctly (aligns with housing slot)
3. Insert from REAR of connector housing
4. Push firmly until CLICK — lock tab engages
5. Tug test — terminal locked
6. Re-engage secondary lock

COMMON MISTAKES:
- Pulling wire without releasing lock tab (bends terminal)
- Wrong release tool size (doesn't reach lock tab)
- Forcing terminal into wrong cavity (damages housing)
```

## Key Takeaways

1. OEM routing exists for a reason — follow it, do not invent your own
2. The four enemies of wiring are heat, chafing, water, and mechanical fatigue — every protection method fights one of these
3. PVC tape for engine bay, fleece tape for cabin, cloth tape for transitions
4. Solder splice requires tinning, hooking, reflowing, and sealed heat shrink
5. Crimp splice requires a ratcheting tool, correct connector size, and a tug test
6. Terminal release tools prevent damage — never force a terminal out
7. Wire gauge: match original or go one size up, never down
