#### Slide 4: The Valve Train — Overview
**Visual:** Animated or sequential cutaway diagram showing the complete valve train: camshaft lobe pushing lifter, pushrod (OHV) or direct acting (DOHC), rocker arm, valve spring, valve moving in the guide, opening into the combustion chamber. Show both OHC and OHV configurations side by side.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The valve train is the mechanism that opens and closes the intake and exhaust valves. Think of it as a chain of components that converts the camshaft's rotary motion into the valve's linear up-and-down motion. Let me walk you through each link in that chain.
>
> At the top, the camshaft spins. It has egg-shaped lobes — and as each lobe rotates, it pushes down on a lifter or a rocker arm. That push compresses a valve spring and opens the valve. When the lobe rotates past, the spring pushes the valve back up — closed. Simple in principle. But every dimension, every clearance, every timing angle matters. Get one wrong, and the engine loses power, burns oil, or destroys itself."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE VALVE TRAIN — THE ENGINE'S DOOR OPERATOR

Function: Open and close intake/exhaust valves at the right time,
for the right duration, by the right amount

The chain of components:
Camshaft lobe → Lifter/Tappet → (Pushrod) → Rocker arm → Valve
                                                           ↕
                                                      Valve spring
                                                      (returns valve to closed)

Two main layouts:
• OHC (Overhead Cam) — camshaft in the cylinder head
  - SOHC: Single Overhead Cam (one cam drives intake + exhaust)
  - DOHC: Dual Overhead Cam (separate cam for intake & exhaust)
• OHV (Overhead Valve / Pushrod) — camshaft in the engine block
  - Uses pushrods and rocker arms to reach valves in the head
```

#### Slide 5: The Camshaft — Lobes, Lift & Duration
**Visual:** Close-up photo of a camshaft with three lobe profiles overlaid: stock mild profile, high-performance profile, and asymmetric profile. Annotations showing lift height, duration in degrees, and base circle.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The camshaft is the brain of the valve train. Each lobe controls one valve. The SHAPE of that lobe determines three critical things. First: LIFT — how far the valve opens. More lift means more airflow. Second: DURATION — how long the valve stays open, measured in crankshaft degrees. More duration means air flows for longer. Third: the PROFILE — the exact shape of the ramp that opens and closes the valve. A gentle ramp is quiet and smooth. An aggressive ramp opens the valve faster for more performance but creates more stress on the components.
>
> The base circle — the round part of the lobe — is where the valve is fully closed. This is where we measure valve clearance. Remember this for the practical."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE CAMSHAFT — SHAPE CONTROLS EVERYTHING

Three critical lobe dimensions:
1. LIFT: How far the valve opens (mm)
   — More lift = more airflow = more power
   — Typical: 8-12 mm (intake), 7-10 mm (exhaust)

2. DURATION: How long the valve stays open (crankshaft degrees)
   — More duration = more time for gas exchange
   — Typical: 200-260 degrees (street engines)

3. PROFILE: The ramp shape (opening/closing rate)
   — Gentle ramp = quiet, smooth, long component life
   — Aggressive ramp = fast opening, more power, more stress

Base circle: The round part — valve is CLOSED here
— This is where valve clearance is measured

REMEMBER: Camshaft turns at HALF crankshaft speed
(1 cam rotation = 2 crank rotations = 1 complete 4-stroke cycle)
```

#### Slide 6: Lifters and Tappets — Hydraulic vs Solid
**Visual:** Cross-section comparison: hydraulic lifter (showing oil chamber, check ball, plunger) next to a solid lifter (simple metal disc/bucket). Photo of each type sitting in a cylinder head.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The lifter — also called a tappet or cam follower — sits between the camshaft lobe and the valve stem. Its job is to transfer the lobe's motion to the valve. There are two types, and understanding the difference matters for your daily work.
>
> SOLID lifters are just that — solid metal. They're simple and reliable, but they need periodic adjustment. As the engine heats up, metal expands. Without the right clearance gap, the valve might not close fully — which means compression loss and burned valves. Too much gap, and you get noisy ticking and reduced lift.
>
> HYDRAULIC lifters solve this automatically. They have an internal oil chamber that expands or contracts to maintain zero clearance at all times. Quiet, maintenance-free — but they depend on clean oil at the right pressure. Dirty oil or low pressure, and hydraulic lifters collapse and tick. You'll hear that sound in the workshop — it's one of the most common engine complaints."

**PPT Content:**
```
LIFTERS / TAPPETS — THE MIDDLE MAN

SOLID LIFTERS (Mechanical):
• Simple metal disc or bucket
• Require periodic valve clearance adjustment
• Clearance set with shims or adjusting screws
• Typical clearance: 0.15-0.30 mm (intake), 0.20-0.35 mm (exhaust)
• Common on: older engines, high-performance engines, most diesels
• Advantage: Predictable, no oil dependency
• Disadvantage: Needs periodic adjustment

HYDRAULIC LIFTERS (Self-adjusting):
• Internal oil chamber with check ball valve
• Automatically maintain zero lash (no clearance)
• Depend on clean oil at correct pressure
• Common on: most modern gasoline engines
• Advantage: Maintenance-free, quiet
• Disadvantage: Sensitive to oil condition and pressure
• Common failure: Ticking noise = collapsed lifter = oil issue

TECHNICIAN TIP: Ticking noise on cold start that fades = hydraulic
lifters bleeding up (usually normal). Ticking that persists = problem.
```

#### Slide 7: Rocker Arms, Valve Springs, Stem Seals & Guides
**Visual:** Exploded diagram of a complete valve assembly: rocker arm, valve spring (with retainer and keepers/collets), valve stem seal, valve guide pressed into the head, and the valve itself showing the head, margin, stem, and tip. Label every part.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's follow the chain to the valve itself. In pushrod engines and some SOHC designs, a ROCKER ARM acts as a lever — the camshaft pushes one end, the other end pushes the valve open. In DOHC direct-acting designs, the cam pushes directly on the lifter bucket sitting on the valve stem — no rocker needed.
>
> The VALVE SPRING is what pulls the valve back closed. It sits around the valve stem, held in place by a retainer and tiny half-moon clips called keepers or collets. If a spring breaks, the valve drops into the cylinder. On an interference engine — catastrophic.
>
> The VALVE GUIDE is a cylindrical sleeve pressed into the head. The valve stem slides up and down inside it. If the guide wears, the valve wobbles — oil gets sucked into the combustion chamber, you see blue smoke on the exhaust.
>
> The VALVE STEM SEAL sits on top of the guide. It controls how much oil is allowed down the stem for lubrication — enough to prevent scuffing, not so much that it burns in the combustion chamber. Worn stem seals are one of the most common causes of oil consumption and blue smoke on startup."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE COMPLETE VALVE ASSEMBLY

ROCKER ARM (where used):
• Lever that transfers cam motion to valve
• Types: Stamped steel, roller-tip, full roller
• Roller rockers reduce friction and wear

VALVE SPRING:
• Returns valve to closed position
• Held by retainer + keepers (collets)
• Spring rate must match camshaft profile
• Broken spring = valve drops = engine damage

VALVE GUIDE:
• Pressed-in sleeve that guides valve stem
• Worn guide = valve wobble = oil burning (blue smoke)
• Clearance: 0.025-0.075 mm typical

VALVE STEM SEAL:
• Controls oil metering on valve stem
• Worn seal = blue smoke on startup
• One of the most common oil consumption causes

THE VALVE ITSELF:
• Intake: larger diameter (needs more flow)
• Exhaust: smaller, made of tougher alloys (higher temps)
• Valve face angle: typically 45 degrees
• Margin: thin edge of valve head — too thin = burned valve
```

#### Slide 8: Variable Valve Timing (VVT) — Why It Exists
**Visual:** Graph showing engine torque curves: one fixed-timing curve vs an envelope of curves achievable with VVT. Annotate "low-RPM economy" at one end and "high-RPM performance" at the other. Show how VVT allows the engine to fill the entire envelope.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Here's the problem with a fixed camshaft: the best timing for low-RPM fuel economy is NOT the best timing for high-RPM power. At low RPM, you want the intake valve to close early — short duration keeps the mixture in the cylinder for efficient combustion. At high RPM, you want the intake valve to stay open longer — the air is moving fast and can fill the cylinder better with more duration.
>
> Variable Valve Timing solves this by MOVING the camshaft relative to the crankshaft while the engine is running. The ECU decides when to advance or retard the cam timing based on RPM, load, temperature, and driver demand. This gives you fuel economy at cruise AND power when you floor it. It's standard on virtually every engine built in the last 15 years."

**PPT Content:**
```
VARIABLE VALVE TIMING (VVT) — WHY?

THE PROBLEM:
• Fixed cam timing = one compromise for all conditions
• Best timing for idle ≠ best timing for full power
• Best timing for economy ≠ best timing for performance

THE SOLUTION: Move the camshaft while the engine runs
• ADVANCE cam timing: Valves open earlier
  → Better low-RPM torque and fuel economy
• RETARD cam timing: Valves open later
  → Better high-RPM power and exhaust scavenging

ECU decides based on:
• Engine RPM
• Throttle position / load
• Coolant temperature
• Target emissions vs performance balance

Result: Engine operates across entire torque envelope
instead of being locked to one fixed compromise
```

#### Slide 9: VVT Systems — How They Work
**Visual:** Cross-section of an oil-controlled cam phaser (vane-type actuator) showing the rotor, housing, oil passages, and OCV (Oil Control Valve). Side diagrams of Toyota VVT-i, BMW VANOS, and Honda VTEC with brief mechanism notes.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Most VVT systems use a cam phaser — a hydraulic actuator bolted to the front of the camshaft. Inside is a vane rotor connected to the camshaft and a housing connected to the timing drive. Oil pressure, directed by an Oil Control Valve controlled by the ECU, pushes the vanes to advance or retard the cam relative to the housing. Change the oil pressure direction, change the cam timing.
>
> Toyota calls theirs VVT-i — Variable Valve Timing with intelligence. BMW calls theirs VANOS — Variable Nockenwellensteuerung, which is German for variable cam control. They're fundamentally the same idea: oil-controlled cam phasers.
>
> Honda VTEC is different — it switches between two different cam lobe profiles rather than rotating the entire cam. At low RPM, a mild cam lobe is used. At high RPM, a pin locks the rocker arms together to follow an aggressive high-lift lobe. That's why Honda engines have that famous 'VTEC kick' — it's literally switching to a different cam profile.
>
> For you as a technician, the key takeaway is this: VVT depends on clean oil at correct pressure. Oil sludge, wrong viscosity, or low oil level will cause VVT faults. When you see a P0010-P0025 code range on a scan tool, think cam phaser and oil condition first."

**PPT Content:**
```
VVT SYSTEMS — HOW THEY WORK

COMMON TYPE: Oil-Controlled Cam Phaser (vane-type)
• Rotor connected to camshaft
• Housing connected to timing sprocket/gear
• Oil Control Valve (OCV/solenoid) directed by ECU
• Oil pressure rotates vanes → advances or retards cam
• Typical range: 25-50 degrees of crankshaft adjustment

MANUFACTURER SYSTEMS:
Toyota VVT-i:   Oil-controlled cam phaser (intake cam)
  Dual VVT-i:   Phasers on both intake AND exhaust cams

BMW VANOS:      Oil-controlled cam phaser
  Double VANOS: Both cams, stepless adjustment

Honda VTEC:     Switches between 2 different cam lobe profiles
  i-VTEC:       VTEC + cam phaser combined

TECHNICIAN KEY POINT:
VVT depends on CLEAN OIL at CORRECT PRESSURE
• Sludge, wrong viscosity, low oil level → VVT failure
• Common DTCs: P0010-P0025 (cam position/timing)
• First check: Oil level, oil condition, OCV filter screen
```

#### Slide 10: Valve Overlap — The Hidden Efficiency Trick
**Visual:** Circular timing diagram showing intake valve opening, intake valve closing, exhaust valve opening, exhaust valve closing, with the overlap period highlighted. Show how VVT can increase or decrease the overlap zone.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "There's one more concept you need before we move to timing systems: valve overlap. This is the brief period when BOTH the intake and exhaust valves are open at the same time — right around Top Dead Centre at the end of the exhaust stroke and the beginning of the intake stroke.
>
> Why would you want both doors open at once? Because the outgoing exhaust gas creates a vacuum effect that helps pull fresh air into the cylinder. More overlap at high RPM means better cylinder filling and more power. But too much overlap at low RPM causes rough idle and poor emissions — exhaust gas re-enters the intake.
>
> This is exactly what VVT controls. At low RPM: minimal overlap for smooth idle. At high RPM: increased overlap for maximum volumetric efficiency. The ECU adjusts this continuously, thousands of times per minute."

**PPT Content:**
```
VALVE OVERLAP — BOTH DOORS OPEN

Overlap = period when BOTH intake and exhaust valves are open
• Occurs around TDC between exhaust and intake strokes
• Typically 10-40 degrees of crankshaft rotation

WHY OVERLAP EXISTS:
• Outgoing exhaust creates suction (scavenging effect)
• Helps pull fresh charge into the cylinder
• Improves volumetric efficiency at high RPM

THE TRADE-OFF:
More overlap:  + Better high-RPM power
               - Rough idle, poor low-RPM emissions

Less overlap:  + Smooth idle, clean emissions
               - Less power at high RPM

VVT SOLVES THIS:
• ECU adjusts overlap continuously
• Idle: Minimal overlap (smooth, clean)
• Full load: Maximum overlap (power, efficiency)
• Part load: Optimized for fuel economy
```

#### Slide 12: The Timing System — The Engine's Heartbeat
**Visual:** Front view of an engine with the timing cover removed, showing the crankshaft sprocket, camshaft sprocket(s), and timing chain connecting them. Highlight the timing marks aligned.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "If the valve train is the mechanism that opens and closes the valves, the timing system is the mechanism that synchronizes WHEN they open and close relative to the piston position. It connects the crankshaft to the camshaft and keeps them in perfect sync.
>
> Think of it like this: the crankshaft is the conductor of the orchestra, and the camshafts are the musicians. The timing belt or chain is the link between them. If that link slips, stretches, or breaks — the conductor and the musicians are out of sync. In a best case, the engine runs terribly. In a worst case — and this is a very real worst case — the piston crashes into the valve. That's catastrophic engine damage. Bent valves, cracked pistons, destroyed head. This is why timing service is one of the highest-stakes jobs you'll do as a technician."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE TIMING SYSTEM — THE ENGINE'S HEARTBEAT

Function: Synchronize crankshaft rotation with camshaft rotation
• Ensures valves open and close at EXACTLY the right moment
  relative to piston position

The connection:
Crankshaft sprocket → Timing belt or chain → Camshaft sprocket(s)

Ratio: 2:1 (crankshaft turns twice for every one camshaft turn)
• 4-stroke cycle = 2 crankshaft revolutions = 1 camshaft revolution

WHY IT'S CRITICAL:
If timing slips or belt breaks:
→ Best case: Engine stops, won't restart
→ Worst case: PISTON HITS VALVE = catastrophic engine damage

This is why timing service is HIGH-STAKES work.
Precision matters. Every. Single. Tooth.
```

#### Slide 13: Timing Belt vs Timing Chain
**Visual:** Side-by-side comparison with cross-section photos: timing belt (rubber with fiberglass/Kevlar reinforcement, toothed profile) and timing chain (roller chain, similar to heavy-duty bicycle chain). Include a worn belt showing cracking and a stretched chain.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "There are two ways to connect crankshaft to camshaft: a belt or a chain. Each has pros and cons, and you need to know both because you'll service both.
>
> A TIMING BELT is made of rubber reinforced with fiberglass or Kevlar cords. It runs on toothed pulleys. It's quiet, lightweight, and doesn't need lubrication. The downside: rubber degrades. Heat, oil contamination, age — they all weaken the belt. That's why timing belts have a fixed service interval, typically every 60,000 to 120,000 km or 5-7 years, whichever comes first. Miss that interval, and you're gambling with the engine.
>
> A TIMING CHAIN is metal — a roller chain running on sprockets. It lives inside the engine, lubricated by engine oil. It lasts much longer — often the life of the engine. But chains stretch over time. A stretched chain retards the cam timing, causes rattle on startup, and eventually can skip a tooth. Chain-driven engines also have hydraulic tensioners and guides that wear.
>
> Neither system is 'better' — they're engineering trade-offs. Your job is to know the service requirements for whichever system is in front of you."

**PPT Content:**
```
TIMING BELT vs TIMING CHAIN

TIMING BELT (Rubber, toothed):
• Material: Rubber + fiberglass/Kevlar reinforcement
• Runs on: Toothed pulleys
• Lubrication: None needed (runs dry, external to engine)
• Noise: Very quiet
• Service interval: 60,000-120,000 km or 5-7 years
• Failure mode: Snaps without warning
• Cost to replace: Moderate (labour-intensive, belt is cheap)
• Common on: Many VW, Audi, Volvo, Fiat, Renault, older Toyota

TIMING CHAIN (Metal, roller):
• Material: Steel roller chain
• Runs on: Metal sprockets
• Lubrication: Engine oil (runs inside the engine)
• Noise: Louder than belt (normal light whirr)
• Service life: Often engine lifetime (150,000+ km)
• Failure mode: Stretches over time → timing drift, rattle
• Cost to replace: Higher (often front-of-engine teardown)
• Common on: BMW, Mercedes, many modern engines

BOTH REQUIRE: Tensioners, guides/idlers, correct alignment
```

#### Slide 14: Tensioners, Idlers & Guides
**Visual:** Annotated diagram of a complete timing belt system showing: crankshaft sprocket, camshaft sprockets (intake + exhaust on DOHC), hydraulic tensioner with spring backup, mechanical tensioner, idler pulley, and belt routing path. Second diagram showing chain tensioner, chain guide rails, and sprockets.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "A timing belt or chain doesn't just run between two sprockets. It needs support components to maintain correct tension and routing.
>
> TENSIONERS keep the belt or chain tight. For belts, you often see a spring-loaded mechanical tensioner or a hydraulic tensioner backed by a spring. For chains, a hydraulic tensioner fed by engine oil pressure is standard — it pushes against a plastic guide rail that contacts the chain.
>
> IDLER PULLEYS redirect the belt path. They're smooth pulleys — no teeth — that route the belt around obstructions or to water pump sprockets.
>
> GUIDE RAILS on chain systems prevent the chain from whipping. They're plastic and they wear over time. Worn guides are a common source of timing chain rattle. On some engines — BMW N47 diesel, for example — guide failure can lead to chain skip and catastrophic valve-to-piston contact.
>
> When you replace a timing belt, you replace ALL of these components as a kit. Belt, tensioner, idler pulleys, and usually the water pump if it's belt-driven. Never reuse old tensioners or idlers — they're wear items."

**PPT Content:**
```
TENSIONERS, IDLERS & GUIDES

BELT TENSIONER:
• Maintains correct belt tension
• Types: Spring-loaded mechanical, hydraulic with spring backup
• Must be replaced with belt (never reuse)

IDLER PULLEYS:
• Smooth (non-toothed) pulleys
• Route belt around engine components
• Bearings wear out → whining noise, then seizure
• Replace as part of timing belt kit

CHAIN TENSIONER:
• Hydraulic piston fed by engine oil pressure
• Pushes against chain guide rail
• Depends on oil pressure — no oil = no tension
• Ratchet mechanism prevents chain slack on shutdown

CHAIN GUIDE RAILS:
• Plastic rails that prevent chain whip
• Wear over time — plastic cracks, breaks
• Failed guide = chain rattle, possible chain skip

SERVICE RULE:
Timing belt replacement = FULL KIT (belt + tensioner + idlers + water pump)
```

#### Slide 15: Interference vs Non-Interference Engines
**Visual:** Cross-section animation or sequential diagram showing: (1) Interference engine with timing intact — piston at TDC, valve fully open, clearance is ZERO (they occupy same space at different times). (2) Timing belt breaks — piston at TDC while valve is stuck open — CONTACT, bent valve, cracked piston. Compare with non-interference engine where there's always clearance even with timing lost.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "This is the slide that should make you take timing service seriously for the rest of your career. An INTERFERENCE engine is designed so that the piston and valve paths overlap. They occupy the same physical space — but at DIFFERENT times, kept apart by the timing system. As long as timing is correct, there's no contact. If the belt breaks or the chain skips, the piston and valve are in the same place at the same time. Metal hits metal. Valves bend. Pistons crack. Connecting rods can bend. The head is destroyed, sometimes the block too.
>
> A NON-INTERFERENCE engine has enough clearance that even with timing lost, the piston can never reach the valve. The engine stops, but nothing is damaged. You just fit a new belt and carry on.
>
> Here's the critical fact: MOST modern engines are interference designs. Almost all diesels. Most modern gasoline direct injection engines. If a customer comes in with a timing belt at 130,000 km and the service interval is 120,000 km — that's an emergency. That belt is a ticking time bomb."

**PPT Content:**
```
INTERFERENCE vs NON-INTERFERENCE ENGINES

INTERFERENCE ENGINE:
• Piston and valve paths OVERLAP
• Timing keeps them separated in TIME, not space
• If timing fails: PISTON HITS VALVE
  → Bent valves (certain)
  → Cracked pistons (likely)
  → Bent connecting rods (possible)
  → Destroyed cylinder head (probable)
  → Repair cost: Often exceeds engine value

NON-INTERFERENCE ENGINE:
• Piston and valve paths do NOT overlap
• If timing fails: Engine stops, no contact damage
• Refit timing belt/chain → engine runs again

CRITICAL FACT:
MOST MODERN ENGINES ARE INTERFERENCE DESIGNS
• Nearly ALL diesels (high compression ratio)
• Most modern gasoline DI engines
• Always check — never assume

THIS IS WHY TIMING SERVICE IS HIGH-STAKES WORK.
```

#### Slide 17: Finding TDC — The Foundation of All Timing Work
**Visual:** Step-by-step photo sequence: (1) Remove spark plug from cylinder 1, (2) Insert TDC stop or place finger over plug hole, (3) Rotate crankshaft clockwise by hand with breaker bar, (4) Feel compression building / piston approaching stop, (5) Verify with dial gauge in spark plug hole showing maximum piston height, (6) Confirm timing marks aligned. Include image of a degree wheel on the crankshaft damper.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before you can align timing marks, you need to find TDC — Top Dead Centre — on cylinder 1. There are several methods.
>
> The simplest: remove the spark plug from cylinder 1, put your thumb or a rubber stopper over the hole, and rotate the crankshaft by hand. When you feel air pressure building, the piston is coming up on the compression stroke. Continue until the timing mark on the crankshaft damper aligns with the zero-degree reference on the block. That's TDC.
>
> For precision work — especially when verifying timing with a dial gauge — you insert a dial indicator into the spark plug hole. Rotate the crank slowly. The gauge reads maximum when the piston is at exact TDC. The positive-stop method uses a tool that physically blocks the piston — you find the midpoint between the stop on both sides of rotation, and that midpoint is true TDC.
>
> You'll practice the thumb method and the dial gauge method today."

**PPT Content:**
```
FINDING TDC — TOP DEAD CENTRE

WHY: TDC is the reference point for all timing work
• Timing marks are set relative to TDC cylinder 1 (compression stroke)

METHOD 1: Thumb/Pressure Test (quick check)
1. Remove cylinder 1 spark plug
2. Cover plug hole with thumb or rubber plug
3. Rotate crankshaft clockwise by hand (breaker bar on crank bolt)
4. Feel air pressure = compression stroke
5. Continue to timing mark = TDC

METHOD 2: Dial Gauge (precision)
1. Thread dial gauge adapter into spark plug hole
2. Rotate crankshaft slowly
3. Maximum reading = TDC (piston at highest point)
4. Accuracy: ± 0.01 mm

METHOD 3: Positive Stop (most accurate)
1. Install stop tool in plug hole
2. Rotate crank until piston contacts stop — note position
3. Rotate crank in opposite direction until contact — note position
4. True TDC = midpoint between the two positions

COMPRESSION TDC vs EXHAUST TDC:
Both have piston at top — verify by checking valve positions
Compression TDC: Both valves CLOSED (this is what you want)
```

#### Slide 20: Station 2 — Valve Clearance Measurement
**Visual:** Close-up photo sequence: (1) Rotating crankshaft to place cam lobe base circle on target valve, (2) Selecting feeler gauge blade, (3) Inserting blade between cam lobe and lifter bucket, (4) Checking drag/fit — light resistance = correct, (5) Recording measurement and comparing to spec. Include a specification table showing typical clearance values.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Station 2 is where your Day 4 feeler gauge skills come back. You'll measure valve clearance on a cylinder head with solid lifters — the gap between the cam base circle and the top of the lifter when the valve is fully closed.
>
> Step 1: Rotate the crankshaft until the cam lobe for your target valve is pointing AWAY from the lifter — you want the base circle in contact, which means the valve is fully closed and there's no lift.
>
> Step 2: Select a feeler gauge blade matching the specification. Try inserting it between the cam and lifter. It should slide in with light resistance — a slight drag. If it won't go in, the clearance is too tight. If it slides in with no resistance, try the next size up — the clearance might be too loose.
>
> Step 3: Record the actual measurement. Compare it to the manufacturer's specification. Note whether it's intake or exhaust — they have different specs.
>
> If you were doing this on a real service job and the clearance was out of spec, you'd calculate the required shim thickness and swap shims. Today, just measure and record."

#### Slide 23: Station 5 — Piston-to-Wall Clearance Measurement
**Visual:** Photo sequence: (1) Measuring cylinder bore diameter with bore gauge/telescoping gauge at top, middle, bottom (both parallel and perpendicular to crankshaft), (2) Measuring piston diameter at the skirt (perpendicular to pin) with outside micrometer, (3) Calculation: Clearance = Bore diameter minus Piston diameter. Include a recording table.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Station 5 connects directly to Day 4 — you're using your micrometer and bore gauge skills again, but now for a real engine measurement. Piston-to-wall clearance is the gap between the piston skirt and the cylinder bore. Too tight, and the piston seizes as it heats up. Too loose, and you get piston slap — a knocking noise — and excessive oil consumption as oil passes the rings.
>
> You'll measure the cylinder bore diameter at three heights — top, middle, and bottom — and in two directions: parallel to the crankshaft and perpendicular to it. This checks for both taper (top-to-bottom wear) and out-of-round (oval wear). The top of the bore wears more because that's where combustion pressure and temperature are highest.
>
> Then measure the piston diameter at the skirt, perpendicular to the wrist pin bore. Subtract piston diameter from bore diameter — that's your clearance.
>
> Typical specification: 0.02 to 0.05 mm for a gasoline engine. Worn beyond specification? The cylinder needs to be bored oversize, or the block is scrap."

**PPT Content:**
```
STATION 5: PISTON-TO-WALL (PTW) CLEARANCE

TOOLS: Outside micrometer, bore gauge or telescoping gauge, calculator

CYLINDER BORE MEASUREMENT:
Measure at 3 heights × 2 directions = 6 readings per cylinder

Heights:      Top (just below ridge)
              Middle
              Bottom

Directions:   Parallel to crankshaft (A)
              Perpendicular to crankshaft (B)

Check for:
• Taper = Top reading minus Bottom reading
  — Max taper: typically 0.05 mm
• Out-of-round = Direction A minus Direction B (at same height)
  — Max out-of-round: typically 0.03 mm

PISTON MEASUREMENT:
• Measure skirt diameter perpendicular to wrist pin bore
• Use outside micrometer at the point specified by manufacturer

CLEARANCE CALCULATION:
PTW Clearance = Bore diameter − Piston diameter
Typical spec: 0.020 – 0.050 mm (gasoline)
              0.035 – 0.060 mm (diesel — higher thermal expansion)

Out of spec → Bore needs honing/reboring, oversize piston required
```

#### Slide 24: Fastener Preload — Torque-to-Yield in Engine Assembly
**Visual:** Diagram showing a torque-to-yield (TTY) bolt stress-strain curve. Mark the elastic zone, the yield point, and the plastic deformation zone. Show how TTY fasteners are tightened INTO the plastic zone for maximum clamping force. Photos of head bolts, connecting rod bolts, and main bearing cap bolts.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before we wrap up, I want to connect something from Day 6 — fastener theory — to what you've seen today. Engine assembly uses some of the most critical fasteners in the entire vehicle. Cylinder head bolts, connecting rod bolts, and main bearing cap bolts are almost always TORQUE-TO-YIELD specification.
>
> Remember what torque-to-yield means: you tighten the bolt to a specific torque value, then continue turning it a specified number of degrees PAST that point. This stretches the bolt just past its elastic limit into the plastic deformation zone. Why? Because a bolt stretched into its yield zone creates the maximum possible clamping force — which is exactly what you need to seal a head gasket against combustion pressures of 50+ bar.
>
> The consequence: TTY bolts CANNOT be reused. Once stretched past yield, they've permanently deformed. Reusing them risks insufficient clamping force or bolt failure. Always use new bolts on head assemblies, con-rod assemblies, and main bearing caps."

**PPT Content:**
```
TORQUE-TO-YIELD (TTY) — ENGINE ASSEMBLY FASTENERS

WHY TTY?
• Maximum clamping force for critical joints
• Essential for sealing against combustion pressure (50+ bar)
• Bolt is stretched PAST elastic limit into plastic zone

TTY PROCEDURE:
1. Apply specified torque value (e.g., 40 Nm)
2. Then turn an additional specified angle (e.g., +90°)
3. Some require multiple stages (e.g., 40 Nm → +90° → +90°)
4. Always follow manufacturer tightening SEQUENCE (pattern)

CRITICAL ENGINE TTY APPLICATIONS:
• Cylinder head bolts (seal head gasket)
• Connecting rod bolts (hold bearing cap to rod)
• Main bearing cap bolts (hold crankshaft in block)
• Flywheel/flexplate bolts

ABSOLUTE RULE: TTY bolts are SINGLE USE
• Once stretched past yield → permanently deformed
• Reuse risks: Insufficient clamp, bolt failure, gasket blow
• ALWAYS use new bolts when reassembling

TIGHTENING SEQUENCE:
Always follow manufacturer pattern (usually inside-out, star pattern)
Wrong sequence → uneven clamping → warped head → blown gasket
```

#### Slide 25: Head Bolt Tightening Sequence — Why Pattern Matters
**Visual:** Top-down diagram of a 4-cylinder head showing 10 head bolt positions numbered in the correct tightening sequence (typically starting from centre, working outward in a spiral or star pattern). 2. BLUE SMOKE ON STARTUP (clears after 30 seconds)
```
   → Cause: Worn valve stem seals — oil pools on guides overnight
   → Fix: Valve stem seal replacement (often head-off job)

3. DTC P0016: Cam/Crank Correlation Error
   → Cause: Timing chain skipped 1+ tooth OR VVT phaser stuck
   → Diagnosis: Check timing marks, check oil condition, test OCV

4. PERSISTENT VALVE TRAIN TICK
   → Hydraulic lifter: Collapsed due to low oil pressure / sludge
   → Solid lifter: Valve clearance out of specification
   → Diagnosis: Identify which cylinder, check oil, measure clearance

TOMORROW (Day 15): Engine Diagnostics — finding these faults systematically
```

## Key Takeaways

1. The valve train converts camshaft rotation into valve linear motion — every component in the chain matters
2. Variable Valve Timing depends on clean oil at correct pressure — oil maintenance directly affects engine performance and efficiency
3. The timing system is the engine's heartbeat — one tooth off can mean catastrophic damage on interference engines
4. Timing belt service is a fixed-interval, high-stakes job — belt, tensioner, idlers, and water pump are always replaced as a kit
5. Piston-to-wall clearance, valve clearance, and head bolt torque are precision measurements where "close enough" destroys engines
6. Torque-to-yield bolts are single-use — always replace on reassembly
