#### Slide 5: ATF — When and How Depending on Type
**Visual:** Four ATF fluid samples in clear containers — fresh (red/pink), slightly used (dark red), degraded (brown), burnt (black). Below: a table comparing conventional ATF change procedures vs. "sealed for life" procedures. Right side: a photo of an ATF dipstick vs. a fill plug on a sealed transmission.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "ATF — Automatic Transmission Fluid — is one of the most debated service items. Some manufacturers say 'lifetime fill' or 'sealed for life.' In practice, no fluid lasts forever. Heat degrades ATF. Metal particles accumulate. The fluid loses its friction properties and its ability to protect seals.
>
> Here's the reality. Conventional automatics with a dipstick — check the level at every major service, change the fluid every 60,000 to 80,000 km. 'Sealed' transmissions — these still have a fill plug, usually on the side of the transmission housing. You check the level by removing the plug with the transmission at operating temperature: if fluid dribbles out, the level is correct; if nothing comes out, it's low. The 'sealed' label means the manufacturer doesn't provide a dipstick to the owner — it does NOT mean the fluid never needs attention.
>
> ATF types matter. Dexron VI is NOT the same as Dexron III. ZF Lifeguard 8 is NOT interchangeable with generic ATF. Use the wrong fluid and you'll cause harsh shifting, slipping, or outright failure. Always — and I mean always — verify the specification in the manufacturer's data before touching the ATF."

**PPT Content:**
```
ATF — AUTOMATIC TRANSMISSION FLUID

Fluid condition (visual check):
 -- FRESH: Red/pink, translucent, mild sweet smell
 -- USED: Darker red, still translucent, acceptable
 -- DEGRADED: Brown, slightly opaque, needs changing
 -- BURNT: Black, opaque, smells burnt — damage may already exist

Service intervals (general guidance):
 -- Conventional (with dipstick): Check every 30,000 km / Change every 60-80,000 km
 -- "Sealed for life": Check every 60,000 km / Change every 80-120,000 km
 -- Heavy use (towing, taxi, hot climate): Halve the intervals

Level check procedure:
 -- Dipstick type: Engine running, transmission in Park, at operating temp (60-80 C)
 -- Sealed type: Fill plug on side of housing, engine running, transmission cycled
   through all gears, at operating temp — fluid should dribble from fill hole

CRITICAL: Use ONLY the specified ATF type
 -- Dexron VI, ZF Lifeguard 8, Toyota WS, Honda DW-1 — NOT interchangeable
 -- Wrong fluid = harsh shift, slip, seal damage, failure
```

#### Slide 6: Gear Oil — Check and Change Intervals
**Visual:** Manual transmission drain plug location photo (bottom of gearbox housing), differential drain and fill plug photo, and a diagram showing the fill-plug-level method. Inset: a comparison of GL-4 vs GL-5 gear oil bottles with specifications highlighted.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Manual transmission gear oil and differential oil are simpler than ATF, but they still need attention. Most manual gearboxes use a GL-4 rated gear oil — 75W-80 or 75W-90 depending on the manufacturer. Differentials often need GL-5, which has more extreme-pressure additives for the hypoid gears. Never put GL-5 in a gearbox that specifies GL-4 — the sulphur-phosphorus additives in GL-5 can attack yellow metals like the brass synchromesh rings.
>
> The level check is the same fill-plug method as sealed automatic transmissions: remove the side fill plug, and if oil is at the level of the hole, you're good. If it's low, top up through the fill hole until it dribbles out. Drain plugs are at the bottom. Typical change interval is 60,000 to 100,000 km or every 3 to 5 years, depending on the manufacturer."

**PPT Content:**
```
GEAR OIL — MANUAL TRANSMISSION & DIFFERENTIAL

Manual transmission:
 -- Typical specification: GL-4 rated, 75W-80 or 75W-90
 -- Check interval: Every 30,000 km or 2 years
 -- Change interval: Every 60,000-100,000 km or 3-5 years
 -- Level check: Fill plug method (remove side plug, oil at plug level = OK)

Differential:
 -- Typical specification: GL-5 rated, 75W-90 or 80W-90
 -- Check interval: Every 30,000 km or 2 years
 -- Change interval: Every 60,000-80,000 km or 3-4 years
 -- Limited-slip differentials: Require LSD additive or specific oil

WARNING: GL-4 vs GL-5 — NOT interchangeable
 -- GL-5 in a GL-4 gearbox can damage brass synchromesh rings
 -- Always verify specification per manufacturer data

Procedure:
 1. Warm the transmission/diff (5-minute drive)
 2. Raise vehicle on lift, level it
 3. Remove fill plug FIRST (if drain plug comes out and fill plug is stuck, you can't refill)
 4. Remove drain plug, drain completely
 5. Replace drain plug with new washer, torque to spec
 6. Fill through fill hole until oil dribbles out
 7. Replace fill plug, torque to spec
```

#### Slide 7: Coolant — Flush Interval and Procedure
**Visual:** Coolant colour comparison (fresh green/pink/blue vs. rusty brown), a cross-section of a corroded heater core vs. a clean one, and a refractometer being used to test coolant freezing point. Callout: "Coolant protects against freezing AND corrosion."

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Coolant — revisited from Week 4, but now in the context of your full service schedule. Coolant does two jobs: it prevents freezing in winter and prevents corrosion year-round. The corrosion inhibitors in coolant degrade over time. Even if the freezing point is still good, the corrosion protection can be exhausted. That's why coolant has a time-based change interval, not just a mileage one.
>
> Typical flush interval is every 5 years or 100,000 to 150,000 km — whichever comes first. Some long-life coolants extend this to 250,000 km, but you still check the condition annually. Use a refractometer to test freezing point — should be minus 35 degrees Celsius or lower. Check pH with test strips — below 7.0 means the inhibitors are depleted. Never mix coolant types — OAT, HOAT, IAT are not interchangeable. Mixing can cause gel formation that blocks the heater core or radiator."

**PPT Content:**
```
COOLANT — FLUSH INTERVAL

Coolant protects against:
 -- Freezing (ethylene glycol / propylene glycol)
 -- Corrosion (inhibitor package — depletes over time)
 -- Overheating (heat transfer medium)

Typical intervals:
 -- Annual CHECK: Freezing point (refractometer) and pH (test strips)
 -- FLUSH & REPLACE: Every 5 years / 100,000-150,000 km
 -- Long-life coolant: Up to 250,000 km (still check annually)

Testing:
 -- Freezing point: Refractometer reading <= -35 C
 -- pH: Should be 7.0-8.5. Below 7.0 = inhibitors depleted
 -- Visual: Clear colour (green/pink/blue). Rusty/murky = change immediately

NEVER MIX COOLANT TYPES:
 -- OAT (Organic Acid Technology) — typically orange/red
 -- HOAT (Hybrid OAT) — typically yellow/turquoise
 -- IAT (Inorganic Additive Technology) — typically green
 -- Mixing causes gel formation, blocked passages, engine damage

#### Slide 11: Diagnostic Thinking — The S-C-T Method
**Visual:** A three-column flow diagram. Column 1: "SYMPTOM — What does the customer describe or what do you observe?" Column 2: "CAUSE — What systems/components could produce this symptom? List all possibilities." Column 3: "TEST — What specific test confirms or eliminates each possible cause?" Arrows flow left to right. Below: "Diagnose by elimination, not by guessing."

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before we dive into scenarios, let's formalise the diagnostic method. You saw this in Week 5 with chassis faults. It applies everywhere. Three steps. First: define the SYMPTOM precisely. Not 'the car is broken' — but 'engine RPM rises but vehicle speed does not increase in third gear.' Precise symptoms lead to precise diagnoses.
>
> Second: list every possible CAUSE. Don't jump to conclusions. A symptom like 'slow cranking' could be a weak battery, corroded cables, a failing starter, a seized engine, or even the wrong oil viscosity in sub-zero temperatures. List them all.
>
> Third: for each possible cause, identify the TEST that confirms or eliminates it. Battery voltage test. Voltage drop test across cables. Starter current draw test. Each test narrows the list until you have your answer. Let's apply this to five real scenarios."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE S-C-T DIAGNOSTIC METHOD

SYMPTOM --> CAUSE --> TEST

S: What EXACTLY is happening?
 -- Be specific: which gear? which speed? which condition?
 -- Customer description + your observation

C: What could CAUSE this symptom?
 -- List ALL possibilities across ALL relevant systems
 -- Don't assume — don't guess — list everything

T: What TEST confirms or eliminates each cause?
 -- Specific measurement, inspection, or procedure
 -- Each test removes one possibility from the list
 -- Continue until root cause is confirmed

RULE: Diagnose by ELIMINATION, not by assumption.
RULE: The most common cause is not always THE cause.
RULE: Verify the repair — confirm the symptom is gone.
```

#### Slide 12: Scenario 1 — Clutch Slip + High RPM
**Visual:** A tachometer showing RPM climbing while a speedometer remains static. Below: a three-column S-C-T analysis table partially filled in. Photos of: a worn clutch disc (friction material gone), an oil-contaminated clutch disc, and a hydraulic clutch slave cylinder leaking.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Scenario one. Customer complaint: 'When I accelerate hard in third gear, the engine revs rise but the car doesn't speed up. I can smell something burning.' Let's apply S-C-T.
>
> Symptom: Engine RPM increases without proportional vehicle speed increase, especially under load. Burning smell from clutch area. This is textbook clutch slip — the clutch disc is not gripping the flywheel firmly enough to transmit full engine torque.
>
> Causes — let's list them. One: worn clutch disc. The friction material is worn below minimum thickness, and the pressure plate can't clamp hard enough. This is the most common cause and it's a wear item. Two: contaminated clutch disc. Oil or hydraulic fluid has leaked onto the friction surfaces. A leaking rear main seal or a leaking slave cylinder can do this. The disc might have plenty of material left but the contamination kills grip. Three: hydraulic release system fault. If the slave cylinder isn't fully releasing — due to a leak, air in the line, or a failing master cylinder — the clutch may be partially disengaged at all times, causing slip.
>
> Tests. Worn disc: road test — slip gets worse under load, especially in higher gears. Contamination: visual inspection when the gearbox is removed — look for oil on the disc and identify the source. Hydraulic fault: check clutch fluid level, check slave cylinder for leaks, bleed the system and retest."

**PPT Content:**
```
SCENARIO 1: CLUTCH SLIP + HIGH RPM

SYMPTOM:
 -- Engine RPM rises, vehicle speed does not follow
 -- Worse under load (uphill, hard acceleration, higher gears)
 -- Burning smell from clutch area

POSSIBLE CAUSES:
1. Worn clutch disc (friction material below minimum)
   TEST: Road test under load — slip intensity correlates with wear
   TEST: Measure clutch disc thickness at inspection (remove gearbox)

2. Contaminated clutch disc (oil/fluid on friction surfaces)
   TEST: Visual inspection at removal — look for oil source
   TEST: Check rear main seal, slave cylinder for leaks

3. Hydraulic release system fault (partial disengagement)
   TEST: Clutch fluid level check (low = leak somewhere)
   TEST: Slave cylinder visual inspection (wet = leaking)
   TEST: Bleed system, check pedal feel, road test

4. Weakened pressure plate springs (loss of clamping force)
   TEST: Only confirmed at removal — spring height measurement

DIAGNOSTIC SEQUENCE:
 Road test to confirm slip --> Fluid level check --> Visual from below
 --> If no external leak found --> Remove gearbox and inspect
```

#### Slide 13: Scenario 2 — Harsh Shifting + Low ATF
**Visual:** An automatic transmission valve body with solenoids highlighted, a dipstick showing low ATF level with dark fluid, and a mechatronic unit photo from a DSG/DCT. Below: the S-C-T table for this scenario.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Scenario two. Customer: 'My automatic car jerks when it changes gears, especially from first to second and second to third. It didn't used to do this.' They also mention the transmission warning light came on last week but went off.
>
> Symptom: Harsh, jerky gear changes in an automatic transmission. Intermittent transmission warning light.
>
> Causes. One: low ATF level. Low fluid means low hydraulic pressure, which means clutch packs engage too quickly — bang instead of smooth. Check the level first — always. Two: degraded ATF condition. Fluid that's burnt or contaminated loses its friction-modifying properties. The clutch packs can't modulate smoothly. Three: solenoid fault. The valve body solenoids control shift timing and pressure. A faulty solenoid delivers the wrong pressure at the wrong time. The ECU may have stored a fault code. Four: mechatronic unit fault. On DSG and some modern automatics, the mechatronic unit is the combined valve body and electronic controller. Internal failures cause erratic shift behaviour.
>
> Tests. ATF level: dipstick or fill-plug check at operating temperature. Condition: visual and smell check of the fluid. Solenoid: scan tool — read transmission fault codes. If a specific solenoid code is stored, you have your answer. Mechatronic: typically diagnosed via manufacturer-specific scan tool with adaptation values and live data."

**PPT Content:**
```
SCENARIO 2: HARSH SHIFTING + LOW ATF

SYMPTOM:

#### Slide 14: Scenario 3 — Slow Crank + Dim Lights
**Visual:** A cranking waveform on an oscilloscope showing slow, laboured cranking. Photos of: a corroded battery terminal, a voltmeter reading 10.8V during cranking, and a starter motor with a worn brush. Below: the S-C-T table.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Scenario three. Monday morning. Customer calls: 'My car barely started this morning. The lights went dim when I turned the key and the engine turned over really slowly.' Classic slow-crank scenario.
>
> Symptom: Engine cranks slowly. Lights dim during cranking. Eventually starts, but barely.
>
> Causes. One: weak battery. The most common cause. Battery is discharged or has lost capacity. A load test or conductance test will confirm. Two: corroded or loose battery cables. Corrosion at the terminals or a loose connection creates resistance. The voltage at the battery might be fine, but the voltage at the starter is low because the resistance is dropping it across the bad connection. This is where voltage drop testing shines. Three: failing starter motor. Worn brushes, a bad commutator, or a dragging armature bearing increases current draw and slows cranking speed. Starter current draw test will identify this. Four: mechanical resistance. An engine with the wrong oil viscosity in cold weather, or — worst case — a partially seized engine, will crank slowly even with a perfect electrical system. Less common but must be considered.
>
> Tests. Battery: measure resting voltage (should be 12.4V or above). Perform load test or conductance test. Cables: voltage drop test — measure the voltage across each cable during cranking. More than 0.5V drop across a cable = problem. Starter: current draw test with an amp clamp during cranking. Compare to specification — typically 100-200A for a healthy petrol starter. Mechanical: if all electrical tests are normal, investigate mechanical causes."

**PPT Content:**
```
SCENARIO 3: SLOW CRANK + DIM LIGHTS

SYMPTOM:
 -- Engine cranks slowly / laboured
 -- Dashboard lights and headlights dim during cranking
 -- Eventually starts (or doesn't)

POSSIBLE CAUSES:
1. Weak/discharged battery
   TEST: Resting voltage (should be >= 12.4V)
   TEST: Load test or conductance test (CCA vs rated CCA)

#### Slide 15: Scenario 4 — Battery Drain Overnight (Parasitic Draw)
**Visual:** A multimeter in series with the battery negative cable, reading 350 mA. A fuse box with fuses being pulled one at a time. A table showing normal parasitic drain ranges. A photo of a relay stuck in the closed position. Callout: "Normal parasitic drain: 20-50 mA. Problem threshold: > 75 mA."

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Scenario four. Customer: 'Every morning my battery is flat. I've replaced the battery twice and it keeps happening.' This is a parasitic drain — something is drawing current when the vehicle should be asleep.
>
> Symptom: Battery dead after sitting overnight or over a weekend. New battery doesn't fix the problem.
>
> Causes. One: a module not entering sleep mode. Modern vehicles have dozens of ECUs that should power down after the car is locked and left for 10 to 30 minutes. If one stays awake — the infotainment unit, the comfort module, the telematics unit — it draws current continuously. Two: a relay stuck closed. A relay that should open when the ignition is off but stays energised will power its circuit continuously. The fuel pump relay, the blower motor relay, a lighting relay — any of these can stick. Three: an aftermarket installation. Dashcams wired to permanent power, aftermarket alarms, LED strips — anything added after the factory is a prime suspect. Four: an internal short in a module or wiring harness. Less common but possible — damaged insulation allowing current to flow where it shouldn't.
>
> The parasitic draw test. Fully charge the battery. Connect your multimeter in series with the battery negative cable, set to the DC amps range — typically 10A scale first, then move to milliamps once modules go to sleep. Lock the vehicle and wait 30 minutes for all modules to shut down. A normal draw is 20 to 50 milliamps. Over 75 mA is a problem. To find the circuit: start pulling fuses one at a time and watch the reading. When you pull a fuse and the reading drops significantly, that circuit is your culprit. Then trace that circuit to the specific component."

**PPT Content:**
```

 -- Battery dead after sitting overnight or 2-3 days
 -- New battery also goes flat (rules out battery itself)
 -- No accessories left on (doors closed, lights off)

PROBLEM THRESHOLD: > 75 mA

1. Module not sleeping (ECU stays active after shutdown)
   -- Common: infotainment, telematics, comfort/body control module

#### Slide 16: Scenario 5 — Vibration at Speed After Clutch Replacement
**Visual:** A prop shaft (driveshaft) with phasing marks highlighted, a dual mass flywheel with a balance weight, a pilot/spigot bearing photo, and a diagram showing propshaft phasing angles. Below: the S-C-T table.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Scenario five. This one is different — it's a comeback. The customer had their clutch replaced at your workshop last week. Now they're back: 'There's a vibration at 80 km/h that wasn't there before you worked on it.' This is every technician's nightmare — a problem you may have caused.
>
> Symptom: Vibration felt through the floor and seat at highway speed, appeared immediately after clutch replacement.
>
> Causes — and these are all related to the recent work. One: flywheel imbalance. If a dual mass flywheel was replaced with a solid flywheel conversion and no rebalancing was done, or if the new flywheel wasn't seated properly on the crankshaft, imbalance will cause vibration at a frequency related to engine RPM. Two: pilot bearing or spigot bearing. This small bearing at the centre of the crankshaft supports the gearbox input shaft. If it wasn't replaced during the clutch job, or if the new one was installed crooked, it causes vibration. Three: propshaft phasing. On RWD vehicles, if the propshaft was removed and reinstalled out of phase — meaning the universal joint angles aren't matched — you get a speed-related vibration. This is why you always mark the flanges before removal. Four: clutch pressure plate imbalance. If the pressure plate wasn't aligned with its balance marks during installation, the rotating assembly is now out of balance.
>
> Tests. Road test to characterise the vibration — does it change with RPM in neutral (engine-related) or only with vehicle speed in gear (driveshaft-related)? Check propshaft phasing marks. Check flywheel runout with a dial indicator. Inspect pilot bearing condition. This scenario teaches a critical lesson: after any major mechanical work, test drive and compare to the pre-repair condition."

#### Slide 25: Quiz Answers & Discussion

## Key Takeaways

1. Scheduled servicing prevents catastrophic failures — the cost of maintenance is always less than the cost of repair
2. ATF, gear oil, coolant, and engine oil all have specific types, specifications, and intervals — never assume interchangeability
3. Service documentation protects the customer (warranty), the workshop (liability), and the vehicle's value (resale)
4. The S-C-T diagnostic method — Symptom, Cause, Test — applies to every system and every fault
5. Start with the simplest and cheapest test before moving to complex diagnostics
6. After any major repair, always verify with a test drive and compare to pre-repair condition
7. Cross-system thinking is what separates a parts-changer from a diagnostician
