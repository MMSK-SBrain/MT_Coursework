# Day 1: The Car — From Nose to Tail
## Instructor-Led Story Framework for PPT Preparation

**Duration:** 180 minutes (70 min theory + 110 min practical/walkthrough)
**Approach:** Discovery-Based — See It Before You Study It
**PPT Target:** 26-28 slides
**Week:** 1 of 8 — "Meet the Machine"

---

## 🎯 Session Objectives (For Instructor)

By the end of this session, learners will be able to:
- Name and locate all major vehicle systems (engine, transmission, electrical, suspension, steering, brakes, HVAC, body)
- Explain the basic function of each system in one sentence
- Identify the difference between a modern vehicle and one from 20 years ago (mechatronic shift)
- Decode a Vehicle Identification Number (VIN) to extract key vehicle data
- Navigate a basic parts catalogue and read a specification sheet

**This is Day 1 of the entire program.** Learners walk in with zero professional automotive experience. The goal is NOT depth — it is orientation, excitement, and the confidence that comes from being able to name things.

## 🔗 Connection to Prior Knowledge

This is the very first session. Build from:
- Everyday experience as drivers or passengers
- General mechanical intuition (things that spin, pump, flow)
- Consumer knowledge (taking a car for service, dashboard warning lights)

**Bridge:** "You've all been in a car. Today we open it up and show you what's inside — and by this afternoon, you'll know more about how a car works than 95% of people who drive one."

#### Slide 5: The Mechatronic Shift
**Visual:** Side-by-side comparison — 1990s engine bay (visible components, accessible) vs. 2024 engine bay (plastic covers, wiring harnesses, sensors everywhere)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before we start, I want you to understand something important. The car you're going to work on is NOT the car your parents knew. Twenty years ago, a car had maybe 10 electronic control units. **PPT Content:**

THEN (2000)                    NOW (2024)

Distributor ignition            Coil-on-plug, ECU-controlled
Carburettor / basic injection   Direct injection, variable timing
Cable-operated parking brake    Electronic parking brake
Hydraulic power steering        Electric power steering

A MODERN TECHNICIAN = Mechanic + Electrician + Diagnostician

#### Slide 6: System 1 — The Engine
**Visual:** Cutaway engine diagram showing pistons, crankshaft, camshaft, valves, intake and exhaust manifolds

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's start at the heart. The engine converts fuel into motion. It pulls in air, mixes it with fuel, compresses the mixture, ignites it, and the explosion pushes a piston down. That piston is connected to a crankshaft that spins. That spinning force — we call it torque — is what eventually turns your wheels.
>
> We'll spend all of Week 3 inside this engine. Right now, just know: it needs air, fuel, spark, and oil. Take any one away, and it stops — or destroys itself."

**PPT Content:**
```
SYSTEM 1: THE ENGINE — "The Heart"

Job: Convert chemical energy (fuel) into mechanical energy (rotation)
Location: Front of vehicle (most cars), under the bonnet
Key parts: Pistons, crankshaft, camshaft, valves, cylinder head, block
Needs: Air + Fuel + Spark (gasoline) or Compression (diesel) + Oil

Two main types:
• Gasoline (petrol) — spark ignition
• Diesel — compression ignition

Week 3 deep dive
```

#### Slide 7: System 2 — The Transmission
**Visual:** Diagram showing engine → clutch → gearbox → driveshaft → differential → wheels

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The engine spins. But how does that spinning get to the wheels? Through the transmission system — the power delivery chain. Think of a bicycle: you have different gears for hills and flat roads. A car is the same. Low gear for starting from standstill, high gear for cruising. The gearbox gives you that range.
>
> Between the engine and gearbox, you have a clutch (manual) or torque converter (automatic) — they connect and disconnect the engine from the wheels. After the gearbox, a driveshaft carries the power to the differential, which splits it between the left and right wheels. We'll cover this in Week 6."

**PPT Content:**
```
SYSTEM 2: THE TRANSMISSION — "The Power Chain"

Job: Transfer engine power to the wheels at the right speed and torque
Location: Connected directly behind the engine

The chain:
Engine → Clutch/Torque Converter → Gearbox → Driveshaft → Differential → Wheels

Types:
• Manual (driver selects gears via clutch pedal + shift lever)
• Automatic (hydraulic torque converter, ECU selects gears)
• DSG/DCT (dual clutch — computer-controlled manual)
• CVT (continuously variable — no fixed gears)

Week 6 deep dive
```

#### Slide 8: System 3 — Electrical & Electronics
**Visual:** Vehicle outline with ECU locations highlighted — ECM, TCU, BCM, ABS module, airbag module, instrument cluster

**Instructor Narration:**
> "This is the nervous system. Every modern car has a 12-volt battery, an alternator that charges it, and a wiring harness that connects everything. But the real story is the ECUs — Electronic Control Units. Think of them as small computers. The Engine Control Module reads 20+ sensors and makes thousands of decisions per second about fuel injection, ignition timing, and emissions.
>
> All these ECUs talk to each other over a network called CAN bus. We'll spend all of Week 2 on this. For now, just remember: electronics run the car."

**PPT Content:**
```
SYSTEM 3: ELECTRICAL & ELECTRONICS — "The Nervous System"

Job: Power everything, sense everything, control everything
Location: Throughout the entire vehicle

Key components:
• Battery (12V) — stores electrical energy
• Alternator — generates electricity while engine runs
• Wiring harness — connects everything (km of wire!)
• ECUs — Electronic Control Units (100+ in modern vehicles)
• CAN bus — the network they all talk on
• Sensors — measure speed, temperature, pressure, position
• Actuators — do things (open valves, fire injectors, switch relays)

Week 2 deep dive
```

#### Slide 9: System 4 — Suspension
**Visual:** Front and rear suspension comparison — MacPherson strut and multi-link, labeled

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Imagine driving on a rough road with no suspension. Every bump would throw you off the seat. The suspension system absorbs road imperfections and keeps the tyres in contact with the road. It's the connection between the car body and the wheels.
>
> Springs absorb the bumps. Dampers (shock absorbers) stop the car from bouncing. And the geometry of the arms and links determines how the tyre meets the road. Get this wrong, and your tyres wear unevenly, the car pulls to one side, or it becomes dangerous in an emergency swerve."

**PPT Content:**
```
SYSTEM 4: SUSPENSION — "The Skeleton & Joints"

Job: Absorb road imperfections, maintain tyre contact, support vehicle weight
Location: At each wheel — front and rear

Key components:
• Springs (coil, leaf, or air) — absorb bumps
• Dampers / shock absorbers — control spring oscillation
• Control arms / wishbones — locate the wheel
• Bushings & ball joints — allow controlled movement
• Anti-roll bar — reduces body roll in corners

Common types:
• MacPherson strut (front — most common)
• Double wishbone (performance / luxury)
• Multi-link (rear — most common modern)

Week 5 deep dive
```

#### Slide 10: System 5 — Steering
**Visual:** Rack-and-pinion steering mechanism with EPS motor, steering column, and tie rods

**Instructor Narration:**
> "You turn the steering wheel, and the car changes direction. Simple, right? But think about what's happening mechanically. Your steering wheel connects through a column to a rack-and-pinion gear. That gear pushes tie rods left or right, which pivot the front wheels.
>
> Modern cars add Electric Power Steering — an electric motor that assists your effort. The assistance varies with speed: lots of help at parking speed, less on the motorway for better feel. All controlled by — you guessed it — an ECU."

**PPT Content:**
```
SYSTEM 5: STEERING — "Direction Control"

Job: Translate driver input into wheel angle change
Location: Steering column → rack (front axle)

The chain:
Steering wheel → Column → Rack & Pinion → Tie Rods → Steering knuckle → Wheels

Power assist types:
• Hydraulic (older — engine-driven pump)
• Electric (EPS — motor on column or rack, ECU-controlled)
• Electro-hydraulic (electric pump, hydraulic rack)

Key sensors:
• Steering angle sensor (tells ESC where driver wants to go)
• Torque sensor (tells EPS how much help to give)

Week 5 deep dive
```

#### Slide 11: System 6 — Brakes
**Visual:** Complete brake system diagram — master cylinder, booster, ABS unit, disc brake at front, drum brake at rear

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The brakes are the most safety-critical system on the car. Their job is simple: convert the car's kinetic energy into heat through friction, and slow you down. You press the pedal, hydraulic fluid amplifies your foot force, and brake pads squeeze against a spinning disc — or shoes press against a drum.
>
> Modern cars add ABS — Anti-lock Braking System — which prevents wheel lock-up so you can still steer while braking hard. And ESC — Electronic Stability Control — which selectively brakes individual wheels to keep the car stable. We'll spend Day 23 entirely on brakes."

**PPT Content:**
```
SYSTEM 6: BRAKES — "The Most Important Safety System"

Job: Convert motion into heat, slow and stop the vehicle
Location: At each wheel + hydraulic unit under bonnet

Key components:
• Brake pedal → Booster → Master cylinder (creates hydraulic pressure)
• Brake lines & hoses (carry fluid to wheels)
• Disc brakes: Caliper + pads + rotor (front, usually)
• Drum brakes: Shoes + wheel cylinder + drum (rear, some cars)
• ABS: Prevents wheel lock-up during hard braking
• ESC: Maintains stability using selective braking
• Parking brake: Mechanical cable or electronic (EPB)

Week 5 deep dive
```

#### Slide 12: System 7 — HVAC
**Visual:** HVAC system diagram showing compressor, condenser, evaporator, heater core, cabin ducts

**PPT Content:**

Job: Heat, cool, and ventilate the passenger cabin
Location: Under dashboard (evaporator/heater core), front of engine bay (condenser)

Air Conditioning path:
Compressor → Condenser → Expansion valve → Evaporator → back to Compressor

Heating:
Engine coolant → Heater core → Warm air into cabin

Key components:
• Compressor (belt-driven or electric on hybrids)
• Condenser & evaporator (heat exchangers)
• Blower motor (pushes air through vents)
• Blend door actuator (hot/cold mix)
• Cabin air filter

#### Slide 21: The VIN — A Car's Fingerprint
**Visual:** A VIN number broken into its 17 characters with each section color-coded and labeled

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Every car has a unique 17-character code called the VIN — Vehicle Identification Number. It's stamped into the body, printed on a plate visible through the windscreen, and stored in every ECU. Think of it as the car's fingerprint. From this one number, you can identify the manufacturer, country of origin, engine type, model year, and production sequence. Let me show you how to decode it."

**PPT Content:**
```
VIN — VEHICLE IDENTIFICATION NUMBER

17 characters — unique to every vehicle ever made

Position 1-3: WMI (World Manufacturer Identifier)
  W = Germany, J = Japan, 1/4/5 = USA, Z = Italy
  BA = BMW, VW = Volkswagen, etc.

Position 4-8: VDS (Vehicle Descriptor Section)
  Model, body type, engine type, restraint system

Position 9: Check digit (North America) / varies (Europe)

Position 10: Model year
  A=2010, B=2011... J=2018, K=2019, L=2020...

Position 11: Assembly plant

Position 12-17: Sequential production number
```
