## Session Objectives (For Instructor)

By the end of this session, learners will be able to:
- Explain the difference between unibody (monocoque) construction and body-on-frame construction, and identify which vehicles use each
- Identify exterior panels — bonnet, doors, boot lid, wings/fenders — and distinguish bolt-on panels from welded panels
- Describe bumper system components (cover, absorber, reinforcement bar) and explain pedestrian protection requirements
- Identify interior trim components and clip types, and demonstrate correct removal technique without damage
- Explain factory corrosion protection layers (e-coat, primer, basecoat, clearcoat) and aftermarket underbody and cavity wax treatments
- Inspect and replace weatherstripping — door seals and window seals

**This session bridges the gap between mechanical systems and the vehicle as a physical object.** Every technician works on and around body panels and trim daily. Knowing how to remove a door card without breaking clips, how to adjust a bonnet, and how to spot corrosion before it becomes structural saves time, prevents damage, and demonstrates professional competence.

#### Slide 2: Unibody vs Body-on-Frame
**Visual:** Split diagram — left shows a unibody/monocoque structure with integrated floor pan, roof, and pillars forming one welded unit; right shows a body-on-frame with separate ladder chassis and bolt-on body shell.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "There are two fundamental construction methods. Unibody — also called monocoque — is used by the vast majority of modern passenger cars. The body and frame are one integrated welded structure. The floor pan, pillars, roof, and inner panels are all welded together into a single rigid shell. There is no separate chassis — the body IS the chassis. Body-on-frame is the older method, still used for large trucks, SUVs, and some pickup trucks. A separate ladder frame carries the engine, transmission, and suspension. The body shell sits on top, bolted to the frame through rubber mounts. Each method has trade-offs in weight, rigidity, repairability, and crash performance."

**PPT Content:**
```
TWO CONSTRUCTION METHODS

UNIBODY (Monocoque):
- Body and frame are ONE welded structure
- Used by: ~95% of modern passenger cars
- Advantages: lighter, better crash performance, stiffer
- Disadvantages: harder to repair after major collision
- Examples: VW Golf, BMW 3 Series, Toyota Corolla, Tesla Model 3

BODY-ON-FRAME:
- Separate ladder chassis + bolt-on body shell
- Used by: full-size trucks, SUVs, some commercial vehicles
- Advantages: easier to repair, body can be removed, high load capacity
- Disadvantages: heavier, higher centre of gravity, less rigid
- Examples: Toyota Land Cruiser, Ford F-150, Jeep Wrangler

How to identify:
- Look underneath — unibody has stamped floor pan visible
- Body-on-frame has visible ladder rails running front to rear
- Check manufacturer specifications if unsure
```

#### Slide 3: Body Structure Zones — Safety Design
**Visual:** Colour-coded body structure showing three zones — front crumple zone (red), passenger safety cell (green), rear crumple zone (red). Arrows showing energy absorption and load paths.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "A modern unibody is designed around three zones. The front crumple zone — the engine bay structure — is designed to DEFORM progressively in a frontal collision, absorbing kinetic energy and increasing the time over which the deceleration occurs. The passenger safety cell — A-pillars, B-pillars, roof, floor — is designed to be RIGID, maintaining survival space for the occupants. The rear crumple zone absorbs energy in rear collisions. This is why you cannot weld, cut, or modify structural members without understanding the crash load paths. Change the structure, change the crash behaviour."

**PPT Content:**
```
BODY SAFETY ZONES

FRONT CRUMPLE ZONE:
- Designed to deform progressively in frontal impact
- Front rails, subframe, bumper reinforcement
- Absorbs energy, extends deceleration time
- Engine may be pushed UNDER the cabin (breakaway mounts)

PASSENGER SAFETY CELL:
- A-pillars, B-pillars, C-pillars, roof rail
- Floor pan (reinforced tunnel), door beams
- Designed to maintain survival space
- MUST NOT deform in survivable crashes
- Ultra-high-strength steel (UHSS) — up to 1,500 MPa

REAR CRUMPLE ZONE:
- Rear rails, boot/trunk floor structure
- Absorbs rear impact energy
- Fuel tank protection (if rear-mounted)

CRITICAL RULE:
Structural modifications affect crash performance.
Never cut, weld, or alter structural members without
manufacturer-approved repair procedures.
```

#### Slide 4: Exterior Panels Overview
**Visual:** Vehicle outline with all major exterior panels labeled and colour-coded — bonnet (blue), front wings (green), doors (yellow), rear quarter panels (orange), boot lid (red), bumper covers front/rear (grey), roof (white).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Exterior panels fall into two categories: bolt-on and welded. Bolt-on panels — bonnet, doors, boot lid, and usually front wings — are attached with bolts to hinges, brackets, or the body structure. They can be removed and replaced without welding. Welded panels — rear quarter panels, roof, sill covers — are part of the body structure and require cutting and welding to replace. As a service technician, you'll regularly remove bolt-on panels for access. You'll adjust them for fit and alignment. And you'll refer welded panel work to a body repair specialist."

**PPT Content:**
```
EXTERIOR PANELS — BOLT-ON vs WELDED

BOLT-ON PANELS (removable by service technician):
- Bonnet/hood — hinged, bolted, adjustable
- Front doors — hinged, bolted, adjustable
- Rear doors — hinged, bolted, adjustable
- Boot lid/tailgate — hinged, bolted, adjustable
- Front wings/fenders — bolted to inner structure
- Bumper covers — clip and bolt attached

WELDED PANELS (body shop repair only):
- Rear quarter panels — welded to body structure
- Roof panel — welded to pillars and rails
- Sill/rocker panels — welded structural member
- Floor pan sections — welded to cross members
- A/B/C pillars — welded structural members

SERVICE TECHNICIAN TASKS:
```

#### Slide 5: Bonnet — Removal, Adjustment, and Safety
**Visual:** Annotated photos showing bonnet hinge bolts, latch mechanism, gas struts/spring hinges, and gap/flush adjustment arrows.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The bonnet is the panel you open most often. It's hinged at the rear and latched at the front. Two points of adjustment: the hinge-to-bonnet bolts control fore-aft and side-to-side position. The latch height and the rubber bump stops control the flush — how level the bonnet sits with the front wings. Adjustment is done by loosening the bolts, repositioning, and retightening. The gap between the bonnet and the wings should be even on both sides — typically 3 to 5mm. Most bonnets also have a safety catch — a secondary latch that prevents the bonnet from flying open if the primary latch fails. Always verify the safety catch operates after any bonnet work."

**PPT Content:**
```
BONNET — ADJUSTMENT & SAFETY

Mounting:
- Two hinges at rear (bolted to body + bolted to bonnet)
- Primary latch at front centre (cable-operated from cabin)
- Safety catch (secondary latch — manually released)
- Support: gas struts or spring-loaded hinges

Adjustment points:
1. Hinge-to-bonnet bolts: fore/aft, left/right position
2. Hinge-to-body bolts: height at rear
3. Latch striker: height at front
4. Rubber bump stops: fine-tune flush alignment

Gap specification: 3-5mm even gap to adjacent panels
Flush specification: bonnet surface level with wings (+/- 1mm)

SAFETY CHECK AFTER ANY BONNET WORK:
[ ] Primary latch engages securely (close and tug)
[ ] Safety catch holds if primary latch released
[ ] Gas struts hold bonnet fully open (no creep down)
[ ] Bonnet closes smoothly from 300mm height (drop test)
```

#### Slide 6: Doors — Hinges, Adjustment, and Check Straps
**Visual:** Door removed from vehicle showing upper and lower hinges, check strap (holds door at intermediate open positions), wiring harness pass-through, and door seal groove.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Doors are the most complex bolt-on panels. Each door hangs on two hinges — upper and lower — bolted to both the door and the B-pillar or A-pillar. The check strap holds the door at intermediate open positions so it doesn't swing freely. The wiring harness passes from the body through the A-pillar into the door through a flexible boot. Door adjustment controls three dimensions: fore-aft position, up-down position, and in-out (how flush the door sits with the body). A sagging door usually means worn hinge pins or loose hinge bolts — it's one of the most common body complaints you'll see."

**PPT Content:**
```
DOORS — STRUCTURE & ADJUSTMENT

Components:
- Upper and lower hinges (bolted to pillar and door)
- Check strap (limits opening angle, holds at detents)
- Latch and striker (on B-pillar, adjustable)
- Window regulator (cable or scissor mechanism)
- Door beam (side impact protection — inside door skin)
- Wiring harness boot (body-to-door transition)
- Door seal (rubber weatherstrip in door frame)

Adjustment:
1. Hinge bolts (door side): up/down, fore/aft
2. Hinge bolts (pillar side): in/out (flush)
3. Striker plate: latch engagement depth and height

Common complaint — sagging door:
- Symptom: door drops when opened, doesn't align with body
- Cause: worn hinge pins, loose hinge bolts, stretched check strap
- Diagnosis: lift door at handle — movement = hinge wear
- Repair: replace hinge pins/bushings or entire hinge assembly

Gap specification: even 3-5mm around entire door perimeter.
```

#### Slide 7: Front Wings/Fenders — Bolt-On Replacement
**Visual:** Step-by-step photo sequence of front wing removal — disconnecting fog light wiring, removing arch liner fasteners, removing wing bolts from inner structure, lifting wing clear.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Front wings are bolt-on panels on most modern vehicles — which means replacing a damaged wing is a service technician task, not a body shop task. The wing bolts to the inner wing structure along the top, front, and door edge. Before removing, disconnect any wiring — side repeater lights, fog lights if wing-mounted, and parking sensor wiring if present. Remove the wheel arch liner to access lower bolts. Remove all bolts. Lift the wing clear. When fitting the new wing, start all bolts loose, adjust the gaps to match the bonnet and door, then tighten in sequence. It's a satisfying job when the gaps are perfect."

**PPT Content:**
```
FRONT WING / FENDER — BOLT-ON REPLACEMENT

Removal procedure:
1. Open bonnet, remove wheel arch liner
2. Disconnect wiring (repeater, fog light, parking sensor)
3. Remove bumper-to-wing fasteners (usually clips + 1-2 bolts)
4. Remove wing bolts along top edge (inner wing to outer wing)
5. Remove wing bolts at A-pillar / door edge
6. Remove wing bolts at lower front (headlight area)
7. Lift wing clear — note any sealant locations

Fitting procedure:
1. Offer new wing — start ALL bolts loose (finger tight)
2. Adjust gaps: wing-to-bonnet, wing-to-door, wing-to-bumper
3. Check flush: wing surface level with bonnet and door
4. Tighten bolts in sequence (top first, then front, then rear)
5. Reconnect all wiring
6. Refit wheel arch liner

Gap check: even 3-5mm all around
Flush check: surfaces level +/- 1mm
```

#### Slide 8: Bumper Systems — More Than a Plastic Cover
**Visual:** Exploded view of a modern bumper system showing three layers — outer plastic cover, foam/honeycomb absorber, steel/aluminium reinforcement bar. Cross-section showing pedestrian protection design.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "What most people call a 'bumper' is actually just the outer cover — a flexible plastic fascia designed to look good and flex back after minor impacts. Behind it sits the energy absorber — a foam or honeycomb block that compresses during low-speed impacts, protecting the reinforcement bar from damage. And behind that is the reinforcement bar — a steel or aluminium beam bolted to the front rails. This three-layer system serves two purposes: protect the vehicle structure in low-speed impacts, and protect pedestrians in higher-speed impacts. Modern bumper covers have specific profile shapes designed to reduce lower leg injury severity. That's why you can't just fit any aftermarket bumper — the geometry matters for pedestrian protection compliance."

**PPT Content:**
```
BUMPER SYSTEM — THREE LAYERS

LAYER 1: BUMPER COVER (outer)
- Material: PP (polypropylene) or TPO (flexible plastic)
- Function: Aesthetics, aerodynamics, minor impact flex
- Attached by: clips, screws, push fasteners
- Contains: fog light openings, sensor apertures, grille sections

LAYER 2: ENERGY ABSORBER (middle)
- Material: EPP foam or plastic honeycomb structure
- Function: Absorbs energy in low-speed impacts (0-15 km/h)
- Crushes and reforms (EPP) or crushes and replaces (honeycomb)
- Reduces damage to reinforcement bar and structure

LAYER 3: REINFORCEMENT BAR (inner)
- Material: Steel or aluminium beam
- Function: Distributes impact force to front/rear rails
- Bolted to crash box / rail extensions
- Designed to deform at specific loads

PEDESTRIAN PROTECTION (EU regulation):
- Bumper profile designed to reduce lower leg injury
- Specific geometry tested: leg-form impactor at 40 km/h
- Aftermarket bumpers must meet same geometric requirements
```

#### Slide 9: Panel Gap Measurement and Standards
**Visual:** Technician using a feeler gauge or plastic gap measuring tool on a bonnet-to-wing gap. Diagram showing ideal vs unacceptable gaps.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Panel gaps and flush alignment are what separate a professional job from a sloppy one. The customer may not consciously notice perfect gaps, but they absolutely notice bad ones. Use a gap gauge or feeler gauge to measure. Typical specification is 3 to 5mm even gap, with no more than 1mm variation across the length of the joint. Flush should be within 1mm — meaning the panel surfaces are level with each other, not stepped. After any panel work — bonnet adjustment, wing replacement, door hinge repair — measure and adjust until the gaps are right."

**PPT Content:**
```
PANEL GAP MEASUREMENT

Tools:
- Plastic gap gauge (automotive body tool)
- Feeler gauge set (for precise measurement)
- Straight edge (for flush/step check)

Typical specifications:
- Gap width: 3-5mm (varies by manufacturer)
- Gap consistency: less than 1mm variation along joint length
- Flush: panel surfaces within +/- 1mm of each other
- Margin: top of door within 0.5mm of roof edge height

Where to measure:
- Bonnet to wing (both sides — must be symmetrical)
- Door to wing (front gap)
- Door to door (B-pillar area)
- Door to rear quarter panel (rear gap)
- Boot lid to rear quarter panel (both sides)

PROFESSIONAL STANDARD:
Gaps should look FACTORY. If you can tell a panel was replaced
by looking at the gaps — the job is not finished.
```

#### Slide 11: Clip Types — The Hidden Fastener System
**Visual:** Catalogue-style grid showing 8 common clip types with names, photos, and descriptions — push rivet, Christmas tree, screw grommet, expanding clip, bayonet clip, quarter-turn, J-clip, edge clip.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "There are dozens of clip types in a modern vehicle, but eight cover about 90 percent of what you'll encounter. The push rivet — press the centre pin to expand the base. The Christmas tree clip — shaped like a tree, squeezes to insert and expands to hold. The screw grommet — a rubber or plastic insert that accepts a screw, forming a captive nut in plastic or sheet metal. The expanding clip — push the pin to expand. Each one has a specific release method. Learning to identify the clip type BEFORE you start prying is the difference between a clean removal and a broken clip."

**PPT Content:**
```
COMMON CLIP TYPES — IDENTIFICATION & RELEASE

1. PUSH RIVET:
   - Push centre pin to expand, pull pin to release
   - Used: arch liners, underbody panels

2. CHRISTMAS TREE CLIP:
   - Squeeze barbs to insert, pull firmly to remove
   - Used: bumper covers, interior trim

3. SCREW GROMMET:
   - Rubber/plastic insert, accepts screw
   - Unscrew first, then remove grommet
   - Used: splash guards, engine covers

4. EXPANDING CLIP:
   - Push centre pin to lock, pry pin out to release
   - Used: headlight assemblies, grille panels

5. BAYONET CLIP:
   - Quarter-turn to lock/unlock
   - Used: engine covers, air filter housings

6. QUARTER-TURN FASTENER:
   - 90-degree turn to release
   - Used: access panels, underbody shields

7. J-CLIP (SPRING CLIP):
   - Slides onto metal edge, holds trim
   - Used: door cards (top edge), dashboards

8. EDGE CLIP:
   - Clips onto panel edge, holds adjacent trim
   - Used: sill covers, A-pillar trim
```

#### Slide 12: Trim Removal Technique — No Damage
**Visual:** Photo sequence showing correct trim removal — using a plastic trim tool to lever a door card, starting at the bottom corner, working around the perimeter, then lifting off the top hooks.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The correct technique for trim removal uses plastic trim tools — not metal screwdrivers, not your fingers, and definitely not brute force. Start by identifying all screw locations — they're often hidden behind caps, plugs, or behind the door handle. Remove all screws FIRST. Then insert your trim tool at the bottom corner of the panel and lever gently. Work around the perimeter, releasing each clip in sequence. Listen for the pop of each clip releasing. Once all perimeter clips are free, lift the panel to unhook the top edge — most door cards have J-hooks along the top that slide upward. The panel comes off cleanly, all clips intact."

**PPT Content:**
```
TRIM REMOVAL — CORRECT TECHNIQUE

RULE: Use PLASTIC trim tools. Never metal screwdrivers on trim.

Door card removal (typical procedure):
1. IDENTIFY all screw locations:
   - Behind door handle trim (pry off cover plate)
   - Bottom edge (1-2 screws)
   - Near mirror triangle (pull off to access screw)
   - Hidden behind arm rest (screw under pull handle)
2. REMOVE all screws
3. DISCONNECT window/mirror switches (unclip harness)
4. INSERT trim tool at bottom corner
5. LEVER gently outward — clip pops free
6. WORK around perimeter — release each clip in sequence
7. LIFT panel upward to unhook top edge
8. DISCONNECT any remaining wiring (speaker, courtesy light)

COMMON DAMAGE CAUSES:
- Missed screw location → broken clip/trim when levering
- Metal tool → scratched trim surface
- Pulling top before releasing bottom clips → broken top hooks
- Not disconnecting wiring before fully removing → stretched/broken connectors
```

#### Slide 13: Practical — Door Card Removal and Refit

#### Slide 14: Dashboard and Pillar Trim — Airbag Awareness
**Visual:** Warning diagram showing airbag locations behind dashboard, A-pillar, B-pillar, and headliner — with warning symbols and disconnection requirements.

**Instructor Narration:**

### **DEVELOPMENT PART 3: Corrosion Protection & Weatherstripping** (Slides 15-20, ~25 minutes)

---

#### Slide 15: Factory Paint and Corrosion Protection Layers
**Visual:** Cross-section diagram of automotive paint system on steel panel — steel substrate, zinc coating, e-coat (electrodeposition primer), filler/primer, basecoat, clearcoat. Each layer labeled with thickness and purpose.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The factory applies up to six layers of protection to every body panel. Starting from the steel: a zinc coating applied during the steelmaking process provides galvanic corrosion protection. Then the entire body shell is dipped in an e-coat bath — an electrically deposited primer that coats every surface, every cavity, every hidden corner. This is the primary corrosion barrier. On top: a filler primer for surface smoothness, a basecoat for colour, and a clearcoat for UV protection and gloss. The total paint thickness is typically 100 to 150 micrometres — about the thickness of a human hair. Scratch through any of these layers and the clock starts ticking on corrosion."

**PPT Content:**
```
FACTORY CORROSION PROTECTION — LAYER BY LAYER

STEEL SUBSTRATE:
- Zinc coated (galvanised) — 7-15 micrometres per side
- Sacrificial corrosion protection (zinc corrodes before steel)

E-COAT (Cathodic Electrodeposition):
- 18-25 micrometres
- Applied by dipping entire body shell in paint bath
- Electrical charge deposits paint into every cavity and seam
- Primary corrosion barrier — covers 100% of surfaces
- Cures in oven at ~180 deg C

FILLER PRIMER:
- 30-40 micrometres
- Provides smooth surface for topcoat
- Additional corrosion resistance

BASECOAT (Colour):
- 12-20 micrometres
- Provides colour and visual appearance
- Metallic/pearl coats have aluminium/mica flakes

CLEARCOAT:
- 35-50 micrometres
- UV protection, scratch resistance, gloss
- The layer you polish and wax

TOTAL: 100-150 micrometres (0.1-0.15mm)
```

#### Slide 16: Underbody Protection
**Visual:** Photo of vehicle underbody showing PVC underbody coating, stone chip protection on wheel arches, and seam sealant at panel joints.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Beyond the paint layers, the underbody gets additional protection. PVC underbody coating — a thick, flexible layer sprayed onto the floor pan and wheel arches — protects against stone chips, road salt, and water spray. Seam sealant fills every panel joint where two panels are welded or bolted together — preventing water from wicking into the joint through capillary action. And stone chip protection — a textured coating on the lower body sides and wheel arches — resists impacts from road debris. During service, inspect these protections. If you see cracked, peeling, or missing underbody coating, it needs to be repaired before corrosion takes hold."

**PPT Content:**
```
UNDERBODY PROTECTION

PVC UNDERBODY COATING:
- Thick, flexible coating (1-3mm)
- Applied to floor pan, wheel arches, rear floor
- Protects against: stone chips, road salt, water, mud
- Inspect: annual service — look for cracks, peeling, impact damage
- Repair: clean area, apply automotive underbody spray/brush coating

SEAM SEALANT:
- Flexible sealant applied to every panel joint
- Prevents water intrusion at welded/bolted seams
- Inspect: look for cracked or missing sealant
- Repair: clean, apply automotive body seam sealant, smooth with wet finger

STONE CHIP PROTECTION:
- Textured coating on lower body sides
- Also applied inside wheel arches over PVC coating
- Resists high-velocity stone impacts

CAVITY WAX:
- Injected into box sections (sills, door bottoms, chassis rails)
- Wax-based — never dries, continues to creep and protect
- Factory applied — can be reapplied as aftermarket treatment
- Access via drain holes or injection nozzle holes
```

#### Slide 17: Corrosion Inspection and Prevention
**Visual:** Photo series showing corrosion progression — surface rust, bubbling under paint, perforation rust, structural corrosion.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Corrosion is progressive. It starts as surface oxidation — barely visible. Then it bubbles under the paint as the iron oxide expands. Then it perforates — you can poke a finger through. And finally it compromises structural integrity. Your job as a technician is to catch it at stage one or two. During every service, do a quick underbody visual inspection while the car is on the lift. Look at the sills, wheel arches, rear arches, and underbody edges. Early detection means a simple treatment. Late detection means panel replacement. The customer will thank you for catching it early — it saves them thousands."

**PPT Content:**
```
CORROSION INSPECTION — CATCH IT EARLY

INSPECTION POINTS (every service while car is on lift):
- Sills / rocker panels (especially behind plastic covers)
- Rear wheel arches (upper edge — most common corrosion point)
- Front subframe mounting points
- Floor pan edges and seams
- Door bottoms (open door, inspect lower edge)
- Boot / trunk floor (lift carpet, inspect underneath)
- Spare wheel well

CORROSION STAGES:
Stage 1: Surface rust — discolouration, can be treated with rust converter
Stage 2: Bubbling under paint — corrosion expanding under intact paint
Stage 3: Perforation — rust has eaten through panel, holes visible
Stage 4: Structural weakness — panel has lost load-bearing capacity

TREATMENT OPTIONS:
Stage 1-2: Wire brush, rust converter, primer, top coat, cavity wax
Stage 3: Patch repair (if non-structural) or panel replacement
Stage 4: Panel replacement mandatory (body shop referral)
```

#### Slide 18: Weatherstripping — Door and Window Seals
**Visual:** Cross-section of a door seal installed in the door frame — showing the rubber profile, mounting lip, hollow bulb section, and how it compresses against the door edge when closed.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Weatherstrips — the rubber seals around doors, windows, boot lids, and sunroofs — keep water, wind, and road noise out of the cabin. The door seal mounts in a groove on the body or the door edge. When the door closes, the hollow bulb section compresses, creating a watertight seal. Over time, seals harden, crack, shrink, and lose their compression. The result is water leaks, wind noise, and increased cabin noise. Inspection is simple: close the door on a piece of paper. Pull the paper out — it should drag with resistance. If it slides out freely, the seal is not compressing and needs replacement."

**PPT Content:**
```
WEATHERSTRIPPING — SEALS & INSPECTION

DOOR SEALS:
- Location: mounted in groove on body door opening
- Profile: rubber with hollow bulb (compresses on door close)
- Function: water seal, wind seal, noise insulation
- Common failures: cracking, hardening, shrinking, tearing
- Replacement: pull old seal out of groove, press new seal in
- Use silicone lubricant (NOT petroleum-based — damages rubber)

WINDOW SEALS (run channel / belt moulding):
- Location: at door window opening, guides glass up/down
- Inner and outer belt mouldings (the felted strips at door top)
- Function: seal around glass, reduce wind noise, prevent water entry
- Common failure: felt wears flat, rubber hardens, seal pulls away

INSPECTION METHOD:
- Visual: look for cracks, tears, flat spots, gaps
- Paper test: close door on paper, pull — should resist
- Water test: pour water along seal, check for cabin intrusion
- Noise test: road test at speed — wind noise indicates seal failure

Seal replacement is straightforward and dramatically improves
cabin comfort. It's a high-value, low-effort service upsell.
```

## Key Takeaways

1. Unibody construction means the body IS the chassis — structural changes affect crash safety
2. Three body zones: front crumple, safety cell, rear crumple — each designed for a specific role
3. Bolt-on panels (bonnet, doors, wings) can be adjusted and replaced by service technicians
4. Interior trim clips have specific types and release methods — plastic trim tools prevent damage
5. Airbags hide behind dashboard, pillar, and headliner trim — disconnect battery before removal
6. Six layers of factory corrosion protection — a scratch through any layer starts the corrosion clock
7. Door seal paper test is a simple, effective way to assess weatherstrip condition
8. Panel gap precision is a mark of professional workmanship
