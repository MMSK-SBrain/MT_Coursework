By the end of this session, learners will be able to:
- Explain why engines overheat and describe the consequences of thermal failure (warped head, blown head gasket, seized engine)

- Conduct a cooling system pressure test using a hand pump tester
- Verify thermostat operation using a hot-water test with infrared thermometer
- Perform a block test (combustion gas leak test) to detect head gasket failure

**Connection to Day 16:** Yesterday covered engine oil — the lubricant that reduces friction and carries heat away from bearing surfaces. Today covers the cooling system — the dedicated thermal management system that carries the remaining waste heat away from the engine and rejects it to the atmosphere. Oil reduces heat generation; coolant removes heat. Together, they keep the engine alive.

#### Slide 2: What Happens When Cooling Fails
**Visual:** Four-panel failure sequence: (1) Temperature gauge climbing into red, (2) Steam erupting from under bonnet, (3) Close-up of a warped cylinder head on a surface plate with feeler gauge showing gap, (4) Seized piston welded to cylinder wall

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let me show you what failure looks like. Stage one: the temperature gauge climbs. Most drivers ignore it. Stage two: the coolant boils, pressure builds beyond what the cap can hold, and steam blows out. Stage three: the aluminium cylinder head — which expands faster than the cast iron block — warps. Now the head gasket cannot seal. Coolant enters the combustion chamber. Oil and water mix. Stage four: if the driver keeps going, the pistons expand until they seize in the bores. The engine is scrap metal.
>
> A new cylinder head and gasket job costs thousands. A seized engine costs the entire powertrain. All because coolant stopped flowing. That is why this system matters."

Stage 2: Coolant boils → steam, pressure release
Stage 3: Cylinder head warps

         → Coolant enters combustion chamber
         → Oil and coolant mix (milky sludge)
Stage 4: Pistons seize in bores → engine destroyed

COST OF FAILURE:
  Head gasket repair: EUR 1,500 - 3,000+

#### Slide 3: The Thermal Balance
**Visual:** Diagram showing an engine at operating temperature (90 degrees C) with heat flow arrows — combustion chamber at 2,000 degrees C, cylinder wall at 150-200 degrees C, coolant jacket at 85-105 degrees C, radiator outlet at 70-85 degrees C, ambient air at 25 degrees C

**Instructor Narration:**
> "A healthy engine runs at about 90 degrees Celsius coolant temperature — not because that is cool, but because that is the optimal balance. Too cold and the fuel does not vaporise properly, oil is too thick, emissions increase, and wear accelerates. Too hot and you start the destruction sequence we just described. The cooling system's job is not to keep the engine cold. It is to keep it at the right temperature — and that temperature is surprisingly specific. The thermostat is the gatekeeper. It holds heat in until the engine is warm enough, then opens to let coolant flow to the radiator."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE THERMAL BALANCE — NOT COLD, BUT CORRECT

Combustion gas temperature:     ~2,000 C
Exhaust valve temperature:      ~700-800 C
Cylinder wall temperature:      ~150-200 C
Coolant operating temperature:  ~85-105 C (target: ~90 C)
Radiator outlet temperature:    ~70-85 C
Ambient air:                    ~-20 to +45 C

TOO COLD (< 75 C):
  Poor fuel vaporisation, increased emissions
  Thick oil, increased wear
  Heater core underperforms

TOO HOT (> 110 C):
  Head gasket stress, detonation risk
  Oil breakdown, accelerated wear
  Potential catastrophic failure

The cooling system REGULATES temperature — not just removes heat.
```

#### Slide 5: Coolant Flow Path — The Complete Circuit
**Visual:** Full-system schematic showing the coolant circuit in two states: thermostat closed (bypass loop — small circuit) and thermostat open (full circuit through radiator). Arrows show flow direction. Every component labeled.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before we study each component, let's see the whole picture. Coolant lives in two circuits. When the engine is cold, the thermostat is closed. Coolant circulates in a small loop — out of the engine, through the heater core, through the bypass passage, and back to the water pump. This keeps the coolant in contact with the engine so it warms up quickly.
>
> Once the engine reaches operating temperature — typically 85 to 90 degrees — the thermostat opens. Now coolant flows through the full circuit: out of the engine, through the upper hose to the radiator, down through the radiator core where it loses heat to the air, out the bottom of the radiator, through the lower hose, and back to the water pump. The pump pushes it back into the engine block and head. It is a continuous loop — pump, engine, thermostat, radiator, pump."

**PPT Content:**
```
COOLANT FLOW PATH — TWO CIRCUITS

COLD ENGINE (Thermostat CLOSED — bypass circuit):
  Water pump → Engine block → Cylinder head →
  Heater core → Bypass passage → Back to pump
  Purpose: Warm up quickly, provide cabin heat

WARM ENGINE (Thermostat OPEN — full circuit):
  Water pump → Engine block → Cylinder head →
  Thermostat (open) → Upper hose → Radiator →
  Lower hose → Back to water pump
  Purpose: Reject heat to atmosphere

ALWAYS FLOWING:
  Heater core circuit (cabin heating)
  Throttle body / EGR cooler (some vehicles)
  Transmission oil cooler (some vehicles)
  Turbo coolant circuit (turbocharged engines)

Flow rate at idle: ~40 L/min
Flow rate at 3,000 RPM: ~100+ L/min
```

#### Slide 6: The Radiator — Heat Exchanger
**Visual:** Cutaway of an aluminium cross-flow radiator showing: plastic end tanks, aluminium core with flat tubes and louvered fins, transmission cooler integrated in one tank, drain petcock at bottom, mounting points

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The radiator is the primary heat exchanger. Hot coolant enters one tank, flows through dozens of thin flat tubes across the core, and exits the other tank cooler. As it passes through those tubes, the fins transfer heat to the air flowing through the front of the car. Modern radiators are aluminium cores with plastic tanks — crimped together with a rubber gasket. That gasket is a common failure point. When it fails, you get a slow leak from the tank seam.
>
> Radiator core designs vary. Cross-flow radiators have tanks on the sides — coolant flows horizontally. Down-flow radiators have tanks on top and bottom — coolant flows vertically. Cross-flow is more common in modern vehicles because it fits the lower bonnet lines. Some radiators also house a transmission oil cooler inside one of the tanks — a separate set of tubes that cools automatic transmission fluid."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE RADIATOR — PRIMARY HEAT EXCHANGER

Construction:
  Core: Aluminium flat tubes + louvered fins
  Tanks: Plastic (crimped to core with rubber gasket)
  Common failure: Tank gasket seepage at crimp joint

Core types:
  Cross-flow — tanks left/right, coolant flows horizontally
  Down-flow — tanks top/bottom, coolant flows vertically

Additional features:
  Transmission oil cooler (inside one tank)
  Drain petcock (bottom — for coolant drain)
  Mounting bushings (rubber isolators — vibration damping)

Heat rejection capacity: 30-60 kW (typical passenger car)

Common failures:
  Plastic tank cracks (age, vibration, pressure cycling)
  Core blockage (internal — corrosion/debris)
  Core damage (external — stone impact, bent fins)
  Tank gasket leak (seepage at crimp)
```

#### Slide 7: The Pressure Cap — More Than a Cap
**Visual:** Cross-section of a radiator/expansion tank pressure cap showing the pressure valve (spring-loaded disc) and vacuum valve. Include pressure-temperature relationship chart showing that at 1.0 bar cap, boiling point rises from 100 degrees C to approximately 121 degrees C.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "This small cap is one of the most important components in the system, and the most misunderstood. The pressure cap does two things. First, it pressurises the system. A typical cap holds the system at 0.9 to 1.4 bar above atmospheric pressure. Why does that matter? Because pressurised water boils at a higher temperature. At atmospheric pressure, water boils at 100 degrees. At 1.0 bar of pressure, the boiling point rises to about 121 degrees. With a 50/50 coolant mix, you push that even higher — to about 129 degrees. That gives you a safety margin above the normal 90-degree operating temperature.
>
> Second, the cap has a vacuum valve. When the engine cools, coolant contracts. The vacuum valve opens and draws coolant back from the expansion tank to prevent air pockets forming in the system. A bad cap — one that cannot hold pressure — lowers the boiling point. The system boils at a lower temperature, and you get overheating even though everything else is fine. Always test the cap."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE PRESSURE CAP — RAISES BOILING POINT

Two valves in one cap:
  PRESSURE VALVE: Opens at rated pressure (0.9 - 1.4 bar typical)
    Releases excess pressure to expansion tank
  VACUUM VALVE: Opens on cooling (negative pressure)
    Draws coolant back from expansion tank

WHY PRESSURIZE?
  Water boils at 100 C at atmospheric pressure
  At +1.0 bar: boiling point rises to ~121 C
  With 50/50 coolant: boiling point rises to ~129 C
  Operating temperature: ~90 C
  Safety margin: ~39 C above normal

COMMON FAILURES:
  Spring weakened — cannot hold rated pressure
  Seal deteriorated — slow pressure loss
  Vacuum valve stuck — air enters system on cool-down

ALWAYS test cap pressure when diagnosing overheating.
Cap rating is printed on the cap (e.g., "1.1 bar").
```

#### Slide 8: Hoses and Clamps
**Visual:** Labelled photo showing upper radiator hose, lower radiator hose, heater hoses, bypass hose. Inset images comparing spring clamps (OEM squeeze type), worm-drive clamps (aftermarket screw type), and constant-tension clamps.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Hoses are the arteries. The upper hose carries hot coolant from the engine to the radiator. The lower hose carries cooled coolant from the radiator back to the water pump. Heater hoses run to and from the heater core inside the dashboard. And there is often a smaller bypass hose near the thermostat housing.
>
> Hoses are made of EPDM rubber — ethylene propylene diene monomer. They resist heat, coolant chemicals, and ozone. But they do age. After 5 to 7 years, the rubber degrades from the inside out. A hose can look fine externally but be crumbling internally. Squeeze it — if it feels mushy or crunchy, it needs replacement.
>
> Clamps matter too. OEM vehicles typically use spring clamps — they apply constant tension as the hose expands and contracts with temperature. Worm-drive clamps — the ones with the screw — are common in aftermarket repairs. They work, but they apply point pressure and can cut into the hose if overtightened. Constant-tension clamps are the best aftermarket alternative. Whatever you use, the clamp must be positioned correctly — behind the bead on the fitting, not on the flexible part of the hose."

**PPT Content:**
```
HOSES — THE ARTERIES

Upper radiator hose: Engine outlet → Radiator inlet (HOT side)
Lower radiator hose: Radiator outlet → Water pump inlet (COOL side)
Heater hoses: Engine → Heater core → Engine (cabin heating)
Bypass hose: Short circuit around thermostat (warm-up)

Material: EPDM rubber (heat/chemical resistant)
Typical life: 5-7 years (inspect at every service)

INSPECTION:
  Squeeze test — mushy or crunchy = replace
  Visual check — cracks, bulges, weeping at connections
  Check for internal collapse (lower hose — spring insert)

CLAMP TYPES:
  Spring clamp (OEM) — constant tension, self-adjusting
  Worm-drive clamp (aftermarket) — screw adjustment
  Constant-tension clamp — best aftermarket replacement

Clamp position: Behind the bead on the fitting, never on hose body
```

#### Slide 9: The Water Pump — The Heart of the Circuit
**Visual:** Side-by-side comparison: (1) Mechanical belt-driven water pump — cutaway showing impeller, shaft, bearing, seal, pulley; (2) Electric water pump — brushless motor with integrated impeller, ECU-controlled. Include a photo of a weep hole with coolant residue.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The water pump is the heart of the cooling circuit. It pushes coolant through the entire system. Most engines use a mechanical pump driven by the serpentine belt or timing belt. Inside, an impeller — a rotating disc with vanes — spins and creates flow. The pump has a shaft bearing and a mechanical seal. When that seal fails, coolant leaks through the weep hole on the bottom of the pump body. That weep hole is a diagnostic indicator — if you see coolant staining or crystallised residue around it, the seal is failing.
>
> Newer vehicles — especially hybrids and some turbocharged engines — use electric water pumps. These are controlled by the ECU and can run at variable speeds independent of engine RPM. They can also circulate coolant after the engine is shut off, which is important for turbo cooling and battery thermal management in hybrids. The downside? They are more expensive to replace and require diagnostic tools to bleed the system after replacement."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE WATER PUMP — MOVES THE COOLANT

MECHANICAL (Belt-Driven):
  Driven by: Serpentine belt or timing belt
  Components: Impeller, shaft, bearing, mechanical seal, pulley
  Flow rate: Proportional to engine RPM
  Typical life: 80,000 - 160,000 km (replace with timing belt)
  Failure sign: Weep hole leak, bearing noise, shaft play

ELECTRIC (ECU-Controlled):
  Driven by: Brushless DC motor
  Controlled by: ECM / thermal management ECU
  Advantages:
    Variable speed (independent of engine RPM)
    After-run capability (turbo cooling, EV battery cooling)
    Reduced parasitic drag on engine
  Disadvantage: Higher replacement cost, needs scan tool for bleed

WEEP HOLE:
  Small hole on pump body below the seal
  Coolant residue here = seal failing → replace pump
  Do NOT plug the weep hole — it is a designed drain path
```

#### Slide 10: The Thermostat — Temperature Gatekeeper
**Visual:** Cutaway of a wax pellet thermostat in closed and open positions, showing the wax element, piston, return spring, and valve plate. Second image showing an electronically heated thermostat with wiring connector. Include a chart: temperature vs. valve opening (start-open at 82 degrees C, fully open at 95 degrees C typical).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The thermostat is a simple but critical valve. Inside is a wax pellet. When the wax is cold, it is solid and the spring holds the valve closed. As coolant temperature rises, the wax melts and expands, pushing against a piston that opens the valve. The thermostat does not just snap open. It begins opening at its rated temperature — typically 82 to 88 degrees — and is fully open about 10 to 15 degrees higher. This progressive opening gives smooth temperature regulation.
>
> Modern vehicles increasingly use electronically heated thermostats. These have a small electric heating element that the ECU can activate to open the thermostat slightly earlier or later than the wax alone would. This gives the ECU precise control over engine temperature — running slightly hotter for fuel efficiency and emissions compliance, or cooler under heavy load.
>
> The bypass circuit is important to understand. When the thermostat is closed, coolant still circulates — just not through the radiator. It flows through a bypass passage back to the pump. This ensures even heat distribution in the engine block during warm-up and keeps coolant flowing over the temperature sensor."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE THERMOSTAT — TEMPERATURE GATEKEEPER

WAX PELLET (Conventional):
  Cold: Wax solid → spring holds valve CLOSED
  Warm: Wax melts → expands → pushes piston → valve OPENS
  Start-open temperature: 82-88 C (stamped on thermostat)
  Fully open: ~95-100 C
  Progressive opening — not on/off

ELECTRONICALLY HEATED (Modern):
  Standard wax pellet + electric heating element
  ECU can force early opening or hold closed longer
  Benefits: Precise temperature control for emissions/efficiency

BYPASS CIRCUIT:
  When thermostat is closed, coolant still circulates
  Flow path: Engine → bypass passage → back to pump
  Purpose: Even heat distribution during warm-up

COMMON FAILURES:
  Stuck CLOSED: Engine overheats rapidly
  Stuck OPEN: Engine never reaches operating temperature
    → Poor heater performance, increased fuel consumption
    → Illuminated MIL (engine temperature below threshold DTC)
```

#### Slide 11: The Expansion / Header Tank
**Visual:** Photo of a translucent expansion tank with MIN/MAX markings, pressure cap on top, and overflow hose. Diagram showing how it connects to the system — acting as an air separation and coolant reserve.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The expansion tank — sometimes called the header tank or overflow tank — serves three purposes. First, it provides a reservoir for coolant expansion. When coolant heats up, it expands — a 50/50 mix expands about 5 percent from cold to operating temperature. That extra volume goes into the expansion tank. When the engine cools, it contracts and draws coolant back from the tank through the vacuum valve in the cap.
>
> Second, it is an air separation device. Any air bubbles in the system rise to the highest point — which is designed to be the expansion tank. This is why it is mounted high in the engine bay. Third, it gives you a visual coolant level indicator. The MIN and MAX marks let you check the level without opening the system. Always check the level when the engine is cold. And never open a hot pressurised system — the coolant is above its atmospheric boiling point and will flash to steam."

**PPT Content:**
```
EXPANSION / HEADER TANK

Three functions:
  1. VOLUME RESERVE: Accommodates coolant expansion (~5%) as
     temperature rises from cold to operating temperature
  2. AIR SEPARATION: Mounted high — air bubbles rise and collect here
  3. LEVEL INDICATOR: Translucent with MIN/MAX marks (check cold)

Connection to system:
  Hose to radiator neck or engine
  Pressure cap on tank (pressurised system) OR
  Pressure cap on radiator, overflow hose to non-pressurised tank

WARNINGS:
  NEVER open a hot pressurised system
  Coolant at 120 C will flash to steam — severe burn risk
  Always check level with engine COLD
  Level between MIN and MAX when cold = correct

INSPECTION:
  Cracks in plastic (especially around cap neck — stress area)
  Discolouration (brown/rusty = internal corrosion)
  Coolant residue on exterior (cap seal or crack)
```

#### Slide 12: Radiator Fans — Forced Airflow
**Visual:** Engine bay photo showing a dual electric fan assembly mounted behind the radiator. Diagram showing fan control logic: temperature sensor signal to ECU, ECU activates relay (low speed/high speed) or sends PWM signal to brushless fan motor.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "When the car is moving at speed, ram air through the grille cools the radiator. But what about in traffic, at idle, or low speed? That is when the electric cooling fan activates. The fan mounts behind the radiator — it pulls air through the core.
>
> Fan control comes in several configurations. Older vehicles use a temperature switch or ECU-controlled relay with two speeds — low speed through a resistor, high speed at full voltage. This gives a noticeable on/off cycling. Modern vehicles use PWM-controlled brushless fans. The ECU varies the fan speed continuously from 0 to 100 percent based on coolant temperature, A/C demand, and even vehicle speed. This is quieter, more efficient, and provides finer temperature control.
>
> Some vehicles have dual fans — one for cooling, one primarily for the A/C condenser mounted in front of the radiator. Both may run together under high thermal load. A fan that does not come on is a common cause of overheating in traffic. Always verify fan operation as part of cooling system diagnostics."

**PPT Content:**
```
RADIATOR FANS — FORCED AIRFLOW

When needed: Low speed driving, idle, traffic, A/C active
Location: Behind radiator (pulling air through core)

CONTROL METHODS:

1. RELAY CONTROL (Older):
   Temperature switch or ECU triggers relay
   Two speeds: Low (via resistor) / High (direct 12V)
   Audible on/off cycling

2. PWM CONTROL (Modern):
   ECU sends pulse-width modulated signal
   Brushless motor — variable speed 0-100%
   Continuous adjustment — quiet, efficient
   Factors: Coolant temp, A/C pressure, vehicle speed

3. DUAL FAN:
   Primary fan — engine cooling
   Secondary fan — A/C condenser + additional cooling
   Both run together under heavy thermal load

DIAGNOSTICS:
  Fan does not run → Check relay, fuse, wiring, fan motor
  Fan runs constantly → Possible sensor fault, ECU fault, overheat
  Fan noise → Bearing wear, cracked blade, debris
```

#### Slide 13: Coolant Types — OAT, IAT, HOAT
**Visual:** Three coolant bottles in different colours (green IAT, orange/red OAT, yellow/turquoise HOAT) with a comparison table. Include molecular diagrams or simplified chemical representation showing the additive differences.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "On Day 3 we touched on coolant types briefly. Now we go deep. All engine coolants are a mixture of water and ethylene glycol — that base provides freeze protection and raises the boiling point. The difference between coolant types is the additive package — the corrosion inhibitors that protect the metals inside the engine.
>
> IAT — Inorganic Additive Technology — is the traditional green coolant. It uses mineral salts like silicates and phosphates as corrosion inhibitors. These form a protective layer on all metal surfaces. The problem? That layer depletes over time. IAT coolant needs replacement every two years or 50,000 kilometres.
>
> OAT — Organic Acid Technology — uses organic acids like sebacic acid and 2-ethylhexanoic acid. These do not form a blanket coating. Instead, they target only the areas where corrosion is actually occurring. This means the inhibitors last much longer — typically five years or 250,000 kilometres. OAT is usually orange or red. Many European manufacturers specify OAT.
>
> HOAT — Hybrid Organic Acid Technology — combines both. It uses organic acids as the primary inhibitor plus small amounts of silicates for additional aluminium protection. HOAT is common in Asian and some European vehicles. It is often yellow or turquoise.
>
> Here is the critical rule: never mix types. Mixing IAT and OAT can cause the inhibitors to react, forming gel and sludge that blocks the radiator and heater core. Always check the manufacturer specification and use only the approved coolant."

**PPT Content:**
```
COOLANT TYPES — THE CHEMISTRY MATTERS

ALL coolants: Ethylene glycol + Water + Additive package

IAT (Inorganic Additive Technology):
  Colour: Usually GREEN
  Inhibitors: Silicates, phosphates (mineral salts)
  Protection: Blanket coating on all metal surfaces
  Service life: 2 years / 50,000 km
  Used by: Older vehicles, some Japanese manufacturers

OAT (Organic Acid Technology):
  Colour: Usually ORANGE or RED
  Inhibitors: Organic acids (sebacate, 2-EHA)
  Protection: Targeted — only where corrosion occurs
  Service life: 5 years / 250,000 km
  Used by: VW/Audi (G12/G13), GM (Dex-Cool), many European OEMs

HOAT (Hybrid Organic Acid Technology):
  Colour: Usually YELLOW or TURQUOISE
  Inhibitors: Organic acids + silicates
  Protection: Targeted + aluminium blanket protection
  Service life: 5 years / 150,000 km
  Used by: Chrysler, Ford, many Asian manufacturers

NEVER MIX TYPES — chemical reaction causes gelling and blockage
Always check manufacturer specification (e.g., VW G13, BMW HT-12)
```

#### Slide 14: Coolant Concentration and Freeze/Boil Protection
**Visual:** Chart showing freeze point and boiling point as a function of ethylene glycol concentration (0% to 100%). Highlight the 50/50 sweet spot. Photo of a refractometer being used — show the scale through the eyepiece.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The concentration of ethylene glycol in the coolant determines your freeze protection and boiling point elevation. Pure water freezes at 0 degrees and boils at 100 degrees. A 50/50 mix of ethylene glycol and water freezes at minus 37 degrees and boils at 129 degrees under a 1-bar pressure cap. That is the standard recommendation for most climates.
>
> Here is the counterintuitive part: more glycol is NOT better. Pure ethylene glycol actually has worse heat transfer than a 50/50 mix, and its freeze point is only minus 13 degrees — worse than a 50/50 mix. The ideal range is 40 to 60 percent glycol. Below 40 percent, you lose freeze protection. Above 60 percent, you lose cooling efficiency.
>
> How do you test it? With a refractometer. You place a drop of coolant on the glass, close the cover, point it at a light source, and read the scale through the eyepiece. It gives you the freeze point directly. Some also show the glycol percentage. We will practice this in the practical session."

**PPT Content:**
```
COOLANT CONCENTRATION — THE 50/50 SWEET SPOT

                    Freeze Point    Boiling Point (1 bar cap)
Pure water:              0 C              121 C
33% glycol / 67% water: -18 C             125 C
50% glycol / 50% water: -37 C             129 C
60% glycol / 40% water: -52 C             132 C
Pure glycol:            -13 C             197 C (but poor heat transfer)

IDEAL RANGE: 40-60% ethylene glycol
RECOMMENDED: 50/50 (best balance of protection and cooling)

MORE IS NOT BETTER:
  Pure glycol = worse freeze protection AND worse heat transfer
  Above 60% — diminishing returns, reduced cooling capacity

TESTING TOOL: Refractometer
  Place drop on glass prism → look through eyepiece
  Read freeze point on internal scale
  Quick, accurate, no mess
  Also available: Test strips, hydrometers (less accurate)
```

#### Slide 15: Corrosion, Degradation, and Why Coolant Goes Bad
**Visual:** Photos showing (1) rusty coolant drained from a neglected system, (2) clogged heater core cut open showing scale buildup, (3) corroded water pump impeller, (4) pH test strip showing acidic coolant. Timeline graphic showing coolant degradation over 0-10 years.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Coolant does not last forever. Even long-life OAT coolant degrades. The organic acids get consumed. The pH drops — coolant becomes acidic. Acidic coolant attacks aluminium, solder, and gasket materials. Corrosion products circulate through the system and deposit in the radiator and heater core, blocking flow. The water pump impeller erodes. Hose rubber deteriorates from the inside.
>
> Using tap water instead of distilled or demineralised water introduces minerals — calcium, magnesium, chlorides — that accelerate scale formation and corrosion. This is why manufacturer specifications often state 'mix with demineralised water only.'
>
> How do you check coolant condition? Concentration with a refractometer — we covered that. pH with test strips — healthy coolant should be between 7.5 and 11.0 depending on type. Below 7.0 means acidic — flush and replace. Visual inspection — healthy coolant is clear and bright in its intended colour. Brown, rusty, or milky coolant means contamination. And always check for oil in the coolant — milky, frothy coolant often indicates a head gasket breach. We will test for that with the block tester."

**PPT Content:**
```
COOLANT DEGRADATION — WHY IT GOES BAD

Over time:
  Corrosion inhibitors deplete → pH drops → coolant becomes acidic
  Acidic coolant attacks: aluminium, solder joints, gaskets, seals
  Corrosion products: rust flakes, scale, sludge
  Result: Blocked radiator, blocked heater core, eroded pump

ACCELERATORS OF DEGRADATION:
  Using tap water (minerals cause scale + chloride corrosion)
  Mixing coolant types (gelling, inhibitor neutralisation)
  Air in system (oxygen accelerates corrosion)
  Excessive heat cycling (thermal stress on additives)

COOLANT CONDITION CHECKS:
  Refractometer — concentration / freeze point
  pH test strips — should be 7.5-11.0 (below 7.0 = replace)
  Visual — bright, clear, correct colour = healthy
           Brown/rusty = corrosion contamination
           Milky/frothy = oil contamination (head gasket suspect)
  Smell — sweet, sharp = normal; burnt = overheating history

MAINTENANCE: Flush and replace per manufacturer schedule
  IAT: Every 2 years / 50,000 km
  OAT/HOAT: Every 5 years / 150,000-250,000 km (verify with OEM)
```

#### Slide 18: Practical 2 — Thermostat Testing (Boil Test)
**Visual:** Step-by-step photo sequence: (1) Removed thermostat on bench — closed position, (2) Thermostat suspended in a pot of water on a hot plate with a thermometer, (3) Thermostat beginning to open — thermometer reads 84 degrees C, (4) Thermostat fully open — thermometer reads 96 degrees C. Infrared thermometer being pointed at the thermostat.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "This is one of the most satisfying tests in automotive diagnostics. We have removed a thermostat from a vehicle. First, inspect it visually — is it closed? Is the wax pellet intact? Is the seal in good condition? Now, we suspend it in a pot of water on the hot plate. Put a thermometer in the water — or better, use the infrared thermometer to monitor temperature as the water heats up.
>
> Watch the thermostat as temperature rises. It should begin to open at its rated temperature — stamped on the body. Typically 82, 85, or 88 degrees. It should be fully open by about 95 to 100 degrees. Record the start-open and fully-open temperatures and compare to the specification. If it opens too early, the engine will run too cold. If it opens too late or not at all, the engine will overheat. If it does not open at all even at boiling — it is stuck closed and must be replaced."

**PPT Content:**
```
PRACTICAL 2: THERMOSTAT TESTING — BOIL TEST (20 min)

EQUIPMENT: Hot plate, pot of water, thermometer or IR thermometer,
           removed thermostat, tongs

PROCEDURE:
  1. INSPECT thermostat: Should be CLOSED at room temperature
     Note rated temperature stamped on body (e.g., 87 C)
  2. Suspend thermostat in pot of water (do not rest on bottom)
  3. Heat water slowly — monitor temperature
  4. OBSERVE: Note temperature when thermostat BEGINS to open
  5. Continue heating — note temperature when FULLY open
  6. Compare to specification

SPECIFICATION (typical):
  Start open: 82-88 C (rated temperature +/- 2 C)
  Fully open: 95-100 C
  Minimum lift (fully open): 8-10 mm

PASS/FAIL:
  Opens within spec range → PASS
  Opens too early (> 5 C below rating) → FAIL (runs cold)
  Opens too late or not at all → FAIL (overheats)
  Does not close fully when cooled → FAIL (runs cold)
```

#### Slide 19: Practical 3 — Cooling System Pressure Test
**Visual:** Photo sequence: (1) Pressure tester adapter fitted to radiator/expansion tank neck, (2) Hand pump being squeezed — gauge reads 1.0 bar, (3) Technician watching gauge for pressure drop, (4) Technician inspecting hose connections and water pump weep hole for leaks while system is pressurised

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The pressure test is the standard diagnostic for finding leaks. The principle is simple — we pressurise the system to the cap's rated pressure and watch for pressure loss. If the gauge drops, there is a leak somewhere.
>
> Here is how to do it. Engine off and cold. Remove the pressure cap. Select the correct adapter for the filler neck — the kit comes with many sizes. Connect the adapter and pump. Pump up to the cap's rated pressure — if the cap is rated at 1.1 bar, pump to 1.1 bar. No higher — you can damage components by over-pressurising. Now watch the gauge for two minutes. A healthy system holds pressure with zero drop. A slow drop means a small leak — inspect every hose connection, the water pump weep hole, the radiator seams, and the heater core area. Look for wet spots, drips, or coolant residue.
>
> You can also test the cap itself. Most pressure tester kits have a cap adapter. Pump it up. The cap should hold its rated pressure and release at or just above that value. If it leaks below rated pressure, replace the cap."

**PPT Content:**
```
PRACTICAL 3: COOLING SYSTEM PRESSURE TEST (20 min)

EQUIPMENT: Cooling system pressure tester (hand pump + adapters)

PROCEDURE:
  1. Engine OFF and COLD
  2. Remove pressure cap
  3. Select correct adapter for filler neck
  4. Attach adapter and pump to cap's rated pressure
     (Read cap rating — typically 0.9-1.4 bar)
  5. DO NOT exceed cap rating
  6. Watch gauge for 2 minutes

RESULTS:
  Pressure holds steady → System sealed, no leaks
  Pressure drops slowly → Small leak — inspect:
    → Hose connections and clamps
    → Radiator tank seams
    → Water pump weep hole
    → Heater core (check carpet for dampness)
    → Freeze plug / core plugs
  Pressure drops rapidly → Major leak — visible coolant

CAP TEST:
  Attach cap to tester adapter
  Pump — cap should hold rated pressure
  Should release at or slightly above rated value
  Replace if it leaks below rated pressure

Always re-install cap after testing.
```

#### Slide 20: Practical 4 — Refractometer Coolant Concentration Test
**Visual:** Close-up photo sequence: (1) Opening the refractometer cover plate, (2) Using a pipette to place two drops of coolant on the glass prism, (3) Closing the cover plate, (4) Looking through the eyepiece at a light source — the scale visible showing freeze point at -37 degrees C / 50% concentration

**Instructor Narration:**
> "This is the quickest and most accurate field test for coolant concentration. Open the daylight plate on the refractometer. Use a pipette to place two drops of coolant on the glass prism. Close the plate — make sure there are no air bubbles. Point the refractometer toward a light source — window or lamp — and look through the eyepiece. You will see a scale with a clear boundary line between light and dark. Read the freeze point where that boundary crosses the scale.
>
> A healthy 50/50 mix should show approximately minus 37 degrees. If you see minus 15 degrees, the coolant is too dilute — not enough glycol. If the boundary is hard to read or the coolant is cloudy, the coolant may be contaminated and needs replacement.
>
> Calibrate the refractometer with distilled water first — the boundary should sit at exactly zero. If it does not, adjust the calibration screw."

**PPT Content:**
```
PRACTICAL 4: REFRACTOMETER TEST (10 min)

EQUIPMENT: Optical refractometer, pipette, distilled water for
           calibration, coolant sample

PROCEDURE:
  1. CALIBRATE: Place distilled water on prism
     → Look through eyepiece → boundary should read 0 C
     → Adjust calibration screw if needed
  2. Wipe prism clean with soft cloth
  3. Place 2 drops of coolant on prism
  4. Close daylight plate — no air bubbles
  5. Point toward light source
  6. Read freeze point where boundary crosses scale

INTERPRETATION:
  -37 C or below: Correct 50/50 mix → GOOD
  -20 to -36 C: Dilute — needs adjustment or topping up with concentrate
  -10 to -19 C: Significantly dilute — flush and refill
  Above -10 C: Almost no protection — likely topped up with water

NOTES:
  Clean prism between samples
  Test is instant — no waiting
  Works for ethylene glycol and propylene glycol (different scales)

#### Slide 21: Practical 5 — Hose, Pump, and Radiator Inspection
**Visual:** Inspection checklist photos: (1) Squeeze testing a hose — finger pressing into rubber, (2) Inspecting a lower hose with internal spring visible, (3) Water pump weep hole with tell-tale coolant stain, (4) Radiator fins — clean vs blocked with debris, (5) Radiator tank seam with minor seepage

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The visual and tactile inspection is something you do at every service — it takes two minutes and can catch problems before they leave someone stranded. Start with the hoses. >

>
> Inspect the water pump weep hole — that small hole on the pump body. Any coolant residue here means the internal seal is leaking. Check the radiator fins — are they bent, blocked with bugs and debris, or damaged from stone impacts? Clean with low-pressure water — never high pressure, which bends the delicate fins. Check the radiator tank seams for any sign of seepage — wet residue along the crimp line."

#### Slide 22: Advanced Diagnostic — Block Test (Head Gasket Leak Detection)
**Visual:** Photo of a block tester (combustion leak detector) — a cylindrical chamber with blue test fluid, placed over the radiator/expansion tank neck. Show the colour change: blue fluid turning yellow/green when combustion gases are detected. Diagram showing how a breached head gasket allows combustion gas into the coolant jacket.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "This is the test you hope comes back negative. The block test — also called a combustion leak test — detects whether combustion gases are leaking into the coolant through a compromised head gasket. The test fluid contains a pH-sensitive chemical that changes colour from blue to yellow or green in the presence of carbon dioxide and hydrocarbons — the byproducts of combustion.
>
> Here is the procedure. Engine warm, running at idle. Remove the pressure cap — carefully, engine is warm. Place the block tester chamber over the filler neck. The chamber contains the blue test fluid. Squeeze the bulb to draw air from the cooling system up through the fluid. If the head gasket is intact, no combustion gases are present and the fluid stays blue. If the gasket is breached, combustion gases enter the coolant and rise to the surface. The fluid turns yellow or green — that is a positive result.
>
> Important: do not let coolant splash up into the tester — it will contaminate the fluid and give a false reading. Some technicians lower the coolant level slightly or use a baffled adapter to prevent this. A positive block test combined with symptoms like white exhaust smoke, coolant loss without visible leak, milky oil, and overheating confirms head gasket failure."

**PPT Content:**
```
BLOCK TEST — HEAD GASKET LEAK DETECTION

PURPOSE: Detect combustion gas leaking into coolant
         (CO2 / hydrocarbons from breached head gasket)

EQUIPMENT: Combustion leak detector (block tester)
  — Cylindrical chamber with pH-sensitive blue fluid

PROCEDURE:
  1. Engine WARM, running at IDLE
  2. Remove pressure cap (CAREFULLY — system is warm)
  3. Lower coolant level slightly (prevent splash contamination)
  4. Place tester chamber over filler neck
  5. Squeeze bulb — draw air from cooling system through fluid
  6. Observe fluid colour change

RESULTS:
  Fluid stays BLUE → No combustion gas detected → PASS
  Fluid turns YELLOW/GREEN → Combustion gas detected → FAIL
    → Head gasket breach confirmed

SUPPORTING SYMPTOMS OF HEAD GASKET FAILURE:
  White smoke from exhaust (coolant burning in cylinder)
  Coolant loss with no visible external leak
  Milky residue on oil filler cap (oil + coolant mixing)
  Overheating, especially under load
  Bubbles in expansion tank at idle
  Exhaust gas smell at expansion tank

CAUTION: Do not let coolant splash into tester = false positive
```

#### Slide 23: Diagnostic Decision Tree — Overheating
**Visual:** Flowchart/decision tree: "Customer complaint: Engine overheating" with branching paths: Check coolant level (low → leak test; normal → check thermostat), check fan operation (not running → relay/fuse/motor), check circulation (blocked radiator → flush/replace), check for combustion leak (block test positive → head gasket)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let me give you a diagnostic framework you can use every time a vehicle comes in with an overheating complaint. Start with the basics — check the coolant level. If it is low, the system has a leak. Pressure test to find it. If the level is correct, check the thermostat — is the upper hose getting hot after the engine reaches temperature? If the upper hose stays cold, the thermostat is stuck closed.
>
> Next, check the fan. With the engine at operating temperature and A/C on, the fan should be running. If not — check the fuse, relay, wiring, and fan motor. If everything is running but still overheating, you may have a blocked radiator — restricted internal flow. A thermal imaging camera or infrared thermometer across the radiator face will show cold spots where flow is blocked.
>
> Finally, if none of the above explains it, perform the block test. A head gasket leak can cause overheating even when all other components are functional — combustion pressure enters the coolant jacket and pushes coolant out of the system."

**PPT Content:**
```
DIAGNOSTIC DECISION TREE — OVERHEATING

  Normal → Go to Step 2

Step 2: CHECK THERMOSTAT

  Upper hose hot → Go to Step 3

## Key Takeaways

1. An engine converts only 30% of fuel energy to motion — the cooling system must remove approximately 30-35% of total fuel energy as waste heat
2. The cooling system REGULATES temperature (not just removes heat) — too cold is also a problem
3. The thermostat is the gatekeeper — it holds heat in until the engine is warm, then releases it to the radiator
4. The pressure cap raises the boiling point — a weak cap can cause overheating even with a perfectly functioning system
5. Coolant type matters — OAT, IAT, and HOAT have different chemistries and must never be mixed
6. The five essential cooling system diagnostic tests: visual inspection, pressure test, thermostat test, refractometer test, and block test
7. Day 16 (oil) + Day 17 (coolant) = the two systems that keep the engine thermally alive
