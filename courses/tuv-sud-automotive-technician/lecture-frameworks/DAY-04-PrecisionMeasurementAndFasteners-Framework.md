### **DEVELOPMENT PART 1: Measurement Tools** (Slides 4-12, ~30 minutes)

#### Slide 4: Outside Micrometer — Anatomy
**Visual:** Labelled diagram — frame, anvil, spindle, sleeve (barrel), thimble, ratchet stop, lock

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The outside micrometer. The most precise hand tool you'll use. The anvil is fixed. The spindle moves. The object sits between them. The sleeve has a linear scale in 0.5 mm divisions. The thimble has 50 divisions around its circumference — each division is 0.01 mm. The ratchet stop ensures consistent measuring pressure — always use it, never force the thimble. The lock holds the reading so you can remove the tool without losing the measurement."

**PPT Content:**
```
OUTSIDE MICROMETER — PARTS

FRAME: C-shaped body (holds everything rigid)
ANVIL: Fixed reference surface (left side)
SPINDLE: Moving surface (right side, advances by rotating thimble)
SLEEVE (BARREL): Linear scale — 1mm and 0.5mm divisions
THIMBLE: Rotary scale — 50 divisions = 0.01mm each
RATCHET STOP: Ensures consistent pressure (ALWAYS use it)
LOCK: Freezes the spindle position for reading

Range: 0-25mm, 25-50mm, 50-75mm, 75-100mm (each mic covers 25mm)
```

#### Slide 5: Reading a Micrometer
**Visual:** Close-up of a micrometer reading with arrows pointing to each scale element

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Reading in three steps. Step 1: Read the sleeve — count the whole millimetres visible. Step 2: Check if a half-millimetre line is exposed beyond the last whole number. Step 3: Read the thimble — which line aligns with the datum line on the sleeve. Add them up. Example: sleeve shows 5 and a half — that's 5.50. Thimble shows 28 — that's 0.28. Total: 5.78 mm. Let me demonstrate with a real micrometer."

**PPT Content:**
```
READING A MICROMETER — 3 STEPS

Step 1: SLEEVE whole mm         → e.g., 5.00 mm
Step 2: SLEEVE half-mm visible? → e.g., + 0.50 mm
Step 3: THIMBLE line at datum   → e.g., + 0.28 mm
                                  ═══════════════
TOTAL:                             5.78 mm

BEFORE MEASURING:
1. Clean anvil and spindle (wipe with lint-free cloth)
2. Zero check (close gently with ratchet — should read 0.00)
3. If not zero → adjust or note the error
```

**Activity:** Instructor demonstrates 3 readings live on a projected micrometer or large teaching model.

#### Slide 6: Vernier Caliper — Anatomy & Reading
**Visual:** Labelled caliper — main scale, vernier scale, inside jaws, outside jaws, depth rod

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The vernier caliper is more versatile than the micrometer — it measures outside diameters, inside diameters, depths, and steps. Resolution is 0.02 mm. The main scale reads in millimetres. The vernier scale has 50 divisions across 49 mm — so each division is 0.02 mm less than 1 mm. To read: take the main scale reading where the zero of the vernier falls, then find which vernier line aligns perfectly with any main scale line."

**PPT Content:**
```
VERNIER CALIPER

THREE MEASUREMENT MODES:
• Outside jaws: External diameters, lengths, thicknesses
• Inside jaws: Bore diameters, slot widths
• Depth rod: Hole depths, step heights

READING (0.02 mm resolution):
1. Main scale: mm reading at vernier zero line
2. Vernier scale: find the line that ALIGNS with main scale
3. Add them: main + (vernier line × 0.02)

Example: Main = 23 mm, Vernier line 17 aligns
→ 23 + (17 × 0.02) = 23.34 mm

Zero check: Close jaws → should read 0.00
```

#### Slide 7: Dial Gauge (DTI)
**Visual:** Dial gauge mounted on magnetic stand, measuring brake disc runout

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The dial test indicator — DTI — measures small movements, not absolute sizes. You mount it on a magnetic stand, position the plunger against the surface, zero it, then rotate or move the component. The needle shows how much the surface deviates. This is how you measure brake disc runout, shaft straightness, and cylinder bore taper. One full revolution of the small hand is typically 1 mm; each division on the large face is 0.01 mm."

**PPT Content:**
```
DIAL GAUGE / DTI (Dial Test Indicator)

PURPOSE: Measure surface deviation (runout, straightness, taper)
NOT for absolute measurement — measures VARIATION from zero

SETUP:
1. Mount magnetic stand on stable surface (turn magnet ON)
2. Position plunger perpendicular to surface being measured
3. Pre-load plunger ~1mm into its travel
4. Zero the dial face (rotate bezel)
5. Move/rotate the component — read deviation

COMMON USES:
• Brake disc lateral runout (max typically 0.05-0.10 mm)
• Crankshaft straightness
• Cylinder bore taper measurement
• Flywheel runout

Resolution: 0.01 mm per division (typically)
```

#### Slide 8: Feeler Gauges
**Visual:** Fan of feeler gauge blades, close-up of a blade being inserted into a valve clearance gap

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Feeler gauges are thin metal blades of known thickness. You slide them into a gap to measure its width. Select the blade that fits snugly — slight resistance on entry and exit, but not forced. The most common application is valve clearance checking, but you'll also use them for spark plug gaps, bearing clearances, and gasket surface checks. You can stack blades to make intermediate sizes — 0.15 mm + 0.10 mm = 0.25 mm."

**PPT Content:**
```
FEELER GAUGES

PURPOSE: Measure gap width by inserting blades of known thickness
RANGE: Typically 0.02 mm to 1.00 mm in a set

TECHNIQUE:
1. Select blade matching the specified gap
2. Insert between surfaces — should feel SNUG drag
3. "Go / No-Go": specified blade fits, next size up doesn't

STACKING: Combine blades for intermediate sizes
e.g., 0.15 + 0.10 = 0.25 mm

COMMON USES:
• Valve clearance (tappet clearance)
• Spark plug gap
• Piston ring end gap
• Gasket surface flatness check

CARE: Keep clean, lightly oiled, never bend blades
```

#### Slide 9: Bore, Ovality & Clearance
**Visual:** Cross-section diagram showing bore measurement at two perpendicular positions, taper at top vs bottom

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Three concepts that connect measurement to engine condition. Bore is the cylinder diameter — measured with a bore gauge or inside micrometer. Ovality is the difference between the bore measured in two perpendicular directions — a worn cylinder becomes oval, not round. Taper is the difference between the bore at the top and bottom — the top wears more because that's where combustion pressure is highest. Clearance is the gap between the piston and the bore wall — too tight and it seizes, too loose and it slaps and burns oil."

**PPT Content:**
```
BORE, OVALITY & CLEARANCE

BORE: Cylinder internal diameter (measured with bore gauge)
Typical: 80.000 mm ± 0.010 mm

OVALITY: Difference between two perpendicular bore measurements
Limit: Typically < 0.05 mm
If exceeded → bore needs honing or reboring

TAPER: Difference between bore at top and bottom of stroke
Limit: Typically < 0.05 mm
Top wears more (highest combustion pressure)

PISTON-TO-WALL CLEARANCE:
= Bore diameter − Piston diameter
Typical: 0.02–0.04 mm
Too tight → seizure | Too loose → slap, oil consumption
```

#### Slide 10: Measurement Errors to Avoid
**Visual:** "Do" and "Don't" comparison images for each tool

**PPT Content:**
```
COMMON MEASUREMENT ERRORS

MICROMETER:
❌ Over-tightening (not using ratchet stop)
❌ Measuring on a dirty surface
❌ Not zeroing before use
✅ Clean surfaces, ratchet stop, zero check

CALIPER:
❌ Tilted jaws (not square to surface)
❌ Excessive jaw pressure (flexing the beam)
✅ Square contact, light consistent pressure

DIAL GAUGE:
❌ Plunger not perpendicular to surface
❌ Loose magnetic stand (moves during measurement)
✅ Rigid mount, perpendicular plunger, pre-load

FEELER GAUGE:
❌ Forcing a blade that doesn't fit
❌ Using bent or dirty blades
✅ Snug drag, clean blades, go/no-go method
```

#### Slide 11: Digital vs. Analogue
**Visual:** Side-by-side — digital caliper/micrometer vs analogue

**Instructor Narration:**
> "You'll see both digital and analogue tools. Digital gives you a number instantly — less skill to read, less chance of reading error. Analogue requires you to read scales — more skill, but no batteries, no electronics to fail. Learn both. In your exam, expect to read analogue scales. In the workshop, you'll likely use digital for speed. But if the battery dies, you need to read the barrel and thimble."

**PPT Content:**
```
DIGITAL vs. ANALOGUE

DIGITAL:
✅ Easy to read, no interpretation
✅ Can zero at any point (relative measurement)
❌ Battery dependent
❌ Can give false confidence

ANALOGUE:
✅ No batteries, always works
✅ You UNDERSTAND the measurement
❌ Requires practice to read accurately
❌ Parallax error possible

LEARN BOTH. Your exam tests analogue reading.
```

### **DEVELOPMENT PART 2: Fasteners & Sealing** (Slides 13-19, ~20 minutes)

#### Slide 13: Bolt Anatomy & Thread Types
**Visual:** Labelled bolt — head, shank, thread, thread pitch, diameter marking

**Instructor Narration:**
> "A bolt seems simple but has precise specifications. The thread is defined by diameter and pitch — M8x1.25 means 8mm diameter, 1.25mm pitch. Metric is standard in modern vehicles. You'll occasionally see UNF and UNC imperial threads in older or American vehicles. Thread pitch matters: a fine-pitch bolt (M8x1.0) has more threads per length and is stronger in tension than a coarse-pitch bolt (M8x1.25), but it's easier to cross-thread."

**PPT Content:**
```
BOLT ANATOMY

HEAD: Hex, flange, socket (Allen), Torx
SHANK: Unthreaded portion (some bolts are full-thread)
THREAD: Helical ridge — defined by diameter + pitch

METRIC THREADS:
M8 x 1.25 = 8mm diameter, 1.25mm pitch (coarse)
M8 x 1.0 = 8mm diameter, 1.0mm pitch (fine)

IMPERIAL THREADS (older/US vehicles):
UNC (Unified National Coarse): 5/16-18 (18 TPI)
UNF (Unified National Fine): 5/16-24 (24 TPI)

Fine pitch = stronger in tension, easier to cross-thread
Coarse pitch = more tolerant of dirty threads, easier assembly
```

---

#### Slide 14: Bolt Grades — Strength Markings
**Visual:** Bolt head markings — metric (8.8, 10.9, 12.9) and SAE (3 lines = Grade 5, 6 lines = Grade 8)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Every bolt has a strength grade stamped on its head. Metric grades: 8.8 is standard, 10.9 is high-strength, 12.9 is the highest common grade. The first number times 100 gives you tensile strength in MPa — so 10.9 is 1,000 MPa tensile strength. SAE grades use radial lines on the head — Grade 5 has 3 lines, Grade 8 has 6 lines. Never replace a 10.9 bolt with an 8.8 — it WILL fail under load."

**PPT Content:**
```
BOLT GRADES — STRENGTH MARKINGS

METRIC (number on head):
| Grade | Tensile (MPa) | Use |
|-------|---------------|-----|
| 4.6 | 400 | Non-critical, light duty |
| 8.8 | 800 | Standard automotive (most bolts) |
| 10.9 | 1,040 | High-strength (suspension, engine) |
| 12.9 | 1,220 | Critical (flywheel, con-rod, head) |

SAE (radial lines on head):
| Grade | Lines | Equivalent |
|-------|-------|-----------|
| 2 | None | ~4.6 |
| 5 | 3 lines | ~8.8 |
| 8 | 6 lines | ~10.9 |

⚠ NEVER substitute a lower grade bolt
```

#### Slide 15: Torque — Why It Matters
**Visual:** Bolt stretch diagram showing clamping force, cross-section of a head gasket joint

**Instructor Narration:**
> "When you tighten a bolt to a specified torque, you're stretching it — creating a controlled clamping force that holds the joint together. Too little torque: the joint leaks or comes loose. Too much: the bolt yields (permanently stretches) or breaks. Torque specifications exist for every critical fastener in the car. The cylinder head, for example, requires a specific torque PLUS a specific angle — this is torque-to-yield, where you intentionally stretch the bolt into its elastic limit for maximum clamping force."

**PPT Content:**
```
TORQUE — CONTROLLED STRETCH = CONTROLLED CLAMP

Tightening a bolt STRETCHES it → creates CLAMPING FORCE

TOO LITTLE: Joint leaks, bolt loosens, component moves
TOO MUCH: Bolt yields, threads strip, bolt breaks
CORRECT: Joint sealed, bolt in elastic range, reliable

TORQUE METHODS:
1. Click wrench: Set value, tighten until click, stop
2. Beam wrench: Read scale while tightening
3. Digital wrench: Electronic readout, stores values
4. Torque-angle: Torque to value THEN rotate additional degrees
   (e.g., 40 Nm + 90° — intentional yield for max clamp)

⚠ Torque-to-yield bolts are SINGLE USE (replace every time)
⚠ Threads must be clean and lightly oiled (unless spec says dry)
```

---

#### Slide 16: Using a Click Torque Wrench
**Visual:** Step-by-step photos — setting the value, tightening, hearing the click, storing at lowest setting

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The click wrench is your everyday torque tool. Setting it: unlock the handle, rotate to the target value reading on the scale, lock it. Using it: attach the correct socket, position on the bolt, pull smoothly and steadily — when you reach the set torque, the wrench clicks and the handle gives slightly. STOP at the click. Do not continue tightening. After use: ALWAYS return to the lowest setting — leaving it set compresses the internal spring and loses calibration."

**PPT Content:**
```
CLICK TORQUE WRENCH — PROCEDURE

SETTING:
1. Unlock handle (twist/push lock ring)
2. Rotate handle to target value on scale
3. Lock the handle

USING:
1. Attach correct socket (no adapters if possible)
2. Position square on bolt
3. Pull SMOOTHLY and STEADILY
4. STOP at the click — do not continue
5. If you miss the click → loosen fully, re-torque

AFTER USE:
⚠ ALWAYS return to LOWEST setting before storage
(Leaving it set damages the calibration spring)

CALIBRATION: Check annually or per workshop policy
```

#### Slide 17: Gasket Types
**Visual:** Array of gasket types — MLS, cork, paper, copper, rubber O-ring

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Gaskets seal the joint between two surfaces. Multi-Layer Steel — MLS — is the modern cylinder head gasket: layers of spring steel with elastomer coating. It requires extremely flat surfaces — any warp beyond specification and it leaks. Cork gaskets are older style — valve covers on classic engines. Paper gaskets for lower-pressure applications like water pump housings. Rubber O-rings for static seals. The key: match the gasket to the application, and NEVER reuse a gasket unless the manual specifically says you can."

**PPT Content:**
```
GASKET TYPES

| Type | Application | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------|
| MLS (Multi-Layer Steel) | Cylinder head | Requires flat surfaces (check with straightedge) |
| Paper/fibre | Water pump, thermostat | Single use, low pressure |
| Cork | Valve cover (older) | Compresses over time, can reuse with care |
| Copper | Exhaust, high temp | Can be annealed and reused |
| Rubber O-ring | Static seals, oil passages | Check size and material compatibility |
| Metal crush washer | Sump plug, banjo bolts | ALWAYS replace — single use |

RULE: Never reuse a gasket unless manufacturer says OK
RULE: Clean both surfaces PERFECTLY before fitting new gasket
```

---

#### Slide 18: Sealants
**Visual:** RTV sealant tubes (black, grey, red), Loctite bottles (blue 243, red 262, green 271)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Sealants supplement or replace gaskets. RTV silicone — Room Temperature Vulcanising — forms a rubber gasket in place. Black RTV for oil pans and timing covers. Grey for general purpose. Red for high temperature — exhaust manifolds. Apply a thin, continuous bead — more is NOT better; excess squeezes into the engine and blocks oil passages. Thread sealants: Loctite blue 243 is medium-strength, removable with hand tools. Red 262 is permanent — needs heat to remove. Green 271 is for press fits."

**PPT Content:**
```
SEALANTS

RTV SILICONE (forms gasket in place):
| Colour | Use | Temp range |
|--------|-----|-----------|
| Black | Oil pans, timing covers | -54 to 260°C |
| Grey | General purpose | -54 to 260°C |
| Red | High temp (exhaust) | -54 to 345°C |
| Blue | Sensor threads | -54 to 260°C |

Apply: Thin continuous bead — excess blocks oil passages

THREAD SEALANT (Loctite):
| Type | Strength | Removal | Use |
|------|----------|---------|-----|
| Blue (243) | Medium | Hand tools | Most bolts needing lock |
| Red (262) | High (permanent) | Heat required | Critical fasteners |
| Green (271) | Wicking grade | Heat required | Press fits, studs |

⚠ Too much RTV = oil passage blockage = engine damage
⚠ Wrong Loctite = stuck bolt or loose critical fastener
```
