#### Slide 4: Pascal's Law — The Physics of Hydraulic Braking
**Visual:** Animated or step-by-step diagram showing Pascal's Law — small piston (master cylinder) connected to large piston (caliper), with force and area values labeled. Arrows showing pressure transmission.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Everything in a hydraulic brake system comes back to one principle — Pascal's Law. Blaise Pascal, 1600s, French mathematician. His law says: pressure applied to a confined fluid is transmitted equally in all directions throughout the fluid.
>
> What does that mean for braking? You press the brake pedal with, say, 30 Newtons of force. The brake booster amplifies that to about 300 Newtons. That force pushes on the master cylinder piston — let's say it has an area of 3 square centimetres. Pressure equals force divided by area: 300 divided by 3 equals 100 bar of hydraulic pressure.
>
> Now that 100 bar travels through the brake lines — rigid steel tubes, flexible hoses — to the caliper. The caliper piston is much larger — say 30 square centimetres. Force equals pressure times area: 100 bar times 30 equals 3,000 Newtons. You pushed with 30. The caliper pushes with 3,000. That's a 100:1 multiplication. THAT is why hydraulic brakes work. A small human foot can stop a 2-tonne vehicle."

**PPT Content:**
```
PASCAL'S LAW — THE FOUNDATION OF HYDRAULIC BRAKING

"Pressure applied to a confined fluid is transmitted
equally in all directions throughout the fluid."

THE FORCE MULTIPLICATION:

Pedal force:           30 N (your foot)
Booster amplification: x10
Force on master cyl:   300 N
Master cyl area:       3 cm²
Pressure generated:    300 / 3 = 100 bar

Brake line carries 100 bar to caliper...

Caliper piston area:   30 cm²
Caliper clamping force: 100 x 30 = 3,000 N

INPUT: 30 N → OUTPUT: 3,000 N
100:1 force multiplication!

Small foot. Big stopping power.
```

#### Slide 5: The Brake Booster — Amplifying Your Foot
**Visual:** Cross-section diagram of a vacuum brake booster showing diaphragm, pushrod, vacuum chamber, atmospheric chamber, check valve. Include a small inset showing electric vacuum pump (for diesel/turbo engines).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before your foot force even reaches the master cylinder, the brake booster multiplies it. Most cars use a vacuum booster. Here's how it works.
>
> The booster is a large canister between the pedal and the master cylinder. Inside is a flexible diaphragm. On one side — the front — engine vacuum pulls the air out, creating low pressure. On the other side — the rear — atmospheric pressure pushes in when you press the pedal. That pressure difference across the diaphragm creates additional force that assists your pedal push.
>
> Naturally aspirated petrol engines generate vacuum from the intake manifold — free assistance. But turbocharged and diesel engines don't always produce enough vacuum at the manifold. So they use an electric vacuum pump — a small pump on the engine or firewall that maintains vacuum in the booster reservoir.
>
> How do you know the booster works? Simple test. Engine off, pump the brake pedal five times to exhaust residual vacuum. Hold the pedal firm and start the engine. If the booster works, the pedal will sink slightly as vacuum builds. If it doesn't move — the booster or its vacuum supply has failed."

**PPT Content:**
```
BRAKE BOOSTER — MULTIPLIES PEDAL FORCE

How it works:
1. Engine vacuum (or electric pump) creates low pressure on front of diaphragm
2. When pedal pressed: atmospheric pressure enters rear chamber
3. Pressure difference across diaphragm = additional force
4. Typical amplification: 3:1 to 5:1

Vacuum sources:
-- Intake manifold (naturally aspirated petrol engines)
-- Electric vacuum pump (diesel, turbocharged, hybrid)

BOOSTER FUNCTION TEST:
1. Engine OFF — pump brake pedal 5 times (exhaust vacuum)
2. Hold pedal firm
3. Start engine
4. Pedal should sink slightly as vacuum builds
   NO sink = booster or vacuum supply fault
```

#### Slide 6: Master Cylinder — Tandem Design & Dual Circuits
**Visual:** Cross-section of a tandem master cylinder showing primary piston, secondary piston, two separate pressure chambers, two outlet ports, reservoir with two compartments. Color-code the two circuits (e.g., red for front-left/rear-right, blue for front-right/rear-left).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The master cylinder converts your mechanical pedal force into hydraulic pressure. But here's the critical design feature — it's a TANDEM master cylinder with TWO independent circuits.
>
> Why two? Safety. If one hydraulic circuit fails — a line ruptures, a caliper leaks — you lose braking on two wheels. But the other circuit still works. You still have brakes on the other two wheels. Reduced, but functional. This is a legal requirement on every road vehicle.
>
> Inside the master cylinder, there are two pistons in series — primary and secondary. The primary piston is pushed directly by the booster pushrod. It pressurizes circuit one. As it moves forward, it compresses the secondary piston through the trapped fluid, which pressurizes circuit two.
>
> The reservoir on top has two separate compartments — one feeding each circuit. If one circuit leaks, the fluid level drops in that compartment only. The other compartment retains its fluid and its circuit continues to function.
>
> Most modern cars use a diagonal split — front-left and rear-right on one circuit, front-right and rear-left on the other. This way, even with one circuit failed, you still have one front brake and one diagonal rear brake — maintaining some steering stability under braking."

**PPT Content:**
```
TANDEM MASTER CYLINDER — DUAL CIRCUIT SAFETY

Two independent hydraulic circuits:
-- If one circuit fails, the other still provides braking
-- Legal requirement on all road vehicles

Internal construction:
-- Primary piston: driven by booster pushrod → pressurises Circuit 1
-- Secondary piston: driven by trapped fluid → pressurises Circuit 2
-- Two separate reservoir compartments (one per circuit)

Circuit split patterns:
-- DIAGONAL (most common): FL+RR = Circuit 1, FR+RL = Circuit 2
   Maintains steering stability even with one circuit failed
-- FRONT/REAR: Both fronts = Circuit 1, Both rears = Circuit 2
   Less common — front circuit failure loses ~70% braking

Residual pressure valve:
-- Maintains slight pressure in drum brake circuit
-- Keeps drum brake shoes in light contact with drum
-- Prevents air ingestion through wheel cylinder seals
```

#### Slide 7: Brake Lines, Hoses & Proportioning
**Visual:** Diagram showing the complete hydraulic path — rigid steel lines along the chassis, flexible rubber hoses at each wheel (to allow suspension movement), and the proportioning valve between master cylinder and rear brakes. Show flare fitting types (ISO/bubble flare, double flare, DIN flare).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "From the master cylinder, hydraulic pressure travels through brake lines to each wheel. The main lines running along the chassis are rigid steel — they don't flex, they resist expansion under pressure, and they're coated or plated to resist corrosion. At each wheel, the rigid line connects to a short flexible rubber hose. This hose must flex because the wheel moves with the suspension. These hoses are the weak point — they deteriorate with age, can swell under pressure if degraded, and must be inspected for cracking, bulging, and chafing.
>
> The connections use flare fittings — metal tube ends shaped into a specific profile that seals against a matching seat. There are different flare standards — ISO bubble flare, SAE double flare, DIN flare. Using the wrong type is dangerous — it may appear to seal but fail under pressure. Always match the flare type to the vehicle specification.
>
> Between the master cylinder and the rear brakes, you'll find a proportioning valve — sometimes called a pressure regulator or load-sensing valve. Its job: reduce hydraulic pressure to the rear brakes compared to the fronts. Why? Under braking, weight transfers forward. The rear tyres lose grip. If rear brakes have the same pressure as fronts, the rears lock up first, causing instability or spin. The proportioning valve prevents this by limiting rear brake pressure — typically to 40-60% of front pressure depending on deceleration."

**PPT Content:**
```
BRAKE LINES, HOSES & PROPORTIONING

Rigid steel lines:
-- Run along chassis/underbody
-- Coated steel or copper-nickel (Cunifer) for corrosion resistance
-- Do NOT flex — fixed mounting with clips

Flexible hoses (at each wheel):
-- Allow suspension travel without line stress
-- Inspect for: cracks, bulging, chafing, leaks, age deterioration
-- Typical replacement interval: every 6-8 years or at inspection failure

Flare fittings:
-- ISO/Bubble flare (most European vehicles)
-- SAE Double flare (older American/Japanese)
-- DIN flare
-- NEVER mix flare types — leak under pressure = brake failure

Proportioning valve (rear pressure regulation):
-- Reduces rear brake pressure to prevent rear wheel lock-up
-- Weight transfers forward under braking → rear tyres lose grip
-- Limits rear pressure to ~40-60% of front pressure
-- Some vehicles: load-sensing valve adjusts based on rear axle load
```

#### Slide 9: Disc Brake Overview — Floating vs Fixed Caliper
**Visual:** Side-by-side cross-section comparison — floating caliper (single piston, carrier with slide pins) and fixed caliper (multi-piston, bolted rigidly to knuckle). Label every component.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Disc brakes are the dominant brake type on modern vehicles — all four wheels on most cars, always on the fronts. The concept is simple: a metal disc — the rotor — spins with the wheel. A caliper straddles the rotor and squeezes brake pads against both sides when hydraulic pressure is applied.
>
> There are two caliper designs. The FLOATING caliper — by far the most common — has a single piston on the inboard side. When pressure is applied, the piston pushes the inboard pad against the rotor. The reaction force pulls the caliper body inward on its slide pins, pressing the outboard pad against the rotor's other face. One piston, both sides clamped. Simple, light, cheap, effective.
>
> The FIXED caliper has pistons on both sides — two, four, or even six pistons. It's bolted rigidly to the steering knuckle and doesn't slide. More expensive, heavier, but provides more even clamping force and better feel. You'll find these on performance cars, heavy SUVs, and premium vehicles."

**PPT Content:**
```
DISC BRAKES — TWO CALIPER TYPES

FLOATING CALIPER (most common):
-- Single piston (inboard side)
-- Caliper body slides on guide pins
-- Piston pushes inboard pad → reaction pulls caliper → outboard pad clamps
-- Components: carrier (bolted to knuckle), caliper body (slides),
   piston, piston seal, dust boot, slide pins, pin boots

FIXED CALIPER (performance):
-- Pistons on BOTH sides (2, 4, or 6 pistons)
-- Caliper bolted rigidly to knuckle — does not slide
-- Direct clamping on both sides — more even force distribution
-- Heavier, more expensive, better pedal feel
-- Found on: sports cars, premium vehicles, heavy SUVs

BOTH types: Pads squeeze a spinning rotor = friction = heat = deceleration
```

#### Slide 10: Floating Caliper Deep Dive — Slides, Seals & Service Points
**Visual:** Exploded diagram of a floating caliper assembly — carrier bracket, caliper body, slide pins, pin boots, piston, piston seal (square-cut), dust boot, anti-rattle clips, pad retaining pins, brake pads

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's look deeper at the floating caliper because this is what you'll service most often. The carrier bracket bolts to the steering knuckle — it doesn't move. Two slide pins allow the caliper body to float back and forth on the carrier. These slide pins MUST be clean and lubricated — if they seize, the caliper can't float, the outboard pad can't contact the rotor, and you get uneven wear, pulling, and reduced braking.
>
> The piston seal is a square-cut O-ring. It does two jobs. First, it seals hydraulic pressure. Second — and this is clever — when pressure is applied, the seal deforms slightly. When pressure is released, the seal's elasticity pulls the piston back a tiny amount — just enough to release the pad from the rotor. This is the automatic pad adjustment mechanism. As pads wear, the piston slides further through the seal, and the seal simply resets to the new position. No manual adjustment needed.
>
> The dust boot keeps dirt and water away from the piston and cylinder bore. A torn boot means contamination enters, the piston corrodes, it seizes in the bore, and the brake drags or fails to apply. Always inspect the boot."

**PPT Content:**
```
FLOATING CALIPER — SERVICE POINTS

Slide pins:
-- MUST be clean, lubricated (silicone grease, NOT petroleum-based)
-- Seized pin = uneven pad wear, brake pull, dragging
-- Check pin boots for tears — torn boot = contamination = seized pin

Piston seal (square-cut):
-- Seals hydraulic pressure
-- Provides automatic retraction (seal deforms under pressure,
   elasticity pulls piston back ~0.1 mm on release)
-- Provides automatic wear adjustment (piston slides through seal
   as pads wear — seal resets to new position)

Dust boot:
-- Protects piston and bore from dirt, water, road salt
-- Torn boot = corrosion = seized piston = brake drag or failure
-- Always inspect during pad replacement

Anti-rattle clips & hardware:
-- Prevent pad vibration and noise
-- Replace with every pad change — hardware kits are cheap, noise is not
```

#### Slide 11: Brake Pads — Friction Material, Backing Plate & Wear Indicators
**Visual:** Photo/diagram showing a brake pad from multiple angles — friction material surface, backing plate, chamfers, slots, shim, wear indicator tab (audible), and electronic wear sensor wire

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The brake pad is the consumable — it wears down over time and must be replaced. A pad has two parts: the backing plate — a stamped steel plate that provides structure — and the friction material bonded to it.
>
> Friction material comes in several types. Semi-metallic — contains steel fibers, good heat dissipation, slightly abrasive on rotors. Ceramic — contains ceramic fibers and copper, quieter, less dust, gentler on rotors, but more expensive. Organic — contains rubber, glass, resin — softest, quietest, but wears fastest and fades at high temperature.
>
> How do you know when pads are worn? Two systems. First, the audible wear indicator — a small metal tab on the pad backing plate. When the friction material wears down to about 2 mm, this tab contacts the rotor and produces a high-pitched squeal. The customer hears it and comes in for service. Second, the electronic wear sensor — a wire embedded in the pad. When the pad wears to the limit, the wire contacts the rotor, the circuit changes, and a dashboard warning light illuminates. Not all cars have electronic sensors — check the vehicle specification.
>
> The minimum pad thickness for most vehicles is 2 mm of friction material remaining. But always check the manufacturer's specification — some specify 3 mm. You measure this with a vernier caliper. You'll do exactly that in the practical."

**PPT Content:**
```
BRAKE PADS — THE CONSUMABLE

Construction:
-- Backing plate (stamped steel) + friction material (bonded/riveted)
-- Chamfers and slots: reduce noise and improve initial bite
-- Shim (anti-squeal): thin steel or rubber layer on backing plate back

Friction material types:
-- Semi-metallic: steel fibers, good heat capacity, more rotor wear
-- Ceramic: ceramic + copper fibers, quiet, low dust, expensive
-- Organic: resin-based, soft, quiet, fades at high temp, wears fast

Wear indicators:
-- AUDIBLE: Metal tab on pad — contacts rotor at ~2 mm remaining
   → produces high-pitched squeal → customer hears it
-- ELECTRONIC: Wire sensor in pad — contacts rotor at wear limit
   → changes circuit resistance → dashboard warning light

Service limits (typical):
-- Minimum friction material thickness: 2 mm (always check spec)
-- Measurement tool: VERNIER CALIPER (from Day 4!)
-- Replace pads in AXLE SETS (both sides, same axle, always)
```

#### Slide 12: Brake Rotors — Types, Specifications & Failure Modes
**Visual:** Four rotor types side by side — solid, vented, drilled, drilled-and-slotted. Include a cross-section of a vented rotor showing internal cooling vanes. Add a photo of a rotor with visible scoring and one with a lip at the outer edge.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The rotor — also called the brake disc — is what the pads clamp against. It's cast iron on most vehicles, sometimes with a carbon-ceramic composite on high-performance cars. The rotor must absorb enormous amounts of heat and dissipate it efficiently.
>
> Four types. SOLID — a single flat disc. Used on lighter applications, rear brakes. VENTED — the most common front rotor. Two disc faces with internal cooling vanes that act like a centrifugal fan, pulling air through the rotor as it spins. Much better heat dissipation. DRILLED — holes through the rotor face, originally for gas dissipation in racing but now mostly cosmetic on road cars. And SLOTTED — shallow grooves in the rotor face that help clear pad debris and gas.
>
> Every rotor has a minimum thickness specification stamped or cast into the hat section — the centre part that bolts to the hub. When the rotor wears below this minimum, it's too thin to absorb heat safely, and it's more likely to crack or warp. You measure this with an outside micrometer — at least six points around the rotor — and compare to the minimum specification. You'll do this in the practical.
>
> Rotor runout — the wobble of the rotor face as it spins — is measured with a dial indicator. Excessive runout causes brake pedal pulsation that the customer feels. Maximum runout is typically 0.05 to 0.10 mm. Again, you'll measure this today."

**PPT Content:**
```
BRAKE ROTORS — TYPES & SPECIFICATIONS

Types:
-- SOLID: Single disc, rear brakes, lighter vehicles
-- VENTED: Two faces + internal cooling vanes — most common front rotor
-- DRILLED: Holes through face — aesthetics, some gas venting
-- SLOTTED: Grooves on face — clears pad debris and gas

Material: Cast iron (standard), carbon-ceramic (high-performance)

CRITICAL SPECIFICATIONS:
-- Minimum thickness: Cast/stamped on rotor hat (e.g., "MIN TH 22.0")
   Below minimum = CONDEMNED — cannot be used
-- Thickness variation (DTV): Max ~0.015 mm around circumference
   Excessive DTV = pedal pulsation
-- Runout: Max ~0.05-0.10 mm (manufacturer spec)
   Excessive runout = pedal pulsation, uneven pad wear

MEASUREMENT TOOLS:
-- Rotor thickness: OUTSIDE MICROMETER — 6+ points around rotor
-- Rotor runout: DIAL INDICATOR — mounted on fixed bracket
-- Both skills from DAY 4!

Failure modes: Scoring, cracking, heat spots (blue discoloration), lip formation
```

#### Slide 13: Rotor Inspection Decision Matrix
**Visual:** Table/flowchart showing: measure thickness at 6 points → compare to minimum spec → check DTV (difference between thickest and thinnest reading) → if below minimum, condemn; if above minimum but DTV excessive, machine or replace; if runout excessive, check hub face, machine or replace.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Here's the decision process you'll follow every time you inspect a rotor. Step one: measure thickness at six points around the rotor with a micrometer. Record all six readings. Step two: compare the thinnest reading to the minimum thickness specification. If any reading is at or below minimum — CONDEMN the rotor. No machining, no exception. Replace it.
>
> Step three: calculate the DTV — Disc Thickness Variation — the difference between your thickest and thinnest readings. If DTV exceeds about 0.015 mm, the customer will feel pedal pulsation. The rotor needs machining or replacement.
>
> Step four: measure runout with a dial indicator while rotating the rotor 360 degrees. If runout exceeds spec, first check the hub mounting face for dirt or corrosion — that's the most common cause. Clean the face, remount, re-measure. If runout is still excessive, machine or replace.
>
> Notice the hierarchy: thickness is the safety gate — below minimum means condemned, full stop. DTV and runout are quality gates — they affect performance and customer satisfaction."

**PPT Content:**
```
ROTOR INSPECTION DECISION MATRIX

STEP 1: Measure thickness at 6 points (micrometer)
         Record all readings

STEP 2: Compare THINNEST reading to minimum spec
         Below minimum → CONDEMN — replace rotor (safety gate)

STEP 3: Calculate DTV (thickest - thinnest reading)
         DTV > 0.015 mm → Machine or replace (quality gate)

STEP 4: Measure runout (dial indicator, full 360° rotation)
         Runout > spec → Clean hub face, remount, re-measure
         Still excessive → Machine or replace

DECISION HIERARCHY:
1. SAFETY: Thickness ≥ minimum? (YES = continue, NO = replace)
2. QUALITY: DTV within spec? (YES = continue, NO = machine/replace)
3. QUALITY: Runout within spec? (YES = pass, NO = investigate cause)

ALWAYS replace rotors in PAIRS (same axle)
```

#### Slide 14: Electronic Parking Brake (EPB)
**Visual:** Cutaway/diagram of an EPB caliper showing the electric motor, reduction gearbox, thrust screw/spindle mechanism pushing the piston, and the EPB ECU. Include a screenshot of a diagnostic tool showing "EPB Service Mode."

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The Electronic Parking Brake has replaced the traditional handbrake cable on most modern vehicles. Instead of a mechanical cable pulling on the rear brake caliper or drum, an electric motor built into the rear caliper pushes the piston against the pad.
>
> When the driver presses the EPB button, the EPB ECU commands the motors — one per rear wheel. The motor drives a reduction gearbox and a thrust screw that mechanically pushes the caliper piston forward. The piston clamps the pad against the rotor with enough force to hold the vehicle on a gradient.
>
> Here's the critical service consideration: you cannot simply push the EPB caliper piston back by hand to replace the pads. The thrust screw mechanism prevents it. You MUST put the EPB into SERVICE MODE using a diagnostic scan tool. Service mode commands the motors to retract the pistons, releasing the clamping force. Only then can you remove the caliper and replace the pads.
>
> After installing new pads, you exit service mode, and the EPB ECU re-initializes — it runs the motor until the pad contacts the rotor, then calibrates the clamping force. Skip this step and the parking brake will not hold. This is documented in every manufacturer's repair procedure. Follow it."

**PPT Content:**
```
ELECTRONIC PARKING BRAKE (EPB)

How it works:
-- Electric motor in each rear caliper
-- Motor → reduction gearbox → thrust screw → pushes piston
-- EPB ECU controls apply/release based on button input
-- Auto-hold function: applies automatically when vehicle stops

SERVICE MODE — CRITICAL FOR PAD REPLACEMENT:
1. Connect diagnostic scan tool
2. Enter EPB Service Mode (retracts pistons via motor)
3. WAIT for confirmation — pistons fully retracted
4. Remove caliper, replace pads
5. Reinstall caliper
6. Exit Service Mode — ECU re-initializes (motor advances
   piston to pad contact, calibrates clamping force)

WARNING: DO NOT attempt to push piston back manually
— thrust screw mechanism prevents it
— forcing it damages the motor/gearbox

ALWAYS follow manufacturer-specific EPB procedure

### **DEVELOPMENT PART 3: Drum Brakes** (Slides 15-17, ~10 minutes)

**Narrative Voice:** Acknowledge that drum brakes are older technology but still widely used on rear axles of economy cars. Practical knowledge is essential.

---

#### Slide 15: Drum Brake Construction & Operation
**Visual:** Exploded diagram of a drum brake assembly — drum, leading shoe, trailing shoe, wheel cylinder, return springs (upper and lower), hold-down springs, self-adjuster mechanism (star wheel type), backing plate. Label every component.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Drum brakes are older than disc brakes but still found on the rear axle of many economy and mid-range vehicles. They're cheaper to manufacture and provide a built-in parking brake mechanism.
>
> Here's how they work. A cast iron drum rotates with the wheel. Inside the drum, two curved brake shoes lined with friction material sit on a fixed backing plate. When you press the brake pedal, the wheel cylinder — a small hydraulic cylinder at the top — pushes the shoes outward against the inside surface of the drum. Friction between shoes and drum creates braking force.
>
> The leading shoe — the one that rotates into the drum's direction of travel — is self-energizing. The drum's rotation actually pulls the shoe tighter against the surface, amplifying the braking force. The trailing shoe works against the rotation and develops less force. This is why drum brakes can feel different to disc brakes — they have a non-linear force characteristic.
>
> When hydraulic pressure releases, the return springs — strong coiled springs at the top and bottom — pull the shoes back away from the drum. The hold-down springs keep the shoes located on the backing plate so they don't rattle."

**PPT Content:**
```
DRUM BRAKES — CONSTRUCTION & OPERATION

Components (all on fixed backing plate):
-- DRUM: Cast iron, rotates with wheel, braking surface is INSIDE
-- LEADING SHOE: Self-energizing (drum rotation amplifies braking)
-- TRAILING SHOE: Works against rotation (less braking force)
-- WHEEL CYLINDER: Hydraulic piston(s) push shoes outward
-- RETURN SPRINGS: Pull shoes back when pedal released
-- HOLD-DOWN SPRINGS: Locate shoes on backing plate
-- BACKING PLATE: Fixed to axle housing, supports all components

Operation:
1. Pedal pressed → hydraulic pressure to wheel cylinder
2. Wheel cylinder pistons push shoes outward
3. Shoes contact inner drum surface → friction → braking
4. Leading shoe self-energizes (drum rotation pulls it tighter)
5. Pedal released → return springs retract shoes

Advantages: Cheap, good parking brake, self-energizing
Disadvantages: Heat dissipation poor, fade at high temp, more maintenance
```

#### Slide 16: Drum Brake Adjuster & Parking Brake Integration
**Visual:** Close-up diagram of a star wheel adjuster mechanism and automatic adjuster arm. Separate inset showing parking brake cable attachment to drum brake lever.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Unlike disc brakes where the piston seal provides automatic adjustment, drum brakes need a separate adjuster mechanism to compensate for shoe wear. The most common type is the star wheel adjuster — a threaded rod between the bottoms of the two shoes. As the shoes wear, the adjuster extends, pushing the shoes closer to the drum and maintaining the correct drum-to-shoe clearance.
>
> Most modern drum brakes have an automatic adjuster. Each time you apply the brakes in reverse — or on some designs, each time the parking brake is applied — a lever engages with the star wheel and turns it one notch. Over time, this keeps the shoes in proper adjustment.
>
> The parking brake on drum brake vehicles is elegant in its simplicity. A cable from the parking brake lever or EPB motor connects to a lever on the rear brake assembly. When activated, this lever physically spreads the shoes against the drum — no hydraulic pressure needed. This is why drum brakes are popular on the rear axle of budget vehicles — the parking brake mechanism is built in."

**PPT Content:**
```
DRUM BRAKE ADJUSTMENT & PARKING BRAKE

Star wheel adjuster:
-- Threaded rod between shoe bases
-- Extends to compensate for shoe wear
-- Maintains correct drum-to-shoe clearance (~0.2-0.5 mm)

Automatic adjustment:
-- Lever engages star wheel during reverse braking

-- Turns one notch per activation as needed
-- Keeps adjustment current without manual intervention

Manual adjustment:
-- Access through slot in backing plate or drum
-- Turn star wheel until shoes drag, then back off 6-8 clicks

PARKING BRAKE INTEGRATION:
-- Cable from handbrake lever → parking brake lever on shoes

#### Slide 17: Drum Brake Inspection Points
**Visual:** Annotated photo of an open drum brake assembly showing inspection points — shoe lining thickness, drum inner diameter measurement location, wheel cylinder for leaks, spring condition, adjuster function, backing plate contact points.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "When you remove a brake drum — and you'll do this in the practical — here's what you inspect. First, shoe lining thickness. Minimum is typically 1.5 mm above the rivet heads (if riveted) or 2 mm total thickness (if bonded). Measure with a vernier caliper or depth gauge.
>
> Second, drum inner diameter. The drum has a maximum diameter specification — typically stamped on the outer face. Measure with an inside micrometer or drum gauge at several points. If the diameter exceeds the maximum, the drum is condemned. Also check for out-of-round — measure at multiple angles. An out-of-round drum causes a pulsating brake pedal.
>
> Third, the wheel cylinder. Look for any sign of brake fluid leakage — pull back the dust boots and check for wetness. A leaking wheel cylinder means contaminated shoes and a failed brake circuit. Fourth, springs — check for stretched, broken, or corroded springs. A broken return spring means the shoes won't retract, causing drag and overheating. Fifth, the adjuster — verify it moves freely and the automatic mechanism works. Sixth, the backing plate contact points — the raised pads where the shoes rest. If they're grooved, the shoes can't slide freely."

**PPT Content:**

1. SHOE LINING THICKNESS:
   -- Minimum: 1.5 mm above rivets (riveted) or 2 mm total (bonded)
   -- Measure with vernier caliper

2. DRUM INNER DIAMETER:

   -- Measure with drum gauge or inside micrometer
   -- Multiple points — check for out-of-round
   -- Beyond maximum = CONDEMNED

3. WHEEL CYLINDER:
   -- Pull back dust boots — check for fluid leakage
   -- Any wetness = rebuild or replace
   -- Leaking cylinder contaminates shoe lining (also replace shoes)

4. SPRINGS (return + hold-down):
   -- Check for stretch, cracks, corrosion
   -- Broken spring = shoes won't retract = drag & overheating

5. ADJUSTER:
   -- Must move freely, automatic mechanism functioning

6. BACKING PLATE CONTACT PADS:
   -- Check for grooves — lubricate with high-temp brake grease

### **DEVELOPMENT PART 4: Brake Fluid** (Slides 18-19, ~10 minutes)

**Narrative Voice:** Connect back to Day 3 fluids session. Brake fluid is the working medium of the entire system — it must be in perfect condition.

---

#### Slide 18: Brake Fluid — DOT Ratings Revisited & Moisture Contamination
**Visual:** Table showing DOT 3, DOT 4, DOT 5, DOT 5.1 — dry boiling point, wet boiling point, composition. Include a diagram showing how moisture enters through the rubber hoses (hygroscopic absorption) and a graph showing boiling point drop versus moisture content.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "You covered brake fluid on Day 3. Let's apply that knowledge to real-world braking. Brake fluid is hygroscopic — it absorbs moisture from the atmosphere, primarily through the flexible rubber hoses and reservoir cap. Over time, moisture content increases. The problem: water boils at 100 degrees Celsius. A rotor can heat brake fluid to well over 200 degrees. If moisture-contaminated fluid boils in the caliper, it creates vapor bubbles. Vapor is compressible — liquid is not. The pedal goes to the floor. You have no brakes. This is called vapor lock.
>
> This is why DOT ratings specify TWO boiling points. The DRY boiling point — fresh fluid, no moisture — and the WET boiling point — fluid with 3.7% moisture content by volume. DOT 3 wet boiling point is only 140 degrees Celsius. DOT 4 is 155. DOT 5.1 is 180. That's why most modern vehicles specify DOT 4 or higher.
>
> You can measure moisture content with an electronic brake fluid tester — it measures the fluid's boiling point or moisture percentage. Anything above 3% moisture or a boiling point below 180 degrees Celsius for DOT 4 fluid — recommend replacement. Most manufacturers specify brake fluid replacement every 2 years regardless of condition."

**PPT Content:**
```
BRAKE FLUID — DOT RATINGS & MOISTURE

        DRY Boiling Point    WET Boiling Point    Base
DOT 3:      205°C                 140°C          Glycol
DOT 4:      230°C                 155°C          Glycol
DOT 5:      260°C                 180°C          SILICONE (not compatible!)
DOT 5.1:    270°C                 180°C          Glycol

HYGROSCOPIC: Absorbs moisture from atmosphere
-- Enters through flexible hoses and reservoir seal
-- 1% moisture per year typical in normal use
-- 3% moisture drops DOT 4 boiling point to ~185°C

DANGER: Moisture → lower boiling point → vapor lock under heavy braking
-- Vapor is compressible → pedal goes to floor → NO BRAKES

Testing:
-- Electronic brake fluid tester (measures boiling point or moisture %)
-- > 3% moisture or boiling point below spec → REPLACE
-- Standard interval: EVERY 2 YEARS regardless of test result

WARNING: DOT 5 (silicone) NOT compatible with DOT 3/4/5.1 (glycol)
Never mix. Confirm vehicle specification before adding fluid.
```

#### Slide 19: Brake Bleeding — Why, When & How
**Visual:** Diagram showing the correct bleeding sequence (furthest wheel from master cylinder first — typically RR, LR, RF, LF). Show both manual bleeding (two-person method with bleed bottle) and pressure bleeding setup. Include a photo of a brake bleeder bottle with clear hose.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Bleeding removes air from the hydraulic system. Air is compressible — even a small bubble in the line makes the pedal feel spongy and reduces braking effectiveness. You bleed brakes whenever you open the hydraulic system — pad replacement on some vehicles, caliper replacement, line replacement, master cylinder replacement, or any time the fluid level dropped low enough to admit air.
>
> The standard procedure — manual bleeding — requires two people. Person one sits in the driver's seat and pumps the brake pedal. Person two is at the wheel with a bleed bottle and clear hose connected to the bleed nipple. The sequence matters — always start at the wheel FURTHEST from the master cylinder and work toward the closest. Typically: right rear, left rear, right front, left front.
>
> Step one: the assistant slowly presses the brake pedal to the floor and HOLDS it. Step two: you open the bleed nipple a quarter turn. Fluid — and any air — flows into the bottle. Step three: close the nipple BEFORE the assistant releases the pedal. Step four: the assistant releases the pedal slowly. Repeat until the fluid runs clear with no bubbles.
>
> Pressure bleeding uses a pressurized reservoir connected to the master cylinder cap. It's faster and can be done by one person — fluid is pushed through the system by constant pressure. We'll demonstrate both methods today."

**PPT Content:**
```
BRAKE BLEEDING — REMOVING AIR FROM THE SYSTEM

When to bleed:
-- After any hydraulic component replacement (caliper, line, hose, master cyl)
-- After fluid change
-- Spongy pedal symptom
-- Any time fluid level dropped below MIN (air may have entered)

Bleeding sequence (furthest from master cylinder first):
1. Right rear
2. Left rear
3. Right front
4. Left front

MANUAL BLEEDING (two-person):
1. Assistant presses pedal SLOWLY to floor and HOLDS
2. Technician opens bleed nipple ~1/4 turn
3. Fluid + air flows into bleed bottle (watch for bubbles)
4. Technician CLOSES nipple BEFORE assistant releases pedal
5. Assistant releases pedal SLOWLY
6. Repeat until fluid runs clear, no bubbles
7. Move to next wheel in sequence

PRESSURE BLEEDING (one-person):
-- Pressurised reservoir on master cylinder (~2 bar)
-- Open each bleed nipple in sequence
-- Fluid flows continuously until clear

CRITICAL: Keep reservoir topped up during bleeding — NEVER let it run dry
```

#### Slide 21: Station 1 — Disc Brake Inspection Procedure
**Visual:** Step-by-step photo sequence — (1) Wheel removed, caliper visible, (2) Vernier caliper measuring pad thickness through caliper window, (3) Micrometer measuring rotor at one of six marked positions, (4) Dial indicator setup on steering knuckle reading rotor face, (5) Example completed measurement form.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Station 1. Step one: the wheel is already removed. Visually inspect the caliper for fluid leaks, the pads for uneven wear, the rotor for scoring, cracking, or heat spots. Step two: measure pad thickness. Place the vernier caliper jaws across the friction material — NOT including the backing plate. Read to 0.02 mm. Record the measurement. Compare to the minimum specification.
>
> Step three: rotor thickness. Position the micrometer about 10 mm inboard from the rotor's outer edge — where the pad actually contacts. Measure at six equally spaced points around the rotor. Record all six readings. The thinnest reading is your critical measurement — compare to the minimum thickness stamped on the rotor. Also calculate DTV — the difference between thickest and thinnest.
>
> Step four: rotor runout. Secure the dial indicator to a solid bracket — the caliper carrier bolt or a magnetic stand on the steering knuckle. Position the plunger perpendicular to the rotor face, about 10 mm from the outer edge. Zero the indicator. Slowly rotate the rotor one full revolution by hand. The Total Indicator Reading — the difference between the highest and lowest points — is your runout measurement. Compare to spec."

**PPT Content:**
```
STATION 1 — DISC BRAKE INSPECTION (Step-by-Step)

VISUAL INSPECTION:
-- Caliper: leaks, slide pin boot condition, dust boot
-- Pads: uneven wear (indicates slide pin or caliper issue)
-- Rotor: scoring, cracks, heat spots (blue discoloration), lip

PAD THICKNESS (Vernier caliper):
-- Measure friction material ONLY (exclude backing plate)
-- Both inboard and outboard pads
-- Minimum spec: _____ mm (check posted specification)
-- Uneven wear between inboard/outboard = caliper slide issue

-- Measure 10 mm inboard from outer edge
-- SIX points equally spaced around rotor
-- Record all readings — thinnest = critical measurement
-- Minimum thickness: stamped on rotor hat "MIN TH ___"
-- Calculate DTV: thickest - thinnest (max ~0.015 mm)

-- Mount indicator on solid bracket (knuckle or carrier)
-- Plunger perpendicular to rotor face, 10 mm from edge
-- Zero indicator, rotate rotor 360° by hand
-- TIR (Total Indicator Reading) = runout
-- Maximum spec: _____ mm (typically 0.05-0.10)
```

## Key Takeaways

1. Pascal's Law enables a 100:1 force multiplication — a small foot force creates enormous caliper clamping force through hydraulic pressure
2. The tandem master cylinder provides two independent circuits — if one fails, the other still provides braking (safety redundancy)
3. Disc brake floating calipers must have free-sliding pins and intact boots — seized pins cause uneven wear and reduced braking
4. Every brake measurement is a safety decision — 0.2 mm on a rotor or 1 mm on a pad can mean the difference between pass and fail
5. Brake fluid is hygroscopic — moisture absorption lowers the boiling point and can cause vapor lock under heavy braking
6. EPB-equipped vehicles require diagnostic scan tool service mode before pad replacement — never force the piston back manually
7. The precision measurement skills from Day 4 (vernier caliper, micrometer, dial indicator) are directly applied to safety-critical brake inspection — Week 1 meets Week 5
