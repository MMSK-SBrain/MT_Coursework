# Day 47: Gearbox Inspection & Re-Assembly
## Instructor-Led Story Framework for PPT Preparation

**Duration:** 180 minutes (27 min theory + 153 min practical)

**PPT Target:** 23 slides

---

## Session Objectives (For Instructor)

By the end of this session, learners will be able to:
- Inspect synchromesh components for wear (cone face scoring/glazing, blocking ring clearance)
- Measure blocking ring clearance using feeler gauges against the gear cone (minimum 0.8 mm)
- Select and install shims for correct gear mesh backlash using a dial gauge
- Set bearing preload correctly (crush type via torque, shim type via measurement and selection)
- Measure end-float/end-play with a dial gauge and correct with shims if required
- Install input and output shaft seals using the correct driver without damaging the seal lip
- Apply RTV sealant correctly on gearbox case halves (continuous bead, alignment dowels)
- Reassemble the gearbox following the correct torque sequences
- Adjust and verify the shift mechanism (shift rod travel, detent engagement, interlock function)

### **DEVELOPMENT: Inspection & Assembly Techniques** (Slides 4-12, ~17 minutes)

#### Slide 4: Synchro Ring Inspection — Cone Face
**Visual:** Close-up of synchro cone face — one good (fine machining lines visible) vs one worn (glazed/scored surface)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The synchro ring's cone face is the friction surface that does the actual speed-matching work when you select a gear. A healthy cone has fine machining lines — tiny grooves that hold gear oil and create friction. A worn cone is glazed — smooth, shiny, sometimes with scoring marks. Glazing means the friction surface can't grip, so the synchro can't match speeds. The result? Gear crunch. If the cone face is glazed or scored, the synchro ring must be replaced. There's no repair — it's a wear item."

**PPT Content:**
```
SYNCHRO RING INSPECTION — CONE FACE

GOOD CONDITION:
• Fine machining lines visible across entire cone surface
• No shiny or smooth patches
• No scoring, gouging, or heat discolouration

WORN / FAILED:
• Glazing — smooth, shiny surface (friction lost)
• Scoring — deep grooves from metal-to-metal contact
• Heat discolouration — blue/purple tinge (overheated)
• Material loss — cone face worn thin

DECISION:
  Glazed or scored → REPLACE (no repair possible)
  Machining lines visible → Proceed to clearance measurement

"If it's shiny where it should be rough — it's done."
```

---

#### Slide 5: Blocking Ring Clearance Measurement
**Visual:** Diagram and photo showing blocking ring placed over gear cone, feeler gauge inserted into gap

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Even if the cone face looks acceptable, you must measure the blocking ring clearance. This is the gap between the blocking ring and the gear cone when the ring is pushed onto the cone. Slide the blocking ring over the matching gear cone — push it down firmly by hand. Now insert a feeler gauge into the gap between the ring face and the gear shoulder. The typical minimum is 0.8 mm. Below that, the synchro can't generate enough friction to block gear engagement until speeds match — and you get crunch. Every gear gets this check."

**PPT Content:**
```
BLOCKING RING CLEARANCE MEASUREMENT

METHOD:
1. Place blocking ring over its matching gear cone
2. Push ring firmly onto cone by hand
3. Insert feeler gauge between ring face and gear shoulder
4. Measure the gap — rotate and check at 3 points (120 apart)

SPECIFICATION:
• Typical MINIMUM clearance: 0.8 mm
• New ring clearance typically: 1.5 - 2.0 mm
• Below 0.8 mm → REPLACE the blocking ring

WHY IT MATTERS:
• Clearance = friction travel available for speed matching
• Too little clearance = ring bottoms out before speeds match
• Result = gear crunch on every shift

CHECK EVERY GEAR — 1st through 5th (and reverse if synchro-equipped)
```

#### Slide 6: Shimming for Correct Gear Mesh — Backlash
**Visual:** Dial gauge mounted on gearbox housing measuring gear backlash, with shim pack visible

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Gear backlash is the small amount of play between meshing gear teeth. Too little backlash and gears bind, overheat, and fail. Too much and you get gear whine, impact loading, and premature wear. We measure backlash with a dial gauge: hold one gear stationary, rock the meshing gear back and forth, and read the total movement on the dial. If backlash is outside spec, we correct it by changing shims — thicker shims push gears apart, thinner shims bring them closer. The manufacturer provides a shim selection chart."

**PPT Content:**
```
GEAR MESH — BACKLASH MEASUREMENT

SETUP:
1. Mount dial gauge plunger against a gear tooth face
2. Hold the mating gear/shaft completely stationary
3. Rock the measured gear back and forth by hand
4. Read TOTAL dial gauge movement = backlash

TYPICAL SPEC: 0.05 - 0.15 mm (always check manufacturer data)

CORRECTION:
• Backlash TOO SMALL → Thicker shim (moves gears apart)
• Backlash TOO LARGE → Thinner shim (brings gears closer)
• Shim kits available in 0.05 mm increments

RULES:
• Measure at minimum 3 tooth positions (rotate 120 each time)
• Use the LARGEST reading as your measurement
• Re-measure after shim change to confirm correction
```

#### Slide 7: Bearing Preload — Crush Type
**Visual:** Cross-section diagram showing crush-type bearing with collapsible spacer, torque wrench on pinion nut

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Bearing preload eliminates internal clearance in the bearing — a small controlled load keeps the rollers in constant contact with the races. There are two methods. Crush type uses a collapsible spacer or a crush sleeve between the bearings. When you torque the pinion nut or shaft nut, the spacer crushes slightly, applying preload to the bearings. The preload is set by torque — typically you torque the nut while measuring rotational resistance with a torque wrench or spring balance. If you overtorque and crush the spacer too much, you MUST replace it — you can't undo crush."

**PPT Content:**
```
BEARING PRELOAD — CRUSH TYPE

HOW IT WORKS:
• Collapsible spacer sits between inner bearing races
• Tightening the shaft nut crushes the spacer
• Crush = axial compression = bearing preload

SETTING PROCEDURE:
1. Install bearings and NEW crush spacer (always new)
2. Torque shaft nut gradually — check preload frequently
3. Measure preload: rotational drag with torque wrench
   on shaft nut (e.g., 1-2 Nm turning torque)
4. STOP when target preload reached

CRITICAL RULES:
• If you OVERTIGHTEN → spacer over-crushed → REPLACE spacer
• You CANNOT back off and re-tighten — crush is permanent
• Always use a NEW crush spacer on reassembly
• Sneak up on the torque — small increments, check often
```

#### Slide 8: Bearing Preload — Shim Type
**Visual:** Shim pack being measured with micrometer, shim placed behind bearing race

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The shim method is more forgiving but requires measurement and selection. You assemble the shaft with bearings, measure the end-play with a dial gauge, then select a shim that eliminates the end-play and applies the specified preload. For example, if you measure 0.15 mm of end-play and the spec calls for 0.05 mm preload, you need a shim that's 0.20 mm thicker than the current one. Shim type is adjustable and repeatable — you can always reopen and change the shim."

**PPT Content:**
```
BEARING PRELOAD — SHIM TYPE

HOW IT WORKS:
• Shim(s) placed behind bearing outer race or between housing and cap
• Shim thickness controls axial position = controls preload

SETTING PROCEDURE:
1. Assemble shaft with bearings (no shim or existing shim)
2. Measure end-play with dial gauge (push-pull on shaft)
3. Calculate required shim:
   Shim = measured end-play + desired preload
4. Select nearest shim from kit
5. Install shim, reassemble, re-measure to verify

EXAMPLE:
  Measured end-play: 0.15 mm
  Desired preload: 0.05 mm
  Required shim increase: 0.15 + 0.05 = 0.20 mm

ADVANTAGE: Adjustable — can reopen and change if needed
```

#### Slide 9: End-Float / End-Play Measurement
**Visual:** Dial gauge mounted on gearbox case, plunger against shaft end, arrows showing push-pull direction

**Instructor Narration:**
> "End-float — also called end-play — is the total axial movement of a shaft within its bearings. Mount a dial gauge so the plunger contacts the shaft end. Push the shaft fully one way, zero the gauge, then push it fully the other way. The reading is your end-float. Every shaft in the gearbox needs this check — input shaft, output shaft, layshaft if separate. Too much end-float means gears wander axially under load — noise, wear, and eventual failure. Too little means bearings are over-preloaded — heat and early failure."

**PPT Content:**
```
END-FLOAT / END-PLAY MEASUREMENT

SETUP:
1. Mount dial gauge on case — plunger on shaft end face
2. Push shaft fully in ONE direction → zero the gauge
3. Push shaft fully in OPPOSITE direction → read gauge
4. Reading = total end-float (axial play)

TYPICAL SPECS:
• Input shaft: 0.05 - 0.15 mm
• Output shaft: 0.05 - 0.15 mm
• Layshaft: 0.02 - 0.10 mm
(Always check manufacturer specification)

CORRECTION:
• Too much end-float → thicker shim or adjust preload
• Too little end-float → thinner shim or reduce preload
• Re-measure after EVERY adjustment

RULE: Check end-float on ALL shafts before closing the case
```

#### Slide 10: Seal Installation
**Visual:** Seal driver being used to press input shaft seal into housing — driver contacts outer metal ring, not rubber lip

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Seals are the last line of defence against oil leaks — and the most commonly botched installation. There are two seals on most manual gearboxes: input shaft seal (behind the clutch) and output shaft seal (where the driveshaft connects). The seal MUST be driven in square using the correct size driver — the driver contacts the seal's outer metal shell, NEVER the rubber lip. A cocked seal leaks from day one. Apply a thin film of oil or assembly lube to the seal lip before installing — a dry lip burns on first rotation and fails within weeks."

**PPT Content:**
```
SEAL INSTALLATION — INPUT & OUTPUT SHAFTS

TOOLS: Correct-size seal driver (matches seal outer diameter)

INPUT SHAFT SEAL:
1. Clean housing bore — remove old seal and any debris
2. Inspect bore for scoring (seal won't seat in a damaged bore)
3. Apply thin film of sealant to seal outer diameter (if specified)
4. Oil the seal lip with gear oil or assembly lube
5. Place seal in bore — lip direction faces OIL (inward)
6. Drive in SQUARE using seal driver on outer shell
7. Drive to correct depth (flush or recessed per spec)

OUTPUT SHAFT SEAL:
• Same procedure — correct driver, lip faces oil, drive square

CRITICAL RULES:
• NEVER hammer directly on the seal — always use a driver
• NEVER contact the rubber lip with the driver
• ALWAYS lubricate the lip — dry lip = instant damage
• Check lip spring is in place (small garter spring behind lip)

"A five-dollar seal installed wrong costs a $500 comeback."
```

#### Slide 11: Sealant Application — Case Halves
**Visual:** RTV sealant bead being applied around gearbox case mating surface — continuous bead running inside bolt holes, alignment dowels visible

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Most modern gearboxes use RTV sealant on the case halves rather than a paper gasket. The rules are the same as engine sealant from Day 43. Continuous bead, about 3 mm diameter, running INSIDE the bolt holes. Too much sealant squeezes inside and can block oil passages or jam gear teeth. Too little and you get a leak that shows up a week later. Clean both surfaces with solvent until spotless. Apply the bead, then join the halves within the open time — typically 10-15 minutes. The alignment dowels locate the halves precisely — let them do their job."

**PPT Content:**
```
SEALANT APPLICATION — CASE HALVES

SURFACE PREP:
• Remove ALL old sealant (plastic scraper — don't gouge aluminium)
• Clean with solvent + lint-free cloth until spotless
• Surfaces must be DRY before applying new sealant

APPLICATION:
• 3 mm continuous RTV bead — INSIDE bolt holes
• No gaps, no breaks in the bead
• Around all oil passages and bearing bores

ASSEMBLY:
• Join halves within 10-15 min (before sealant skins)
• Alignment dowels locate the case — DO NOT force
• Install bolts hand-tight, then torque in sequence
• Torque: typically 20-25 Nm (check spec) — do NOT overtighten

CURE TIME: 24 hours before filling with oil

SEALANT TYPE: Use manufacturer-specified RTV
(anaerobic or silicone — they are NOT interchangeable)
```

#### Slide 12: Shift Mechanism — Adjustment & Verification
**Visual:** Cutaway diagram of shift mechanism showing shift rods, forks, detent balls and springs, interlock pins

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The shift mechanism is where the driver's hand movement becomes gear selection inside the box. Three things must be right. First: shift rod travel — each rod must move far enough to fully engage the gear, and return far enough to fully disengage. Second: detent engagement — spring-loaded balls that click into grooves on the shift rods. These give the driver that positive 'click' into gear and hold it there under load. Third: the interlock. This is a set of pins or a plate that physically prevents two shift rods from moving simultaneously. If the interlock fails, you can select two gears at once — which locks the gearbox instantly and catastrophically."

**PPT Content:**
```
SHIFT MECHANISM — THREE CRITICAL FUNCTIONS

1. SHIFT ROD TRAVEL
   • Full engagement: gear dogs/synchros fully meshed
   • Full disengagement: complete clearance in neutral
   • Check: move each rod by hand — full travel, no binding

2. DETENT ENGAGEMENT
   • Spring-loaded ball sits in groove on shift rod
   • Provides positive 'click' feel into each gear position
   • Check: detent holds rod in gear — doesn't pop out under load
   • Worn detent spring → gear jumps out under acceleration

3. INTERLOCK FUNCTION
   • Pins/plate between shift rods prevent simultaneous selection
   • If rod 1 is engaged, rods 2 and 3 are physically locked
   • CRITICAL SAFETY CHECK: attempt to select two gears at once
   • Must be impossible — if it moves, interlock has failed

"The interlock prevents the most destructive thing a gearbox
 can do: select two gears at the same time."
```

**PPT Content:**

□ Install shift forks on correct rods (matched marks from Day 46)
□ Install detent springs and balls in housing bores
□ Install interlock pins/plate between shift rod bores

VERIFICATION — SHIFT ROD TRAVEL:
□ Move each rod: neutral → gear → neutral
□ Full engagement confirmed (fork fully pushes sleeve)
□ Full disengagement confirmed (clean neutral position)

□ Each gear position: positive click felt
□ Rod stays in position — does not spring out
□ Neutral detent holds — rods centre correctly

VERIFICATION — INTERLOCK (CRITICAL SAFETY CHECK):
□ Select 1st gear — attempt to move 3rd/4th rod → MUST NOT MOVE
□ Select 3rd gear — attempt to move 1st/2nd rod → MUST NOT MOVE

## Assessment Checkpoint

- Synchro cone face inspection decision made correctly (pass/fail with justification)
- Blocking ring clearance measured accurately (within 0.1 mm of instructor measurement)
- Bearing preload set to specification using correct method
- Backlash measured with dial gauge and corrected if needed
- End-float measured on all shafts and within spec
- Seals installed square with lubricated lip and correct orientation
- RTV sealant applied as continuous bead inside bolt holes
- Case bolt torque sequence followed correctly
- Shift mechanism interlock tested — double selection prevented
- Quality checklist completed and signed

---

## Key Takeaways

1. Synchro cone faces must show machining lines — glazing or scoring means replacement
2. Blocking ring clearance below 0.8 mm means the ring cannot do its job — replace it
3. Bearing preload requires precision — crush type cannot be undone, shim type can be adjusted
4. Backlash and end-float are corrected with shims — measure, calculate, select, verify
5. Seal installation demands the right driver, the right direction, and lubrication on the lip
6. RTV sealant: 3 mm continuous bead, inside bolt holes, assemble within 10-15 minutes
7. The interlock is a critical safety device — always test it before closing the gearbox
8. Document everything — measurements, decisions, and torque values go in the job file
