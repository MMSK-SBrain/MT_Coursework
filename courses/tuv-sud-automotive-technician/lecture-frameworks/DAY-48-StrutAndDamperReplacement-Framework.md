# Day 48: Strut & Damper Replacement
## Instructor-Led Story Framework for PPT Preparation

**Duration:** 180 minutes (27 min theory + 153 min practical)
**Approach:** Safety-First Demonstration — Spring Compressor Respect Drilled In Before Any Tool Is Touched
**PPT Target:** 23 slides
**Week:** 10 of 11 — "Undercar: Suspension, Steering & Brakes" (Optional)

---

## Session Objectives (For Instructor)

By the end of this session, learners will be able to:
- Operate a spring compressor safely with correct jaw placement, gradual even compression, safety pins, and body positioning discipline
- Remove a MacPherson strut assembly from a vehicle following the correct disconnection sequence
- Disassemble a strut using a spring compressor (compress spring, remove top mount nut, controlled release)
- Inspect top mount bearing, bump stop, dust boot, and spring for wear and damage
- Assemble a new damper onto the existing (or new) spring with correct orientation and seat alignment
- Reinstall the strut assembly on the vehicle and torque all fasteners to specification
- Explain why wheel alignment is MANDATORY after any strut or suspension work

#### Slide 2: The Energy Inside a Coil Spring
**Visual:** Diagram showing a compressed spring with force vectors, and a comparison — "A compressed MacPherson spring stores roughly the same energy as a .45 caliber bullet"

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's put this in perspective. A typical MacPherson front spring has a rate of about 30 N/mm and is compressed roughly 150mm when installed. That's over 2,200 Newtons of force — about 225 kilograms — trying to expand. If a compressor jaw slips, if a thread strips, if you release unevenly — that spring launches like a projectile. It will break bones. It will crush a skull. It has killed technicians who got complacent. Every single time you use a spring compressor, you treat it like a loaded weapon."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE ENERGY IN A COIL SPRING

Typical MacPherson front spring:
  Spring rate: ~30 N/mm
  Compression: ~150 mm installed
  Stored force: ~2,200 N (225 kg / 500 lbs)

This is a LETHAL amount of stored energy.

Technicians HAVE DIED from spring compressor failures.
This is not theory. This is documented fact.

TREAT A COMPRESSED SPRING LIKE A LOADED WEAPON.
```

#### Slide 3: Spring Compressor Safety Rules
**Visual:** Annotated photo of a spring compressor correctly installed on a spring — jaws seated deep on coils, safety pins engaged, both sides even

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Here are the rules. They are non-negotiable. First: jaw placement. The jaws must seat FULLY on the coils — at least two full coils engaged, deep into the coil, not resting on the edge. Second: compress BOTH sides evenly. Never let one side get ahead of the other — that creates a twisting force that can eject the spring sideways. Third: safety pins or safety chains must be engaged before you proceed. Fourth — and this is the one that saves lives — NEVER put your face or body directly over a compressed spring. Always stand to the side. If something lets go, you are not in the launch path."

**PPT Content:**
```
SPRING COMPRESSOR SAFETY — NON-NEGOTIABLE RULES

1. JAW PLACEMENT
   Seat jaws FULLY on coils — minimum 2 coils engaged
   Deep onto the coil, NOT perched on the edge
   Verify jaws are locked and secure before compressing

2. COMPRESS BOTH SIDES EVENLY
   Alternate sides — 3-4 turns left, 3-4 turns right
   Never let one side get more than 1 full turn ahead
   Uneven compression = twisting force = spring ejects sideways

3. SAFETY PINS / CHAINS
   Engage ALL safety retention devices
   If your compressor has no safety pins — DO NOT USE IT

4. BODY POSITIONING
   NEVER put face or body OVER a compressed spring
   ALWAYS stand to the SIDE
   If the spring releases — you are NOT in the path

THESE RULES ARE NOT OPTIONAL. EVER.
```

#### Slide 4: Spring Compressor Types and Inspection
**Visual:** Three types of spring compressors — wall-mounted (professional), external claw type (common workshop), internal fork type. Highlight worn thread on a dangerous example.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before you use any spring compressor, inspect it. Check the threads on the drive rods — if they're worn, stripped, or damaged, the compressor gets thrown in the bin, not used one more time. Check the jaw hooks — if they're bent, cracked, or don't grip properly, it's scrap. Check the safety pins — if they're missing or don't engage, you don't use it. A wall-mounted compressor is the safest option — the spring is contained inside a cage. Claw-type compressors are common but require absolute discipline. Never use a cheap, unbranded spring compressor with no safety features. Your life is worth more than the price difference."

**PPT Content:**
```
SPRING COMPRESSOR TYPES

WALL-MOUNTED (Safest)
  Spring contained in cage during compression
  Preferred for professional workshops

EXTERNAL CLAW TYPE (Common)
  Jaws clamp outside the coils
  Requires strict safety discipline
  Must have safety pins/hooks

INTERNAL FORK TYPE
  Forks between coils from inside
  Less common, specific applications

BEFORE EVERY USE — INSPECT:
  Thread condition on drive rods — worn = SCRAP
  Jaw hooks — bent or cracked = SCRAP
  Safety pins — missing = DO NOT USE
  Overall condition — any doubt = DO NOT USE

A cheap compressor is not a bargain. It's a gamble with your life.
```

### **DEVELOPMENT: Strut R&R Procedure** (Slides 5-12, ~17 minutes)

#### Slide 5: MacPherson Strut Removal — Overview
**Visual:** Annotated diagram of a MacPherson strut installed on a vehicle — showing stabilizer link, steering knuckle connection, top mount bolts, brake line bracket

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before we touch the spring compressor, the strut has to come out of the vehicle. The MacPherson strut connects to the vehicle at three points: the steering knuckle at the bottom — usually two pinch bolts or a pinch clamp. The stabilizer bar link attaches to the strut body. And the top mount bolts come through the strut tower in the engine bay. We also need to disconnect the brake line bracket and, on some vehicles, the ABS sensor wiring bracket. The sequence matters. Get it wrong and you're fighting gravity with a heavy assembly."

**PPT Content:**
```
MacPHERSON STRUT — REMOVAL OVERVIEW

CONNECTION POINTS:
  TOP:    Top mount bolts (3x typically) through strut tower
  MIDDLE: Stabilizer link connection to strut body
  BOTTOM: Steering knuckle — pinch bolts or clamp bolts

ALSO DISCONNECT:
  Brake line bracket (clipped to strut body)
  ABS sensor wire bracket (if routed on strut)

REMOVAL SEQUENCE:
  1. Loosen (don't remove) top mount bolts from above
  2. Disconnect stabilizer link
  3. Disconnect steering knuckle
  4. Remove brake line bracket
  5. Support strut, remove top mount bolts
  6. Lower strut assembly out of wheel arch

ALWAYS: Support the knuckle/hub with wire — don't let it
hang on the brake hose or CV joint!
```

---

#### Slide 6: Step-by-Step Removal
**Visual:** Numbered photo sequence showing each disconnection point

**Instructor Narration:**
> "In detail. Vehicle on stands, wheel off. Step one: from above in the engine bay, loosen the three top mount nuts — but don't remove them yet. This lets you hold the strut later when you remove the bottom connection. Step two: disconnect the stabilizer link from the strut — usually a nut on a ball joint stud. Hold the stud with an Allen key while you undo the nut. Step three: the knuckle connection. This is usually two large bolts clamping the knuckle to the strut. These may require significant force — penetrating oil first. Once the knuckle is separated, the strut wants to drop. Step four: support the strut from below, go back to the top, remove the three top mount nuts, and lower the strut assembly out."

**PPT Content:**
```
STRUT REMOVAL — STEP BY STEP

1. FROM ABOVE: Loosen 3x top mount nuts (do NOT remove yet)
   Leave 2-3 threads engaged for safety

2. DISCONNECT STABILIZER LINK
   Hold ball stud with Allen key, undo nut with spanner
   Some links: torx or hex internal drive

3. DISCONNECT STEERING KNUCKLE
   Remove 2x pinch bolts (penetrating oil, breaker bar)
   Spread knuckle with pry bar if tight
   Pull strut DOWN out of knuckle
   (Some designs: knuckle bolts from behind — check first)

4. DISCONNECT BRAKE LINE BRACKET
   Unclip or unbolt from strut body
   DO NOT disconnect the brake hose itself

5. SUPPORT STRUT FROM BELOW
   Return to top — remove 3x top mount nuts
   Lower strut carefully from wheel arch

6. SUPPORT KNUCKLE/HUB ASSEMBLY
   Wire it to the spring or body — do NOT let it hang
   on brake hose or CV boot

WEIGHT WARNING: Strut assembly is 10-15 kg — control it.
```

#### Slide 7: Strut Disassembly with Spring Compressor
**Visual:** Sequence showing spring compressor jaws being placed, spring being compressed, top mount nut being removed

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Now the strut is on the bench, and now we use the spring compressor. Clamp the strut securely in a vice — use soft jaws to protect the body. Install the compressor jaws on the spring — remember, minimum two coils engaged on each jaw, deep seating, safety pins in. Begin compressing: alternate sides, 3-4 turns at a time, keeping compression even. You're compressing until the top mount plate is unloaded — meaning there's a visible gap between the spring and the top mount. You should be able to wiggle the top mount by hand. Once unloaded, hold the damper shaft with an Allen key or hex socket and remove the top mount nut. Then carefully lift off the top mount, bearing, bump stop, dust boot, and spring seat. The spring stays on the compressor — still compressed — until it goes back together."

**PPT Content:**
```
STRUT DISASSEMBLY — SPRING COMPRESSOR

1. Clamp strut body in vice (soft jaws — protect the tube)
2. Install compressor jaws:
   - Minimum 2 coils engaged per jaw
   - Jaws seated DEEP on coils, not on edges
   - Safety pins / chains ENGAGED
3. Compress spring:
   - ALTERNATE sides — 3-4 turns each side
   - EVEN compression — both sides equal
   - NEVER put face/body OVER the spring
4. Compress until top mount is UNLOADED:
   - Visible gap between spring and top mount plate
   - Top mount can be wiggled by hand
5. Remove top mount nut:
   - Hold damper shaft with Allen key/hex socket
   - Undo nut with ring spanner on top
6. Remove (in order):
   - Top mount nut
   - Top mount plate
   - Bearing
   - Upper spring seat
   - Bump stop + dust boot
   - Spring (stays on compressor)

THE SPRING REMAINS COMPRESSED UNTIL REASSEMBLY.
```

#### Slide 8: Component Inspection — Top Mount Bearing
**Visual:** Side-by-side comparison: smooth-spinning top mount bearing vs. a gritty, corroded one

**Instructor Narration:**
> "With everything apart, we inspect. Start with the top mount bearing — this is the bearing that allows the strut to rotate when you steer. Hold it flat and spin the inner race with your fingers. It should spin smoothly and freely with no noise. If it feels gritty, rough, or notchy — replace it. A bad top mount bearing causes a creaking or groaning noise when turning the steering wheel at low speed, especially when stationary. It's cheap insurance — many technicians replace the bearing automatically every time they have the strut apart."

**PPT Content:**
```
INSPECTION: TOP MOUNT BEARING

TEST: Hold flat, spin inner race by hand

GOOD: Smooth, free rotation, no noise
BAD:  Gritty, rough, notchy, grinding feel

SYMPTOMS OF A FAILED TOP MOUNT BEARING:
  Creaking / groaning when turning steering at low speed
  "Springing back" feeling in steering wheel
  Noise worse when stationary or parking manoeuvres

RECOMMENDATION:
  Replace top mount bearing every time strut is apart
  Cost: low  |  Labour: already done  |  Insurance: high

If in doubt — replace. You're already there.
```

---

#### Slide 9: Component Inspection — Bump Stop, Dust Boot, Spring
**Visual:** Three-panel photo: crumbled bump stop, torn dust boot, cracked coil spring

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Next: the bump stop. This is the polyurethane block that prevents metal-to-metal contact when the suspension bottoms out over a big bump. If it's crumbled, compressed flat, or missing chunks — replace it. Without a good bump stop, the damper shaft can impact the top mount and damage the body structure. The dust boot protects the damper shaft from grit and water. If it's torn, split, or missing — replace it. A exposed shaft gets scored, the seal fails, and the damper leaks. Finally, the spring itself. Look for cracks, chips in the coating, and broken coils — especially near the ends where stress is highest. Measure the free length and compare to specification. A spring that's sagged more than 10mm below spec has lost its rate and needs replacing."

**PPT Content:**
```
INSPECTION: BUMP STOP, DUST BOOT, SPRING

BUMP STOP (polyurethane block on shaft):
  GOOD: Firm, full shape, no chunks missing
  BAD:  Crumbled, compressed flat, missing pieces
  Replace if ANY deterioration — prevents metal-on-metal impact

DUST BOOT (rubber bellows over shaft):
  GOOD: Flexible, no tears, no cracks
  BAD:  Torn, split, missing, hardened
  Replace if damaged — exposed shaft = damper failure

COIL SPRING:
  Visual check for:
    Cracks (especially near top and bottom coils)
    Chips in protective coating (corrosion initiation)
    Broken coils (vehicle sat noticeably lower on one side)
  Measure FREE LENGTH — compare to specification:
    Sagged > 10mm below spec = REPLACE
    Always compare LEFT vs RIGHT — uneven = replace the pair

SPRINGS: Always replace in PAIRS (both sides of same axle)
```

#### Slide 10: Reassembly — New Damper onto Spring
**Visual:** Exploded view diagram showing correct order: spring on compressor, lower seat, damper inserted, bump stop, dust boot, upper seat, bearing, top mount plate, nut

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Reassembly. The spring is still on the compressor from disassembly — or if fitting a new spring, compress the new one the same way. Place the lower spring seat on the damper body — note the orientation. The seat usually has a flat or a notch that indexes with the strut body. Slide the spring and compressor over the damper shaft. Fit the bump stop and dust boot onto the shaft. Place the upper spring seat — again, orientation matters, the spring end must sit in the seat's locating step. Fit the top mount bearing — grease it if it's the reusable type. Fit the top mount plate. Thread the top mount nut on and torque to spec — usually 40-60 Nm, check the manual."

**PPT Content:**
```
REASSEMBLY — NEW DAMPER ONTO SPRING

ORDER (bottom to top):
1. Lower spring seat on damper body (orientation! index notch)
2. Spring (still on compressor) over damper shaft
3. Bump stop onto shaft (new if old was damaged)
4. Dust boot onto shaft (new if old was torn)
5. Upper spring seat (orientation! spring end in locating step)
6. Top mount bearing (grease if reusable type)
7. Top mount plate (correct orientation — bolt pattern)
8. Top mount nut — hand-tight first

TORQUE: Top mount nut — typically 40-60 Nm
  (ALWAYS verify from manufacturer specification)
  Hold shaft with Allen key while torquing nut

ORIENTATION MATTERS:
  Spring ends must sit in seat locating steps
  Damper shaft must be centred in top mount
  Misalignment = noise, premature bearing failure
```

#### Slide 11: Spring Compressor Release
**Visual:** Close-up of spring compressor being gradually released — highlighting even release on both sides

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Now the critical moment — releasing the spring compressor. This is just as dangerous as compressing. Release GRADUALLY. Alternate sides — 3-4 turns on one side, 3-4 turns on the other. EVEN release. As the spring expands, watch that it seats properly in both the upper and lower spring seats. If the spring is shifting, twisting, or not seating — STOP. Recompress and correct the alignment. Only when the spring is fully seated and the compressor is slack do you remove the jaws. And even during release — your face and body stay to the SIDE, never over the spring."

**PPT Content:**
```
SPRING COMPRESSOR RELEASE

THIS IS JUST AS DANGEROUS AS COMPRESSION.

1. Release GRADUALLY — 3-4 turns per side, alternating
2. Release EVENLY — both sides at the same rate
3. WATCH spring seating as it expands:
   - Spring ends must stay in locating steps
   - No twisting or shifting
   - If misaligned → STOP → recompress → correct
4. Continue until compressor is fully slack
5. Remove compressor jaws

BODY POSITION: To the SIDE. Never over the spring.
FACE POSITION: To the SIDE. Never over the spring.

Releasing a compressor with your face over the spring
is the mistake that kills. Do not make it.
```

#### Slide 12: Reinstallation and Torque Specifications
**Visual:** Annotated diagram showing all torque specifications for a typical MacPherson strut installation

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Reinstallation is the reverse of removal. Lift the strut into the wheel arch — it's heavy, get a helper if needed. Push the shaft studs through the strut tower and hand-thread the top mount nuts. Guide the strut body into the steering knuckle and insert the pinch bolts. Reconnect the stabilizer link. Reconnect the brake line bracket. Now torque everything to specification. Top mount nuts are typically 25-40 Nm. Knuckle-to-strut bolts are typically 80-120 Nm — these are load-bearing structural fasteners. Stabilizer link nuts are typically 40-60 Nm. Always check the manufacturer specification — never guess on suspension fasteners."

**PPT Content:**
```
REINSTALLATION & TORQUE SPECIFICATIONS

PROCEDURE:
1. Lift strut into wheel arch (helper recommended — 10-15 kg)
2. Guide shaft studs through strut tower — hand-start top nuts
3. Guide strut body into steering knuckle — insert pinch bolts
4. Reconnect stabilizer link
5. Reconnect brake line bracket
6. Reconnect ABS sensor bracket (if applicable)

TYPICAL TORQUE VALUES (ALWAYS verify per manufacturer):
  Top mount nuts:                  25-40 Nm
  Knuckle-to-strut pinch bolts:   80-120 Nm
  Stabilizer link nut:            40-60 Nm
  Brake line bracket:             8-12 Nm

THESE ARE STRUCTURAL FASTENERS.
Under-torque = loose suspension = vehicle control loss
Over-torque = stretched/broken bolt = same result
USE A CALIBRATED TORQUE WRENCH. No guessing.
```

#### Slide 17: Stage 4 — Reassembly
**Instructor Action:**

#### Slide 20: Stage 7 — Post-Installation Checks
**Visual:** Wheel alignment screen showing camber, caster, and toe readings

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The strut is in. Everything is torqued. But the job is NOT finished. When you disturb the strut-to-knuckle connection, you change the wheel alignment. Camber and caster are directly affected by strut position in the knuckle. If you don't realign, the customer gets uneven tyre wear, the vehicle pulls to one side, and the steering wheel is off-centre. Every single time you do strut work, the vehicle goes on the alignment rack. No exceptions. If the workshop doesn't have an alignment rack, you tell the customer it needs to go somewhere that does. This is not optional."

**PPT Content:**
```
POST-INSTALLATION: MANDATORY WHEEL ALIGNMENT

WHY:
  Strut removal changes camber and caster geometry
  Even if bolts go back in "the same holes" — position shifts
  Result without alignment:
    Uneven tyre wear (inside or outside edge)
    Vehicle pulls to one side
    Steering wheel off-centre

WHAT TO CHECK:
  Camber: Typically -0.5 to -1.5 degrees (vehicle specific)
  Caster: Typically 3-7 degrees (vehicle specific)
  Toe:    Typically 0 to +0.2 degrees (vehicle specific)

WHEN:
  AFTER every strut replacement
  AFTER every suspension component replacement
  AFTER every steering component replacement

THIS IS NOT OPTIONAL. THIS IS PART OF THE JOB.

A strut replacement without alignment is an incomplete job.
```

#### Slide 22: Spring Compressor Safety — Final Reinforcement
**Visual:** Text-only slide, bold, no distractions

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before we talk about tomorrow, I want this to be the last thing in your mind today. This slide stays up while you clean your bays."

**PPT Content:**
```
SPRING COMPRESSOR SAFETY — FINAL WORD

1. Jaws seated DEEP on minimum 2 coils
2. Compress BOTH SIDES evenly — alternate turns
3. Safety pins / chains ALWAYS engaged
4. NEVER — face or body over a compressed spring
5. Release as carefully as you compress
6. Inspect the tool BEFORE every use
```

## Key Takeaways

1. The spring compressor is the most dangerous hand tool in the workshop — jaw placement, even compression, safety pins, and body position are non-negotiable safety rules
2. A compressed coil spring stores lethal energy — treat it with the same respect as any other energy source that can kill
3. MacPherson strut removal follows a specific sequence: loosen top mount, disconnect stab link, disconnect knuckle, support hub, remove top mount bolts, lower strut
4. Always inspect top mount bearing (spin test), bump stop (crumble), dust boot (tear), and spring (cracks, free length) when the strut is apart
5. Reassembly requires correct orientation of spring seats — the spring end must locate in the seat step
6. Spring compressor release is just as dangerous as compression — gradual, even, controlled, body to the side
7. Wheel alignment after strut work is MANDATORY, not optional — camber and caster are directly affected
