# Day 50: Rebuild Verification & Road Test
## Instructor-Led Story Framework for PPT Preparation

**Duration:** 180 minutes (54 min theory + 126 min practical)
**Approach:** Systematic Verification — Bench Tests First, Then Road Validation
**PPT Target:** 23 slides
**Week:** 10 of 11 — "The Rebuilder: Gearbox & Suspension" (Day 5 of 5 — Week Finale)

---

## Session Objectives (For Instructor)

By the end of this session, learners will be able to:
- Perform a gearbox function test (shift through all gears, check for noise, grinding, pop-out, and reverse engagement)
- Verify suspension geometry visually (ride height, vehicle level, wheel alignment by eye)
- Execute a structured road test procedure covering low-speed maneuvering, acceleration, cruising, braking, cornering, and rough-road evaluation
- Listen for and identify abnormal noises and vibrations during a road test
- Assess shift quality, steering feel, returnability, and brake pedal feel under real driving conditions
- Document road test findings on a standardised checklist with pass/fail per system
- Summarise the complete Week 10 accomplishment: gearbox strip, inspection, rebuild, strut replacement, arm and bush replacement, and final road test verification

### **DEVELOPMENT: Verification Procedures** (Slides 4-12, ~30 minutes)

#### Slide 4: Gearbox Function Test — Bench Method
**Visual:** Gearbox on bench stand with shift lever mechanism attached, hand demonstrating gear selection, stethoscope on the case

**Instructor Narration:**
> "If the gearbox is still on the bench, we can do a basic function test before installation. With the input shaft free to rotate by hand, shift through every gear position: first, second, third, fourth, fifth, reverse. Each gear should engage positively with a clean, definitive click. No grinding — grinding means synchro damage or incorrect installation. No resistance to engagement — that means a selector fork is bent or misaligned. Spin the input shaft while in each gear and listen — bearing noise shows up as a whine or rumble. For reverse, you may hear normal straight-cut gear whine — that's acceptable."

**PPT Content:**
```
GEARBOX FUNCTION TEST — BENCH

WITH GEARBOX ON BENCH STAND:

1. Attach shift mechanism or shift rod
2. Rotate input shaft by hand while shifting:

   □ 1st gear — Positive engagement, smooth rotation
   □ 2nd gear — Positive engagement, smooth rotation
   □ 3rd gear — Positive engagement, smooth rotation
   □ 4th gear — Positive engagement, smooth rotation
   □ 5th gear — Positive engagement, smooth rotation
   □ Reverse  — Positive engagement (straight-cut whine = normal)
   □ Neutral  — Free rotation, no drag

LISTEN FOR:
• Grinding on engagement = synchro problem
• Whine/rumble in gear = bearing problem
• Difficulty engaging = selector fork issue

Use stethoscope on casing to amplify bearing noise
```

#### Slide 5: Gearbox Function Test — In-Vehicle Method
**Visual:** Vehicle on lift, wheels off the ground, engine running, technician in driver's seat with door open, second technician listening near the gearbox

**Instructor Narration:**
> "If the gearbox is already installed, we test with the vehicle on a lift, wheels off the ground, engine running. With the clutch engaged, shift through every gear. Then release the clutch gently in each gear and let the wheels spin freely. Listen for noise in every gear. The critical test is pop-out: engage a gear, release the clutch, and apply moderate load — have someone gently brake one wheel. If the gearbox pops out of gear under load, the detent mechanism or selector fork has a problem. This is a safety failure — the gearbox must come out again."

**PPT Content:**
```
GEARBOX FUNCTION TEST — IN VEHICLE

SETUP: Vehicle on lift, wheels off ground, engine running

SHIFT TEST:
• Clutch in → shift to each gear → clutch out gently
• Check engagement quality in each gear
• Check for any grinding during shifts

NOISE TEST:
• Let wheels spin freely in each gear at low RPM
• Listen from underneath (ear protection if loud)
• Note any whine, rumble, or clicking per gear

POP-OUT TEST (CRITICAL):
• Engage each gear, clutch out, apply light brake to wheel
• Gearbox must HOLD gear under load
• Pop-out = detent or fork failure → must be repaired

REVERSE ENGAGEMENT:
• Must engage cleanly from standstill
• Brief clash noise on cold gearbox = acceptable
• Persistent grinding = synchro ring issue
```

#### Slide 6: Suspension Geometry Verification — Visual Method
**Visual:** Rear view of vehicle on level ground — measuring tape from wheel arch to ground on both sides, spirit level on the roof

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before we road-test, we check the suspension geometry visually. Park the vehicle on level ground. Stand behind it and look: is it sitting level left to right? Are both sides at the same ride height? Measure from the centre of the wheel hub to the wheel arch lip on both sides — left and right should match within 10-15 mm. Stand in front: are the front wheels pointing straight ahead? Look at the tyre wear patterns — are they even? This isn't a substitute for a full four-wheel alignment on a machine, but it catches gross errors like a spring not seated, a strut top mount not located, or an arm fitted at the wrong angle."

**PPT Content:**
```
SUSPENSION GEOMETRY — VISUAL CHECK

ON LEVEL GROUND, VEHICLE UNLOADED:

RIDE HEIGHT:
• Measure hub centre to arch lip — left vs right
• Front: L ___mm  R ___mm  (should match within 15mm)
• Rear:  L ___mm  R ___mm  (should match within 15mm)
• Uneven = spring not seated, broken mount, wrong spring

VEHICLE LEVEL:
• View from rear — does it lean?
• View from side — is it nose-up or tail-down?
• Place spirit level on roof if available

WHEEL ALIGNMENT (VISUAL):
• Stand in front — both wheels pointing straight?
• Check tyre wear pattern — even across the tread?
• Gross toe-in or toe-out visible as feathering

BOUNCE TEST:
• Push down firmly on each corner, release
• Should bounce once and settle — not keep bouncing
• Continued bouncing = damper (shock absorber) issue

NOTE: This is a pre-road-test check, NOT a substitute
for professional 4-wheel alignment
```

#### Slide 7: Road Test — The Structured Approach
**Visual:** Map showing a marked route with numbered waypoints: parking area, residential street, main road, motorway/dual carriageway, roundabout, speed bump section

**Instructor Narration:**
> "A road test is not just driving around the block. It's a structured test with specific phases designed to test specific systems. We follow a route that gives us every condition: low-speed maneuvering to test steering lock-to-lock and clutch bite point; acceleration through all gears to test shift quality and engine response; steady-state cruise to listen for vibrations at speed; braking from various speeds to test pedal feel and stopping power; cornering in both directions to test suspension behaviour and steering returnability; and rough road surfaces to listen for knocks and rattles. Each phase has a purpose. Each observation gets documented."

**PPT Content:**
```
STRUCTURED ROAD TEST — THE ROUTE

Phase 1: LOW SPEED MANEUVERING (Parking area)
  → Full lock left and right, forward and reverse
  → Clutch bite point, gear selection from standstill

Phase 2: ACCELERATION (Main road)
  → Accelerate through 1st → 2nd → 3rd → 4th → 5th
  → Note shift quality, synchro engagement, engine pull

Phase 3: STEADY-STATE CRUISE (Dual carriageway)
  → Hold constant speed (80-100 km/h)
  → Listen for vibrations, drone, wind noise baseline

Phase 4: BRAKING (Safe straight road)
  → Light braking, moderate braking, firm braking
  → Does vehicle pull? Pedal feel? ABS activation?

Phase 5: CORNERING (Roundabouts, curves)
  → Left-hand and right-hand turns
  → Steering feel, body roll, returnability after turn

Phase 6: ROUGH ROAD (Speed bumps, uneven surface)
  → Listen for knocks, clunks, rattles
  → Feel for harshness, looseness, wander
```

#### Slide 8: Road Test — What to Listen For
**Visual:** Diagram of a vehicle with callout bubbles: front wheels (clicking = CV joint), gearbox area (whine = bearings), rear suspension (clunk = bush), brakes (squeal = pad glazing), steering (knock = rack bush)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Your ears are diagnostic tools. During the road test, turn off the radio, turn off the climate control fan, close the windows. Listen. A clicking noise on full lock during turns — that's a CV joint. A whine that changes pitch with speed but not engine RPM — that's a wheel bearing or gearbox bearing. A whine that changes with engine RPM — that's a gearbox gear mesh issue or an auxiliary belt. A clunk over bumps — that's a worn bush, a loose link, or a ball joint with play. A squeal under braking — pad glazing or wear indicator. The key is to isolate: does the noise change with speed, engine RPM, gear selection, steering angle, or road surface?"

**PPT Content:**
```
ROAD TEST — LISTEN FOR

NOISE vs CONDITION MATRIX:

CLICKING on full lock turns → CV joint wear
WHINE changing with ROAD SPEED → Wheel/gearbox bearing
WHINE changing with ENGINE RPM → Gear mesh / auxiliary belt
CLUNK over bumps → Worn bush / loose link / ball joint play
RUMBLE at cruise speed → Wheel bearing (changes when weaving)
GRINDING during gear shift → Synchro ring failure
SQUEAL under braking → Pad wear indicator / glazed pads
KNOCK on steering input → Steering rack bush / track rod end

ISOLATION TECHNIQUE:
• Does it change with SPEED? → Drivetrain/wheel related
• Does it change with RPM? → Engine/gearbox related
• Does it change with STEERING? → Steering/CV related
• Does it change with ROAD SURFACE? → Suspension related
• Does it change with GEAR? → Gearbox specific
```

#### Slide 9: Road Test — What to Feel For
**Visual:** Close-up of hands on steering wheel at 10-and-2, with arrows indicating pull left, vibration through wheel, and returnability arc

**Instructor Narration:**
> "After sound, feel. Through the steering wheel: does it vibrate at speed? That's a wheel balance issue or a warped brake disc. Does it pull to one side under braking? That's a caliper issue or uneven pad contact. Does the steering return to centre after a turn? If it doesn't, the strut top bearing or steering geometry is wrong. Through the seat: do you feel vibrations through the body? That's usually an engine mount, propshaft balance, or exhaust contact. Through the brake pedal: is it firm and progressive? A spongy pedal means air in the system. A pulsating pedal means a warped disc. Through the gear lever: are shifts smooth and precise? Does it feel notchy? Does it slip out of gear?"

**PPT Content:**
```
ROAD TEST — FEEL FOR

THROUGH THE STEERING WHEEL:
• Vibration at speed → Wheel balance / warped disc
• Pull under braking → Caliper issue / uneven pads
• Heavy or light patches → Steering rack / PAS issue
• Returnability after turn → Strut bearing / geometry

THROUGH THE SEAT:
• Vibration at idle → Engine mount
• Vibration at speed → Propshaft / exhaust contact
• Harshness over bumps → Damper / spring issue

THROUGH THE BRAKE PEDAL:
• Spongy → Air in hydraulic system
• Pulsating → Warped disc
• Progressive and firm → CORRECT
• Long travel before bite → Adjustment needed

THROUGH THE GEAR LEVER:
• Smooth positive shifts → CORRECT
• Notchy / resistant → Linkage or synchro
• Slips out of gear → Detent or fork failure (SAFETY ISSUE)
```

#### Slide 10: Road Test — Shift Quality Assessment
**Visual:** Shift pattern diagram (H-pattern 5-speed + reverse) with quality ratings annotated: "smooth," "notchy," "grinding"

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Since we rebuilt the gearbox this week, shift quality is our primary focus during the road test. Test every upshift and every downshift. First to second is the hardest on synchros because the speed change is greatest. Third to second on a downshift is a good test too — the synchro has to slow a lot of rotating mass. Shift at various RPMs — low RPM shifts test synchro effectiveness at low differential speeds; high RPM shifts test it at high differential speeds. Note the clutch — does it disengage fully? Incomplete disengagement makes every shift feel bad even if the gearbox is perfect. The clutch bite point should be smooth and progressive, not grabby or slipping."

**PPT Content:**
```
SHIFT QUALITY ASSESSMENT

TEST EACH SHIFT:
□ 1→2  (upshift, various RPMs)
□ 2→3  (upshift, various RPMs)
□ 3→4  (upshift, various RPMs)
□ 4→5  (upshift, various RPMs)
□ 5→4  (downshift)
□ 4→3  (downshift)
□ 3→2  (downshift — hardest on synchros)
□ 2→1  (downshift — at low speed only)
□ N→R  (from standstill, vehicle stationary)

RATE EACH: Smooth / Acceptable / Notchy / Grinding

CLUTCH ASSESSMENT:
• Bite point — smooth and progressive?
• Full disengagement — shifts clean with clutch pressed?
• No judder on take-up?
• No slipping under load in high gear?
```

#### Slide 11: Road Test — Steering and Braking Assessment
**Visual:** Two-panel image: left shows a slalom course with cones, right shows brake test markings on a test surface

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Steering returnability is one of the most telling tests after suspension work. Make a turn — left or right — and let go of the wheel. On a well-set-up vehicle, the steering should return to centre on its own. If it doesn't, the strut top mount is binding, or the castor angle is wrong. Steering should feel consistent left to right — no heavy spots, no dead zones. For braking: do three progressive stops from increasing speeds. The vehicle should stop straight with no pull, the pedal should feel firm and progressive, and ABS should activate smoothly on a slippery surface if available. A pull under braking after our suspension work could mean we've disturbed the brake hoses or caliper brackets."

**PPT Content:**
```
STEERING ASSESSMENT:
□ Returnability — wheel returns to centre after turn
□ Consistency — same effort left and right
□ No dead zone at centre
□ No heavy spots through the range
□ No vibration or shimmy at speed
□ Full lock achievable both sides (no obstruction)

BRAKING ASSESSMENT:
□ Pedal firm and progressive
□ No pull left or right under braking
□ No pulsation through pedal (warped disc)
□ No squeal or grinding noise
□ ABS activation smooth (test on gravel/wet if safe)
□ Handbrake holds on incline

BRAKING STOPS:
• 30 km/h → gentle stop: ________
• 50 km/h → moderate stop: ________
• 80 km/h → firm stop: ________
• Emergency stop from 50 km/h (if safe area): ________
```

#### Slide 16: Stage 3A — Road Test: Low Speed & Acceleration
**Instructor Action:**
- Instructor drives first to establish baseline and demonstrate technique
- Then learner drives (if licence permits) or observes from passenger seat
- Low-speed maneuvering in car park: full lock left and right, forward and reverse
- Clutch assessment: bite point, smoothness, judder check

#### Slide 17: Stage 3B — Road Test: Cruise & Braking
**Instructor Action:**
- Reach steady cruise speed (80-100 km/h if road permits)
- Windows up, radio off, AC off — listen
- Note any vibrations, drone, or wind noise
- Perform braking tests: light, moderate, firm
- Check for pull, pedal pulsation, ABS activation
- Learner documents speed, pedal feel, stopping behaviour

---

#### Slide 18: Stage 3C — Road Test: Cornering & Rough Road
**Instructor Action:**
- Execute left and right turns at various speeds
- Test steering returnability: turn and release the wheel
- Note body roll behaviour — excessive roll indicates spring or ARB issue
- Drive over speed bumps and uneven surfaces
- Listen for knocks, clunks, rattles from suspension
- Feel for harshness or looseness through steering wheel and seat
- Final: downshift through gears on return to workshop — note quality

#### Slide 19: Stage 4 — Documentation
**Instructor Action:**
- Return to workshop, park vehicle
- Each learner completes the full road test checklist individually
- Include: vehicle ID, date, tester name, route description
- Rate each system: pass, pass with advisory, or fail
- Write specific observations — not just tick boxes
- Instructor reviews each checklist, discusses discrepancies between learners
- Group agrees on final verdict

**PPT Content:**

```
EACH LEARNER COMPLETES INDIVIDUALLY:

3. Suspension section — ride height, bounce test, clunks

5. Braking section — pedal feel, straight-line stopping

```

#### Slide 20: Handling Failures
**Visual:** Decision tree: Road test finding -> Is it safety-critical? -> Yes: Vehicle off road, repair immediately -> No: Advisory to customer, schedule repair

**Instructor Narration:**
> "What if the road test finds a problem? It happens — and that's exactly why we test. The question is: is it safety-critical? If the gearbox pops out of gear under load — that's safety-critical. The vehicle does not leave the workshop. If there's a slight vibration at 110 km/h — that's an advisory for wheel balancing. The customer can drive, but it needs attention. The road test checklist records the finding, the severity, and the recommended action. Never release a vehicle with a known safety defect. Your name is on that checklist."

**PPT Content:**
```
HANDLING ROAD TEST FAILURES

SAFETY-CRITICAL FAILURE:
• Gearbox pops out of gear under load
• Vehicle pulls sharply under braking
• Steering does not return to centre
• Excessive play in steering or suspension
• Strange noise + vibration getting worse

→ VEHICLE STAYS IN WORKSHOP
→ Identify root cause → repair → re-test

NON-CRITICAL ADVISORY:
• Slight vibration at high speed (wheel balance)
• Minor noise on rough road (expected break-in)
• Steering slightly heavier one side (alignment needed)

→ DOCUMENT ON CHECKLIST
→ Inform customer with recommended action
→ Vehicle can be released with advisory

YOUR SIGNATURE = YOUR PROFESSIONAL GUARANTEE
```

## Key Takeaways

1. A rebuild is not complete until the vehicle drives correctly — bench work is only half the job
2. Gearbox pop-out under load is a safety-critical failure that must be resolved before the vehicle leaves
3. Visual geometry checks catch gross suspension errors but do not replace professional alignment
4. A structured road test follows a deliberate route: low speed, acceleration, cruise, braking, cornering, rough road
5. Ears and hands are diagnostic tools: listen for noise type, feel for vibrations, correlate with conditions
6. Shift quality assessment requires testing every upshift and downshift at various RPMs
7. Steering returnability is one of the best indicators of correct suspension geometry
8. Documentation is not paperwork — it is professional evidence and your personal guarantee
9. Never release a vehicle with a known safety defect, regardless of customer pressure
10. Week 10 achievement: complete gearbox rebuild + suspension replacement + verified road test — that is technician-level work
