#### Slide 2: The Wear Assessment Method — Visual + Dimensional
**Visual:** Two-column comparison — "Visual tells you WHAT happened" (scoring, discolouration, pitting) vs. "Measurement tells you HOW MUCH" (taper, ovality, clearance)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Wear assessment has two phases. Visual inspection comes first — you're looking for cracks, scoring, pitting, discolouration from overheating, erosion, corrosion, and contact marks where there shouldn't be any. Each visual finding tells you what happened. Then dimensional measurement quantifies the damage. A visual scratch on a journal becomes '0.05mm below minimum diameter' when you measure it. Both are required. Visual without measurement is guessing. Measurement without visual is missing context."

**PPT Content:**
```
WEAR ASSESSMENT METHODOLOGY

PHASE 1 — VISUAL INSPECTION (What happened?)
  Cracks — stress failure, overheating, manufacturing defect
  Scoring — contamination in oil, bearing failure, dry running
  Pitting — corrosion, cavitation, electrical discharge
  Discolouration (blue/purple) — overheating
  Contact marks — misalignment, bearing failure
  Erosion — coolant leak across gasket, detonation

PHASE 2 — DIMENSIONAL MEASUREMENT (How much?)
  Bore: taper, ovality, absolute diameter
  Crankshaft: journal diameter, out-of-round, taper
  Camshaft: lobe lift, journal diameter
  Bearings: oil clearance (Plastigage)
  Rings: end gap, side clearance

BOTH PHASES REQUIRED FOR GO/NO-GO DECISIONS
```

#### Slide 3: Specifications and Tolerances
**Visual:** Page from a service manual showing bore specifications — nominal diameter, standard tolerance, service limit, and oversize options

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Every measurement is meaningless without a specification to compare it to. The service manual gives you three numbers for each dimension. The nominal — what the factory intended. The tolerance — the acceptable range for a new component. And the service limit — the maximum wear before the component must be machined or replaced. If your measurement is within tolerance, it's reusable. If it's between tolerance and service limit, it may be reusable with new bearings or rings. If it's beyond the service limit, it needs machining or it's scrap. Always have the specification table open when you measure."

**PPT Content:**
```
SPECIFICATIONS & TOLERANCES

Three key numbers for every dimension:

NOMINAL: Factory-intended dimension
  Example: Bore diameter = 86.000 mm

TOLERANCE: Acceptable range for new/rebuilt
  Example: 86.000 - 86.019 mm

SERVICE LIMIT: Maximum allowable wear
  Example: 86.050 mm

Decision logic:
  Within tolerance → REUSE as-is
  Between tolerance and service limit → REUSE with new wear parts
  Beyond service limit → MACHINE or REPLACE

ALWAYS have the spec table in front of you when measuring.
No measurement from memory. No guessing.
```

### **PRACTICAL BLOCK 1: Cylinder Bore Measurement** (Slides 5-9, ~40 minutes)

**Narrative Voice:** Step-by-step precision coaching. No rushing.

#### Slide 5: Bore Measurement Theory — Taper and Ovality
**Visual:** Cross-section diagram of a cylinder bore showing three measurement heights (top, middle, bottom) and two axes (thrust and non-thrust). Arrows indicating taper direction (wider at top) and ovality (wider in thrust axis)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "A new cylinder bore is perfectly round and perfectly straight. A worn bore is neither. The piston rocks at the top of its stroke — this causes the bore to wear wider at the top than the bottom. That's taper. The piston is pushed sideways by the connecting rod force — this causes the bore to wear wider in the thrust direction than the non-thrust direction. That's ovality, also called out-of-round. To capture both, we measure at three heights and two axes per cylinder — six readings per bore. The difference between top and bottom on the same axis is taper. The difference between the two axes at the same height is ovality."

**PPT Content:**
```
BORE MEASUREMENT — TAPER AND OVALITY

Measurement positions per cylinder:
  HEIGHT 1: 25mm below deck (top of ring travel)
  HEIGHT 2: Centre of bore
  HEIGHT 3: 25mm above bottom of bore (bottom of ring travel)

At each height, measure TWO axes:
  AXIS A: Thrust direction (perpendicular to crankshaft)
  AXIS B: Non-thrust direction (parallel to crankshaft)

6 readings per cylinder = (3 heights x 2 axes)

Calculations:
  TAPER = Height 1 reading - Height 3 reading (same axis)
  OVALITY = Axis A reading - Axis B reading (same height)

Typical limits:
  Taper: max 0.05 mm
  Ovality: max 0.03 mm
  If exceeded: rebore or hone required
```

---

#### Slide 6: Setting the Bore Gauge
**Visual:** Photo sequence showing bore gauge being zeroed against an outside micrometer set to the nominal bore diameter

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The bore gauge doesn't read absolute diameter directly. You set it to a reference diameter first — the nominal bore size — using an outside micrometer as your standard. Lock the micrometer at 86.000mm, for example. Insert the bore gauge between the micrometer anvils and zero the dial indicator. Now when you put the bore gauge in the cylinder, the dial reads the deviation from 86.000mm. If the dial reads +0.035, the bore is 86.035mm. This comparative measurement technique is more accurate than trying to read an absolute dimension inside a bore."

**Instructor Action:** Demonstrate bore gauge setup and zeroing procedure. Each learner zeroes their own bore gauge under instructor verification before proceeding.

**PPT Content:**
```
SETTING THE BORE GAUGE

Step 1: Determine nominal bore diameter from specification
  Example: 86.000 mm

Step 2: Set outside micrometer to nominal diameter
  Lock micrometer at 86.000 mm

Step 3: Insert bore gauge between micrometer anvils
  Rock gently to find the minimum reading (true diameter)

Step 4: Zero the dial indicator at this position
  Now dial reads DEVIATION from nominal

Step 5: Verify — reinsert in micrometer, confirm zero

Reading in the bore:
  Dial reads +0.035 → Bore is 86.035 mm
  Dial reads -0.010 → Bore is 85.990 mm

ACCURACY DEPENDS ON CORRECT ZEROING.
Verify zero between every cylinder.
```

#### Slide 7: Bore Measurement — Hands-On
**Visual:** Learner measuring a bore with the gauge, showing correct insertion angle (perpendicular to bore wall) and rocking motion to find minimum reading

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Insert the bore gauge into the cylinder at the first height — 25mm below the deck. Angle it slightly to get it past the bore, then straighten it perpendicular to the axis you're measuring. Now rock the gauge gently side to side. You're looking for the point where the dial indicator reverses — that's the minimum reading, which is the true diameter. Record it. Move to the other axis and repeat. Then move to the middle height, both axes. Then the bottom height, both axes. Six readings per cylinder. Write them all on your data sheet. Do NOT try to remember them."

**Instructor Action:** Demonstrate measurement of one cylinder (all 6 readings). Learners then measure their assigned cylinders. Instructor circulates to verify technique — especially the rocking motion to find true minimum.

**PPT Content:**
```
BORE MEASUREMENT — TECHNIQUE

Insertion:
  1. Tilt gauge to enter bore
  2. Straighten to perpendicular to wall
  3. Position at correct height (25mm below deck for top)

Finding true diameter:
  4. Rock gauge gently side-to-side
  5. Watch dial indicator — it sweeps through a minimum
  6. The MINIMUM reading = true diameter at that point
  7. Record to 0.01 mm precision

Repeat for:
  Height 1, Axis A → Height 1, Axis B
  Height 2, Axis A → Height 2, Axis B
  Height 3, Axis A → Height 3, Axis B

4-CYLINDER ENGINE = 24 READINGS TOTAL

Write every number down. Memory is not a measurement tool.
```

#### Slide 9: Block Deck Flatness Check
**Visual:** Straight edge placed diagonally across block deck surface, feeler gauge being inserted under the edge to check for warpage

**Instructor Narration:**
> "While we have the block accessible, check deck flatness. Place a precision straight edge across the deck surface diagonally, then lengthwise, then widthwise. Try to insert feeler gauges under the straight edge. If anything thicker than the specification limit slides under, the deck is warped and needs machining. Typical limit for a passenger car block is 0.05mm. A warped deck means the head gasket won't seal properly, no matter how good the gasket is."

**Instructor Action:** Demonstrate straight edge and feeler gauge technique on the block. Learners check their block and record results.

**PPT Content:**
```
BLOCK DECK FLATNESS CHECK

Method:
  1. Clean deck surface thoroughly
  2. Place precision straight edge across deck:
     - Diagonally (corner to corner, both directions)
     - Lengthwise (end to end)
     - Widthwise (multiple positions)
  3. Attempt to insert feeler gauges under the edge
  4. The thickest gauge that fits = warpage amount

Typical specification:
  Maximum warpage: 0.05 mm (check service manual)

Decision:
  Within spec → Reuse as-is
  0.05 - 0.10 mm → Machine (skim the deck surface)
  > 0.10 mm → Assess feasibility (minimum deck height spec)

ALSO CHECK: Cylinder head flatness (same method on head surface)
```

#### Slide 10: Crankshaft Journal Measurement — Setup
**Visual:** Crankshaft on V-blocks with micrometer positioned on a main bearing journal. Diagram showing measurement axes and positions for out-of-round and taper checks

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Place the crankshaft on V-blocks — clean ones, on a flat bench. We measure every journal — main journals and rod journals. Each journal gets measured at two positions along its length for taper, and at two axes 90 degrees apart for out-of-round. That's four readings per journal. The same logic as bore measurement: we're checking for uneven wear. A crankshaft that's 0.02mm out of round will hammer the bearing and fail within thousands of kilometres."

**PPT Content:**
```
CRANKSHAFT JOURNAL MEASUREMENT

Setup:
  - Crankshaft on V-blocks, cleaned
  - Outside micrometer, appropriate size range

Measurement positions per journal:
  POSITION 1: Front of journal  | Axis A (vertical)
  POSITION 1: Front of journal  | Axis B (horizontal, 90 degrees)
  POSITION 2: Rear of journal   | Axis A (vertical)
  POSITION 2: Rear of journal   | Axis B (horizontal)

4 readings per journal

Calculations:
  OUT-OF-ROUND = Axis A - Axis B (same position)
  TAPER = Front reading - Rear reading (same axis)

Number of journals:
  4-cylinder: 5 main journals + 4 rod journals = 36 readings
```

#### Slide 11: Crankshaft Measurement — Hands-On
**Visual:** Close-up of correct micrometer placement on a crankshaft journal — anvils perpendicular to journal axis, positioned at the front of the journal

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Position the micrometer anvils perpendicular to the journal axis. Close gently until the ratchet clicks — never force it. Read to 0.01mm. Record it. Rotate 90 degrees and read again. Move to the rear of the journal and repeat both axes. Move to the next journal. It's repetitive, but every reading matters. If you find a journal out of spec, the crankshaft needs regrinding — a machine shop takes it to the next undersize and you fit undersize bearings. If it's too far gone, it's a replacement crank."

**Instructor Action:** Demonstrate measurement of one main journal and one rod journal. Learners measure remaining journals, recording all readings on the data sheet.

**PPT Content:**
```
CRANKSHAFT MEASUREMENT — TECHNIQUE

Micrometer placement:
  - Anvils perpendicular to journal axis
  - Centred on journal (not on fillet radius!)
  - Close with ratchet stop — consistent pressure

Typical specifications (example):
  Main journal: 52.000 mm nominal, tolerance 51.987-52.000
  Rod journal: 47.000 mm nominal, tolerance 46.987-47.000
  Out-of-round limit: 0.02 mm
  Taper limit: 0.02 mm
  Service limit: 0.05 mm undersize available

Decision matrix:
  Within tolerance → Reuse with standard bearings
  Worn but within service limit → Regrind to undersize 1 (-0.25mm)
  Beyond undersize 2 → Replace crankshaft

ALSO INSPECT: Fillet radii for cracks (visual + magnifying glass)
```

#### Slide 12: Crankshaft End Play (Thrust Clearance)
**Visual:** Dial indicator set up on the end of the crankshaft with a pry bar, showing the method to push the crank forward and backward to measure end play

**Instructor Narration:**
> "End play is the amount the crankshaft can slide forward and backward in the block. It's controlled by the thrust bearing — usually on the centre main. To measure it, reinstall the main caps finger-tight. Set up a dial indicator on the crankshaft nose. Push the crank fully rearward with a pry bar, zero the indicator, then push fully forward and read. Typical spec is 0.05 to 0.30mm. Too much end play means the thrust surfaces are worn — and that causes the clutch release to not fully disengage, or the torque converter to run out of clearance."

**Instructor Action:** Demonstrate end play measurement setup and procedure. Learners measure and record.

**PPT Content:**
```
CRANKSHAFT END PLAY (THRUST CLEARANCE)

Setup:
  1. Install main bearing caps (finger-tight — this is temporary)
  2. Mount dial indicator on block, plunger on crankshaft nose end
  3. Pry crankshaft fully REARWARD — zero indicator
  4. Pry crankshaft fully FORWARD — read indicator

Reading = End Play

Typical specification: 0.05 - 0.30 mm

Decision:
  Within spec → Thrust bearing surfaces acceptable
  Excessive → Install oversize thrust washers
  Severely worn → Machine thrust face + oversize washers

Symptoms of excessive end play:
  - Clutch drag (manual transmission)
  - Torque converter contact (automatic)
  - Knocking sound at idle under load
```

#### Slide 13: Camshaft Lobe Lift Measurement
**Visual:** Micrometer measuring across the base circle of a cam lobe (minimum reading) and then across the nose (maximum reading). Diagram showing base circle diameter vs. lobe height

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Camshaft lobe lift determines how far each valve opens. A worn lobe means less valve opening, less airflow, and reduced engine performance. Measure each lobe with a micrometer — across the base circle for the minimum reading, then across the nose for the maximum. The difference is the lobe lift. Compare each lobe to the specification. If any lobe is worn below the minimum lift, the camshaft is replaced. You can't regrind cam lobes — the hardened surface layer is only a few tenths deep."

**Instructor Action:** Demonstrate lobe lift measurement on two lobes — one intake and one exhaust. Learners measure all lobes and record.

**PPT Content:**
```
CAMSHAFT LOBE LIFT MEASUREMENT

Method:
  1. Clean camshaft lobes thoroughly
  2. Measure BASE CIRCLE diameter (micrometer across the flat)
     Record: e.g., 34.50 mm
  3. Measure LOBE HEIGHT (micrometer across the nose)
     Record: e.g., 42.20 mm
  4. LOBE LIFT = Lobe Height - Base Circle
     Result: 42.20 - 34.50 = 7.70 mm

Repeat for EVERY lobe — intake and exhaust

Typical specification:
  Intake lobe lift: 7.80 mm minimum
  Exhaust lobe lift: 7.60 mm minimum
  Wear limit: 0.10 mm below nominal

Decision:
  Within spec → Reuse
  Below minimum → Replace camshaft (cannot regrind — hardened layer)

ALSO CHECK: Journal bearing surfaces, sprocket keyway for wear
```

#### Slide 14: Bearing Clearance with Plastigage
**Visual:** Step-by-step photo sequence — laying Plastigage strip on journal, installing cap and torquing to spec, removing cap, comparing crushed strip width to scale on packaging

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Plastigage is brilliant in its simplicity. It's a strip of soft plastic with a precise diameter. You lay it across the bearing journal, install the cap, and torque the bolts to specification. DO NOT rotate the crankshaft — the strip must not move. Then remove the cap. The strip has been crushed. Compare its width to the scale printed on the Plastigage package. Wider crush = less clearance. Narrower crush = more clearance. If the clearance is within spec, the bearing is good. If it's too loose, you need the next undersize bearing or a reground journal."

**Instructor Action:** Demonstrate Plastigage on one main journal. CRITICAL — emphasise not rotating the crank. Learners do one rod journal each.

**PPT Content:**
```
BEARING CLEARANCE — PLASTIGAGE METHOD

Steps:
  1. Clean journal and bearing shell surfaces (dry — no oil)
  2. Place Plastigage strip across journal (parallel to axis)
  3. Install bearing cap with shell
  4. Torque cap bolts to SPECIFICATION (this is not optional)
  5. DO NOT ROTATE CRANKSHAFT
  6. Remove cap bolts and cap
  7. Compare crushed Plastigage width to scale on package

Reading the scale:
  - Package shows width-to-clearance conversion
  - Green Plastigage: 0.025 - 0.076 mm range
  - Red Plastigage: 0.051 - 0.152 mm range

Typical clearance specification:
  Main bearings: 0.020 - 0.058 mm
  Rod bearings: 0.020 - 0.053 mm

Decision:
  Within spec → Bearing clearance acceptable
  Excessive → Undersize bearings or regrind journal
  Extremely tight → Wrong bearing shell or journal issue
```

#### Slide 16: Piston Ring End Gap Measurement
**Visual:** Piston ring inserted squarely into a cylinder bore using an inverted piston as a pusher, with feeler gauge measuring the gap between ring ends

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Piston ring end gap determines how well the ring seals against blow-by. Too tight — the ring expands when hot and locks up, scoring the bore. Too loose — excessive blow-by, loss of compression, oil consumption. Push a new ring squarely into the bore using an inverted piston to keep it perpendicular. Then measure the gap between the ring ends with a feeler gauge. The gap must be within specification for the bore diameter. If you've rebored, the gap changes — recalculate for the new bore size."

**Instructor Action:** Demonstrate ring insertion and gap measurement. Each learner measures the gap on at least one compression ring and one oil ring.

**PPT Content:**
```
PISTON RING END GAP MEASUREMENT

Method:
  1. Insert ring into bore (no piston — ring alone)
  2. Push ring down ~25mm using inverted piston as a guide
     (keeps ring SQUARE in bore — critical for accuracy)
  3. Measure gap between ring ends with feeler gauge

Typical specifications:
  Top compression ring:   0.20 - 0.40 mm
  Second compression ring: 0.30 - 0.50 mm
  Oil control ring:        0.25 - 0.60 mm

Per bore size: Gap increases with bore diameter
  Rule of thumb: 0.003-0.004 mm per mm of bore diameter

Decision:
  Within spec → Ring is correct for bore
  Too tight → File ring ends carefully (ring filer tool)
  Too loose → Wrong ring set or bore is oversized

NEVER install a ring with insufficient gap — it WILL seize.
```

#### Slide 17: Piston Inspection and Measurement
**Visual:** Piston with measurement points marked — skirt diameter (perpendicular to pin bore), ring groove width (measured with feeler gauge and new ring), pin bore diameter

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Inspect each piston visually first. Look at the crown — detonation damage shows as erosion or pitting. Look at the skirt — scoring means the bore was too tight or oil was insufficient. Check the ring grooves — worn grooves allow the ring to rock and pump oil. Measure the skirt diameter perpendicular to the pin bore — that's where it's designed to be measured. Calculate the piston-to-bore clearance by subtracting piston diameter from bore diameter. Too tight: the piston seizes when hot. Too loose: piston slap noise and accelerated wear."

**PPT Content:**
```
PISTON INSPECTION & MEASUREMENT

Visual inspection:
  Crown: Detonation damage, carbon pattern, erosion
  Skirt: Scoring, scuffing, contact pattern
  Ring grooves: Width wear, carbon buildup
  Pin bore: Wear, discolouration from heat

Measurement:
  Skirt diameter — measured PERPENDICULAR to pin bore
    (at the point specified by manufacturer, usually at skirt bottom)
  Example: 85.960 mm

Piston-to-bore clearance:
  = Bore diameter - Piston diameter
  = 86.020 - 85.960 = 0.060 mm
  Typical spec: 0.02 - 0.06 mm

Ring groove side clearance:
  Insert new ring in groove, measure gap with feeler gauge
  Typical: 0.03 - 0.07 mm (top), 0.02 - 0.06 mm (second)

Excessive groove clearance → Ring flutter → Oil consumption
```

#### Slide 18: Visual Inspection — Bearing Shell Analysis
**Visual:** Six bearing shells showing different wear patterns — normal even wear, eccentric wear (misalignment), scoring (contamination), overlay erosion (overloading), fatigue cracking, copper showing through (extreme wear)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The bearing shells are the engine's diary. Normal wear shows even, smooth removal of the overlay coating — silvery and uniform. Eccentric wear — heavy on one side — means misalignment. Scoring means contamination got into the oil. Dark spots or pitting mean corrosion from acidic oil or coolant contamination. Copper colour showing through the overlay means the bearing ran out of material — this is severe. Fatigue cracks in the overlay mean cyclic overloading. Read the bearings. They tell you what killed the engine."

**PPT Content:**
```
BEARING SHELL ANALYSIS — READING THE EVIDENCE

Normal wear:
  Even, smooth, silver overlay visible → Acceptable

Eccentric wear (heavy on one edge):
  → Misalignment, bent rod, bore geometry issue

Scoring (lines parallel to rotation):
  → Contamination in oil (dirt, metal particles)

Overlay erosion (dark patches):
  → Overloading, insufficient oil film, wrong viscosity

Copper visible through overlay:
  → SEVERE — bearing has worn through protective layers

Fatigue cracking (spider web pattern):
  → Cyclic overload, flexing, or overlay adhesion failure

Corrosion / pitting:
  → Acidic oil (extended drain interval) or coolant in oil

DOCUMENT with photos — useful for root cause reporting
```

## Key Takeaways

1. Visual inspection identifies WHAT happened; dimensional measurement quantifies HOW MUCH
2. Bore measurement at three heights and two axes captures taper and ovality — both must be within spec
3. Crankshaft journals must be measured for out-of-round and taper at each journal
4. Cam lobe lift is the difference between base circle and nose height — below spec means replacement
5. Plastigage measures bearing clearance without installing the engine — critical go/no-go tool
6. Every decision must be backed by a number compared to a specification — never by opinion
