#### Slide 3: The Five Jobs of Engine Oil
**Visual:** Central oil drop icon with five branches radiating out, each with an icon and label: Lubricate (friction reduction), Cool (heat transfer), Seal (ring-to-wall gap), Clean (suspend contaminants), Protect (corrosion inhibition)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Most people think oil does one thing: lubricate. Reduce friction. And yes, that's the primary job. But engine oil actually performs five critical functions. First, it lubricates — it creates a film between moving parts to prevent metal-to-metal contact. Second, it cools — oil carries heat away from bearings, pistons, and turbocharger shafts. About 40% of engine cooling is done by oil, not coolant. Third, it seals — oil fills the microscopic gaps between piston rings and cylinder walls, keeping combustion gases where they belong. Fourth, it cleans — modern oil contains detergent and dispersant additives that suspend soot, carbon, and metal particles so the filter can catch them. Fifth, it protects — anti-corrosion additives prevent acids from attacking bearing surfaces.
>
> Take away any one of these five functions, and engine life drops dramatically."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE 5 JOBS OF ENGINE OIL

1. LUBRICATE — Reduce friction between moving parts
   Creates hydrodynamic oil film at bearings

2. COOL — Carry heat away from hot components
   Pistons, bearings, turbo shaft: ~40% of engine cooling

3. SEAL — Fill gaps between piston rings and cylinder walls
   Prevents combustion gas blow-by

4. CLEAN — Suspend contaminants (soot, carbon, metal particles)
   Detergent + dispersant additives carry debris to filter

5. PROTECT — Prevent corrosion and acid attack
   Anti-corrosion + anti-oxidation additives

Remove ANY one function = accelerated engine wear
```

#### Slide 5: Oil System Overview — Follow the Flow
**Visual:** Full-colour schematic of a complete engine oil circuit: sump -> pickup tube with strainer -> oil pump -> oil filter -> main oil gallery -> crankshaft bearings -> connecting rod bearings -> camshaft bearings -> hydraulic lifters -> turbocharger (if equipped) -> oil cooler -> return to sump. Flow arrows in amber/gold colour throughout.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's trace the oil from start to finish. It begins at the bottom — the sump, also called the oil pan. This is the reservoir. A pickup tube with a strainer reaches down into the oil. The oil pump, driven by the crankshaft, sucks oil up through that tube and pressurises it. The pressurised oil passes through the oil filter, which catches particles. Clean oil then enters the main oil gallery — think of it as the motorway running through the engine block.
>
> From the main gallery, oil branches off to every critical point: crankshaft main bearings, connecting rod bearings, camshaft bearings, hydraulic valve lifters, and if there's a turbocharger, it gets fed too. Some engines also spray oil onto the underside of the pistons for cooling. After doing its job at each bearing, the oil drains back down to the sump by gravity. One continuous loop, running non-stop whenever the engine is on."

**PPT Content:**
```
OIL SYSTEM FLOW PATH

SUMP (reservoir)
  |
PICKUP TUBE + STRAINER (coarse debris guard)
  |
OIL PUMP (creates pressure, driven by crankshaft)
  |
OIL FILTER (removes particles > 20-40 microns)
  |
MAIN OIL GALLERY (highway through the engine block)
  |
BRANCHES TO:
  +-- Crankshaft main bearings (5 on inline-4)
  +-- Connecting rod bearings (via drilled crankshaft)
  +-- Camshaft bearings / journals
  +-- Hydraulic valve lifters / lash adjusters
  +-- Timing chain tensioner
  +-- Turbocharger shaft (if equipped)
  +-- Piston cooling jets (some engines)
  |
OIL COOLER (if equipped — removes excess heat)
  |
DRAIN BACK TO SUMP (gravity return)
```

#### Slide 6: The Sump and Pickup Tube
**Visual:** Cross-section of an oil pan/sump — wet sump design showing baffles, drain plug, pickup tube with strainer mesh, and oil level markings. Optionally show a dry sump comparison in an inset.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The sump sits at the very bottom of the engine. In a wet sump system — which is what 95% of road cars use — this is where all the oil lives when it's not circulating. Typical capacity is 4 to 6 litres. The sump has baffles inside to prevent oil surge during hard cornering or braking. If oil sloshes away from the pickup tube, the pump sucks air instead of oil — and bearings starve.
>
> The pickup tube sits near the bottom of the sump with a coarse mesh strainer. This strainer catches large debris — gasket fragments, broken parts — before oil reaches the pump. It's not a fine filter; that comes later. At the bottom of the sump, a drain plug with a sealing washer allows you to drain the oil during a service. Simple component, critical function."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE SUMP (OIL PAN)

Type: Wet sump (95% of road cars)
Capacity: Typically 4-6 litres

Features:
- Cast aluminium or pressed steel
- Internal baffles: prevent oil surge during cornering/braking
- Drain plug: with crush washer or O-ring seal
- Pickup tube: reaches to lowest point of sump
- Strainer mesh: coarse pre-filter (~500 micron)

DRY SUMP (race/high-performance):
- External oil tank, separate scavenge pump
- Allows lower engine mounting, no oil surge
- Not common in standard road vehicles

COMMON FAULT: Overtightened drain plug -> stripped thread
  -> oil leak -> engine damage
```

#### Slide 7: Oil Pump Types — Gear and Rotor
**Visual:** Side-by-side cutaway diagrams of (1) an external gear oil pump showing two meshing gears, inlet and outlet ports, and (2) a gerotor/internal rotor pump showing the inner and outer rotor. Arrows show oil flow direction in both.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The oil pump is the heart of the lubrication system. It's mechanically driven — usually directly off the crankshaft or via a chain — so it spins whenever the engine runs. There are two main types you'll encounter.
>
> First, the gear pump. Two meshing gears spin inside a housing. Oil enters on one side, gets trapped in the spaces between the gear teeth and the housing, and is carried around to the outlet side. Simple, reliable, and produces steady pressure.
>
> Second, the gerotor or internal rotor pump. An inner gear with fewer teeth spins inside an outer gear with more teeth. As they rotate, the spaces between them expand on the inlet side — sucking oil in — and contract on the outlet side — pushing oil out. Many modern engines use this type because it's compact and can be mounted directly on the crankshaft nose.
>
> Both types are positive displacement — they move a fixed volume of oil per revolution. More RPM means more flow. To prevent over-pressure at high RPM, there's always a pressure relief valve that opens and redirects excess oil back to the sump."

**PPT Content:**
```
OIL PUMP TYPES

1. EXTERNAL GEAR PUMP
   - Two meshing spur gears in a housing
   - Oil trapped between gear teeth and housing wall
   - Carried from inlet to outlet
   - Very reliable, steady output
   - Common in older and heavy-duty engines

2. GEROTOR / INTERNAL ROTOR PUMP
   - Inner rotor (fewer teeth) inside outer rotor (more teeth)
   - Expanding cavities create suction (inlet)
   - Contracting cavities create pressure (outlet)
   - Compact — mounts directly on crankshaft
   - Common in modern passenger car engines

BOTH types: Positive displacement
(fixed volume per revolution — more RPM = more flow)

PRESSURE RELIEF VALVE:
Opens at ~4-5 bar to prevent over-pressure
Diverts excess oil back to sump
```

#### Slide 8: Oil Filter — Full-Flow and Bypass
**Visual:** Cross-section of a spin-on oil filter showing the filter media (pleated paper), anti-drainback valve, bypass valve, centre tube, and sealing gasket. Inset shows a cartridge-type filter for comparison. Diagram of full-flow vs. bypass filtering concepts.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "After the pump, oil passes through the filter. The standard system is full-flow filtration — meaning ALL the oil goes through the filter before reaching the engine. The filter media is pleated paper or synthetic, typically catching particles down to 20-40 microns. There's a bypass valve built into the filter or the filter housing. Why? Because if the filter gets clogged — or the oil is ice-cold and too thick to pass through — you'd rather have unfiltered oil reaching the bearings than no oil at all. So the bypass valve opens and lets oil skip the filter media. That's a safety mechanism.
>
> Some engines add a bypass filtration circuit — a smaller secondary filter that processes only a fraction of the oil flow but filters much finer, down to 5 microns. Over time, all the oil passes through it.
>
> You'll see two physical formats: spin-on canister filters that screw directly onto the engine, and cartridge filters that sit inside a reusable housing. The industry is moving toward cartridge because they generate less waste — you only replace the paper element, not a metal canister."

**PPT Content:**
```
OIL FILTER TYPES

FULL-FLOW FILTER (standard — all oil passes through)
- Filter media: pleated cellulose or synthetic
- Filtration: 20-40 microns
- Anti-drainback valve: prevents oil draining from filter at shutdown
- Bypass valve: opens if filter clogs or oil too cold
  (unfiltered oil > no oil)

BYPASS FILTER (secondary — some engines)
- Processes ~10% of oil flow at a time
- Finer filtration: 5-10 microns
- All oil eventually passes through over multiple cycles

PHYSICAL FORMATS:
Spin-on canister: Screw directly onto engine block
  - Includes metal housing, media, valves in one unit
  - Generates metal waste

Cartridge / element: Paper element inside reusable housing
  - Only replace the element
  - Less waste, more common in modern engines
  - Requires proper O-ring sealing on housing cap
```

#### Slide 9: Oil Galleries and Bearing Lubrication
**Visual:** Transparent engine block view showing the main oil gallery running the length of the block, with cross-drilled passages to each main bearing cap. Include a magnified view of a crankshaft journal showing the oil feed hole, the oil wedge/film, and the bearing shell.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Once oil passes the filter, it enters the main oil gallery — a drilled passage running the length of the engine block. From this gallery, smaller passages branch off to each crankshaft main bearing. The crankshaft itself is drilled — oil enters through holes in the main journals, travels through internal passages, and exits at the connecting rod journals. This is how the con-rod bearings get their oil.
>
> At each bearing, the magic of hydrodynamic lubrication happens. As the shaft spins, it drags oil into a wedge-shaped gap. This pressurised wedge lifts the shaft off the bearing surface. Metal never touches metal — the shaft literally floats on oil. But this only works if there's enough oil pressure and the oil is the right viscosity. Too thin, and the film collapses. Too thick, and it can't flow into the gap fast enough.
>
> From the main gallery, oil also feeds up to the cylinder head — camshaft bearings, hydraulic lifters, and the timing chain tensioner. If you have a turbocharger, it gets a dedicated feed line from the block. That turbo shaft spins at over 100,000 RPM — it needs constant oil or it destroys itself in seconds."

**PPT Content:**
```
OIL GALLERIES & BEARING LUBRICATION

MAIN OIL GALLERY
- Drilled passage running length of engine block
- Typically 10-15 mm diameter
- Operating pressure: 2-5 bar at idle, 4-7 bar at speed

BEARING FEED PATH:
Main gallery -> Cross-drilled passages -> Main bearing caps
  -> Through drilled crankshaft -> Con-rod bearings

HYDRODYNAMIC LUBRICATION:
- Rotating shaft drags oil into a wedge
- Wedge pressure lifts shaft off bearing surface
- Shaft "floats" on oil film (0.025-0.050 mm thick)
- REQUIRES: correct pressure + correct viscosity

ADDITIONAL FEED POINTS:
- Camshaft bearings (via cylinder head passages)
- Hydraulic valve lifters / lash adjusters
- Timing chain tensioner
- Turbocharger shaft bearing (dedicated feed line)
- Piston cooling jets (spray oil on piston underside)
```

#### Slide 10: Oil Cooler
**Visual:** Two types of oil cooler illustrated — (1) oil-to-coolant heat exchanger (plate type, mounted on engine block near oil filter) and (2) oil-to-air cooler (finned radiator, mounted in front airflow). Include a simple temperature flow diagram.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Oil absorbs a lot of heat — from bearings, pistons, and especially the turbocharger. If oil gets too hot, it breaks down. The molecules literally crack apart, viscosity drops, and it loses its ability to protect. The ideal operating range for engine oil is 90 to 120 degrees Celsius. Above 130, degradation accelerates rapidly.
>
> To keep oil temperature in check, many engines have an oil cooler. The most common type is an oil-to-coolant heat exchanger — a small plate-type unit mounted on the engine block, usually near the oil filter. Engine coolant flows on one side, oil on the other, and heat transfers from the hotter fluid to the cooler one. Performance vehicles sometimes use an oil-to-air cooler — essentially a small radiator for oil, mounted in the front airflow.
>
> Not every engine has an oil cooler. Naturally aspirated engines with modest power outputs may not need one. But turbocharged engines, diesel engines, and anything towing heavy loads almost always have one."

**PPT Content:**
```
OIL COOLER

PURPOSE: Keep oil temperature in optimal range

Ideal oil temperature: 90-120 degC
Above 130 degC: accelerated degradation, viscosity loss
Above 150 degC: additive breakdown, varnish formation

TYPE 1: OIL-TO-COOLANT (most common)
- Plate heat exchanger on engine block
- Oil and coolant flow through alternating channels
- Compact, reliable, uses existing coolant circuit

TYPE 2: OIL-TO-AIR (performance / heavy-duty)
- Finned radiator in front airflow
- Standalone cooling, independent of coolant temp
- Larger, requires plumbing and mounting

WHICH ENGINES HAVE THEM?
- Turbocharged engines: almost always
- Diesel engines: almost always
- Performance engines: always
- Small NA engines: often not fitted
```

#### Slide 11: SAE Oil Grades — Multi-Grade Explained
**Visual:** A large chart showing SAE grades. Left column shows winter (W) grades (0W, 5W, 10W, 15W, 20W) with corresponding cold-crank temperatures. Right column shows hot grades (20, 30, 40, 50) with kinematic viscosity at 100 degC. A large "5W-30" in the centre is broken apart: "5W = cold behaviour" and "30 = hot behaviour". Include a visual of oil flow at -30 degC vs +100 degC.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's talk about oil grades — the numbers on the bottle. You've all seen things like 5W-30 or 0W-20. What do they mean?
>
> The SAE — Society of Automotive Engineers — grades oil by viscosity, which is resistance to flow. Think of it as thickness. Water has low viscosity. Honey has high viscosity. Engine oil needs to be thin enough to flow at minus 30 degrees on a winter morning, and thick enough to protect at 150 degrees inside a running engine.
>
> A multi-grade oil like 5W-30 has two numbers. The first — 5W — is the winter grade. The 'W' literally stands for Winter. It tells you how the oil behaves when cold. 0W flows best in extreme cold. 10W is thicker at cold temperatures. The second number — 30 — is the hot grade. It tells you the viscosity at 100 degrees Celsius. A higher number means thicker at operating temperature.
>
> So 0W-20 is very thin in the cold AND very thin when hot — designed for modern, tight-tolerance engines seeking maximum fuel economy. 10W-40 is thicker both cold and hot — better for older engines with larger tolerances or high-heat applications."

**PPT Content:**
```
SAE OIL GRADES — MULTI-GRADE EXPLAINED

Example: 5W-30

  5W            30
  |              |
  WINTER grade   HOT grade
  (cold flow)    (viscosity at 100 degC)

WINTER GRADES (cold cranking ability):
  0W  = Flows at -35 degC (best cold flow)
  5W  = Flows at -30 degC
  10W = Flows at -25 degC
  15W = Flows at -20 degC

HOT GRADES (protection at operating temp):
  20 = Thinnest (fuel economy focus)
  30 = Standard
  40 = Thicker (high load, high heat)
  50 = Thickest (heavy duty, racing)

COMMON MODERN GRADES:
  0W-20: Hybrids, modern fuel-efficient engines
  5W-30: Most common passenger car grade
  5W-40: European turbocharged engines
  10W-40: Older engines, warm climates
```

#### Slide 12: API and ACEA Ratings
**Visual:** Two certification stamp images: the API "donut" (showing API SN PLUS, SAE grade, Energy Conserving) and the ACEA logo. A table comparing key API categories (SP, SN) and ACEA categories (A3/B4, C3, C5) with their applications.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The SAE grade tells you about viscosity. But what about the quality and the additive package? That's where API and ACEA ratings come in.
>
> API — the American Petroleum Institute — rates oil with letter codes. For petrol engines, the current top grade is API SP. For diesel, it's CK-4. The letters go up alphabetically as standards improve — SP is better than SN, which was better than SM. An API SP oil meets the latest requirements for turbo protection, timing chain wear, and low-speed pre-ignition prevention.
>
> In Europe, ACEA ratings are more important. ACEA — the European Automobile Manufacturers' Association — uses codes like A3/B4 for standard engines, C3 for engines with diesel particulate filters, and C5 for the latest low-viscosity, fuel-saving formulations. The key difference: ACEA C-rated oils are low-SAPS — low Sulphated Ash, Phosphorus, and Sulphur — to protect exhaust aftertreatment systems like DPFs and catalytic converters.
>
> Here's what matters in practice: always use the oil specification the manufacturer calls for. Not just the viscosity grade — the performance rating too. A BMW might require BMW LL-04, which corresponds to ACEA C3. Use a cheap oil that doesn't meet that spec, and you risk clogging the DPF."

**PPT Content:**
```
API & ACEA RATINGS — OIL QUALITY STANDARDS

API (American Petroleum Institute):
  Petrol: SP (current) > SN > SM > SL > ...
  Diesel: CK-4 (current) > CJ-4 > CI-4 > ...
  Higher letter = newer, better protection

ACEA (European Automobile Manufacturers):
  A/B category: Standard petrol/diesel
    A3/B4 = High performance, extended drain
  C category: Catalyst/DPF compatible (LOW-SAPS)
    C3 = Mid-SAPS, suits most Euro engines with DPF
    C5 = Low-SAPS + low viscosity (fuel economy)

LOW-SAPS = Low Sulphated Ash, Phosphorus, Sulphur
  -> Protects DPF and catalytic converter from poisoning

MANUFACTURER SPECIFICATIONS (always follow these):
  BMW LL-04, VW 504.00/507.00, MB 229.52
  Dexos1 Gen3 (GM), WSS-M2C961-A1 (Ford)

RULE: Match the OEM spec, not just the viscosity grade
```

#### Slide 13: Synthetic vs Mineral vs Semi-Synthetic
**Visual:** Three oil bottles side by side: Mineral (basic label), Semi-Synthetic (mid-range label), Full Synthetic (premium label). Below each, a molecular structure diagram — mineral showing irregular, varied chain lengths; synthetic showing uniform, engineered molecules. A comparison table below.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Now let's talk about the base oil itself. All engine oil starts with a base stock — the bulk liquid — plus an additive package.
>
> Mineral oil is refined directly from crude petroleum. The molecules are naturally varied in size and shape. It works, but it's less uniform, breaks down faster at high temperatures, and flows less freely in extreme cold. It's the cheapest option.
>
> Full synthetic oil is chemically engineered. The molecules are uniform, purpose-built for performance. Synthetic flows better at cold temperatures, resists breakdown at high temperatures, lasts longer between changes, and offers better fuel economy. It costs more — roughly double or triple the price per litre.
>
> Semi-synthetic is a blend — typically 20-30% synthetic mixed with mineral. A compromise on price and performance.
>
> Nearly all modern manufacturers specify full synthetic oil. The engine is designed around its properties — tighter tolerances, thinner oil films, longer service intervals. Using mineral oil in an engine designed for synthetic is asking for trouble."

**PPT Content:**
```
BASE OIL TYPES

MINERAL OIL (Group I/II)
- Refined from crude petroleum
- Irregular molecule sizes
- Lower cost (~$5-8 per litre)
- Shorter service life (5,000-8,000 km)
- Less stable at extreme temperatures

SEMI-SYNTHETIC (Group III blend)
- Mix of mineral + synthetic base stocks
- Mid-range performance and price
- Service life: 8,000-12,000 km
- Suitable for older vehicles, moderate use

FULL SYNTHETIC (Group IV/V — PAO, Ester)
- Chemically engineered molecules
- Uniform size, superior performance
- Higher cost (~$12-25 per litre)
- Extended service life (15,000-30,000 km)
- Best cold flow, high-temp stability, fuel economy

MODERN VEHICLES: Almost all specify full synthetic
Using wrong base type = voided warranty + accelerated wear
```

#### Slide 14: Oil Sensors — Pressure, Temperature, Level
**Visual:** Three sensor images with their locations on the engine: (1) oil pressure sensor on the main gallery (typically near the oil filter), (2) oil temperature sensor (often in the sump or oil cooler), (3) oil level sensor (capacitive or float type in the sump). Each with a sample dashboard warning light or gauge reading.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The engine monitors its own oil with three types of sensors. First, the oil pressure sensor — sometimes called the oil pressure switch. It's usually screwed into the main oil gallery or the block near the oil filter. The basic type is a simple switch: if pressure drops below a threshold — usually around 0.5 bar — it triggers the red oil pressure warning light. More advanced versions are analog senders that feed a gauge or the ECU with continuous pressure data.
>
> If that red oil pressure light comes on while driving, you stop the engine immediately. Not in a minute. Not when you find a safe place. Now. Low oil pressure means bearings are about to be destroyed.
>
> Second, the oil temperature sensor. This is often in the sump or integrated into the oil cooler. The ECU uses it for engine management — adjusting fuelling and timing during warm-up, and triggering warnings if oil overheats.
>
> Third, the oil level sensor. Modern cars check oil level electronically — a capacitive or ultrasonic sensor in the sump measures the level and displays it on the dashboard. Older cars rely entirely on the dipstick. Even on cars with electronic level sensors, the dipstick remains the technician's primary tool."

**PPT Content:**

#### Slide 15: What Happens When Oil Goes Wrong
**Visual:** Four quadrant image: (1) sludge-clogged engine internals, (2) scored bearing shells from oil starvation, (3) a clogged DPF cut open showing ash buildup, (4) milky oil on a dipstick showing coolant contamination. Each quadrant labeled with the cause.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Now the consequences. What happens when you use the wrong oil, skip oil changes, or ignore warning signs?
>
> First scenario: extended oil change intervals or cheap oil in a turbocharged engine. The oil oxidises, forms varnish and sludge. Sludge blocks oil galleries and the pickup strainer. Oil flow drops. Bearings starve. The engine needs a rebuild or a replacement — thousands of euros.
>
> Second scenario: wrong viscosity or low oil level. The oil film at the bearings gets too thin. Metal touches metal. Bearing shells score, overheat, and eventually weld to the crankshaft journal. That's a seizure. Engine is scrap.
>
> Third scenario: using a non-low-SAPS oil in an engine with a diesel particulate filter. The high ash content in the oil burns in the combustion chamber, passes into the exhaust, and accumulates in the DPF. Unlike soot, ash cannot be burned off during regeneration. The DPF clogs prematurely — replacement costs over a thousand euros.
>
> Fourth: coolant leaking into the oil — head gasket failure. The oil turns milky, loses its lubricating properties, and corrosion attacks bearing surfaces. Catching this early on a dipstick check can save the engine."

**PPT Content:**
```
WHEN OIL GOES WRONG

SLUDGE BUILDUP (wrong oil / missed changes)
- Oil oxidises -> varnish -> sludge
- Blocks galleries and pickup strainer
- Result: oil starvation, bearing failure
- Cost: engine rebuild or replacement

BEARING DAMAGE (wrong viscosity / low level)
- Oil film too thin to support bearing load
- Metal-to-metal contact -> scoring -> seizure
- Result: crankshaft and bearings destroyed
- Cost: engine replacement

DPF DAMAGE (non-low-SAPS oil in DPF-equipped engine)
- High ash passes into exhaust
- Ash accumulates in DPF (cannot regenerate)
- Result: premature DPF clogging
- Cost: DPF replacement (1,000-3,000 EUR)

COOLANT CONTAMINATION (head gasket failure)
- Coolant mixes with oil -> milky appearance
- Lubrication fails, corrosion attacks bearings
- Result: engine damage if not caught early
- Detection: dipstick check (milky, grey, mayonnaise)
```

#### Slide 21: Oil Change — Step-by-Step (Part 1: Drain)
**Visual:** Detailed annotated photos of the drain procedure — positioning the drain pan offset from the plug (oil arcs outward), correct socket on the drain plug, the moment of plug removal with oil flow, and inspecting the removed plug and washer.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Step one: warm the engine. Run it for 3-5 minutes — warm oil drains faster and carries more contaminants with it. Don't get it fully hot; you'll burn yourself. Step two: raise the vehicle on the lift to a comfortable working height. Step three: position the drain pan. Here's a tip — put it slightly behind the drain plug, not directly under it. When you pull the plug, the oil arcs outward before it drips straight down.
>
> Step four: remove the drain plug. Loosen with a socket, then unscrew the last few turns by hand. Pull the plug away quickly and let the oil drain. >
> Step five: let it drain for at least five minutes. While it drains, we deal with the filter."

**PPT Content:**
```
OIL CHANGE STEP-BY-STEP — PART 1: DRAIN

1. WARM THE ENGINE (3-5 min idle)

   - Do NOT get fully hot (burn risk)

2. RAISE VEHICLE on lift (safe working height)

3. POSITION DRAIN PAN
```

#### Slide 22: Oil Change — Step-by-Step (Part 2: Filter & Fill)
**Visual:** Photos showing: removing old filter (spin-on with filter wrench, or cartridge cap removal), prepping new filter (applying thin film of oil to gasket), installing new drain plug with torque wrench, pouring new oil through funnel, checking level on dipstick.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Step six: remove the old filter. For a spin-on type, use a filter wrench — strap wrench or cap type. It may be tight. Oil will spill from the filter when you remove it, so keep the drain pan positioned. For a cartridge type, unscrew the housing cap, pull the old element out, and inspect the housing for debris. Clean the sealing surface.
>
> Step seven: prepare the new filter. For spin-on filters, apply a thin film of fresh oil to the rubber gasket — this prevents it from sticking and ensures a proper seal. For cartridge types, install new O-rings on the housing cap — they're always included with the element.
>
> Step eight: install the drain plug with a NEW sealing washer. Tighten to the manufacturer's specified torque — typically 20-30 Nm. Overtightening strips the sump thread, and that's an expensive repair.
>
> Step nine: install the new filter. Spin-on: hand-tighten only — typically three-quarters of a turn after the gasket contacts the surface. Cartridge: insert element, replace cap, torque to spec.
>
> Step ten: fill with the correct oil. Use a funnel. Pour the specified quantity minus half a litre. Start the engine, let it idle for 30 seconds — this fills the filter and galleries. Stop the engine, wait two minutes, then check the dipstick. Top up to the correct level."

#### Slide 24: Activity 4 — Dipstick Inspection & Diagnosis
**Visual:** Five dipstick photos side by side, each labelled: (A) Normal — amber/brown, correct level; (B) Overfilled — oil above max mark; (C) Dark black — overdue for change; (D) Milky/grey — coolant contamination; (E) Foamy — possible head gasket or overfill. **Instructor Narration:**
> "Final practical activity. The dipstick is the simplest and most powerful diagnostic tool for engine oil. Pull it out, wipe it, reinsert, pull again, and read. You're checking five things: level, colour, smell, texture, and contamination.
>
> Level: between the min and max marks. Below min is dangerous. Above max can cause foaming and damage. Colour: fresh oil is amber or light brown. As it works, it darkens. Texture: rub it between your fingers. It should feel smooth and slippery. Gritty means contamination. Contamination: milky grey or 'mayonnaise' on the dipstick or under the oil filler cap means coolant is mixing with oil — almost certainly a head gasket issue. Foam or bubbles mean air is entering the oil — overfill, wrong oil level, or internal leak.
>
> I have five dipstick samples prepared. Each of you will inspect all five and write down your diagnosis. Go."

**PPT Content:**
```

2. Read oil level against MIN and MAX marks
3. Assess colour, smell, texture, contamination

WHAT TO CHECK:

LEVEL:
  Between MIN and MAX = correct
  Below MIN = top up immediately, investigate consumption
  Above MAX = overfilled, drain excess (foaming risk)

COLOUR:
```

## Key Takeaways

1. Engine oil performs five critical functions: lubricate, cool, seal, clean, protect
2. The oil system is a continuous loop: sump -> pump -> filter -> galleries -> bearings -> sump
3. SAE grades have two numbers — cold flow (W) and hot viscosity — both matter
4. API/ACEA ratings and OEM specifications are non-negotiable — always match the manufacturer requirement
5. Wrong oil specification causes sludge, bearing wear, and DPF damage — expensive failures
6. The dipstick is a diagnostic tool — level, colour, smell, foam, and contamination tell a story
7. A red oil pressure warning light means stop the engine immediately — seconds matter
