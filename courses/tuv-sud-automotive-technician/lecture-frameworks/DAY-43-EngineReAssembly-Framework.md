# Day 43: Engine Re-Assembly
## Instructor-Led Story Framework for PPT Preparation

**Duration:** 180 minutes (27 min theory + 153 min practical)
**Approach:** Guided Assembly — Every Step Verified Before the Next
**PPT Target:** 22 slides
**Week:** 9 of 11 — "The Surgeon: Engine & Turbo" (Optional)

---

## 🎯 Session Objectives (For Instructor)

By the end of this session, learners will be able to:
- Follow the correct engine assembly sequence (lower end first → upper end)
- Install main bearings and rod bearings with correct crush and orientation
- Install pistons with ring compressor, correct ring gap staggering and orientation
- Apply torque sequences correctly for main caps, rod caps, and cylinder head
- Install gaskets (MLS head gasket) with correct alignment and no sealant (unless specified)
- Apply RTV sealant correctly (continuous bead, correct thickness, cure time awareness)
- Verify piston-to-wall clearance and bearing clearance after assembly

---

**From Day 41:** Disassembled the engine methodically — tagged, bagged, documented.
**From Day 42:** Inspected and measured every component — bore, journals, cam lobes, ring gaps. Made go/no-go decisions.

**Bridge:** "Two days ago you took it apart. Yesterday you measured everything. Today you put it back together — and every step must be right. A single bearing shell installed backwards, a single ring gap not staggered, a single torque value missed — and this engine fails on first start."

#### Slide 2: Assembly Sequence Overview
**Visual:** Flowchart showing the bottom-up assembly order

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The assembly sequence is the reverse of disassembly, and we work from the bottom up. First: crankshaft and main bearings into the block. Then: pistons and connecting rods with rod bearings. Then: timing system — chain or belt, tensioner, guides. Then: cylinder head with head gasket. Then: valve train — cams, lifters, rockers. Then: external components — intake, exhaust manifolds, accessories. Each stage has a verification step before we proceed."

**PPT Content:**
```
ASSEMBLY SEQUENCE — BOTTOM UP

1. CRANKSHAFT & MAIN BEARINGS → Verify clearance
2. PISTONS & CON-RODS → Verify ring gaps, orientation
3. TIMING SYSTEM → Verify alignment marks
4. CYLINDER HEAD & HEAD GASKET → Verify torque sequence
5. VALVE TRAIN → Verify clearances
6. EXTERNAL COMPONENTS → Manifolds, accessories
7. FINAL CHECKS → Rotate by hand, verify freedom

RULE: Verify each stage BEFORE proceeding to next
```

#### Slide 3: Cleanliness Is Non-Negotiable
**Visual:** Clean assembly bench with lint-free cloths, assembly lube, fresh oil

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before we touch a single part: cleanliness. Every mating surface must be spotless — no old gasket material, no debris, no fingerprints on bearing surfaces. Use lint-free cloths. Oil every bearing shell with assembly lube before installation. A single grain of sand between a bearing and a journal creates a hot spot that fails in 500 km."

**PPT Content:**
```
CLEANLINESS RULES

• Clean EVERY mating surface (scraper + solvent + lint-free cloth)
• Oil ALL bearing shells with assembly lube before fitting
• Oil ALL piston rings and cylinder bores before insertion
• Keep the work area immaculate — no rags inside the engine
• Wear nitrile gloves — fingerprints = corrosion initiation

"One grain of dirt = one point of failure"
```

### **DEVELOPMENT: Assembly Techniques** (Slides 4-10, ~17 minutes)

#### Slide 4: Main Bearing Installation
**Visual:** Bearing shell being placed into block saddle — alignment tab visible

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Main bearings first. Each shell has an alignment tab that locates it in the saddle — it can only go one way. Upper shells (in the block) typically have an oil hole aligned with the block's oil gallery. Lower shells (in the cap) typically don't. Never mix them. Apply assembly lube generously to the bearing surface. Lower the crankshaft carefully — don't drag it across the bearing surfaces."

**PPT Content:**
```
MAIN BEARING INSTALLATION

1. Clean block saddles and bearing caps thoroughly
2. Place UPPER shells in block (oil hole aligns with gallery)
3. Place LOWER shells in caps (no oil hole, tab locates)
4. Apply assembly lube to ALL bearing surfaces
5. Lower crankshaft gently into position
6. Install main caps in correct order and orientation
7. Torque to spec in sequence (center outward)
8. Check crankshaft rotates freely by hand

VERIFY: Bearing clearance (Plastigage if not done on Day 42)
VERIFY: Crankshaft end-float with dial gauge
```

---

#### Slide 5: Piston Ring Installation & Gap Staggering
**Visual:** Piston with rings installed, showing gap stagger positions around the piston

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Piston rings go on in order: oil control ring first (bottom), then second compression ring, then top compression ring. Each ring has an orientation — usually a dot or 'TOP' marking that faces upward. The critical step is gap staggering. If all ring gaps align, you get a direct blow-by path. Stagger them 120 degrees apart — and keep them away from the thrust face and pin bore."

**PPT Content:**
```
PISTON RING INSTALLATION

ORDER: Oil ring (bottom) → 2nd compression → 1st compression (top)
ORIENTATION: Dot or 'TOP' marking faces UP toward combustion chamber

GAP STAGGERING (viewed from above):
• Top ring gap: 0° position (e.g., front of engine)
• 2nd ring gap: 120° from top ring
• Oil ring gap: 120° from 2nd ring (240° from top)

⚠ Gaps must NOT align — creates blow-by path
⚠ Gaps must NOT sit on thrust face or pin bore axis
⚠ Check end gap was verified on Day 42 (0.15-0.40mm typical)
```

---

#### Slide 6: Piston Installation with Ring Compressor
**Visual:** Ring compressor being used to slide piston into bore, wooden hammer handle tapping

**Instructor Narration:**
> "With rings installed and gaps staggered, oil the bore and the rings. Slide the ring compressor over the piston and tighten it — the rings must be fully compressed with no gaps showing. Position the piston over the bore — correct orientation (often an arrow pointing toward the front of the engine). Tap the piston into the bore using the wooden end of a hammer — firm, controlled taps. If it resists, STOP. Don't force it. Something is wrong — a ring has popped out, or the compressor slipped."

**PPT Content:**
```
PISTON INSTALLATION

1. Oil bore walls and piston rings generously
2. Fit ring compressor over piston — tighten snug
3. Verify correct piston orientation (arrow → front of engine)
4. Position assembly over bore
5. Tap firmly with wooden hammer handle — controlled force
6. Guide connecting rod onto crankshaft journal
7. Install rod bearing shell (tab located)
8. Install rod cap — correct orientation (numbers match)
9. Torque rod bolts to spec

⚠ If it RESISTS → STOP. Never force. Investigate.
⚠ Protect rod journals from rod bolt contact (rubber hose over bolts)
```

---

#### Slide 7: Torque Sequences — Head Bolts
**Visual:** Cylinder head bolt torque sequence diagram (numbered, typically center-outward spiral)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Cylinder head bolts follow a specific sequence — always center outward, in a spiral or star pattern. This ensures even clamping force across the head gasket. Most modern engines use torque-to-yield head bolts: you torque to a base value, then rotate an additional angle — often 90 degrees in two stages. These bolts are designed to stretch into their plastic zone for maximum clamping. They are SINGLE USE — never reuse TTY bolts."

**PPT Content:**
```
HEAD BOLT TORQUE SEQUENCE

PATTERN: Center-outward spiral (always per manufacturer diagram)

TYPICAL TORQUE-TO-YIELD PROCEDURE:
Stage 1: 30 Nm (all bolts in sequence)
Stage 2: 60 Nm (all bolts in sequence)
Stage 3: + 90° angle (all bolts in sequence)
Stage 4: + 90° angle (all bolts in sequence — if specified)

RULES:
• Follow EXACT manufacturer sequence — no shortcuts
• TTY bolts are SINGLE USE — always fit new
• Clean bolt holes — debris gives false torque readings
• Oil bolt threads if specified (dry vs wet torque matters!)
• Head gasket alignment: dowel pins locate it — do NOT use sealant
  unless manufacturer specifically requires it
```

---

#### Slide 8: MLS Head Gasket Installation
**Visual:** MLS gasket being positioned on block with alignment dowels visible

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The MLS — Multi-Layer Steel — head gasket is a precision component. It seals combustion gases at extreme pressures, coolant passages, and oil passages simultaneously. The block and head surfaces must be absolutely flat — check with a straightedge and feeler gauge. The gasket locates on alignment dowels. Never apply sealant to an MLS gasket unless the manufacturer specifically calls for it — sealant can compress and cause blow-through."

**PPT Content:**
```
MLS HEAD GASKET — INSTALLATION RULES

PRE-INSTALLATION:
✅ Block deck surface flat (check with straightedge + 0.05mm feeler)
✅ Head surface flat (same check)
✅ All bolt holes cleaned and dry
✅ Alignment dowels present and undamaged
✅ Gasket orientation correct (usually marked 'TOP' or 'FRONT')

INSTALLATION:
1. Lower gasket onto dowels — DO NOT slide
2. Lower head onto dowels — DO NOT slide
3. Install bolts hand-tight
4. Torque in sequence per manufacturer spec

⚠ NO SEALANT on MLS gasket (unless manufacturer specifies)
⚠ Surface finish is critical — never use abrasive on MLS surface
```

---

#### Slide 9: Sealant Application — Timing Cover & Oil Pan
**Visual:** RTV sealant bead being applied to an oil pan flange — continuous, 3mm diameter

**Instructor Narration:**
> "For surfaces that use RTV sealant instead of a gasket — timing cover, oil pan, cam cover on some engines — the bead must be continuous, about 3mm diameter, running inside the bolt holes. More is NOT better. Excess sealant squeezes inside and can block oil pick-up screens or galleries. Apply the bead, bolt up within the sealant's open time (usually 10-15 minutes), and let it cure before adding oil."

**PPT Content:**
```
RTV SEALANT APPLICATION

BEAD: 3mm continuous, INSIDE bolt holes
OPEN TIME: 10-15 minutes (bolt up before it skins over)
CURE TIME: 24 hours (fully cure before adding oil or starting)

COMMON RTV USES:
• Oil pan to block
• Timing cover to block
• Cam/valve cover (some engines)
• Thermostat housing (some engines)

⚠ TOO MUCH = blocks oil galleries or pick-up screen
⚠ TOO LITTLE = leak within 1,000 km
⚠ Apply to CLEAN, DRY surfaces only
⚠ Use the correct colour (black for oil, grey general, red high-temp)
```

---

#### Slide 10: Timing System & Final Assembly Checks
**Visual:** Timing chain installed with alignment marks highlighted, crankshaft and camshaft sprockets aligned

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Timing installation is where Week 3 Day 14 knowledge becomes critical. Align the crankshaft to TDC. Align the camshaft marks. Install the chain or belt with correct tension. Verify marks AGAIN after tensioner is released. One tooth off and you have a misfiring engine — or on an interference engine, bent valves. After timing, install the valve train, set clearances if needed, install external components, and do the final check: rotate the crankshaft two full turns by hand. If it's smooth with no resistance — you're good. If it catches — something is wrong. Investigate before you EVER attempt a start."

**PPT Content:**
```
TIMING SYSTEM INSTALLATION

1. Set crankshaft to TDC on compression stroke (cylinder 1)
2. Align camshaft timing marks per manufacturer diagram
3. Install chain/belt with marks aligned
4. Install tensioner — verify marks STILL aligned after release
5. Rotate crankshaft 2 full turns by hand
6. Re-check all timing marks — must still be aligned

FINAL ASSEMBLY CHECKS:
✅ Crank rotates freely by hand (2 full turns, no catching)
✅ All external bolts torqued
✅ All sensors connected
✅ No tools or rags left inside
✅ Drain plugs installed
✅ Ready for Day 45 first start preparation
```

#### Slide 17: Final Rotation Check
**Visual:** Hand on crankshaft pulley bolt with breaker bar, rotating smoothly

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The moment of truth. Rotate the crankshaft two complete revolutions by hand using a breaker bar on the crank bolt. It should turn smoothly with even resistance from compression strokes. If it catches, stops, or feels wrong — STOP. Do NOT attempt to start this engine until you've identified the problem. Most commonly: timing off by one tooth (valves hitting pistons on interference engines), or a foreign object left in a cylinder."

**PPT Content:**
```
FINAL ROTATION CHECK

Using breaker bar on crankshaft pulley bolt:
• Rotate 2 FULL turns (720°)
• Should feel smooth, even compression resistance
• Should NOT catch, bind, or feel different from normal

IF IT CATCHES → STOP
• Do NOT start the engine
• Most likely: timing off by 1 tooth
• Or: foreign object in cylinder
• Or: incorrect piston installed (wrong compression height)

This check costs 30 seconds. Skipping it can cost the engine.
```

## 🔑 Key Takeaways

1. Assembly is bottom-up: crankshaft → pistons → timing → head → valve train → externals
2. Every stage has a verification gate — don't proceed without confirmation
3. Bearing shells have orientation — upper vs lower, oil hole alignment
4. Ring gaps must be staggered 120° apart, away from thrust face
5. TTY head bolts are single-use — never reuse
6. The 2-turn rotation check is your final safety net before first start
7. Cleanliness is not optional — one grain of dirt creates one point of failure
