#### Slide 5: Case Study 1 — "My Engine Misfires"

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Misfire. The engine stumbles, shakes, loses power. The check engine light may flash. This is the single most common engine complaint you'll see. And it has the widest range of possible causes.
>
> Let's map them out. Ignition: failed coil-on-plug, worn spark plug with excessive gap, damaged HT lead if equipped. IGNITION:

• Damaged HT lead / poor coil connector

FUEL DELIVERY:
• Clogged fuel injector (no spray or poor pattern)
• Low fuel pressure (weak pump, blocked filter)

AIR SYSTEM:

#### Slide 6: Case Study 1 — Narrowing It Down
**Visual:** Decision tree starting from "P0303 — Cylinder 3 Misfire" with branching test paths

**Instructor Narration:**
> "So the scan tool gives you P0303 — Cylinder 3 misfire detected. That's already valuable because it's cylinder-specific. Now watch how a diagnostic technician narrows it down.
>
> First test: swap the ignition coil from Cylinder 3 with Cylinder 1. Clear codes. Run engine. If the misfire moves to Cylinder 1, the coil is faulty. Done. If it stays on Cylinder 3, the coil is fine — move on.
>
> Second test: swap the fuel injector from Cylinder 3 to another cylinder. Same logic — does the misfire follow? If yes, bad injector.
>
> Third test: compression test on Cylinder 3. If compression is significantly lower than the other cylinders — say 120 PSI versus 170 PSI on the others — you have a mechanical issue. Burnt valve, worn rings, or gasket failure.
>
> Fourth test: listen with a stethoscope or use a smoke machine to find a vacuum leak near Cylinder 3 intake runner.
>
> See the pattern? You're swapping and testing, not guessing and replacing."

**PPT Content:**
```
DIAGNOSING P0303 — CYLINDER 3 MISFIRE

STEP 1: SWAP COIL (Cyl 3 ↔ Cyl 1)
  → Misfire moves to Cyl 1? → FAULTY COIL ✓
  → Stays on Cyl 3? → Coil is OK, continue...

STEP 2: SWAP INJECTOR (Cyl 3 ↔ another)
  → Misfire follows? → FAULTY INJECTOR ✓
  → Stays on Cyl 3? → Injector is OK, continue...

STEP 3: COMPRESSION TEST (Cyl 3 vs others)
  → Low compression on Cyl 3? → MECHANICAL ISSUE ✓
  → Normal compression? → Continue...

STEP 4: VACUUM / AIR LEAK TEST
  → Smoke test at intake runner 3
  → Leak found? → AIR LEAK ✓

STEP 5: TIMING CHECK
  → Check timing marks, chain tension, VVT operation

KEY PRINCIPLE: Swap and test. Never guess and replace.
```

#### Slide 7: Case Study 2 — "My Engine Runs Rough at Idle"
**Visual:** RPM gauge showing unsteady needle fluctuating between 500-900 RPM instead of steady 750 RPM. Five cause categories listed below.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Second scenario. The engine starts fine but idles roughly — you can feel it vibrating, the RPM needle bounces, and it might almost stall at traffic lights. Different from a misfire? Not always — rough idle CAN be caused by a low-grade misfire. But there are also idle-specific causes.
>
> Vacuum leak is the number one cause of rough idle. At idle, the throttle is nearly closed, so engine vacuum is high. Even a tiny leak — a cracked vacuum hose, a failed brake booster check valve, a leaking intake gasket — lets unmetered air in and upsets the mixture.
>
> A dirty throttle body restricts airflow at idle position. Carbon builds up around the throttle plate over time and changes the idle air passage geometry.
>
> Injector imbalance — one injector flowing slightly more or less than the others — creates an uneven combustion across cylinders.
>
> A failed idle air control valve — on older vehicles that have them — directly controls idle speed. If it sticks, idle speed drops or surges."

**PPT Content:**
```
CASE STUDY 2: ROUGH IDLE

MOST COMMON CAUSES:
1. Vacuum leak
   — Cracked hose, intake gasket, brake booster line
   — Unmetered air enters → mixture too lean at idle

2. Dirty throttle body
   — Carbon buildup restricts idle air passage
   — Cleaning often restores smooth idle

3. Injector imbalance
   — One injector flows differently from others
   — Causes uneven cylinder contribution at idle

4. Failed idle air control (IAC) valve
   — Older vehicles: IAC sticks open or closed
   — Modern vehicles: electronic throttle does this job

5. Low-grade misfire
   — Worn spark plug, marginal coil
   — May not trigger DTC until it worsens

DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH:
Scan tool → Check for lean codes (P0171/P0174) → Smoke test
→ Clean throttle body → Check fuel trim balance → Compression test
```

#### Slide 8: Case Study 3 — "My Fuel Economy Is Terrible"
**Visual:** Fuel gauge dropping noticeably fast. Infographic showing six common causes with icons: O2 sensor, MAF sensor, thermostat, dragging brakes, tyre pressure, air filter.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Third scenario — and this is one customers notice because it hits their wallet. 'I used to get 700 kilometres on a tank, now I barely get 500.' What's eating the fuel?
>
> Start with the obvious: tyre pressure. Under-inflated tyres increase rolling resistance. This isn't an engine fault, but it's the easiest thing to check and the most commonly overlooked.
>
> Dragging brakes — a seized caliper slide pin or a binding parking brake cable means the engine has to work harder to maintain speed. You won't feel it driving, but fuel consumption goes up and the wheel gets hot.
>
> Now the engine causes. A failing O2 sensor that reads lean when the mixture is actually correct will cause the ECU to add fuel — the engine runs rich. A contaminated MAF sensor under-reports airflow, so the ECU adds less fuel than needed — engine runs lean — then the O2 sensor correction adds it back. Confused? This is where fuel trim data tells the story.
>
> A thermostat stuck open keeps the engine below operating temperature. The ECU keeps the engine in 'warm-up enrichment' mode — extra fuel — longer than necessary.
>
> A clogged air filter restricts intake airflow and reduces engine efficiency."

**PPT Content:**
```
CASE STUDY 3: POOR FUEL ECONOMY

NON-ENGINE CAUSES (check first!):
• Low tyre pressure → increased rolling resistance
• Dragging brakes → seized caliper, binding cable
• Driving style → aggressive acceleration, high-speed cruising

ENGINE CAUSES:
• Failing O2 sensor → incorrect mixture feedback → runs rich
• Contaminated MAF sensor → under-reports airflow → trim shift
• Thermostat stuck OPEN → engine never reaches operating temp
  → ECU stays in warm-up enrichment (extra fuel)
• Clogged air filter → restricted intake → poor combustion
• Leaking injector → excess fuel into cylinder → washes oil too

DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH:
Read DTCs → Check fuel trims (STFT/LTFT) → Monitor coolant temp
→ Inspect air filter → Test tyre pressure → Check brake temps
```

**Instructor Narration:**

#### Slide 11: Cylinder-Specific Misfire Codes (P0301-P0312)
**Visual:** Diagram of a 4-cylinder engine with each cylinder numbered, next to a table of P030X codes

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The generic misfire code is P0300 — random or multiple cylinder misfire. But the really useful codes are P0301 through P0312, because they tell you WHICH cylinder is misfiring. P0301 is Cylinder 1. P0302 is Cylinder 2. And so on up to P0312 for a 12-cylinder engine.
>
> A single-cylinder code like P0303 means the problem is isolated to that cylinder — swap the coil, swap the injector, test compression on that specific cylinder. A P0300 random misfire code with no cylinder-specific codes usually points to something that affects ALL cylinders equally — like low fuel pressure, a major vacuum leak, or a timing issue.
>
> Always check which cylinders are misfiring. The scan tool misfire counter shows how many misfires each cylinder has had in the last 200 crankshaft revolutions. That's your roadmap."

**PPT Content:**
```
CYLINDER-SPECIFIC MISFIRE CODES

P0300 — Random/Multiple Cylinder Misfire
P0301 — Cylinder 1 Misfire Detected
P0302 — Cylinder 2 Misfire Detected
P0303 — Cylinder 3 Misfire Detected
P0304 — Cylinder 4 Misfire Detected
(continues to P0312 for V12 engines)

SINGLE-CYLINDER CODE (e.g., P0303):
→ Problem isolated to Cylinder 3
→ Swap coil / injector / check compression on THAT cylinder

RANDOM MISFIRE CODE (P0300):
→ Problem affects ALL cylinders
→ Check: fuel pressure, major vacuum leak, timing, fuel quality

MISFIRE COUNTERS (live data):
Cyl 1: 0    Cyl 2: 0    Cyl 3: 47    Cyl 4: 0
→ Cylinder 3 is the problem cylinder
```

#### Slide 12: Fuel Trim Data — Short-Term and Long-Term
**Visual:** Two gauges — one labeled STFT (Short-Term Fuel Trim) showing rapid fluctuation around 0%, and one labeled LTFT (Long-Term Fuel Trim) showing a steady +14%. Color zones: green (-10% to +10%), yellow (-15% to -10% and +10% to +15%), red (beyond +/-15%).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Fuel trims are the most powerful diagnostic data the scan tool gives you — and most beginners ignore them. Let me explain what they mean.
>
> The ECU's job is to maintain a stoichiometric air-fuel ratio — 14.7 parts air to 1 part fuel for gasoline. The O2 sensor tells the ECU if the mixture is rich or lean, and the ECU adjusts the injector pulse width to compensate. That adjustment is the fuel trim.
>
> Short-term fuel trim — STFT — is the immediate, moment-to-moment correction. It fluctuates rapidly as the ECU responds to O2 sensor readings. It should bounce between roughly minus 10 and plus 10 percent.
>
> Long-term fuel trim — LTFT — is the learned average correction. If the STFT is consistently positive — meaning the ECU keeps adding fuel — the LTFT learns that offset and stores it. Think of STFT as fine-tuning and LTFT as the baseline shift.
>
> Here's the diagnostic gold: if LTFT is plus 15 percent or higher, the engine is consistently running lean and the ECU is adding fuel to compensate. What causes a lean condition? Vacuum leak — unmetered air enters. Weak fuel pump — not enough fuel pressure. Dirty injectors — not enough fuel delivered. Failing MAF — under-reports airflow, ECU calculates too little fuel.
>
> Negative fuel trims — the ECU is REMOVING fuel because the engine is running rich. Leaking injector, fuel pressure too high, or a saturated charcoal canister purging too much vapour."

**PPT Content:**
```
FUEL TRIM DATA — YOUR DIAGNOSTIC SUPERPOWER

SHORT-TERM FUEL TRIM (STFT):
• Immediate correction — changes every engine cycle
• Normal range: -10% to +10%
• Positive = ECU adding fuel (engine running lean)
• Negative = ECU removing fuel (engine running rich)

LONG-TERM FUEL TRIM (LTFT):
• Learned average correction — updates slowly
• Normal range: -10% to +10%
• Represents a persistent shift in mixture

INTERPRETING THE DATA:
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│ LTFT > +15%  →  ENGINE IS RUNNING LEAN       │
│   Causes: Vacuum leak, low fuel pressure,    │
│           dirty injectors, MAF fault          │
│                                              │
│ LTFT < -15%  →  ENGINE IS RUNNING RICH       │
│   Causes: Leaking injector, high fuel        │
│           pressure, EVAP purge stuck open    │
│                                              │
│ LTFT between -10% and +10%  →  NORMAL        │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

#### Slide 13: O2 Sensor Waveforms
**Visual:** Oscilloscope-style waveform showing a healthy upstream O2 sensor switching between 0.1V and 0.9V at a normal rate, next to a lazy sensor with slow switching and reduced amplitude

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The upstream O2 sensor — also called the pre-cat sensor — sits in the exhaust manifold before the catalytic converter. A healthy narrowband O2 sensor switches rapidly between about 0.1 volts (lean) and 0.9 volts (rich), cycling several times per second. This rapid switching tells you the sensor is responsive and the ECU is in closed-loop fuel control.
>
> A lazy O2 sensor switches slowly — it takes too long to respond to mixture changes. The ECU can't correct fast enough, so fuel economy drops and emissions rise. A stuck-lean sensor reads low voltage constantly, causing the ECU to add fuel — you'll see high positive fuel trims. A stuck-rich sensor reads high voltage, causing the ECU to remove fuel — negative fuel trims.
>
> The downstream O2 sensor — after the catalytic converter — should show a nearly flat line around 0.5 to 0.7 volts if the cat is working properly. If it mirrors the upstream sensor's switching pattern, the catalytic converter is not converting — it's failed."

**PPT Content:**
```
O2 SENSOR WAVEFORM ANALYSIS

UPSTREAM (Pre-Cat) SENSOR — Mixture Feedback:
Healthy: Rapid switching 0.1V ↔ 0.9V (1-3 Hz)
Lazy:    Slow switching, low amplitude — replace sensor
Stuck lean:  Fixed near 0.1V → ECU adds fuel → +LTFT
Stuck rich:  Fixed near 0.9V → ECU removes fuel → -LTFT

DOWNSTREAM (Post-Cat) SENSOR — Catalyst Monitor:
Healthy cat:  Steady ~0.5-0.7V (flat line)
Failed cat:   Mirrors upstream switching pattern
              DTC: P0420 "Catalyst Efficiency Below Threshold"

WHAT TO LOOK FOR ON SCAN TOOL:
• O2 sensor voltage (Bank 1 Sensor 1, Bank 1 Sensor 2)
• O2 sensor switching rate
• Correlation with fuel trim data
```

#### Slide 14: Connecting Week 1 Measurement Skills to Engine Inspection
**Visual:** Two photos side by side — (1) feeler gauge being used to measure valve clearance on a cylinder head, (2) compression gauge screwed into a spark plug hole reading 175 PSI

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Remember Week 1? Day 4, you learned to use feeler gauges and precision measurement tools. Back then, you were measuring bolts and washers. Now those skills have a real engine application.
>
> Valve clearance measurement uses a feeler gauge — the same technique you practiced. You slide blades between the cam lobe and the valve bucket or rocker arm. If the clearance is too tight, the valve won't close fully and you get compression loss. Too loose, and you get noisy valve train and reduced valve lift.
>
> Compression testing uses a gauge screwed into the spark plug hole. You crank the engine and measure peak pressure in each cylinder. A healthy gasoline engine should read between 150 and 200 PSI, and all cylinders should be within 10% of each other. If one cylinder is significantly low, you've found your mechanical issue — worn rings, burnt valve, or head gasket failure.
>
> A wet compression test — adding a small amount of oil to the cylinder and retesting — helps distinguish rings from valves. If compression rises with oil, the rings are worn. If it stays low, the valves or head gasket are the problem."

**PPT Content:**
```
WEEK 1 SKILLS → ENGINE INSPECTION

FEELER GAUGE → VALVE CLEARANCE CHECK:
• Measure gap between cam lobe and valve follower
• Typical spec: 0.15-0.25 mm intake, 0.25-0.35 mm exhaust
• Too tight: valve doesn't close → compression loss, burning
• Too loose: noisy valve train, reduced lift

COMPRESSION TEST → MECHANICAL HEALTH CHECK:
• Remove spark plug, insert compression gauge
• Crank engine, record peak pressure per cylinder
• Healthy gasoline: 150-200 PSI (10-14 bar)
• All cylinders within 10% of each other

WET COMPRESSION TEST → RINGS vs VALVES:
• Add ~15 ml oil into low-compression cylinder
• Retest compression
• Pressure rises → WORN RINGS (oil temporarily seals)
• Pressure unchanged → VALVE or HEAD GASKET issue

BORE GAUGE → CYLINDER WEAR MEASUREMENT:
• Measures cylinder bore diameter at multiple heights
• Detects taper wear (top wider than bottom)
• Detects ovality (out-of-round wear)
```

## Key Takeaways

1. The same customer symptom can originate from ignition, fuel, air, mechanical, or timing systems — diagnostic skill means considering ALL layers
2. The 7-step diagnostic framework (Verify > Read > List > Test > Confirm > Repair > Verify Fix) prevents expensive guesswork
3. Fuel trim data (STFT and LTFT) is the most powerful diagnostic indicator for air/fuel mixture faults
4. Cylinder-specific misfire codes (P0301-P0312) tell you WHERE to focus; freeze frame data tells you WHEN and under what conditions
5. Week 1 measurement skills (feeler gauges, compression testing) directly apply to engine mechanical diagnosis
6. Week 3 carries the highest exam weight (18 marks) — the diagnostic reasoning taught today is directly tested
