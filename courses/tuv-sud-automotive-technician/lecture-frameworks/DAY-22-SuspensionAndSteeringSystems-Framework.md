#### Slide 4: MacPherson Strut — Construction
**Visual:** Detailed cutaway diagram of a MacPherson strut assembly — show the strut housing (outer tube), piston rod, coil spring mounted on the strut, top mount/bearing, lower control arm connection, steering knuckle integration. Label every component.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The MacPherson strut is the most common front suspension design on the planet. You'll find it on the majority of passenger cars — hatchbacks, sedans, small SUVs. Here's why it's so popular: it combines the shock absorber and the upper suspension mount into one unit. The strut itself IS the upper locating member for the wheel.
>
> Let me walk you through the construction. At the top, you have the top mount — a rubber-bonded bearing that attaches the strut to the body shell and allows it to rotate when you steer. The coil spring sits on a perch welded to the strut housing. Inside the strut housing is a piston and oil — this is your damper. The piston rod comes out the top through a seal. At the bottom, the strut bolts directly to the steering knuckle — usually with two pinch bolts.
>
> The lower control arm connects the knuckle to the subframe, controlling the wheel's fore-aft and lateral position. An anti-roll bar link connects to the strut or control arm to reduce body roll.
>
> Key point: because the strut IS the upper pivot, the entire assembly tilts when you steer. That's why the top mount contains a bearing — it must allow rotation under the full weight of the car."

**PPT Content:**
```
MACPHERSON STRUT — CONSTRUCTION

Components (top to bottom):
1. Top mount (rubber isolator + bearing) -- attaches to body, allows rotation
2. Upper spring seat -- supports the top of the coil spring
3. Coil spring -- absorbs vertical impacts
4. Strut housing (outer tube) -- contains damper oil and piston
5. Piston rod -- connected to top mount, moves through oil
6. Lower spring seat -- welded to strut housing
7. Steering knuckle attachment -- 2 pinch bolts (typical)

Supporting components:
-- Lower control arm (connects knuckle to subframe)
-- Anti-roll bar link (controls body roll)
-- Bump stop (limits compression travel)
-- Dust boot (protects piston rod seal)

THE STRUT IS THE UPPER SUSPENSION MEMBER
It combines the damper + upper locating point in one unit
```

#### Slide 5: MacPherson Strut — Where Used, Pros & Cons
**Visual:** Left side: photos of three vehicles that use MacPherson struts (economy hatchback, mid-range sedan, compact SUV). Right side: pros/cons table.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "You'll find the MacPherson strut on most front-wheel-drive cars and many all-wheel-drive vehicles — especially at the front. It's the default choice for mass-market vehicles. Why? Because it's simple, it's compact, and it's cheap to manufacture. It takes up very little lateral space in the engine bay, which is critical for front-wheel-drive packaging where you need room for the engine, gearbox, and driveshafts.
>
> The downsides? Because the strut acts as both the damper and the upper suspension member, it has to handle bending loads. That limits how much you can tune the geometry independently. The camber changes more during suspension travel than a double wishbone, which means the tyre contact patch angle changes more over bumps. For a family car, that's acceptable. For a sports car or a vehicle where precise handling matters, it's a compromise."

**PPT Content:**
```
MACPHERSON STRUT — APPLICATION

Where used:
-- Most front-wheel-drive vehicles (front axle)
-- Small to mid-size SUVs (front axle)
-- Economy to mid-range vehicles globally
-- Estimated 70%+ of all new passenger cars

PROS:
+ Simple construction, fewer parts
+ Compact — saves lateral space for FWD packaging
+ Lower manufacturing cost
+ Lighter weight than double wishbone
+ Easier to service (spring/strut is one assembly)

CONS:
- Limited geometry control (camber changes with travel)
- Strut handles bending loads (not ideal for high performance)
- Top mount bearing is a wear item
- NVH (road noise transmitted through strut to body)
- Difficult to tune camber independently of other angles
```

#### Slide 6: Double Wishbone — Construction
**Visual:** Detailed diagram of a double wishbone (double A-arm) suspension — upper arm, lower arm, coil spring (separate from damper), shock absorber, ball joints at both arm tips, steering knuckle between the two arms.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Now the double wishbone — also called double A-arm. This is the suspension of choice for performance and luxury vehicles. You'll find it on the front of most BMWs, Mercedes, Audis in the mid-range and above, and on many sports cars.
>
> The key difference from MacPherson: here you have TWO control arms — an upper wishbone and a lower wishbone. The steering knuckle is sandwiched between them, connected at the top and bottom by ball joints. The shock absorber and the coil spring are separate components, and neither one is a structural member of the suspension — they just handle the forces.
>
> Why does this matter? Because the geometry of the two arms can be designed to control exactly how the wheel moves through its travel. You can tune the camber change curve, the roll centre height, the anti-dive geometry — all independently. The tyre stays flatter to the road surface through corners and over bumps. That's why race cars almost universally use double wishbone or multi-link."

**PPT Content:**
```
DOUBLE WISHBONE — CONSTRUCTION

Components:
1. Upper wishbone (A-arm) -- connects to body/subframe via bushings
2. Lower wishbone (A-arm) -- connects to body/subframe via bushings
3. Upper ball joint -- connects upper wishbone to steering knuckle
4. Lower ball joint -- connects lower wishbone to steering knuckle
5. Steering knuckle -- sandwiched between upper and lower arms
6. Coil spring -- sits between lower arm and body (typically)
7. Shock absorber -- separate from spring, mounted between arm and body
8. Anti-roll bar -- connects to lower wishbone

KEY DIFFERENCE FROM MACPHERSON:
-- Two arms control geometry independently
-- Spring and damper are NOT structural members
-- Ball joints at both top and bottom of knuckle
-- Each arm's length and angle can be tuned for precise geometry control
```

#### Slide 7: Double Wishbone — Independent Geometry Control
**Visual:** Animation-style diagram showing the wheel moving through bump travel on a double wishbone — the camber angle stays nearly constant because the arm lengths are designed to compensate. Contrast with a MacPherson strut where camber changes visibly through the same travel.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Here's the real advantage. Watch what happens when this wheel hits a bump. On the double wishbone, the arm geometry is designed so that as the wheel moves up, the camber barely changes. The tyre stays flat on the road — maximum contact patch through the entire suspension travel. Now look at the MacPherson. Same bump. The strut tilts as the wheel moves up, and the camber changes significantly. Part of the tyre starts lifting off the road surface.
>
> In normal driving, you won't notice. But in hard cornering at high speed or emergency avoidance, that difference in contact patch control can be the difference between grip and a skid. That's why performance cars invest in double wishbone or multi-link. The cost is complexity — more arms, more bushings, more ball joints, more things to inspect and replace."

**PPT Content:**
```
DOUBLE WISHBONE — GEOMETRY ADVANTAGE

CAMBER CONTROL THROUGH SUSPENSION TRAVEL:

Double wishbone:
-- Arm lengths are tuned so camber stays near 0 deg through travel
-- Tyre stays flat on the road during bumps and cornering
-- Maximum contact patch maintained at all times

MacPherson strut:
-- Camber changes with strut angle as wheel moves
-- Contact patch narrows during body roll
-- Acceptable for normal driving, compromised at limits

DESIGN PARAMETERS THAT CAN BE TUNED:
-- Camber gain curve (how camber changes with travel)
-- Roll centre height (affects body roll behavior)
-- Anti-dive / anti-squat geometry (braking / acceleration)
-- Scrub radius (steering feel and stability)

More control = more complex = more components to inspect
```

#### Slide 8: Multi-Link Suspension — Construction & Tunability
**Visual:** Rear multi-link suspension diagram — typically 3 to 5 separate links (trailing arm, upper lateral link, lower lateral link, toe link, camber link), all connecting the hub carrier to the subframe. Each link labeled with its function.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Multi-link is an evolution of the double wishbone concept. Instead of two solid A-arms, you have three, four, or five individual links — each controlling one specific aspect of wheel geometry. You'll most commonly find multi-link on the rear axle of modern vehicles, from mid-range and up.
>
> Each link has a specific job. The trailing arm controls the wheel's fore-aft position. The upper and lower lateral links control camber. The toe link controls — you guessed it — toe. By making each link independent, engineers can tune every angle of the rear wheel's geometry separately. That's why multi-link rear suspensions deliver such good ride quality and handling balance.
>
> The trade-off? Five links means five pairs of bushings. Five links means five potential failure points. Five links means more inspection time, more parts cost, and a more complex alignment procedure. But the result is worth it — multi-link rear suspension is the gold standard for ride and handling balance."

**PPT Content:**
```
MULTI-LINK SUSPENSION — CONSTRUCTION

Typically 3-5 separate links per wheel:
1. Trailing arm / integral link -- controls fore-aft position
2. Upper lateral link -- controls upper camber point
3. Lower lateral link -- controls lower camber point
4. Toe link -- controls toe angle independently
5. Camber link (some designs) -- additional camber control

All links connect the hub carrier to a rear subframe via bushings.

MOST COMMON APPLICATION: Rear axle
-- Mid-range to premium vehicles
-- BMW 3/5 Series, Mercedes C/E Class, VW Golf (rear), Audi A4+

TUNABILITY ADVANTAGE:
-- Each link controls ONE geometric parameter
-- Engineers can optimize ride, handling, NVH independently
-- Toe can be set to provide passive rear steer for stability

TRADE-OFF:
-- More links = more bushings = more wear points
-- More complex to inspect and align
-- Higher parts and labor cost for replacement
```

#### Slide 10: Shock Absorbers — Twin-Tube vs Mono-Tube
**Visual:** Cutaway diagrams of both types side by side. Twin-tube: inner and outer tube, base valve, piston. Mono-tube: single tube, floating piston separating oil from gas charge. Label oil flow paths in both.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Now let's go inside the damper. The shock absorber's job is to control the spring's oscillation. Without a damper, the car would bounce up and down like a trampoline after every bump. The damper converts the kinetic energy of the spring into heat by forcing oil through small orifices.
>
> Two main designs. The twin-tube has an inner tube where the piston moves and an outer tube that acts as a reservoir. Oil flows between them through a base valve. It's cheap, reliable, and fits most applications. The downside: under hard use, the oil can aerate — mix with air — which causes temporary damping loss called 'fade.'
>
> The mono-tube has a single tube with a floating piston inside that separates the oil from a pressurized gas charge — usually nitrogen. The gas charge prevents aeration because the oil stays under pressure. Mono-tubes run cooler, respond faster, and are more consistent under hard use. That's why you find them on performance vehicles. But they're more expensive, more sensitive to stone damage on the outer tube, and they require more precise manufacturing."

**PPT Content:**
```
SHOCK ABSORBERS — TWIN-TUBE vs MONO-TUBE

TWIN-TUBE:
-- Inner tube: piston moves here, oil flows through piston valves
-- Outer tube: reservoir, oil flows through base valve
-- Gas charge: low pressure or none
-- Pros: Cost-effective, compact, tolerant of stone damage
-- Cons: Can aerate under hard use (damping fade)
-- Application: 80%+ of standard passenger vehicles

MONO-TUBE:
-- Single tube: piston + floating gas separator piston
-- Gas charge: high-pressure nitrogen (typically 25-30 bar)
-- Oil and gas separated by floating piston
-- Pros: No aeration, better heat dissipation, faster response
-- Cons: More expensive, longer for same travel, stone damage risk
-- Application: Performance vehicles, motorsport, premium OE

BOTH TYPES: Wear over time -- damping force decreases gradually
Typical replacement interval: 80,000-120,000 km (depends on use)
```

#### Slide 11: Springs — Coil, Leaf, and Air
**Visual:** Three images side by side — coil spring on a passenger car strut, leaf spring on a commercial vehicle rear axle, air spring on an SUV rear axle. Label key parts of each.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The spring supports the vehicle's weight and absorbs vertical impacts. Three types you'll encounter.
>
> Coil springs — by far the most common. Wound from spring steel, they sit on a perch on the control arm or strut. The wire diameter, coil diameter, number of coils, and pitch determine the spring rate — how stiff it is. You'll see them on almost every passenger car.
>
> Leaf springs — layered steel strips bolted to the chassis. You'll find these on the rear axle of trucks, vans, and some older SUVs. They're simple, durable, and can handle heavy loads. But they're heavy and provide a less refined ride.
>
> Air springs — a rubber bladder inflated with compressed air. An air compressor, a reservoir, and solenoid valves control the pressure. The ECU adjusts ride height and spring rate based on load and driving mode. You'll find these on premium SUVs, luxury sedans, and commercial vehicles where load leveling is important. When air springs fail — and they do — the vehicle sags on the failed corner. It's a common repair on vehicles over 8-10 years old."

**PPT Content:**
```
SPRINGS — THREE TYPES

COIL SPRINGS:
-- Wound spring steel, most common type
-- Spring rate determined by wire diameter, coil count, pitch
-- Progressive-rate springs: softer initially, firmer under load
-- Application: Almost all passenger cars

LEAF SPRINGS:
-- Layered steel strips (leaves) clamped together
-- Mounted longitudinally, bolted to chassis
-- Simple, strong, handle heavy loads
-- Application: Trucks, vans, commercial vehicles, older SUVs

AIR SPRINGS:
-- Rubber bladder inflated by air compressor
-- ECU controls air pressure via solenoid valves
-- Adjustable ride height and spring rate
-- Requires: compressor, air reservoir, valve block, height sensors
-- Application: Premium SUVs (Range Rover, Audi Q7), luxury sedans
-- Common failure: air leaks, compressor wear, valve block faults

REMEMBER: The spring holds the weight. The damper controls the motion.
Never confuse the two.
```

#### Slide 12: Bushings — Rubber and Polyurethane
**Visual:** Cross-section diagram of a typical rubber control arm bushing — inner metal sleeve, rubber element, outer metal sleeve. Next to it, a worn bushing showing cracking and deformation. Also show a polyurethane bushing for comparison.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Every suspension arm connects to the body or subframe through bushings. A bushing is a simple component — an inner metal sleeve, a rubber element, and an outer metal sleeve. The rubber allows controlled movement while absorbing vibration and noise. Without bushings, every road imperfection would transmit directly into the cabin as a harsh metallic bang.
>
> Rubber bushings are standard from the factory. They're soft, quiet, and provide good NVH isolation — that's Noise, Vibration, and Harshness. Over time, the rubber deteriorates — it cracks, splits, becomes soft, or separates from the metal sleeves. When that happens, the arm has too much movement, alignment changes under load, and you hear clunking over bumps.
>
> Polyurethane bushings are a performance aftermarket option. They're harder, more durable, and maintain geometry better under load. But they transmit more road noise and vibration. You'll see them on track cars and tuned vehicles. For standard service work, you'll almost always be replacing with OEM-spec rubber bushings.
>
> How do you check? With the vehicle on a lift, use a pry bar to apply force to the control arm. The bushing should deflect slightly but spring back — no visible tearing, no excessive movement, no clunking."

**PPT Content:**
```
BUSHINGS — THE SILENT WORKERS

RUBBER BUSHINGS (OEM standard):
-- Construction: Inner sleeve + rubber element + outer sleeve
-- Function: Allow controlled pivot, isolate vibration and noise
-- Material: Natural or synthetic rubber compound
-- Lifespan: Typically 100,000-150,000 km (varies with conditions)
-- Failure signs: Cracking, tearing, separation, excessive deflection

POLYURETHANE BUSHINGS (aftermarket/performance):
-- Harder material, less deflection under load
-- Better geometry retention during hard cornering
-- Increased NVH (road noise and vibration)
-- Application: Track cars, performance upgrades

INSPECTION METHOD:
1. Vehicle on lift, wheels hanging free
2. Pry bar on control arm near bushing
3. Apply force — observe bushing deflection
4. Look for: cracking, tearing, oil contamination, excessive play
5. Compare left to right — they should feel similar

WORN BUSHINGS = Changed alignment under load = Unpredictable handling
```

#### Slide 13: Ball Joints — Construction, Wear, and Testing
**Visual:** Cutaway of a ball joint — ball stud, housing, bearing surface, dust boot, grease. Also show a worn ball joint with a torn boot and corroded ball stud. Include a diagram showing how to test for play with a pry bar.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Ball joints are the pivot points in the suspension. They allow multi-directional movement — the wheel needs to move up and down AND pivot left and right for steering. A ball joint has a hardened steel ball stud sitting in a polymer or metal bearing surface, packed with grease, and sealed by a rubber dust boot.
>
> The dust boot is the first line of defense. If the boot tears, water and dirt get in, grease leaks out, and the joint wears rapidly. A torn boot means the joint's life is now measured in weeks, not years. Always check the boots.
>
> Wear indicators vary by manufacturer. Some ball joints have a grease fitting — if you can pump grease until it comes out the boot, the boot is torn. Some have a visual wear indicator — a shoulder on the ball stud that recedes into the housing as the joint wears. When it's flush, the joint is worn.
>
> To test for play: with the car on a lift and the wheel on the ground (loaded), grab the tyre at 12 and 6 o'clock and rock it. Excessive play indicates a worn ball joint or wheel bearing. To differentiate, have someone watch the ball joint while you rock the wheel — if the play is in the joint, you'll see movement at the stud.
>
> A failed ball joint is a catastrophic failure. The wheel can separate from the vehicle. This is not a 'watch and wait' item. If it's worn, it gets replaced."

**PPT Content:**
```
BALL JOINTS — CRITICAL PIVOT POINTS

CONSTRUCTION:
-- Hardened steel ball stud
-- Polymer or metal bearing surface (socket)
-- Grease fill for lubrication
-- Dust boot (rubber seal) -- CRITICAL for longevity
-- Retaining clip or pressed fit

FAILURE MODES:
-- Torn dust boot --> contamination --> rapid wear
-- Excessive play (vertical or horizontal)
-- Seized joint (lack of lubrication)
-- CATASTROPHIC: Complete separation (wheel detaches)

WEAR INDICATORS:
-- Visual indicator: receding shoulder on stud
-- Grease fitting: pump grease, check boot integrity
-- Physical play: rock wheel at 12 and 6 o'clock

PLAY TESTING PROCEDURE:
1. Vehicle on lift, wheels loaded (on ground or with jack under arm)
2. Grab tyre at 12 and 6 o'clock, rock firmly
3. Have assistant observe ball joint for movement
4. Compare with wheel bearing play (different location of movement)
5. Any detectable play = replace

TORN BOOT = REPLACE THE JOINT (not just the boot)
Ball joint failure is a SAFETY-CRITICAL event
```

#### Slide 14: Rack-and-Pinion Steering — The Mechanism
**Visual:** Complete rack-and-pinion diagram from steering wheel to road wheel. Show: steering column, universal joints, pinion gear, rack bar, inner tie rod ends (within the rack boot), outer tie rod ends, steering knuckle connection. Label the path clearly as a numbered sequence.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's trace the steering from your hands to the road. You turn the steering wheel. That rotates the steering column — a shaft that runs down through the dashboard, usually through two universal joints that allow for angle changes. The column connects to the pinion gear — a small circular gear. That pinion meshes with the rack — a flat bar with teeth cut into it. As the pinion rotates, the rack slides left or right inside its housing.
>
> At each end of the rack, an inner tie rod end is threaded into the rack. This tie rod passes through a rubber boot — the rack boot — that keeps dirt out and grease in. Outside the boot, the inner tie rod connects to the outer tie rod end via a threaded adjuster. This adjuster is how toe is set during wheel alignment. The outer tie rod end is a ball joint that connects to the steering knuckle. When the rack moves, it pushes or pulls the tie rods, which pivot the knuckles, which turn the wheels.
>
> That's the complete chain. Steering wheel to column to pinion to rack to inner tie rod to outer tie rod to knuckle to wheel. Every link in that chain must be tight, lubricated, and free of play."

**PPT Content:**
```
RACK-AND-PINION STEERING — THE PATH

THE COMPLETE CHAIN:
1. Steering wheel -- driver input
2. Steering column -- shaft with universal joints
3. Pinion gear -- small round gear at the end of column
4. Rack bar -- flat toothed bar, slides left/right
5. Inner tie rod end -- threaded into rack end
6. Rack boot -- rubber bellows, seals the rack housing
7. Outer tie rod end -- ball joint, connects to knuckle

#### Slide 15: Tie Rod Ends — Inner and Outer
**Visual:** Close-up photos and diagrams of inner and outer tie rod ends. Show the outer tie rod end as a visible ball joint with a dust boot and castellated nut. Show the inner tie rod end inside the rack boot, noting it's hidden from direct view.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Tie rod ends are among the most commonly replaced steering components. The outer tie rod end is a ball joint — just like a suspension ball joint but smaller. >

#### Slide 16: Electronic Power Steering (EPS) — System Overview
**Visual:** Diagram showing two EPS variants side by side: column-assist (motor on steering column) and rack-assist (motor on steering rack). Show the torque sensor, steering angle sensor, and EPS ECU. Arrows show signal flow: torque sensor to ECU to motor.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Almost every new car today uses Electric Power Steering — EPS. Gone are the hydraulic pumps, fluid reservoirs, and hoses. Instead, an electric motor provides the steering assist, controlled by a dedicated ECU.
>
> Two main types. Column-assist: the motor is mounted on the steering column, inside the cabin or at the firewall. It's simpler and cheaper. Most economy to mid-range cars use this. Rack-assist: the motor is mounted directly on the steering rack. It provides assist at the rack itself, which gives a more direct feel. Premium and performance vehicles favor this approach.
>
> The system needs two key sensors. The torque sensor sits on the steering column and measures how much force the driver is applying — it's the input signal. The steering angle sensor measures the absolute position and rotation speed of the steering wheel — it also feeds data to the ESC and ADAS systems.
>
> The EPS ECU takes these inputs plus vehicle speed from the ABS module and calculates how much motor assist to provide. At low speed — parking — you get maximum assist for light steering. At high speed — motorway — assist is reduced so the steering feels stable and direct. This is variable assist, and it's the main advantage of EPS over hydraulic systems."

**PPT Content:**
```
ELECTRONIC POWER STEERING (EPS)

COLUMN-ASSIST EPS:
-- Motor mounted on steering column (above or below firewall)
-- Belt or worm gear drives the column
-- Simpler, lower cost, adequate for most vehicles
-- Application: Economy to mid-range (Toyota, VW, Hyundai)

RACK-ASSIST EPS:
-- Motor mounted directly on the steering rack
-- Ball screw or belt drives the rack bar
-- More direct feel, higher assist capacity
-- Application: Premium, performance, heavier vehicles (BMW, Audi)

KEY COMPONENTS:
1. Electric motor (brushless DC, typically 50-100A peak)
2. Torque sensor -- measures driver input effort
3. Steering angle sensor -- measures wheel position and rate
4. EPS ECU -- calculates required assist
5. Vehicle speed signal (from ABS module via CAN)

VARIABLE ASSIST:
-- Low speed / parking: HIGH assist (light steering)
-- Medium speed / urban: MEDIUM assist
-- High speed / motorway: LOW assist (stable, direct feel)
-- Adjust by speed, torque input, and steering rate
```

#### Slide 17: EPS Sensors — Torque and Steering Angle
**Visual:** Cutaway of a torque sensor on a steering column — show the torsion bar, two sensor rings, and how twisting the column causes a phase shift between the rings. Separately, show a steering angle sensor (digital encoder) mounted on the column.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The torque sensor is the heart of EPS. Without it, the system doesn't know when or how much to assist. It works using a torsion bar — a thin metal bar within the steering column that twists slightly when you apply steering force. Two magnetic or optical sensor rings are mounted on either side of the torsion bar. When the bar twists, the rings shift relative to each other, and the sensor measures that phase difference. More twist equals more force equals more assist request.
>
> If the torque sensor fails, the EPS shuts down and you lose power steering assist. The steering still works — it's just very heavy, especially at low speed. That's a designed-in safety feature: mechanical connection is always maintained. The EPS warning light illuminates, and there's usually a DTC stored.
>
> The steering angle sensor is a digital rotary encoder. It tells every system on the car where the steering wheel is pointed and how fast it's turning. ESC needs this to compare where the driver wants to go versus where the car is actually going. Lane keeping assist needs it. Adaptive headlights need it. If this sensor loses calibration — for example, after a wheel alignment or suspension work — you may need to recalibrate it using a diagnostic tool."

**PPT Content:**
```
EPS SENSORS

TORQUE SENSOR:
-- Location: On steering column, integrated with torsion bar
-- Principle: Torsion bar twists under driver effort
-- Two sensor rings detect phase shift from twist
-- Output: Proportional to driver steering effort (Nm)
-- Failure mode: EPS shuts down, heavy steering, warning light
-- SAFETY: Mechanical steering always maintained (motor off = manual)

STEERING ANGLE SENSOR (SAS):
-- Location: Steering column (often behind the airbag clock spring)
-- Type: Digital rotary encoder (absolute position)
-- Output: Steering angle (degrees) + rotation rate (deg/s)
-- Resolution: Typically 0.1 degree
-- Used by: EPS, ESC, lane keeping assist, adaptive headlights

CALIBRATION REQUIREMENTS:
-- SAS may need recalibration after:
   * Wheel alignment
   * Steering rack replacement
   * Subframe removal/refit
   * Any work that changes the straight-ahead position
-- Procedure: Diagnostic tool + lock-to-lock rotation + straight-ahead set
-- Failure to calibrate = ESC malfunction, ADAS errors
```

#### Slide 18: EPS vs Hydraulic Power Steering — Why the Industry Switched
**Visual:** Two-column comparison. Left: hydraulic system diagram (pump, reservoir, hoses, rack cylinder). Right: EPS system (motor, ECU, sensors, wiring). Include a fuel efficiency comparison bar chart.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Why did the industry move from hydraulic to electric? Three reasons. First, fuel efficiency. A hydraulic pump runs off the engine belt — it's consuming power all the time, even when you're driving straight and don't need steering assist. An EPS motor only draws power when you actually turn the wheel. That saves 2-4% fuel economy — which matters enormously for fleet CO2 targets.
>
> Second, tunability. A hydraulic system provides the same assist characteristic regardless of driving conditions. An EPS system can change its behavior through software — different assist curves for sport mode versus comfort mode, speed-dependent assist, active return-to-centre, lane keeping assist integration. The steering feel can be updated with a software flash.
>
> Third, packaging and maintenance. No pump, no reservoir, no high-pressure hoses, no fluid to leak. EPS is simpler to package, lighter, and has fewer maintenance items. The trade-off? When the motor or ECU fails, the repair cost is typically higher than a hydraulic component. But the failure rate is significantly lower."

**PPT Content:**
```
EPS vs HYDRAULIC — WHY EPS WON

                    | HYDRAULIC             | ELECTRIC (EPS)
--------------------+-----------------------+------------------------
Power source        | Engine-driven pump    | Electric motor
Energy consumption  | Constant (pump always | On-demand (motor only
                    | running)              | when steering)
Fuel saving         | Baseline              | 2-4% improvement
Assist variability  | Fixed characteristic  | Software-tuneable
Speed-dependent     | Requires extra valve  | Standard (CAN speed input)
Active features     | Not possible          | Lane keep, park assist
Components          | Pump, reservoir,      | Motor, ECU, sensors,
                    | hoses, rack cylinder  | wiring
Fluid               | Power steering fluid  | None
Maintenance         | Fluid check/change,   | Minimal (sensor cal only)
                    | hose inspection       |
Failure mode        | Loss of assist        | Loss of assist
                    | (fluid leak common)   | (electrical failure rare)
Repair cost         | Low-moderate          | Moderate-high

INDUSTRY TRANSITION:
-- 2005: ~30% EPS penetration
-- 2015: ~70% EPS penetration
-- 2025: ~95%+ EPS on new vehicles

#### Slide 19: Caster — Stability and Returnability
**Visual:** Side-view diagram showing the caster angle — the tilt of the steering axis (line through upper and lower pivot points) relative to vertical. Show positive caster with the upper pivot point behind the lower pivot point. Show the trail distance on the ground. Include a shopping trolley caster wheel analogy.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Wheel alignment has three primary angles: caster, camber, and toe. Let's start with caster.
>
> Caster is the angle of the steering axis when viewed from the side of the vehicle. If I draw a line through the upper strut mount and the lower ball joint, that line tilts backward at the top — that's positive caster. Think of a shopping trolley wheel — the pivot point is ahead of the contact point. That's why the wheel trails behind and self-centres. Your car's steering works the same way.
>
> Positive caster creates a trail — the contact patch is behind the steering axis where it meets the ground. This trail creates a self-centring force. The more caster, the stronger the steering return-to-centre feel and the more stable the car is in a straight line at speed. Too much caster makes the steering heavy, especially at low speed.
>
> Caster is not adjustable on most MacPherson strut vehicles — it's set by the subframe and strut mount positions. If the caster is out of spec, you're usually looking at a bent strut, shifted subframe, or collision damage. On double wishbone or multi-link front ends, caster may be adjustable via eccentric bolts or shims."

**PPT Content:**
```
CASTER — STABILITY AND RETURNABILITY

DEFINITION:
Angle of the steering axis viewed from the SIDE of the vehicle
Measured in degrees from vertical

POSITIVE CASTER (normal):
-- Upper pivot is BEHIND the lower pivot (axis tilts backward at top)
-- Creates trail: contact patch is behind the steering axis ground point
-- Effect: Self-centring steering, straight-line stability

TRAIL:
-- Distance between steering axis ground point and tyre contact centre
-- More trail = stronger self-centring force
-- Analogy: Shopping trolley caster wheel always trails behind its pivot

TOO LITTLE CASTER: Wander, poor return-to-centre, unstable at speed
TOO MUCH CASTER: Heavy steering, excessive kickback over bumps

TYPICAL VALUES: +3 to +8 degrees (vehicle dependent)

ADJUSTABILITY:
-- MacPherson strut: Usually NOT adjustable (fixed by design)
   If out of spec: suspect bent strut, shifted subframe, collision damage
-- Double wishbone/multi-link: May be adjustable (eccentric bolts, shims)
-- Left-to-right difference: Should be < 0.5 degrees (causes pull)
```

#### Slide 20: Camber — Tyre Contact Angle
**Visual:** Front-view diagram showing the camber angle — tyre tilted inward (negative camber) and outward (positive camber) relative to vertical. Show how negative camber affects the contact patch under cornering load. Include tyre wear pattern illustrations for excessive negative and positive camber.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Camber is the tilt of the tyre when viewed from the front of the vehicle. If the top of the tyre leans inward toward the car, that's negative camber. If it leans outward, that's positive camber.
>
> Most modern vehicles are set with a slight negative camber — between -0.5 and -2.0 degrees. Why? Because when a car corners, body roll pushes the outer wheel into positive camber — the tyre leans away from the turn. Starting with a bit of negative camber compensates for this, keeping the tyre flatter on the road through the corner for better grip.
>
> But here's the trade-off. Too much negative camber means the inner edge of the tyre carries more weight in straight-line driving, and that inner edge wears faster. You'll see this on lowered vehicles and performance-tuned cars that have been set up for cornering at the expense of tyre life.
>
> Too much positive camber wears the outer edge. And a left-to-right camber difference causes the car to pull toward the side with more positive camber — the tyre on that side creates more rolling resistance toward its lean.
>
> On the alignment printout, camber is in degrees. You're looking for the value to be within spec AND for left-to-right to be within 0.5 degrees of each other."

**PPT Content:**
```
CAMBER — TYRE CONTACT ANGLE

DEFINITION:
Angle of the tyre viewed from the FRONT of the vehicle
Measured in degrees from vertical

NEGATIVE CAMBER: Top of tyre leans INWARD
-- Compensates for body roll in cornering
-- Improves cornering grip
-- Excess: Inner edge tyre wear

POSITIVE CAMBER: Top of tyre leans OUTWARD
-- Uncommon on modern vehicles
-- Excess: Outer edge tyre wear

ZERO CAMBER: Tyre perfectly vertical
-- Maximum contact patch in straight line
-- Loses grip in cornering due to body roll

TYPICAL VALUES: -0.5 to -2.0 degrees (vehicle dependent)

TYRE WEAR PATTERNS:
-- Excessive negative camber: Inner shoulder wear
-- Excessive positive camber: Outer shoulder wear
-- Left/right difference > 0.5 deg: Vehicle pulls to MORE POSITIVE side

ADJUSTABILITY:
-- MacPherson: Limited (eccentric bolts, camber plates, slotted holes)
-- Double wishbone: Adjustable via upper arm (shims or eccentric bolts)
-- Multi-link: Adjustable via camber link
```

#### Slide 21: Toe — Rolling Direction
**Visual:** Top-down view of front wheels showing toe-in (fronts of tyres closer together) and toe-out (fronts of tyres further apart). Show the tyre scrub angle and its effect on tyre wear. Include feathered tyre wear pattern illustration.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Toe is the most commonly adjusted alignment angle and the one that causes the most dramatic tyre wear when it's wrong.
>
> Viewed from above, if the fronts of the tyres point inward toward each other, that's toe-in. If they point outward, that's toe-out. Most vehicles are set with a slight toe-in — typically 0 to 2mm total, or 0 to 0.2 degrees per side. This provides straight-line stability because any steering disturbance is corrected by the tyre wanting to roll back to centre.
>
> Rear toe is equally important. Rear toe-in provides stability. Some vehicles with multi-link rear suspension set a small amount of rear toe-in for exactly this reason.
>
> When toe is wrong, the tyre is constantly scrubbing sideways as it rolls forward. It's like dragging your shoe slightly sideways across the floor — it wears fast and in a distinctive pattern. Excessive toe causes feathered wear — you can feel it by running your hand across the tread. It feels smooth in one direction and rough in the other, like stroking a cat the wrong way.
>
> Toe is adjusted at the tie rod adjuster sleeve — the threaded connection between the inner and outer tie rod ends. Turn the sleeve to change the effective length of the tie rod, which changes the toe angle. Always tighten the locknuts after adjustment. And always check that the steering wheel is centred after a toe adjustment — if the rack is off-centre, the steering wheel will be crooked even though the toe readings look perfect."

**PPT Content:**
```
TOE — ROLLING DIRECTION

DEFINITION:
Angle of the tyres viewed from ABOVE the vehicle
Measured in degrees or millimeters

TOE-IN: Fronts of tyres closer together than rears
-- Provides straight-line stability
-- Most vehicles set slight toe-in (0-2mm total / 0-0.2 deg per side)

TOE-OUT: Fronts of tyres further apart than rears
-- Improves turn-in response (some front-wheel-drive track setups)
-- Causes straight-line instability on road vehicles

REAR TOE:
-- Multi-link rear: set toe-in for stability
-- Rear toe out = unstable rear end, oversteer tendency, rapid tyre wear

TYRE WEAR FROM INCORRECT TOE:
-- Feathered wear pattern (sawtooth across tread blocks)
-- Run hand across tyre: smooth one direction, rough the other
-- Most rapid wear of all alignment errors
-- Incorrect toe can destroy a new tyre in 5,000 km

ADJUSTMENT:
-- Tie rod adjuster sleeve (between inner and outer tie rod ends)
-- Turn sleeve to lengthen or shorten tie rod = changes toe
-- Lock nuts MUST be tightened after adjustment
-- Confirm steering wheel is centred after toe adjustment
```

#### Slide 22: Reading an Alignment Printout
**Visual:** A realistic alignment printout from a typical alignment machine (Hunter/John Bean style). **Instructor Narration:**
> "This is what comes off an alignment machine. You need to be able to read this like you read a blood pressure result — instantly recognise what's normal and what's not.
>

## Key Takeaways

1. Suspension is a safety system that controls the tyre contact patch — MacPherson strut is the most common, double wishbone offers superior geometry control, multi-link provides maximum tunability
2. Dampers control spring oscillation — twin-tube is standard, mono-tube is performance-oriented; both wear gradually and lose damping force over time
3. Bushings and ball joints are the most common wear items in the suspension — torn boots lead to rapid failure, and ball joint separation is catastrophic
4. Rack-and-pinion steering is a direct mechanical path from driver to road wheel; EPS adds electrically-controlled variable assist using torque and angle sensors
5. Caster provides stability and return-to-centre; camber controls tyre contact angle; toe controls rolling direction — incorrect toe causes the fastest tyre wear
6. Always read an alignment printout by checking rear angles first, then front; look for left-to-right differences as well as absolute values
