## Session Objectives (For Instructor)

By the end of this session, learners will be able to:
- Identify and describe the correct operation of workshop lifting equipment (2-post, 4-post, scissor lifts, trolley jacks, jack stands)
- Locate manufacturer-specified jacking points on a vehicle and explain why incorrect placement is lethal
- Select the correct PPE for common workshop tasks including high-voltage work, chemical handling, and mechanical repair
- Inspect PPE before use and identify when equipment must be replaced
- Name the six categories of modern workshop hazards and describe at least one mitigation for each
- Reference the relevant OSHA and ISO standards governing workshop safety

**This is Day 2 of the program.** Yesterday, learners met the car. Today, they learn that the workshop can kill them — and exactly how to prevent it. The tone is serious but empowering: respect, not fear.

#### Slide 5: The 2-Post Lift — Most Common, Most Dangerous
**Visual:** Annotated diagram of a 2-post lift — columns, swing arms, rubber pads, mechanical safety lock mechanism, control panel, equalizer cable. Side view showing vehicle correctly positioned between posts.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The 2-post lift is the workhorse of the industry. Two vertical columns bolted to the workshop floor, four adjustable swing arms with rubber or nylon pads. The vehicle drives between the columns, you position the arms under the manufacturer's specified jacking points, and hydraulic pressure raises the car. Most 2-post lifts have a capacity of 3 to 5.5 tonnes.
>
> Here's what kills people with 2-post lifts: incorrect pad placement. If you position the arms on a body seam, a sill pinch weld that's corroded through, or on a non-structural component like the floor pan, the car can slip off at full height. That's 1,500 kilograms falling from 1.8 metres. The survival rate for a direct hit from that is close to zero. Every car has specific lift points documented by the manufacturer. You MUST consult the service manual before lifting an unfamiliar vehicle. Every single time."

**PPT Content:**
```
2-POST LIFT — The Workshop Workhorse

Components:
- Two vertical columns (floor-mounted or base-plate)
- Four adjustable swing arms with rubber/nylon pads
- Hydraulic cylinder(s) and equalizer cable/chain
- Mechanical safety locks (engage automatically at intervals)
- Control panel (raise / lower / lock release)

Capacity: Typically 3,000 -- 5,500 kg
Full access: Entire vehicle underside exposed

CRITICAL SAFETY POINTS:
- ALWAYS use manufacturer-specified jacking points
- VERIFY mechanical locks engage before going under vehicle
- NEVER exceed rated capacity (check vehicle weight vs lift rating)
- INSPECT pads for wear, cracking, or damage before every use
- LISTEN for the lock click at each safety increment
```

#### Slide 6: The 4-Post Lift
**Visual:** Annotated diagram of a 4-post lift — four columns, two runway platforms, optional jacking beam underneath. Vehicle shown driving onto runways.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The 4-post lift uses four columns and two runway platforms. The vehicle drives onto the platforms and sits on its own tyres. This is inherently more stable than a 2-post lift because there are four points of floor contact and the car is resting on a large surface area. It's excellent for wheel alignment work, general inspection, and long-term vehicle storage. Some 4-post lifts include a jacking beam that runs between the runways — this lets you lift the vehicle off the platforms for wheel removal and brake work.
>
> The advantage: stability. The risk: rolling. If you don't chock the wheels before raising, and the parking brake isn't applied, the car can roll off the edge of the runway. A vehicle rolling off a 4-post lift at 1.5 metres height onto someone standing behind it has killed technicians. Always chock. Always apply the parking brake. Always."

**PPT Content:**
```
4-POST LIFT — Stability and Versatility

Components:
- Four vertical columns with safety locks
- Two runway platforms (vehicle drives on)
- Optional jacking beam (lifts car off runways for wheel work)
- Wheel stops at runway edges (not a substitute for chocks)

Capacity: Typically 3,500 -- 6,000 kg

ADVANTAGES:
- Very stable — car sits on its own wheels
- No jacking points needed for basic raising
- Ideal for alignment, inspection, storage

CRITICAL SAFETY:
- ALWAYS chock wheels before raising
- ALWAYS apply parking brake
- VERIFY all four column locks engage
- Use jacking beam for any wheel removal work
- Never stand behind or in front of runway during raising
```

#### Slide 7: The Scissor Lift
**Visual:** Annotated diagram of a scissor lift — platform, crossed scissor mechanism, control panel. Show both in-ground (flush mount) and above-ground (portable) variants side by side.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Scissor lifts use a crossed-arm mechanism — like a pair of scissors opening — to raise a platform. Some are installed in-ground, flush with the floor when lowered. These are excellent for saving workshop space. Others are above-ground portable units used for quick undercar tasks: oil changes, exhaust inspection, brake checks.
>
> The primary risk with scissor lifts is different from post lifts: pinch points. The scissor mechanism itself creates powerful closing forces during lowering. Fingers, hands, tools, or air hoses caught in the mechanism will be crushed. Always clear the area below AND around the lift before lowering. Also, because many scissor lifts don't raise as high as post lifts, technicians often work in awkward crouched positions underneath — this leads to chronic back injuries over time. Use a proper creeper, adequate lighting, and take posture breaks."

**PPT Content:**
```
SCISSOR LIFT — Space-Efficient Lifting

Types:
- In-ground (flush when lowered — saves workshop floor space)
- Above-ground / portable (mobile between bays)
- Full-rise (~1.8m) or mid-rise (~1.0m)

Mechanism: Hydraulic crossed-arm ("scissor") linkage

Capacity: Typically 2,500 -- 4,000 kg

ADVANTAGES:
- Space-efficient (especially in-ground)
- Fast deployment for quick undercar tasks
- Low-profile access for tyre/wheel work (mid-rise)

CRITICAL SAFETY:
- PINCH HAZARD — clear all objects/hands from scissor area
- Clear area below AND around the platform before lowering
- Use creepers and adequate lighting for undercar work
- Check platform alignment and lock engagement before working
- Mid-rise lifts: ensure vehicle is centred on platform
```

#### Slide 8: Jacking Points — The Non-Negotiable Rule
**Visual:** Underside diagram of a vehicle showing correct jacking points highlighted in bright green with checkmarks vs. WRONG points marked with red X's. Include 2-3 different vehicle examples (sedan, SUV, van) to show variation.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "This slide might save your life. Every vehicle has manufacturer-specified jacking points — reinforced areas of the body structure specifically designed to bear the full weight of the vehicle. They're usually along the pinch weld at the bottom of the sills, at front and rear subframe mounting bolts, or at specific reinforced cross-members. Many vehicles mark them with a small triangle, notch, or arrow stamped into the sill.
>
> If you jack or lift at the wrong point, three things can happen. One: you crush the body panel — expensive. Two: you crack or fold the sill — now you've created a structural repair that costs thousands. Three — and worst: the car shifts, slips, and falls. I have personally seen a car fall off a trolley jack because a technician put the jack under the oil pan. The pan deformed, the jack tilted, and the car dropped. The technician was reaching for a socket underneath. He pulled his arm out with half a second to spare. Half a second was the difference between a scare story and a funeral.
>
> ALWAYS check the owner's manual, workshop manual, or lift-point database before lifting any vehicle. If you don't know the jacking points, STOP and look them up. There is no shame in checking. There is enormous shame in a collapsed vehicle."

**PPT Content:**
```
JACKING POINTS — THE NON-NEGOTIABLE RULE

CORRECT lift points (manufacturer-specified):
- Reinforced sill pinch welds (marked with triangles or notches)
- Front subframe mounting points
- Rear axle beam or subframe mounts
- Specific cross-member locations (varies by vehicle)

NEVER lift on:
- Engine oil pan or transmission case (deforms, vehicle falls)
- Body panels or floor pan (thin metal, no structural strength)
- Exhaust components (bend, break, vehicle shifts)
- Fuel tank or fuel lines (puncture risk, fire risk)
- Suspension arms (unless specifically documented by manufacturer)

RULE: If you don't know the jacking point — LOOK IT UP.
Sources: Workshop manual, manufacturer database, door jamb sticker
No guessing. No "it looks strong enough." VERIFY.
```

#### Slide 9: Trolley Jacks & Jack Stands — The Partnership That Saves Lives
**Visual:** Photo sequence showing correct procedure: (1) trolley jack positioned at jacking point, (2) vehicle raised, (3) jack stand positioned at support point, (4) vehicle lowered onto jack stand, (5) jack removed, (6) vehicle stable on stand. Include a separate panel showing WRONG: car on jack only, with a red X.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "A trolley jack is for LIFTING. A jack stand is for HOLDING. These are two different jobs performed by two different pieces of equipment. Never work under a vehicle supported only by a trolley jack. I'll say that again, and I'll say it every time I see a jack in this workshop: NEVER work under a vehicle supported only by a trolley jack. Hydraulic seals fail. Release valves leak. Jacks tip on uneven surfaces. A slow leak in the seal means the car lowers silently while you're underneath — you won't hear it coming.
>
> The procedure: jack raises the car. You slide a jack stand under a structural support point. You lower the jack so the car's weight transfers to the stand. Then you remove the jack entirely. The stand is mechanical — a ratchet pin or screw thread. No hydraulics to fail. Use them in pairs — never a single stand on one side. And always use stands rated at least 50 percent above the weight on that corner."

**PPT Content:**
```
TROLLEY JACKS & JACK STANDS — THE PARTNERSHIP

TROLLEY JACK (hydraulic):
- Purpose: LIFTING only — never for holding
- Hydraulic seals can fail silently
- Place only on correct manufacturer jacking points
- Use on flat, solid surface only (never dirt, gravel, or slopes)

JACK STANDS (mechanical):
- Purpose: HOLDING the vehicle while you work
- Ratchet pin or screw-thread mechanism — no hydraulics to fail
- Rated capacity must EXCEED the load by 50%
- Always use in PAIRS — never a single stand

THE PROCEDURE:
1. Jack LIFTS the vehicle
2. Stand is PLACED at a structural support point
3. Vehicle is LOWERED onto the stand
4. Jack is REMOVED
5. Vehicle TESTED for stability (push test)

THE RULE: NEVER work under a jack-only supported vehicle.
A jack lifts. A stand holds. A jack is not a stand.
```

#### Slide 10: Safe Lift Operation — The Complete 10-Step Procedure
**Visual:** Numbered step-by-step infographic arranged in a flow, each step with a small icon. Steps 5 and 8 highlighted in amber as critical verification gates.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let me walk you through the complete procedure for safely lifting a vehicle on a 2-post lift. This is a 10-step process. Every step matters. Skipping one is gambling with your life.
>
> Step 1: Check the lift's inspection certificate is current — it should be displayed on the column. Step 2: Visually inspect the lift — arms, pads, cables, hydraulic hoses — look for leaks, cracks, wear. Step 3: Centre the vehicle between the posts — equal distance on each side. Step 4: Identify the correct jacking points from the workshop manual or manufacturer database. Step 5: Position all four arms and pads precisely on the jacking points. Step 6: Raise the lift just enough to contact — about 10 centimetres off the ground — then STOP. Walk around. Verify every pad is correctly seated. This is the critical check. Step 7: Raise to full working height. Step 8: Verify mechanical safety locks have engaged — you should hear them click at each interval. Step 9: Gently push the vehicle laterally to test stability — if it rocks, something is wrong. Step 10: THEN — and only then — go underneath.
>
> We will practice this in the practical session. You will do this correctly every single time, or you will not use the lift. There is no middle ground."

**PPT Content:**
```
SAFE LIFT OPERATION — 10-STEP PROCEDURE

 1. CHECK lift inspection certificate (current and displayed?)
 2. INSPECT lift visually (arms, pads, cables, hoses — any damage?)
 3. CENTRE vehicle between posts (equal distance both sides)
 4. IDENTIFY correct jacking points (consult manual/database)
 5. POSITION all four arms/pads precisely on jacking points
 6. RAISE to contact (~10cm) — STOP — WALK AROUND — VERIFY every pad
 7. RAISE to full working height
 8. VERIFY mechanical safety locks engaged (listen for click)
 9. PUSH-TEST vehicle laterally — test for stability
10. CLEAR to work underneath

Steps 5-6: THE CRITICAL CHECK — this is where most failures start
Step 8: SAFETY LOCKS — your failsafe if hydraulics fail

Skip a step = gamble with your life. No exceptions.
```

#### Slide 12: PPE Philosophy — The Hierarchy of Controls
**Visual:** Classic hierarchy of controls pyramid — elimination at top, substitution, engineering controls, administrative controls, PPE at the bottom. The PPE level is highlighted and enlarged. Side panel shows the "Swiss cheese model" with PPE as the final barrier layer.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "PPE — Personal Protective Equipment — is your last line of defence. Not your first. The hierarchy of controls tells us: first, try to eliminate the hazard entirely. Then substitute something less dangerous. Then engineer a physical barrier. Then use administrative controls like procedures and training. Only then — at the bottom — do you reach PPE.
>
> Think of it like the Swiss cheese model. Each layer of protection has holes. Engineering controls have gaps. Procedures get skipped. PPE is the final slice of cheese before the hazard reaches you. If every other layer fails, your PPE is what stands between you and injury. That's why it must be correct for the task, properly fitted to your body, and inspected before every single use. It's not a formality. It's your personal survival equipment."

**PPT Content:**
```
HIERARCHY OF CONTROLS

1. ELIMINATION — Remove the hazard entirely
2. SUBSTITUTION — Replace with something less hazardous
3. ENGINEERING CONTROLS — Physical barriers, ventilation, guards
4. ADMINISTRATIVE CONTROLS — Procedures, training, signage
5. PPE — Personal Protective Equipment (LAST line of defence)

In a workshop, many hazards CANNOT be eliminated:
- You cannot remove the weight of the vehicle
- You cannot eliminate the voltage in a hybrid battery
- You cannot substitute something less toxic than battery acid

PPE is therefore CRITICAL, not optional.
It must be: CORRECT for the task | FITTED to you | INSPECTED every use
```

#### Slide 13: Eye Protection — Your Most Irreplaceable Asset
**Visual:** Photo array showing safety glasses with side shields, splash-proof chemical goggles, and full face shield. Close-up of ANSI Z87.1 stamp on lens. Close-up of EN 166 marking. Photo of safety glasses with a crack through the lens — replacement required.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Your eyes are irreplaceable. There is no surgery, no prosthetic, no treatment that restores lost vision from a chemical splash or metal fragment. Safety glasses are the absolute minimum — worn at ALL times in any workshop area. They protect against flying debris: wire wheel fragments, metal filings, spring clips, grinding sparks. For chemical work — brake cleaner, battery acid, coolant flush — you need splash-proof goggles that seal completely around your eyes with indirect ventilation. For grinding, cutting, or any risk of arc flash, a full face shield worn OVER safety glasses.
>
> Check the marking on the lens. In the US, ANSI Z87.1 means it's been impact-tested and certified. In Europe and internationally, EN 166 with the appropriate grade. If your safety glasses don't have one of these markings, they are fashion accessories, not protection. I'm going to pass some around now — find the stamp."

**PPT Content:**
```
EYE PROTECTION — IRREPLACEABLE

SAFETY GLASSES — Minimum standard, worn AT ALL TIMES in workshop
- Impact protection: flying debris, wire wheel, spring clips, sparks
- MUST have side shields
- Rating: ANSI Z87.1 (USA) or EN 166 (EU/International)

SPLASH-PROOF GOGGLES — Chemical work
- Sealed around eyes — no liquid entry path
- Brake cleaner, battery acid, coolant, solvents, fuel
- Indirect ventilation to prevent fogging

FACE SHIELD — Grinding, cutting, arc-flash, high-risk splash
- Worn OVER safety glasses (never instead of)
```

#### Slide 14: Hand Protection — Matched to the Hazard
**Visual:** Photo array with clear labels: (1) nitrile disposable gloves, (2) leather mechanics gloves, (3) chemical-resistant neoprene gloves, (4) Class 0 HV insulating gloves shown with leather protector overs, (5) welding gauntlets. Each paired with its use-case icon.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Your hands are your livelihood. Different hazards demand different gloves — there is no one-size-fits-all. Nitrile disposables for any job involving fluids: oil, coolant, brake fluid, fuel. Brake fluid is especially dangerous — it's a glycol-based solvent that absorbs through your skin and damages your liver and kidneys with chronic exposure. Change nitrile gloves frequently; they permeate over time. Leather mechanics gloves for handling sharp sheet metal edges, exhaust components, or anything that could cut or abrade. Chemical-resistant neoprene or butyl gloves for concentrated acids, heavy solvent exposure, or parts washing.
>
> And then there are these. Hold your hand out. These are Class 0 high-voltage insulating gloves — rated to 1,000 volts AC, 1,500 volts DC. You MUST wear these before touching any orange-cabled component on a hybrid or EV. They come with leather protector gloves worn over them to prevent punctures from sharp edges. Before EVERY use, you roll the cuff to trap air, squeeze gently, and check for pinholes by listening and feeling. A pinhole in an HV glove can kill you. I will demonstrate this inflation test in the practical session and every one of you will perform it."

**PPT Content:**
```
HAND PROTECTION — MATCHED TO THE HAZARD

NITRILE DISPOSABLE GLOVES
- For: Oil, coolant, brake fluid, fuel, solvents, general chemical
- Change frequently — chemicals permeate over time
- Dispose after single use. Never reuse torn gloves.

LEATHER MECHANICS GLOVES
- For: Sharp edges, sheet metal, exhaust, hot components
- Cut and abrasion resistant with finger dexterity
- Check for cuts, holes, or worn spots before use

CHEMICAL-RESISTANT GLOVES (Neoprene / Butyl)
- For: Battery acid, heavy solvent exposure, parts washing
- Thicker, reusable, rated for specific chemical families
- Check chemical compatibility chart before selecting

CLASS 0 HV INSULATING GLOVES (Rated 1,000V AC / 1,500V DC)
- For: ANY work near hybrid/EV high-voltage components
- Worn WITH leather protectors over them (puncture protection)
- MUST be inflate-tested before EVERY use (roll cuff, trap air, squeeze)
- Replace if punctured, cracked, discoloured, or past expiry date
- Must be laboratory-tested every 6 months (EN 60903 / ASTM D120)
- Stored flat in protective bag — NEVER folded or compressed
```

#### Slide 15: Body, Foot & Hearing Protection
**Visual:** Full-length photo of a properly equipped technician: steel-toed boots (cutaway showing composite toe cap), fitted coveralls with no loose fabric, hearing protection (earmuffs shown, foam plugs in inset). Side panel shows arc-flash rated coverall with rating label visible.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Steel-toed boots — non-negotiable in any workshop, every day. A brake disc weighs 8 to 12 kilograms. A transmission casing weighs 40 to 80 kilograms. Drop either from waist height onto an unprotected foot and you will break bones. Steel or composite toe caps are rated to withstand a 200-joule impact — roughly equivalent to a 20-kilogram weight falling from one metre. Look for the S3 rating in Europe or ASTM F2413 in the US — that gives you impact protection, puncture-resistant midsole, oil-resistant and slip-resistant outsole, and water resistance.

#### Slide 19: Hazard Focus — High Voltage: The Danger That Didn't Exist
**Visual:** Diagram of a hybrid/EV vehicle showing HV battery location under the floor, orange HV cabling routes, HV components labeled (inverter, electric motor, DC-DC converter, electric A/C compressor, onboard charger). Warning labels shown in detail.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Twenty years ago, the most dangerous electrical component in a car was the 12-volt battery — capable of producing a nasty spark, enough current to weld a wrench to a terminal, but rarely lethal. For reference, household electricity in Europe is 230 volts AC — and it kills people every year.
>
> At 400 volts DC, contact with exposed conductors can cause ventricular fibrillation — cardiac arrest — in under one second. You will not feel a warning tingle. You will not have time to pull away. Your muscles will lock, and your heart will stop.
>
> High-voltage components are identified internationally by orange cabling and connectors. If you see orange anywhere on a vehicle, assume it can kill you until proven otherwise. You do not touch, disconnect, measure, or work near any orange-cabled component without specific HV training, the correct PPE, and a verified isolation procedure. We cover HV safety in full during Week 7. For today, the rule is absolute: orange means lethal. Stay away unless you are trained and equipped."

#### Slide 20: Hazard Focus — Chemical and Mechanical

#### Slide 21: Hazard Focus — Thermal, Pressurized, Ergonomic
**Visual:** Three-panel layout: (1) thermal image of exhaust manifold/turbo showing temperature gradients, (2) tyre inflation diagram with safety cage, (3) silhouette of technician showing common injury zones — lower back, shoulders, wrists, knees

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Thermal hazards. An exhaust manifold can reach 800 degrees Celsius after sustained driving. A turbocharger housing: 900 degrees. ## Key Takeaways

1. Workshop safety is built on three pillars: knowledge, equipment, and habit — all three are required, none alone is sufficient
2. Lifts, jacks, and jack stands each have a specific role — confusing them kills people every month, worldwide
3. Manufacturer-specified jacking points are non-negotiable — never guess, never assume, always verify
4. PPE is your last line of defence — it must be correct for the task, fitted to you, and inspected before every use
5. Modern workshops have six categories of hazard: high voltage, chemical, mechanical, thermal, pressurized, and ergonomic
6. Safety is a personal commitment — someone is waiting for you to come home
