---

## 🎯 Session Objectives (For Instructor)

By the end of this session, learners will be able to:
- Remove a turbocharger following correct procedures (oil/coolant line disconnect, exhaust/intake)
- Disassemble a turbocharger into its main assemblies (compressor housing, turbine housing, CHRA)
- Measure turbo shaft radial and axial play using a dial gauge
- Inspect compressor and turbine wheels for damage, erosion, and contact marks
- Explain the cartridge rebuild process and what components are replaced
- Inspect wastegate/VGT mechanism for free movement and correct operation
- Understand that turbo balancing is a specialist operation requiring dedicated equipment

**Visual:** Exploded view — compressor housing, compressor wheel, CHRA (centre housing rotating assembly), turbine wheel, turbine housing, V-band clamps

**PPT Content:**
```
TURBOCHARGER — THREE MAIN ASSEMBLIES

1. COMPRESSOR HOUSING (cold side):
   Aluminium, scroll shape, houses compressor wheel
   Connected to intake piping

2. TURBINE HOUSING (hot side):
   Cast iron, scroll shape, houses turbine wheel
   Connected to exhaust manifold
   May contain wastegate or VGT mechanism

3. CHRA — Centre Housing Rotating Assembly:
   Bearing housing + shaft + both wheels
   Oil-fed bearings (journal or ball)
   Often water-cooled as well
   This is the "cartridge" that gets rebuilt

CONNECTIONS: V-band clamps or bolted flanges
```

### **DEVELOPMENT: Turbo Inspection Theory** (Slides 4-8, ~25 minutes)

#### Slide 4: Removal Procedure
**Visual:** Turbo mounted on engine with oil lines, coolant lines, exhaust, and intake connections highlighted

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before disassembly, you remove it from the engine. Disconnect the oil feed line (top, pressurised) and oil drain line (bottom, gravity return). Disconnect coolant lines if water-cooled. Unbolt or unclamp the exhaust side from the manifold and the intake side from the charge pipe. Support the turbo — they're heavier than they look. Inspect the oil drain for restrictions — a restricted drain causes bearing flooding and oil smoke."

**PPT Content:**
```
TURBO REMOVAL PROCEDURE

1. Let engine cool completely (turbine housing = 600°C+ when hot)
2. Disconnect oil FEED line (top — pressurised, smaller)
3. Disconnect oil DRAIN line (bottom — gravity, larger)
4. Disconnect coolant lines (if water-cooled)
5. Disconnect exhaust pipe from turbine housing
6. Disconnect intake pipe from compressor housing
7. Unbolt turbo from exhaust manifold
8. Remove and set on clean, soft surface

INSPECT DURING REMOVAL:
• Oil feed line — blocked? Carbon deposits?
• Oil drain line — kinked? Restricted?
• Exhaust manifold studs — corroded? Broken?
```

---

#### Slide 5: Disassembly Sequence
**Visual:** Step-by-step disassembly photos

**PPT Content:**
```
TURBO DISASSEMBLY

1. Remove V-band clamp(s) — mark orientation first
2. Separate compressor housing from CHRA
3. Separate turbine housing from CHRA
4. Inspect the CHRA as an assembly first:
   • Spin the shaft by hand — should be smooth, no grinding
   • Check for axial play (shaft moves in and out)
   • Check for radial play (shaft moves side to side)
5. If full teardown: remove compressor nut, press off compressor wheel
   (SPECIALIST ONLY — requires balancing after reassembly)

NOTE: Most workshop rebuilds replace the complete CHRA
      rather than rebuilding individual bearings
```

#### Slide 6: Shaft Play Measurement
**Visual:** Dial gauge positioned to measure radial play on turbo shaft, then axial play

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Shaft play tells you the bearing condition. Radial play — the shaft moves perpendicular to its axis. Mount a dial gauge on the bearing housing, plunger on the shaft, and push the shaft in one direction then the other. Typical limit: 0.05-0.08mm. Axial play — the shaft moves along its axis. Push and pull the shaft and read the dial gauge. Typical limit: 0.025-0.05mm. Beyond these limits, the turbo needs a rebuild or replacement."

**PPT Content:**
```
SHAFT PLAY MEASUREMENT

RADIAL PLAY (side-to-side):
• Dial gauge on housing, plunger on shaft
• Push shaft fully one way → zero → push fully other way → read
• Typical limit: 0.05 - 0.08 mm
• Excessive = bearing wear → wheel contacts housing → catastrophic

AXIAL PLAY (in-and-out):
• Dial gauge aligned with shaft axis
• Push shaft in → zero → pull shaft out → read
• Typical limit: 0.025 - 0.05 mm
• Excessive = thrust bearing worn

BEYOND LIMITS → CHRA replacement or professional rebuild
```

---

#### Slide 7: Wheel Inspection
**Visual:** Comparison photos — good compressor wheel vs damaged (blade tips bent, erosion, foreign object damage)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Compressor wheel — aluminium. Check every blade for damage. Bent tips mean foreign object ingestion (a piece of filter, a nut, a bolt left in the intake). Erosion on leading edges means long-term particulate ingestion. Any contact marks on the blade tips mean the wheel touched the housing — bearing failure in progress. Turbine wheel — nickel alloy. Same checks but also look for heat discolouration, cracking at blade roots (thermal fatigue), and erosion from exhaust particles."

**PPT Content:**
```
WHEEL INSPECTION

COMPRESSOR WHEEL (aluminium):
✅ All blade tips intact (no bending)
✅ No leading edge erosion
✅ No rub marks on blade tips (housing contact)
✅ No cracks at blade roots
✅ Compressor nut tight

TURBINE WHEEL (nickel alloy):
✅ No blade tip damage
✅ No cracking at blade roots (thermal fatigue)
✅ No excessive heat discolouration
✅ No erosion from exhaust particles

ANY DAMAGE → CHRA replacement (wheels cannot be repaired)
```

---

#### Slide 8: Wastegate & VGT Inspection
**Visual:** Wastegate actuator arm movement, VGT vane ring mechanism

**PPT Content:**
```
WASTEGATE INSPECTION:
• Actuator moves freely (push arm by hand)
• Valve seats properly when closed (visual)
• Actuator diaphragm holds vacuum (hand pump test)
• Electronic actuator responds to command (scan tool)

VGT/VNT INSPECTION:
• Vane ring moves freely through full range
• No sticking or binding (carbon buildup is #1 cause)
• Actuator linkage secure and undamaged
• Electronic actuator responds to scan tool command

VGT STUCK VANES:
• Most common turbo fault on diesel
• Caused by carbon/soot buildup from EGR gas
• Sometimes freed with penetrating oil + manual exercise
• If seized → turbine housing replacement or specialist clean
```

### **PRACTICAL: Turbo Disassembly & Inspection** (Slides 9-19, ~140 minutes)

#### Slide 9: Practical Overview
**PPT Content:**
```
PRACTICAL STATIONS (140 min)

Station 1 (40 min): DISASSEMBLY
• Remove V-band clamps, separate housings from CHRA
• Handle compressor and turbine wheels carefully

Station 2 (30 min): SHAFT PLAY MEASUREMENT
• Set up dial gauge on CHRA
• Measure radial play and axial play
• Record readings, compare to spec, verdict: pass/fail

Station 3 (30 min): WHEEL & HOUSING INSPECTION
• Inspect compressor wheel (all blades)
• Inspect turbine wheel (all blades)
• Check housings for rub marks (shaft was off-centre)

Station 4 (25 min): WASTEGATE/VGT CHECK
• Test wastegate actuator movement and vacuum hold
• Or test VGT vane movement through full range

DEBRIEF (15 min): Findings review, rebuild decision
```

**Instructor Action:** Provide at least one turbocharger per 2-3 learners. Used/core turbos work perfectly for this exercise. If VGT turbo available, prioritize it for Station 4.

#### Slide 18: Cartridge Rebuild Discussion
**Visual:** New CHRA next to worn CHRA — bearings, seals, piston ring visible

**Instructor Narration:**
> "In most workshops, a turbo rebuild means replacing the complete CHRA — the centre housing with new bearings, seals, shaft, and sometimes new wheels. The old housings are reused if undamaged. Full cartridge rebuilds with individual bearing replacement and wheel rebalancing are done by specialist turbo rebuild shops using VSR (Very High Speed) balancing machines. As a technician, your job is to inspect, diagnose, and decide: service (e.g., free stuck VGT vanes), replace CHRA, or replace complete turbo."

**PPT Content:**
```
CARTRIDGE REBUILD — WORKSHOP vs SPECIALIST

WORKSHOP LEVEL:
• Diagnose turbo fault (shaft play, wheel damage, VGT stuck)
• Replace complete CHRA (pre-balanced from supplier)
• Replace complete turbo assembly (new/remanufactured)
• Service wastegate/VGT if mechanism is serviceable

SPECIALIST LEVEL (turbo rebuild shop):
• Individual bearing replacement
• Shaft repair/replacement
• Wheel replacement
• VSR balancing (Very High Speed — up to 250,000 RPM)
• This requires £50,000+ equipment — not workshop-level

YOUR DECISION TREE:
Shaft play OK + wheels OK → Service VGT/wastegate if needed
Shaft play exceeded → Replace CHRA or complete turbo
Wheel damage → Replace CHRA or complete turbo
Housing damage → Replace complete turbo
```

#### Slide 19: Rebuild Reassembly Notes
**Visual:** CHRA being installed into turbine housing with new gaskets

**PPT Content:**
```
TURBO REASSEMBLY NOTES

IF REPLACING CHRA:
1. Clean both housings thoroughly (carbon removal)
2. New gaskets/seals between CHRA and housings
3. Hand-tighten V-band clamps first, check alignment
4. Tighten V-band clamps to spec
5. PRE-LUBE: Fill oil feed with clean engine oil
   (prevents dry-start bearing damage on first run)
6. New oil feed and drain line gaskets/seals
7. Connect coolant lines with new seals

⚠ ALWAYS pre-lube — the #1 cause of premature turbo failure
   after rebuild is dry-start bearing damage
⚠ ALWAYS use new oil feed/drain gaskets
⚠ ALWAYS check oil drain is not restricted
```

#### Slide 22: Turbo Care Golden Rules
**PPT Content:**
```
TURBO GOLDEN RULES

1. OIL is the turbo's lifeblood — correct grade, clean, correct level
2. ALWAYS pre-lube after any oil system work or turbo replacement
3. Let the engine IDLE for 30 seconds before shutdown after hard driving
   (lets the turbo cool with oil flowing — prevents coking)
4. NEVER rev a cold engine hard (oil hasn't reached the turbo bearings)
5. Restricted oil drain = turbo death (oil floods bearings, smoke, failure)
6. Dirty air filter = compressor wheel erosion over time
7. If shaft play is excessive → DON'T RUN IT (wheel contacts housing)
```

## 📋 Assessment Checkpoint

- Turbo correctly disassembled without blade damage
- Shaft radial play measured within ±0.01mm of instructor's reading
- Shaft axial play measured within ±0.01mm of instructor's reading
- All blades inspected — any damage correctly identified and documented
- Wastegate/VGT mechanism checked for free operation
- Correct rebuild decision stated (service/CHRA/complete) with justification

---

## 🔑 Key Takeaways

1. The CHRA (Centre Housing Rotating Assembly) is the turbo's core — shaft, bearings, wheels
2. Radial play limit ~0.05-0.08mm, axial play limit ~0.025-0.05mm
3. Any wheel blade damage = CHRA or turbo replacement
4. VGT stuck vanes is the most common diesel turbo fault
5. Turbo balancing is specialist equipment — workshops replace the CHRA
6. ALWAYS pre-lube before first start after turbo or oil system work
