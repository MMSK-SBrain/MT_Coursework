# Day 52: Harness Rebuild Exercise
## Instructor-Led Story Framework for PPT Preparation

**Duration:** 180 minutes (20 min briefing/theory + 160 min practical build)
**Approach:** Full-Build Project — Plan, Execute, Test, Document
**PPT Target:** 20-22 slides
**Week:** 11 of 11 — "The Craftsman" (Optional — Wiring, Welding, Body & Finishing)

---

## Session Objectives (For Instructor)

By the end of this session, learners will be able to:
- Plan a harness build from a pin-out diagram — identify wire count, lengths, connector types, and routing path
- Cut wires to specified lengths with appropriate service loops
- Crimp pin terminals onto wire ends to OEM standard (correct crimp zone, insulation grip, conductor grip)
- Assemble connector shells with correctly positioned terminals and engaged secondary locks
- Apply loom tape wrapping in the correct technique (half-overlap spiral) using appropriate tape type
- Fit grommets at panel pass-through points
- Route a completed harness on a mock-up board following a specified path
- Test every circuit for continuity and every terminal for mechanical retention (tug test)
- Create a pin-out diagram documenting the completed harness

**This is the most practical day of the entire 11-week program.** The theory-to-practice ratio is 10:90. Learners spend nearly three hours building a real harness section with their own hands. This is the capstone craft exercise that gives them something physical to be proud of.

#### Slide 8: Step 3 — Strip and Crimp Terminals

**Instructor Narration:**
> "This is the step where quality matters most. Strip 4 to 6mm of insulation — check your terminal specification sheet for the exact strip length. **PPT Content:**
```
STEP 3: STRIP & CRIMP (30 minutes — 12 terminals)

Strip procedure:
1. Select correct stripper slot for wire gauge
2. Strip 4-6mm (check terminal spec)
3. Inspect: clean strands, no nicks, no missing strands
4. If damaged: cut 10mm back and re-strip

Crimp procedure:
1. Select correct terminal for wire gauge
2. Insert stripped end into crimp barrel
3. Conductor visible in inspection window
4. Insulation sits under insulation grip tabs
5. Place in ratcheting crimper — correct die position
6. Squeeze until ratchet releases (full compression)
7. Inspect crimp profile
8. TUG TEST — firm pull, terminal must not separate

You are crimping 12 terminals (6 wires x 2 ends).
Take your time on the first 4. Speed comes naturally.

FAILED CRIMP = cut terminal off, strip again, re-crimp.
Do not attempt to re-crimp a failed terminal.
```

#### Slide 9: Step 3 — Crimp Quality Reference
**Visual:** Comparison chart — good crimp vs bad crimp. Shows: correct crimp (conductor visible, insulation gripped, correct profile), over-crimped (crushed barrel), under-crimped (loose, fails tug test), conductor too short (not visible in window), insulation in crimp zone (no conductor contact).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Here's your quality reference. A good crimp looks like this — the barrel wraps symmetrically around the conductor, strands are visible in the window, and the insulation grip holds the wire jacket. An over-crimp crushes the barrel and can sever strands. An under-crimp doesn't compress enough — it'll pass the tug test in your hand but fail under vibration. If the conductor doesn't reach the window, your strip length was too short. If insulation is in the crimp zone, the conductor isn't making full contact. Compare every one of your crimps against this chart."

**PPT Content:**
```
CRIMP QUALITY REFERENCE

GOOD CRIMP:
- Conductor visible in inspection window
- Barrel symmetrically compressed
- Insulation grip holds wire jacket
- No stray strands
- Passes tug test with firm pull

COMMON DEFECTS:
Over-crimped:   Barrel crushed, strands may be severed
Under-crimped:  Loose barrel, fails tug test under vibration
Short strip:    Conductor not visible in window — poor contact
Insulation in barrel: Conductor not fully seated — high resistance
Bell-mouth:     Barrel flared at entry — conductor not captured

IF IN DOUBT: Cut it off and crimp a new terminal.
A marginal crimp today is a breakdown next year.
```

#### Slide 11: Step 5 — Loom Tape Wrapping
**Visual:** Photo sequence showing half-overlap spiral wrapping technique — start with anchor wrap, spiral at 45 degrees, finish with anchor wrap.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Now the harness starts to look like a harness. Gather the six wires for the main trunk — the section from Connector A to the branch point. Hold them parallel, not twisted. Start wrapping at the Connector A end with two anchor wraps — full overlaps to secure the starting point. Then spiral at 45 degrees with half-overlap. Consistent tension — tight enough to hold, not so tight that you deform the tape. Use PVC tape for the section from Connector A to the grommet (simulated engine bay), and fleece tape from the grommet to the branch point (simulated cabin)."

**PPT Content:**
```
STEP 5: LOOM TAPE WRAPPING (20 minutes)

Main trunk (Connector A to branch point at 800mm):

Section 1 — Connector A to grommet (0-500mm): PVC TAPE
Section 2 — Grommet to branch point (500-800mm): FLEECE TAPE

Wrapping technique:
1. Align wires PARALLEL (not twisted)
2. Start with 2x anchor wraps (full overlap) at connector end
3. Spiral wrap at ~45 degree angle
4. Half-overlap: each wrap covers half of previous wrap
5. Consistent tension throughout
6. End with 2x anchor wraps at branch point

Branch (branch point to Connectors B & C):
- Main continuation (4 wires to Connector B): Fleece tape
- Branch leg (2 wires to Connector C): Fleece tape
- Branch junction: wrap both legs together for 30mm, then separate

COMMON MISTAKES:
- Wrapping too loosely (tape slides, exposes wire)
- Wrapping too tightly (tape stretches, reduces coverage)
- Forgetting to change tape type at grommet transition
```

#### Slide 14: Step 8 — Testing Every Circuit
**Visual:** Multimeter connected to harness at Connector A pins, probing Connector B and C pins. Test matrix chart.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The build is not complete until EVERY circuit is tested. Set your multimeter to continuity mode. Probe Pin A1 at one end, Pin B1 at the other end. You should hear the continuity beep and see near-zero ohms. Work through every circuit on the test matrix. Then check for short circuits — probe between adjacent pins to confirm they're NOT connected. A single failed circuit means you go back and find the fault — bad crimp, wrong pin position, broken wire. A single short means wires are touching where they shouldn't."

#### Slide 16: Common Build Problems & Solutions
**Visual:** Troubleshooting flowchart — failed continuity leads to check crimp, check pin position, check wire for break. Short circuit leads to check for stray strands, check tape integrity, check pin positions.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "If your continuity test fails on a circuit, work backwards. Is the terminal properly crimped? Pull it out of the connector and inspect the crimp. Is it in the correct pin position? Compare against your diagram. Is the wire intact? Flex the harness gently while monitoring continuity — an intermittent break will show up. If you find a short circuit between adjacent pins, look for stray conductor strands bridging between crimp barrels in the connector, or wires touching through damaged insulation."

**PPT Content:**
```
TROUBLESHOOTING BUILD PROBLEMS

FAILED CONTINUITY (open circuit):
1. Check CRIMP — extract terminal, inspect
   → Bad crimp: re-crimp with new terminal
2. Check PIN POSITION — is terminal in correct cavity?
   → Wrong pin: extract and re-position
3. Check WIRE — flex harness while monitoring meter
   → Broken wire: replace wire section

SHORT CIRCUIT (unwanted connection):
1. Check for STRAY STRANDS bridging in connector
   → Trim strands, re-crimp if needed
2. Check TAPE integrity — wires touching through gap?
   → Add tape at damage point
3. Check PIN POSITIONS — two wires in same cavity?
   → Extract and correct

FAILED TUG TEST (loose terminal):
1. Check crimp — under-crimped (barrel too loose)
   → Cannot re-crimp — must cut and use new terminal
2. Check terminal lock tab — not engaged?
   → Push terminal further in until click

Every problem has a solution. Diagnose, don't guess.
```

---

## Key Takeaways

1. Plan the work, then work the plan — study the pin-out before cutting a single wire
2. Crimp quality is everything — inspect every terminal before insertion
3. The tug test is non-negotiable — every terminal, every connector, every time
4. Loom tape selection matches the environment — PVC for underbonnet, fleece for cabin
5. Grommets make the difference between a 10-year repair and a 10-month failure
6. Test every circuit AND check for shorts between adjacent circuits
7. Documentation turns a repair into a professional work product
8. Self-inspection before asking for inspector sign-off is a professional habit
