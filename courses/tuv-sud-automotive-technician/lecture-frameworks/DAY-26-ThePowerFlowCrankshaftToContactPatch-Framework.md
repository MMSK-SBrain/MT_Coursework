#### Slide 6: Link 1 — The Flywheel
**Visual:** Cross-section showing crankshaft rear end bolted to a heavy disc flywheel. Cutaway reveals the flywheel's mass, ring gear on the perimeter, and clutch friction surface. Inset diagram: smooth vs jerky power pulses.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The crankshaft doesn't spin smoothly. Remember from Week 3 — a four-cylinder engine fires once every 180 degrees of crankshaft rotation. Between power strokes, momentum dips. The flywheel fixes this. It's a heavy disc — typically 8 to 15 kilograms — bolted to the back of the crankshaft. Its mass stores rotational energy and smooths out the power pulses into a more constant rotation. Think of it as a momentum reservoir. The flywheel also has two other jobs: its outer ring gear meshes with the starter motor to crank the engine, and its rear face is the friction surface the clutch presses against. Three jobs, one heavy disc."

**PPT Content:**
```
LINK 1: THE FLYWHEEL

What it is:
- Heavy disc (8-15 kg) bolted to the crankshaft rear end

Three functions:
1. SMOOTHS ROTATION — stores rotational energy between power strokes
   Engine fires unevenly; flywheel's inertia fills the gaps
2. STARTER ENGAGEMENT — ring gear on perimeter meshes with starter motor pinion
3. CLUTCH FRICTION SURFACE — rear face is machined smooth for clutch disc contact

Types:
- Solid flywheel (single mass — simple, robust)
- Dual-mass flywheel (DMF) — two masses connected by springs
  Absorbs torsional vibration, smoother, but wears out (common service item)

KEY POINT: Without the flywheel, the engine would vibrate
violently and the clutch would have nothing to grip.
```

#### Slide 7: Link 2 — The Clutch (Manual Transmission)
**Visual:** Exploded view of a clutch assembly — flywheel, clutch disc (friction material both sides), pressure plate, diaphragm spring, release bearing (throwout bearing), clutch fork. Arrows showing clamping force and release action.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The clutch answers our second problem: how do you disconnect the engine from the wheels? You need to disconnect when you stop, when you change gears, and when you start from standstill. Here's how it works. The clutch disc sits between the flywheel and the pressure plate. When the clutch pedal is released — foot off — the diaphragm spring pushes the pressure plate hard against the clutch disc, which is pressed against the flywheel. Friction locks everything together. The engine and gearbox spin as one. When you press the clutch pedal, the release bearing pushes into the centre of the diaphragm spring, which releases the clamping force. The clutch disc is now free — engine spins but the gearbox input shaft doesn't. Disconnected. Simple, elegant, and it wears out — the friction material gets thinner over time, just like brake pads. Clutch replacement is a major job because you have to remove the gearbox to access it."

**PPT Content:**
```
LINK 2: THE CLUTCH (Manual Transmission)

Purpose: CONNECT and DISCONNECT engine from gearbox

Components:
- FLYWHEEL — engine side, always spinning with engine
- CLUTCH DISC — friction material on both sides, splined to gearbox input shaft
- PRESSURE PLATE — clamps the clutch disc against the flywheel
- DIAPHRAGM SPRING — provides clamping force (300-500 kg!)
- RELEASE BEARING (throwout bearing) — actuated by clutch pedal
- CLUTCH FORK / HYDRAULIC ACTUATOR — transfers pedal force to release bearing

Operation:
PEDAL UP (engaged): Spring clamps disc → friction locks engine to gearbox
PEDAL DOWN (disengaged): Release bearing pushes spring → disc released → disconnected

Wear item: Friction material wears thin over time (80,000-150,000 km typical life)
Service note: Gearbox must be removed for clutch replacement
```

#### Slide 8: Link 2 (Alternate) — The Torque Converter (Automatic Transmission)
**Visual:** Cutaway of a torque converter showing impeller (pump), turbine, stator, and lock-up clutch. Fluid flow arrows showing hydraulic coupling. Comparison diagram: clutch = mechanical friction, torque converter = hydraulic coupling.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Automatic transmissions don't use a friction clutch. Instead, they use a torque converter — a fluid coupling. Think of it like two fans facing each other. One fan blows air — that's the impeller, connected to the engine. The other fan spins because the air hits it — that's the turbine, connected to the gearbox. But instead of air, we use transmission fluid. The impeller slings fluid outward. The fluid hits the turbine and makes it spin. There's no mechanical contact — which is why an automatic car doesn't stall when you stop. The stator in the middle redirects the fluid to multiply torque at low speeds. At higher speeds, a lock-up clutch physically connects the impeller and turbine for maximum efficiency — no more fluid slippage. We'll go deep into this on Day 28."

**PPT Content:**
```
LINK 2 (AUTOMATIC): THE TORQUE CONVERTER

Purpose: Hydraulic coupling — connects engine to gearbox WITHOUT mechanical friction

Components:
- IMPELLER (pump) — bolted to flywheel/flexplate, always spins with engine
- TURBINE — connected to gearbox input shaft
- STATOR — redirects fluid flow to multiply torque (1.5x to 2.5x at stall)
- LOCK-UP CLUTCH — mechanically locks impeller to turbine at cruise speed

How it works:
- Engine spins impeller → fluid is flung outward → hits turbine → turbine spins
- No mechanical contact = car won't stall at standstill
- Stator multiplies torque at low speed (like an extra gear ratio)
- Lock-up clutch eliminates slip at highway speed (fuel efficiency)

CLUTCH vs TORQUE CONVERTER:
Manual clutch: Friction, binary (on/off), driver-controlled
Torque converter: Fluid, progressive, automatic, torque multiplication

Day 28: Deep dive into torque converters and automatic transmissions
```

#### Slide 9: Link 3 — The Gearbox: Why We Need One
**Visual:** Two-part diagram. Left: graph showing engine torque curve (peaks at ~3500 RPM) and power curve (peaks at ~5500 RPM). Right: diagram showing the same engine trying to move a car at 10 km/h (needs high torque, low speed) vs 120 km/h (needs lower torque, higher speed). The mismatch is highlighted.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "This is the core concept of the entire day. The engine has a limited RPM range — let's say 800 to 6500 RPM. Its torque peaks at maybe 3500 RPM and then falls off. But the wheels need to turn at vastly different speeds: very slowly from standstill, very fast at highway speed. And the torque demand is the opposite — enormous torque to get the car rolling from zero, much less torque to cruise at 120. The engine can't do both at the same time. The gearbox is a speed-and-torque converter. Low gear: the gearbox multiplies engine torque but reduces output speed. High gear: less torque multiplication but higher output speed. It keeps the engine in its sweet spot — the RPM range where it makes useful power — across the entire speed range of the vehicle."

**PPT Content:**
```
WHY WE NEED A GEARBOX

THE MISMATCH:
- Engine RPM range: ~800 to 6500 RPM
- Wheel RPM at 10 km/h: ~70 RPM
- Wheel RPM at 120 km/h: ~850 RPM

Engine alone CANNOT span this range with useful torque.

THE SOLUTION: GEAR RATIOS
- LOW GEAR: High ratio (e.g., 3.5:1)
  Engine turns 3.5 times for every 1 turn of output
  Result: HIGH TORQUE at output, LOW SPEED
  Use: Starting from standstill, climbing hills

- HIGH GEAR: Low ratio (e.g., 0.8:1)
  Engine turns 0.8 times for every 1 turn of output
  Result: LOW TORQUE at output, HIGH SPEED
  Use: Motorway cruising, fuel economy

THE GEARBOX KEEPS THE ENGINE IN ITS POWER BAND
ACROSS THE FULL SPEED RANGE OF THE VEHICLE.
```

#### Slide 10: Gear Ratios — The Maths
**Visual:** Clear diagram of two meshing gears — a small driving gear (15 teeth) meshing with a large driven gear (45 teeth). Arrows showing rotation direction. Formulas displayed prominently. Numerical example worked through step by step.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's do the maths — and I promise it's simple. A gear ratio is the number of teeth on the driven gear divided by the number of teeth on the driving gear. If the driving gear has 15 teeth and the driven gear has 45 teeth, the ratio is 45 divided by 15, which equals 3:1. What does that mean? For every 3 turns of the input, the output turns once. Speed is divided by 3. But torque is multiplied by 3. That's the fundamental trade-off — gears trade speed for torque, or torque for speed. Nothing is free. Let's work through a real example. If the engine produces 200 Nm at 3000 RPM, and we're in a gear with a 3:1 ratio — what's the output? Output torque is 200 times 3 equals 600 Nm. Output speed is 3000 divided by 3 equals 1000 RPM. We tripled the torque but cut the speed to one-third. That's what first gear does."

**PPT Content:**
```
GEAR RATIOS — THE MATHS

GEAR RATIO = Teeth on DRIVEN gear / Teeth on DRIVING gear
           = Output turns needed per Input turn

EXAMPLE:
Driving gear: 15 teeth
Driven gear: 45 teeth
Ratio: 45 / 15 = 3.0 : 1

WHAT A 3:1 RATIO MEANS:
- Input turns 3 times → Output turns 1 time
- Output SPEED = Input Speed / Ratio = 3000 RPM / 3 = 1000 RPM
- Output TORQUE = Input Torque x Ratio = 200 Nm x 3 = 600 Nm

THE FUNDAMENTAL TRADE-OFF:
+-----------+------------------+------------------+
| Gear      | Speed            | Torque           |
+-----------+------------------+------------------+
| Low (3:1) | DIVIDED by 3     | MULTIPLIED by 3  |
| High (1:1)| Same as input    | Same as input    |
| OD (0.8:1)| MULTIPLIED by 1.25| DIVIDED by 1.25 |
+-----------+------------------+------------------+

SPEED x TORQUE is roughly constant (minus friction losses)
```

#### Slide 11: Real Gearbox Ratios — A Worked Example
**Visual:** Table showing a real 6-speed manual gearbox with gear ratios, final drive ratio, and calculated output for each gear at 3000 RPM engine speed with 200 Nm engine torque. Include overall ratio (gear ratio x final drive ratio).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's look at a real gearbox — a typical 6-speed manual. First gear: 3.77 to 1. Second: 2.09. Third: 1.47. Fourth: 1.10. Fifth: 0.93. Sixth: 0.78. And the final drive — that's the differential ratio — is 3.94 to 1. Here's the key: the overall ratio is the gear ratio TIMES the final drive ratio. So in first gear, the overall ratio is 3.77 times 3.94 equals 14.85 to 1. With the engine at 3000 RPM producing 200 Nm, the wheels get 200 times 14.85 equals 2,970 Nm at only 202 RPM. That's massive torque at low speed — exactly what you need to pull away from a standstill. In sixth gear: 0.78 times 3.94 equals 3.07 overall. Output torque drops to 614 Nm but wheel speed jumps to 977 RPM — that's about 115 km/h. You've traded torque for speed."

#### Slide 12: Link 4 — Output Shaft, Propshaft & Driveshaft
**Visual:** Three drivetrain layouts showing power flow after the gearbox: (1) RWD with propshaft running to rear differential, (2) FWD with short driveshafts to front wheels, (3) AWD with propshaft to rear plus driveshafts to front. Universal joints and CV joints highlighted.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Power exits the gearbox through the output shaft. What happens next depends on the layout. In a rear-wheel-drive car, a propshaft — a long spinning shaft — carries the power from the gearbox at the front to the differential at the rear axle. The propshaft uses universal joints — Hooke's joints — to accommodate the angle between the gearbox and the differential, and to allow the rear axle to move up and down with the suspension. In a front-wheel-drive car, the gearbox and differential are combined into one unit — the transaxle — mounted at the front. Short driveshafts with CV joints — Constant Velocity joints — connect directly to the front wheels. CV joints are essential because the front wheels steer and bounce simultaneously — they need to transmit power through constantly changing angles. The rubber CV boot protects the joint and keeps grease in — when it tears, dirt gets in, the joint wears out, and you get clicking on turns."

**PPT Content:**
```
LINK 4: PROPSHAFT & DRIVESHAFTS

After the gearbox, power must reach the driven wheels:

RWD (Rear-Wheel Drive):
- PROPSHAFT: Long shaft from front gearbox to rear differential
- Uses UNIVERSAL JOINTS (Hooke's joints) to handle angles
- Centre bearing supports the shaft mid-length

FWD (Front-Wheel Drive):
- No propshaft needed — gearbox + differential = TRANSAXLE (front-mounted)
- Short DRIVESHAFTS (half shafts) connect transaxle to front wheels
- Uses CV JOINTS (Constant Velocity) — inner and outer
  Inner CV: Handles plunge (length change as suspension moves)
  Outer CV: Handles steering angle + power delivery

CV BOOT — rubber cover protecting the CV joint
- Contains grease, keeps out dirt
- When it tears → joint fails → clicking noise on turns
- COMMON SERVICE ITEM — inspect on every service

AWD: Propshaft to rear + driveshafts to front (both systems present)
```

#### Slide 13: Link 5 — The Differential
**Visual:** Animated-style cutaway of an open differential showing ring gear, pinion gear, spider gears (planet gears), and side gears. Two diagrams: (1) driving straight — both wheels same speed, (2) turning — outer wheel faster, inner wheel slower. Speed difference values shown.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Here's the third engineering problem we identified. When a car turns a corner, the outside wheel travels a longer path than the inside wheel. If both wheels were locked to the same shaft, one would have to scrub — skid — through every turn. That would destroy tyres, stress the drivetrain, and make the car undriveable. The differential solves this. The propshaft turns the pinion gear, which meshes with the ring gear. The ring gear carries the differential cage. Inside the cage, spider gears — also called planet gears — sit between the two side gears connected to the axle shafts. When the car drives straight, the spider gears don't spin on their own axis — both side gears turn at the same speed. But in a corner, the spider gears rotate, allowing the outer wheel to speed up and the inner wheel to slow down. The total speed of both wheels always equals twice the ring gear speed. It's a beautiful piece of engineering — simple gears solving a complex problem."

**PPT Content:**
```
LINK 5: THE DIFFERENTIAL

THE PROBLEM:
In a corner, outer wheel must travel FURTHER than inner wheel
- 90-degree turn: outer wheel may travel 5-10% more distance
- If both wheels are locked: tyres scrub, drivetrain binds

THE SOLUTION: THE DIFFERENTIAL
Components:
- PINION GEAR — driven by propshaft/driveshaft
- RING GEAR — meshes with pinion, carries the diff cage
- SPIDER GEARS (planet gears) — mounted on a cross pin in the cage
- SIDE GEARS — connected to left and right axle shafts

How it works:
STRAIGHT: Spider gears don't rotate → both wheels turn same speed
TURNING: Spider gears rotate on cross pin → outer wheel speeds up,
         inner wheel slows down

RULE: Left wheel speed + Right wheel speed = 2 x Ring gear speed

FINAL DRIVE RATIO: The ring/pinion gear ratio
(e.g., 3.94:1 — the "last" gear ratio before the wheels)

Types: Open differential (standard), LSD, locking diff — more in Day 27
```

#### Slide 14: Link 6 — Axle Shafts to Wheels
**Visual:** Diagram showing axle shafts (half shafts) from the differential to the wheel hubs. Close-up of wheel hub assembly — bearings, hub, brake disc mounting, wheel studs. Show CV joint at the differential end and wheel bearing at the hub end.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The final mechanical links. Axle shafts — also called half shafts — carry the torque from the differential to the wheel hubs. Each shaft has a splined connection at the differential end and a splined or flanged connection at the wheel hub. The wheel hub contains the wheel bearing, which supports the entire weight of that corner of the car while allowing the wheel to rotate freely. The brake disc bolts to the hub. The wheel bolts to the hub. So the hub is the intersection of the power delivery system, the braking system, and the suspension system — three systems meeting at one point. When a wheel bearing fails, you hear a humming or growling noise that changes with speed and direction. It's one of the most common drivetrain wear items."

**PPT Content:**
```
LINK 6: AXLE SHAFTS & WHEEL HUBS

AXLE SHAFTS (Half Shafts):
- Connect differential to wheel hubs
- Splined at both ends for torque transfer
- FWD: include CV joints (handle steering + suspension movement)
- RWD: simpler — semi-floating or full-floating design

WHEEL HUB ASSEMBLY:
- WHEEL BEARING — supports vehicle weight, allows rotation
  (pressed-in or bolt-on, sealed for life or serviceable)
- HUB — central mounting point
- Brake disc mounts to hub face
- Wheel studs/bolts attach wheel to hub

THE HUB IS WHERE THREE SYSTEMS MEET:
1. Power delivery (axle shaft → hub → wheel)
2. Braking (caliper → pads → disc on hub)
3. Suspension (steering knuckle → bearing → hub)

Common failure: Wheel bearing wear
Symptom: Humming/growling that changes with speed or direction
Service: Press-fit replacement or bolt-on hub unit
```

#### Slide 15: The Complete Chain — Summary Diagram
**Visual:** Full-width horizontal flow diagram showing every component in sequence with representative torque and RPM values at each stage. Engine: 200 Nm @ 3000 RPM. After 1st gear + final drive: 2970 Nm @ 202 RPM at wheels. Friction losses noted (~15% total).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's pull it all together. Engine produces 200 Nm at 3000 RPM. Flywheel smooths it. Clutch transmits it. Gearbox in first gear multiplies torque by 3.77. The final drive in the differential multiplies again by 3.94. Overall multiplication: 14.85 times. The wheels receive nearly 3000 Newton-metres of torque at about 200 RPM. We've lost roughly 15% to friction in bearings, gears, seals, and fluid — so the actual figure at the tyre contact patch is about 2500 Nm. But that's still more than ten times what the engine originally produced. That's the beauty of gear ratios. You can't create energy from nothing — but you CAN convert high-speed low-torque rotation into low-speed high-torque rotation. It's like a lever: the gearbox is a rotary lever."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE COMPLETE CHAIN — POWER FLOW SUMMARY

ENGINE:           200 Nm @ 3,000 RPM
   ↓ Flywheel (smooths pulses)
   ↓ Clutch (transmits or disconnects)
GEARBOX (1st):    200 x 3.77 = 754 Nm @ 796 RPM
   ↓ Output shaft
   ↓ Propshaft / driveshaft
DIFFERENTIAL:     754 x 3.94 = 2,971 Nm @ 202 RPM
   ↓ Axle shafts
WHEELS:           ~2,525 Nm @ 202 RPM (after ~15% friction losses)
   ↓
CONTACT PATCH:    Tyre converts torque into TRACTIVE FORCE on the road

TORQUE MULTIPLIED: 200 Nm → ~2,525 Nm (12.6x)
SPEED REDUCED:     3,000 RPM → 202 RPM (1/14.85)
ENERGY CONSERVED:  Speed x Torque ≈ constant (minus losses)

THE GEARBOX IS A ROTARY LEVER.
```

#### Slide 17: The Parallel Electrical Power Chain
**Visual:** Circular diagram showing the electrical chain: Battery --> Starter Motor --> cranks Engine --> Engine runs --> drives Alternator --> charges Battery --> powers all Electronics. Arrows show energy flow in a loop. Red "break" symbol at each link showing what happens if one fails.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "While you've been following the mechanical chain, there's been a parallel chain running alongside it — the electrical power chain. And here's the critical insight: these two chains depend on each other completely. The battery stores electrical energy. When you turn the key, the starter motor draws 100 to 300 amps from the battery to crank the engine. Once the engine is running, it drives the alternator through a belt. The alternator generates electricity — typically 13.5 to 14.8 volts — which charges the battery and powers every electrical system in the car: lights, ECUs, fuel pumps, ignition coils, sensors, actuators, everything. If the battery is dead, the starter can't crank, the engine can't run, the alternator can't generate. If the alternator fails, the battery drains, the engine eventually stops because the ECU and ignition system lose power. If the engine stops, the alternator stops, and you're running on battery alone — maybe 30 minutes before everything dies. Both chains are one system."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE PARALLEL ELECTRICAL POWER CHAIN

BATTERY (stores electrical energy — 12V, 40-90 Ah)
   ↓
STARTER MOTOR (cranks engine — draws 100-300 A briefly)
   ↓
ENGINE RUNS (combustion cycle sustains itself)
   ↓
ALTERNATOR (belt-driven by engine — generates 13.5-14.8V)
   ↓
CHARGES BATTERY + POWERS ALL ELECTRONICS
   ↓
(cycle continues...)

THE DEPENDENCY LOOP:
- No battery → No starter → No engine → No alternator
- No alternator → Battery drains → No power → Engine dies
- No engine → No alternator → No charging → Battery dies

BOTH CHAINS ARE ONE SYSTEM.
The mechanical chain makes the car move.
The electrical chain keeps the car alive.
They cannot exist without each other.
```

#### Slide 18: Battery, Starter & Alternator — Key Specs

**Instructor Narration:**

#### Slide 19: FWD — Front-Wheel Drive Layout
**Visual:** Top-down and side-view diagram of a FWD vehicle. Engine mounted transversely at front. Transaxle (gearbox + differential combined) visible. Short driveshafts to front wheels with CV joints. Rear axle shown as simple beam or trailing arm — no drivetrain components.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Now let's look at how the mechanical chain is physically arranged in different vehicles. Front-wheel drive is the most common layout in passenger cars today. The engine sits transversely — sideways — at the front. The gearbox and differential are combined into one unit called a transaxle, also at the front. Short driveshafts with CV joints connect directly to the front wheels. There's no propshaft, no rear differential. The rear axle is purely for support — a simple beam or trailing arm design. Advantages: compact, efficient, lighter, more interior space, good traction in the wet because engine weight sits over the driven wheels. Disadvantages: the front wheels do everything — steer, drive, and carry most of the weight — which limits handling and tyre life."

**PPT Content:**
```
LAYOUT 1: FWD — FRONT-WHEEL DRIVE

Configuration:
- Engine: TRANSVERSE (sideways) at front
- Gearbox + Differential = TRANSAXLE (single unit, front-mounted)
- Driveshafts: Short, with CV joints to FRONT WHEELS
- Rear axle: No drivetrain — simple beam or trailing arm

Power path:
Engine → Transaxle (gearbox + diff) → CV driveshafts → Front wheels

Advantages:
+ Most compact layout — more cabin space
+ Lighter (fewer components)
+ Good wet-weather traction (weight over driven wheels)
+ Lower manufacturing cost

Disadvantages:
- Front wheels steer + drive + carry most weight (overloaded)
- Limited torque handling (CV joints have torque limits)
- Torque steer under hard acceleration
- Less balanced handling (front-heavy)

MOST COMMON: ~60% of passenger cars worldwide
Examples: VW Golf, Toyota Corolla, Honda Civic
```

#### Slide 20: RWD — Rear-Wheel Drive Layout
**Visual:** Top-down and side-view diagram of a RWD vehicle. Engine mounted longitudinally at front. Gearbox behind engine. Long propshaft running to rear differential. Short axle shafts to rear wheels. Front wheels steer only — no driveshaft.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Rear-wheel drive was the original layout and remains the choice for performance and luxury vehicles, trucks, and most commercial vehicles. The engine sits longitudinally — pointing front to back. The gearbox bolts directly behind the engine. A propshaft runs underneath the car to the rear differential, which is a separate unit bolted to the rear axle. Axle shafts deliver power to the rear wheels. The front wheels only steer — they're not driven. Advantages: better weight distribution front-to-rear, the front and rear wheels share the workload — fronts steer, rears drive. Better handling balance, can handle more torque, better for towing. Disadvantages: the propshaft takes up space through the cabin floor — that hump in the middle of the back seat. Heavier system, worse traction in the wet on flat ground because less weight sits over the rear driven wheels."

**PPT Content:**
```
LAYOUT 2: RWD — REAR-WHEEL DRIVE

Configuration:
- Engine: LONGITUDINAL (front-to-back) at front
- Gearbox: Directly behind engine
- PROPSHAFT: Runs front to rear, with universal joints
- Rear DIFFERENTIAL: Separate unit at rear axle
- Axle shafts: Short, to REAR WHEELS
- Front wheels: Steering only (not driven)

Power path:
Engine → Gearbox → Propshaft → Rear diff → Axle shafts → Rear wheels

Advantages:
+ Better weight distribution (50:50 ideal)
+ Front steers, rear drives — shared workload
+ Higher torque capacity (no CV joint limits)
+ Better handling balance
+ Better for towing (weight transfers rearward under load)

Disadvantages:
- Propshaft tunnel reduces cabin space
- Heavier (more components)
- Less traction on wet flat roads (less weight over driven wheels)
- Higher manufacturing cost

USED BY: BMW, Mercedes, sports cars, trucks, most commercial vehicles
```

#### Slide 21: AWD — All-Wheel Drive Layout
**Visual:** Top-down diagram showing full AWD system — front transaxle with driveshafts to front wheels, transfer case, propshaft to rear differential, driveshafts to rear wheels. Centre differential / transfer case highlighted.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "All-wheel drive combines both layouts. Power goes to all four wheels. A transfer case or centre differential sits between the front and rear axles and distributes torque between them. Some systems are full-time AWD — power always goes to all four wheels. Others are part-time or on-demand — normally FWD, but a clutch pack or electronic coupling can send torque to the rear when the front wheels slip. AWD gives maximum traction but at the cost of weight, complexity, and fuel consumption. You have essentially two of everything — front and rear differentials, driveshafts on both axles, a transfer case, and a propshaft. More components means more potential failure points and more expensive service."

**PPT Content:**
```
LAYOUT 3: AWD — ALL-WHEEL DRIVE

Configuration:
- Engine drives front axle AND rear axle
- TRANSFER CASE or CENTRE DIFFERENTIAL distributes torque front/rear
- Front driveshafts to front wheels + Propshaft to rear diff + Rear driveshafts

Types:
FULL-TIME AWD: All 4 wheels always driven (centre diff allows speed difference)
  Example: Audi quattro (Torsen), Subaru Symmetrical AWD
PART-TIME / ON-DEMAND AWD: Normally FWD, engages rear when slip detected
  Example: VW 4MOTION (Haldex), BMW xDrive (transfer case)

Power path:
Engine → Gearbox → Transfer case → Front diff → Front wheels
                                  → Propshaft → Rear diff → Rear wheels

Advantages:
+ Maximum traction (rain, snow, gravel, acceleration)
+ Improved stability and cornering grip

Disadvantages:
- Heaviest (most components)
- Most complex (more wear items)
- Highest fuel consumption
- Most expensive to service

GROWING: ~30% of new passenger cars in Europe, ~50% in North America
```

## Key Takeaways

2. A gearbox trades speed for torque (low gear) or torque for speed (high gear) — the fundamental gear ratio relationship

4. The clutch (manual) or torque converter (automatic) allows the engine to be disconnected from the drivetrain
5. The differential allows inner and outer wheels to turn at different speeds during cornering
6. FWD, RWD, and AWD each have distinct physical signatures visible under the vehicle
7. The electrical power chain (battery-starter-engine-alternator) is inseparable from the mechanical chain — each depends on the other
