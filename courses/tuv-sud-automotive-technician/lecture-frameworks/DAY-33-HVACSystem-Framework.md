**Narrative Voice:** Methodical, step-by-step. Follow the refrigerant molecule around the loop.

#### Slide 5: The Refrigeration Cycle — Overview
**Visual:** Complete refrigeration cycle diagram showing all four stages in a loop: Evaporation (inside cabin, low pressure, absorbs heat) -> Compression (raises pressure and temperature) -> Condensation (outside vehicle, high pressure, releases heat) -> Expansion (drops pressure) -> back to Evaporation. Colour-coded: blue for low-pressure side, red for high-pressure side. Each component labeled at its position in the cycle.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Here's the full refrigeration cycle. We'll follow the refrigerant molecule all the way around. Start at the evaporator — that's inside the dashboard. The refrigerant enters as a cold, low-pressure liquid mist. Warm cabin air blows across the evaporator fins. The refrigerant absorbs that heat and evaporates into a gas. That's where the cooling happens.
>
> The gas flows to the compressor — the heart of the system. The compressor squeezes it, dramatically raising its pressure and temperature. Now it's a hot, high-pressure gas. It flows to the condenser — mounted in front of the radiator, facing the outside air. Air flows across the condenser and the hot gas releases its heat to the atmosphere and condenses back into a liquid. Then it hits the expansion device — a valve or orifice tube that creates a restriction. Pressure drops suddenly, temperature drops, and the refrigerant becomes a cold, low-pressure liquid mist again. It re-enters the evaporator and the cycle repeats. Over and over, moving heat from inside to outside."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE REFRIGERATION CYCLE — FOUR STAGES

1. EVAPORATION (Evaporator — inside cabin)
   Cold low-pressure liquid --> absorbs cabin heat --> becomes gas
   THIS IS WHERE COOLING HAPPENS

2. COMPRESSION (Compressor — engine bay)
   Low-pressure gas --> compressed --> hot high-pressure gas
   THIS IS THE PUMP THAT DRIVES THE CYCLE

3. CONDENSATION (Condenser — front of vehicle)
   Hot high-pressure gas --> releases heat to outside air --> becomes liquid
   THIS IS WHERE HEAT IS DUMPED OUTSIDE

4. EXPANSION (TXV or Orifice Tube)
   High-pressure liquid --> pressure drops --> cold low-pressure mist
   THIS CREATES THE COLD ENTERING THE EVAPORATOR

CYCLE REPEATS CONTINUOUSLY
```

#### Slide 6: Stage 1 — Evaporation (Where Cooling Happens)
**Visual:** Cross-section of an evaporator inside the HVAC housing behind the dashboard. Warm cabin air flowing in from the top, cold air flowing out the bottom. Refrigerant path through evaporator tubes visible. Temperature labels: air entering at 35 degrees C, air leaving at 5-10 degrees C. Refrigerant entering at approximately 0-5 degrees C as a liquid-gas mix, leaving as a gas at approximately 5-10 degrees C.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The evaporator sits inside the HVAC housing under the dashboard. It looks like a small, thin radiator — a dense matrix of aluminium tubes and fins. Cold refrigerant — about 0 to 5 degrees Celsius — enters as a low-pressure liquid mist. The blower motor pushes warm cabin air across the evaporator surface. The refrigerant absorbs the heat and evaporates — changes from liquid to gas. That's the phase change doing the work. The air leaving the evaporator is now cold, typically 5 to 10 degrees Celsius.
>
> Notice something else: as the warm, humid air hits the cold evaporator surface, moisture condenses out of the air. That's the water you see dripping under a parked car with the AC running. The evaporator is also a dehumidifier — which is why you use AC for demisting even in winter."

#### Slide 7: Stage 2 — Compression (The Heart of the System)
**Visual:** Two compressor types side by side — left: belt-driven swash-plate compressor cutaway showing clutch, pulley, swash plate, and pistons; right: electric scroll compressor from an EV showing the integrated electric motor and three-phase HV connector. Arrows showing low-pressure gas entering and high-pressure gas exiting.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The compressor is the pump that drives the entire cycle. It takes the low-pressure gas from the evaporator and compresses it into a high-pressure, high-temperature gas. Without it, nothing moves.
>
> On an ICE vehicle, the compressor is belt-driven off the engine crankshaft. It has an electromagnetic clutch — the AC clutch — that engages and disengages the compressor from the belt. When you press the AC button, the ECU energises the clutch, the compressor engages, and the cycle starts.
>
> On EVs and hybrids, there's no engine belt. Instead, the compressor has its own built-in electric motor, powered by the high-voltage battery. This is significant for two reasons. One: the compressor works at any speed, regardless of engine RPM — better efficiency. Two: it's a high-voltage component, typically 200-400 volts. That means HV safety protocols apply when servicing it. Remember your Day 32 HV safety training — it applies here.
>
> A failing compressor is loud — grinding, squealing, or seizing. And if it seizes on a belt-driven system, it can snap the serpentine belt and take out your alternator and power steering pump at the same time."

**PPT Content:**
```
STAGE 2: COMPRESSION — THE COMPRESSOR

Job: Pump refrigerant through the system
     Raise pressure and temperature of the gas

BELT-DRIVEN (ICE vehicles):
• Driven by serpentine belt from crankshaft
• Electromagnetic clutch engages/disengages
• Common type: swash-plate (variable displacement)
• Failure risk: seized compressor can snap belt

ELECTRIC (EV / Hybrid vehicles):
• Built-in electric motor (three-phase, 200-400V HV)
• Runs independently of engine speed
• Better efficiency — variable speed via inverter
• HV SAFETY: Treat as high-voltage component!

Compressor oil: PAG oil (ICE) or POE oil (EV)
— lubricates internal components
— specific to refrigerant type — NEVER mix oils
```

#### Slide 8: Stage 3 — Condensation (Dumping Heat Outside)
**Visual:** Front-of-vehicle photo showing the condenser mounted directly in front of the engine radiator. Air flow arrows showing ambient air passing through condenser fins. Diagram inset: hot gas enters top, cooled liquid exits bottom. Temperature labels: gas entering at approximately 60-70 degrees C, liquid exiting at approximately 40-50 degrees C.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The condenser is where the system dumps heat to the atmosphere. It's mounted right at the front of the vehicle, in front of the engine radiator. Driving speed forces air through it, and an electric fan provides airflow when stationary.
>
> The hot, high-pressure gas from the compressor enters the top of the condenser at 60 to 70 degrees Celsius. As ambient air flows across the fins, the gas releases its heat and condenses — changes from gas back to liquid. The liquid exits the bottom at around 40 to 50 degrees Celsius, still under high pressure.
>
> If the condenser can't shed heat, the entire system suffers. Blocked condenser fins — bugs, debris, damaged fins — reduce AC performance dramatically. A front-end collision that damages the condenser kills the AC. And if the cooling fan fails, AC performance drops to near zero in traffic when there's no airflow from vehicle speed."

**PPT Content:**
```
STAGE 3: CONDENSATION — THE CONDENSER

Location: Front of vehicle, AHEAD of engine radiator
Type: Multi-flow aluminium heat exchanger

WHAT HAPPENS:
Hot high-pressure gas enters (~60-70 deg C)
Ambient air passes through condenser fins
Gas RELEASES heat to outside air
Gas condenses into high-pressure LIQUID (~40-50 deg C)

AIRFLOW SOURCES:
• Ram air from vehicle speed
• Electric cooling fan(s) at low speed / standstill

COMMON PROBLEMS:
• Blocked fins (bugs, road debris) — reduced performance
• Bent/damaged fins — restricted airflow
• Front-end collision damage — condenser replacement

CONDENSER = THE OUTDOOR HEAT EXCHANGER
```

#### Slide 9: Stage 4 — Expansion (The Pressure Drop)
**Visual:** Two expansion devices shown side by side — left: Thermostatic Expansion Valve (TXV) with sensing bulb, equalizer tube, and diaphragm labeled; right: Fixed Orifice Tube (simple brass tube with mesh screens). Pressure/temperature graph showing the sudden drop as refrigerant passes through.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Between the condenser and evaporator sits the expansion device. Its job is simple but critical: create a sudden pressure drop. The high-pressure liquid from the condenser hits this restriction and its pressure drops dramatically. When pressure drops, temperature drops. The refrigerant becomes a cold, low-pressure liquid-gas mist — ready to absorb heat in the evaporator.
>
> There are two main types. The Thermostatic Expansion Valve — TXV — is a precision device. It has a sensing bulb clamped to the evaporator outlet that measures superheat — how much the gas has warmed above its boiling point. Based on that reading, the TXV adjusts its opening to meter exactly the right amount of refrigerant into the evaporator. Too much and you get liquid flooding back to the compressor — that damages it. Too little and the evaporator doesn't cool efficiently.
>
> The second type is the orifice tube — a fixed restriction with no moving parts. Simpler, cheaper, but less precise. Systems with orifice tubes use an accumulator instead of a receiver-drier, which we'll cover next. Know which your vehicle uses — the service procedure differs."

**PPT Content:**
```
STAGE 4: EXPANSION — THE PRESSURE DROP

Job: Reduce refrigerant pressure and temperature
     before it enters the evaporator

TYPE 1: THERMOSTATIC EXPANSION VALVE (TXV)
• Variable opening — adjusts based on demand
• Sensing bulb on evaporator outlet measures superheat
• Meters precise amount of refrigerant
• Used with RECEIVER-DRIER on high-pressure side
• More efficient, more complex

TYPE 2: FIXED ORIFICE TUBE
• Simple brass tube with calibrated restriction
• No moving parts — fixed flow rate
• Used with ACCUMULATOR on low-pressure side
• Simpler, cheaper, less precise
• Common on older US and Asian vehicles

RESULT: High-pressure warm liquid --> cold low-pressure mist
        Ready to absorb heat in the evaporator again
```

#### Slide 10: Supporting Component — Receiver-Drier and Accumulator
**Visual:** Left: receiver-drier canister (mounted between condenser and TXV on the high-pressure side) with cutaway showing desiccant bag and filter screen. Right: accumulator (mounted between evaporator and compressor on the low-pressure side) with cutaway showing desiccant bag, filter, and oil return orifice. Both labeled with their position in the system.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "One more component to complete the circuit. Moisture is the enemy of a refrigeration system. Water mixes with refrigerant and oil to form corrosive acids, and it can freeze at the expansion device and block flow. So every AC system has a moisture-removing component.
>
> If the system uses a TXV, it has a receiver-drier — a canister on the high-pressure side, between the condenser and TXV. Inside are a desiccant bag that absorbs moisture and a filter screen that catches debris. The receiver-drier also stores reserve liquid refrigerant to ensure a steady supply to the TXV.
>
> If the system uses an orifice tube, it has an accumulator — a larger canister on the low-pressure side, between the evaporator and compressor. Same job: remove moisture and filter debris. But the accumulator also prevents liquid refrigerant from reaching the compressor — liquid doesn't compress, and trying to compress it destroys the compressor. There's a small oil return orifice at the bottom that lets trapped compressor oil circulate back.
>
> Both components have a limited lifespan — the desiccant saturates over time. Replace the receiver-drier or accumulator any time you open the system. This is not optional."

**PPT Content:**
```
MOISTURE REMOVAL: RECEIVER-DRIER vs ACCUMULATOR

RECEIVER-DRIER (used with TXV systems)
Location: High-pressure side (condenser to TXV)
Contains: Desiccant (moisture absorber) + filter screen
Also: Stores reserve liquid refrigerant
Replace: Every time the system is opened

ACCUMULATOR (used with orifice tube systems)
Location: Low-pressure side (evaporator to compressor)
Contains: Desiccant + filter + oil return orifice
Also: Prevents liquid from reaching compressor
       (liquid slugging destroys compressors)
Replace: Every time the system is opened

CRITICAL RULE:
Moisture + refrigerant = acid formation = system damage
ALWAYS replace the drier/accumulator when system is opened
```

#### Slide 11: The Heating System — Heater Core
**Visual:** Diagram showing engine coolant circuit branching to the heater core inside the HVAC housing. Heater control valve and blend door shown. Second diagram below: EV heating system with PTC electric heater element and heat pump system.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Heating is simpler than cooling — at least on ICE vehicles. Engine coolant, which runs at 80 to 100 degrees Celsius, is routed through a small heat exchanger called the heater core. It sits inside the HVAC housing next to the evaporator. When you select heat, a blend door swings to direct air across the heater core instead of — or in addition to — the evaporator. Warm air enters the cabin. Simple and effective.
>
> On EVs, there's no engine coolant to tap for heat. Two solutions exist. The first is a PTC electric heater — Positive Temperature Coefficient — which is essentially a high-voltage ceramic heating element. It heats air directly, like an electric fan heater. It works, but it consumes significant power — 3 to 5 kilowatts — and that directly reduces range.
>
> The second, more efficient solution is a heat pump. This reverses the refrigeration cycle — the system absorbs heat from outside air and pumps it into the cabin. A heat pump uses 1 kilowatt of electrical energy to move 2 to 3 kilowatts of heat. Most modern EVs use heat pumps, sometimes supplemented by a PTC heater for extreme cold when the heat pump's efficiency drops.
>
> The heater core on an ICE vehicle can fail in two ways: it can leak — you'll smell sweet coolant inside the cabin and find a wet carpet on the passenger side — or it can clog, reducing heat output. Replacement often requires removing the entire dashboard."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE HEATING SYSTEM

ICE VEHICLES — HEATER CORE:
• Small radiator inside HVAC housing
• Uses hot engine coolant (80-100 deg C)
• Blend door directs air over heater core
• Failure: leak (sweet smell, wet carpet) or clog (no heat)
• Replacement: often requires dashboard removal

EV VEHICLES — TWO APPROACHES:

1. PTC ELECTRIC HEATER
   • High-voltage ceramic heating element
   • Direct air heating — simple but power-hungry
   • 3-5 kW draw = significant range reduction

2. HEAT PUMP (preferred, most modern EVs)
   • Reverses refrigeration cycle
   • Absorbs heat from outside air, pumps it inside
   • COP 2-3 (1 kW electric input = 2-3 kW heat output)
   • Supplemented by PTC in extreme cold (<-10 deg C)
   • Better efficiency = less range impact
```

#### Slide 12: The Blend Door and Temperature Control
**Visual:** HVAC housing cutaway showing the evaporator, heater core, and blend door in three positions — full cold (all air over evaporator, bypasses heater core), mixed (air split between both), and full hot (all air over heater core). Blend door actuator motor visible.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "How does the system blend hot and cold? With a blend door — a flap inside the HVAC housing driven by a small electric actuator motor. When you set the temperature to maximum cold, the blend door directs all air across the evaporator only. Maximum hot, all air goes across the heater core. Anywhere in between, the door partially opens to both, mixing the airstreams.
>
> Automatic climate control systems use a cabin temperature sensor — often a small thermistor near the dashboard with a tiny aspirator fan drawing cabin air over it — and an algorithm that continuously adjusts the blend door position, blower speed, and AC compressor engagement to maintain the set temperature. Manual systems leave those decisions to the driver.
>
> Blend door actuator failure is common. Symptoms: clicking noise from behind the dashboard when temperature is adjusted, stuck on hot or stuck on cold, or only hot air from one side on a dual-zone system. Diagnosis requires accessing the actuator behind the dashboard — not always easy."

**PPT Content:**
```

BLEND DOOR:

• Driven by electric actuator motor
• Controls ratio of air over evaporator vs heater core

POSITIONS:
Full cold  = 100% air over evaporator

#### Slide 13: Ventilation — Blower Motor and Cabin Air Filter
**Visual:** Left: blower motor assembly showing squirrel-cage fan, motor, and resistor pack or PWM module. Right: cabin air filter location behind glove box, showing a dirty filter next to a new one. Airflow path diagram showing outside air intake at base of windscreen, through cabin filter, through blower, over evaporator/heater core, out through dashboard vents.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The blower motor is the lungs of the HVAC system. It pushes air through the entire housing — across the evaporator, across the heater core, and out through the dashboard vents. It's a squirrel-cage fan driven by a DC motor, located in the HVAC housing, usually behind the glove box area.
>
> Speed control comes in two flavours. Older systems use a resistor pack — a bank of resistors that drop voltage to the motor for different speed settings. If one resistor burns out, you lose a speed — commonly you lose all speeds except the highest, because full speed bypasses the resistors entirely. Modern systems use PWM — Pulse Width Modulation — a transistor module that gives infinitely variable speed control. Quieter, more efficient, and allows automatic climate control to fine-tune airflow.
>
> Before air reaches the blower, it passes through the cabin air filter. This traps pollen, dust, road particulates, and on activated-carbon versions, odours and gases. Replacement interval is typically every 15,000 to 20,000 km or annually. A clogged cabin filter reduces airflow, overworks the blower motor, and compromises air quality. It's a quick, cheap service item that customers appreciate."

**PPT Content:**
```
VENTILATION: BLOWER MOTOR & CABIN AIR FILTER

BLOWER MOTOR:
• Squirrel-cage fan + DC electric motor
• Location: HVAC housing (behind glove box area)
• Pushes air over evaporator/heater core and out vents

SPEED CONTROL:
Old: Resistor pack (stepped speeds)
  — Common failure: lose low speeds, high speed still works
New: PWM transistor module (infinitely variable)
  — Smoother, quieter, enables auto climate control

CABIN AIR FILTER:
• Located in air intake path (often behind glove box)
• Types: Particulate only / Activated carbon (odour removal)
• Replacement: 15,000-20,000 km or annually
• Clogged filter = reduced airflow + poor air quality
• Quick service item — customer satisfaction builder

AIRFLOW PATH:
Outside air intake (windscreen base) --> cabin filter -->
  blower motor --> evaporator/heater core --> vents
```

#### Slide 14: Airflow Distribution — Mode Doors
**Visual:** Dashboard vent diagram showing different airflow modes — face vents, foot vents, defrost (windscreen) vents, and combinations. Mode door positions for each setting shown in simplified cross-section.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Beyond temperature, the system also controls where the air goes. Mode doors — additional flaps inside the HVAC housing — direct air to different outlets. Face mode pushes air through the dashboard vents. Foot mode directs it to the footwell. Defrost mode sends it up to the windscreen and side windows. Recirculation mode cools the cabin faster because you're cooling already-cooled air instead of hot outside air. **Instructor Narration:**
> "A modern AC system is ECU-controlled with multiple sensors feeding data for optimised operation. The high-pressure sensor monitors the discharge side — if pressure gets too high, the compressor is shut off to protect the system. The low-pressure sensor monitors the suction side — if pressure is too low, it indicates low refrigerant charge and the compressor is disabled to prevent running without lubrication.
>
> On the comfort side, the cabin temperature sensor, outside temperature sensor, and sun load sensor all feed the automatic climate control algorithm. The ECU outputs are compressor engagement and speed, blend door position, mode door position, blower speed, and cooling fan activation. Diagnosing HVAC problems often means reading these sensor values with a scan tool — just like engine diagnostics."

#### Slide 16: Refrigerant Types — R-134a and R-1234yf
**Visual:** Comparison table of R-134a and R-1234yf properties. Colour-coded refrigerant cylinders: R-134a in light blue, R-1234yf in light blue with red stripe. Warning symbol for R-1234yf flammability.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Two refrigerants dominate automotive AC today. R-134a has been the standard since the mid-1990s when it replaced R-12 — the ozone-depleting Freon. R-134a doesn't damage the ozone layer, but it is a potent greenhouse gas with a Global Warming Potential — GWP — of 1,430. That's 1,430 times more warming effect than CO2 per kilogram released.
>
> Since 2017, all new vehicles sold in the EU must use a refrigerant with a GWP below 150. Enter R-1234yf — GWP of just 4. Almost climate-neutral. But there's a catch: R-1234yf is classified as mildly flammable — A2L classification. It can ignite under specific conditions, and when it burns it can produce hydrogen fluoride, which is toxic. This means special handling precautions, dedicated recovery equipment, and specific workshop safety measures.
>
> You cannot mix refrigerants. R-134a and R-1234yf require different service equipment, different oils, different seals, and different service port fittings specifically to prevent cross-contamination. Always identify the refrigerant type before servicing — there's a label on the vehicle specifying the type and charge weight."

#### Slide 17: Recovery, Recycling, and Recharging
**Visual:** Three-step process diagram: Step 1 — Recovery (refrigerant extracted from vehicle into machine, scales showing weight removed). Step 2 — Recycling (machine filters, dries, and purifies recovered refrigerant). Step 3 — Recharging (vacuum pump evacuates system, then precise weight of refrigerant charged back). AC service machine shown with connected hoses, gauges, and display.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Refrigerant must never be vented to atmosphere — that's illegal in virtually every country. When servicing an AC system, you follow a three-step process: recover, recycle, recharge.
>
> Recovery means extracting all the refrigerant from the vehicle's system into the service machine. The machine weighs what comes out — this gives you a clue about system condition. If significantly less comes out than the specified charge, you have a leak.
>
> Recycling means the machine filters the recovered refrigerant, removes moisture with a drier, and removes contaminants. The cleaned refrigerant can then be reused.
>
> Recharging starts with a vacuum. The machine's vacuum pump evacuates the system down to about minus 1 bar for typically 20 to 30 minutes. This pulls out any remaining moisture and air. If the vacuum holds, the system is sealed — no leaks. If it doesn't hold, you have a leak to find before proceeding. Only once the vacuum test passes does the machine charge the precise weight of refrigerant specified on the vehicle label — measured to the gram.
>
> The machine also adds the correct type and quantity of compressor oil. Getting the charge weight wrong — even by 50 grams — affects performance and compressor life."

**PPT Content:**
```
RECOVERY, RECYCLING, AND RECHARGING

STEP 1: RECOVERY
• Extract ALL refrigerant from vehicle into machine
• Illegal to vent refrigerant to atmosphere
• Weigh what comes out (check against specified charge)

STEP 2: RECYCLING
• Machine filters and dries recovered refrigerant
• Removes moisture, oil, and contaminants
• Recycled refrigerant can be reused

STEP 3: RECHARGING
a) VACUUM (20-30 min at ~-1 bar / 75-100 mTorr)
   — Removes moisture and air
   — VACUUM HOLD TEST: if pressure rises, there's a leak
b) CHARGE BY WEIGHT
   — Precise amount per vehicle label (e.g., 550g +/- 10g)
   — Machine adds correct oil type and quantity
   — Weighed to the gram — accuracy matters

Wrong charge = poor performance or compressor damage

#### Slide 18: Leak Detection Methods
**Visual:** Four leak detection methods illustrated — (1) Electronic leak detector (sniffer wand near a connection), (2) UV dye injection with UV lamp illuminating a fluorescent leak trail, (3) Nitrogen pressure test with pressure gauge connected to system, (4) Bubble test with soapy water on a connection. Each method with pros/cons listed.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "If the vacuum test fails, or if the customer's complaint is gradual loss of cooling, you need to find the leak. Four methods, each with its place.
>
> The electronic sniffer is a handheld detector that senses refrigerant vapour. You move the wand slowly around every connection, the compressor shaft seal, the condenser, the evaporator seams, and the service ports. When it detects refrigerant, it alarms. Fast and convenient, but sensitive to wind and cross-contamination from previous leaks.
>
> UV dye is injected into the system with the refrigerant. The dye circulates with the refrigerant and seeps out at the leak point. Under a UV lamp, it glows bright green or yellow. Excellent for pinpointing the exact location, especially slow leaks. Takes some operating time for the dye to reach the leak.
>
> Nitrogen pressure test is the gold standard for confirming a sealed system after a repair. You pressurise the system with dry nitrogen — not refrigerant — to the specified test pressure and monitor with a precision gauge. If pressure drops over time, the leak persists.
>
> Soapy water — the old-fashioned method — works too. Apply soapy water around connections and watch for bubbles. Simple and effective for accessible areas, but you can't reach the evaporator this way."

**PPT Content:**
```
LEAK DETECTION METHODS

1. ELECTRONIC SNIFFER
   • Handheld detector senses refrigerant vapour
   • Move wand slowly around joints, seals, ports
   • Fast, convenient, good for initial sweep
   • Limitation: wind sensitivity, cross-contamination

2. UV DYE + UV LAMP
   • Dye injected into refrigerant system
   • Circulates and seeps out at leak point
   • Fluoresces bright green/yellow under UV light
   • Excellent for pinpointing slow leaks
   • Requires operating time for dye to reach leak

3. NITROGEN PRESSURE TEST
   • Pressurise with dry nitrogen (not refrigerant)
   • Monitor pressure gauge for drop over time
   • Gold standard for confirming post-repair seal
   • Does not identify exact location alone

4. SOAPY WATER / BUBBLE TEST
   • Apply to accessible connections
   • Watch for bubbles
   • Simple, effective, limited access to evaporator
```

## Assessment Checkpoint

**Formative (not graded):**
- **Component identification:** During Practical 1, each learner must correctly identify and name all five major AC components (compressor, condenser, receiver-drier/accumulator, expansion device, evaporator) on the vehicle
- **Cycle explanation:** Verbal check — describe what happens to the refrigerant at each stage of the cycle (evaporation, compression, condensation, expansion)
- **Performance test:** Each learner records a vent temperature reading and states whether it indicates a functioning system
- **Regulation awareness:** Quick-fire questions: "Can you vent refrigerant?" (No) / "What certification do you need?" (F-Gas) / "Is R-1234yf flammable?" (Yes, mildly — A2L)
- **Cabin filter:** Successfully replace the cabin air filter with correct airflow direction

---

## Key Takeaways

1. Air conditioning moves heat — it doesn't create cold. The refrigeration cycle exploits evaporation (absorbs heat) and condensation (releases heat) in a continuous loop.
2. Five core AC components: compressor (pump), condenser (outdoor heat dump), receiver-drier or accumulator (moisture removal), expansion device (pressure drop), evaporator (indoor heat absorption).
3. EV HVAC is fundamentally different: electric compressors, PTC heaters or heat pumps for heating, and cabin climate directly impacts driving range.
4. Refrigerant handling is legally regulated: F-Gas certification required, never vent to atmosphere, dedicated equipment per refrigerant type, R-1234yf requires flammability precautions.
5. Systematic service approach: performance test first (vent temperature), then diagnose (sensors, pressures, leak detection), then service (recover, vacuum, charge by weight).
