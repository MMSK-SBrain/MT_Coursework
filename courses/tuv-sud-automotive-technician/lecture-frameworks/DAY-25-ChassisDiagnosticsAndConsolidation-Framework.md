**Instructor Narration:**
> "Here is the framework you will use every single day as a technician. Step one: listen to the symptom. What exactly does the customer feel, hear, or see? When does it happen? At what speed? #### Slide 4: Case Study 1 — Vehicle Pulls to One Side
**Visual:** Top-down diagram of a vehicle with arrows showing leftward drift. Annotated suspect zones highlighted in different colours at each corner

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Case one. Customer says 'my car always drifts to the left. I have to hold the steering wheel to keep it straight.' This is one of the most common chassis complaints. Let's build our suspect list.
>
> First suspect: wheel alignment. If the car has more toe-out on one side, or significantly different camber left to right, it will pull. Day 22 told you why — camber creates a lateral force called camber thrust. Even half a degree of difference can make the car pull noticeably.
>
> Second suspect: brake drag. If a caliper slide pin is seized on one side, the pad never fully releases from the rotor. That's constant friction on one wheel — it slows that side down, pulling the car toward it. You'd feel the wheel getting hot after a short drive.
>
> Third suspect: uneven tyre pressure. If the left tyre is at 28 psi and the right is at 35 psi, the left tyre has a bigger contact patch, more rolling resistance, and the car drifts left. This is the easiest one to check and the most embarrassing one to miss.
>
> Fourth suspect: mismatched tyre sizes. Different tread depth front-to-rear on the same axle, or worse, different tyre sizes left to right. Different rolling circumferences mean different wheel speeds — the car drifts toward the smaller tyre.
>
> Fifth suspect: damaged suspension component. A bent control arm, a broken spring, a collapsed bushing — anything that shifts the geometry on one side. This usually follows a pothole impact or kerb strike."

**PPT Content:**
```
CASE 1: VEHICLE PULLS TO ONE SIDE

Customer says: "My car drifts left. I have to hold the wheel straight."

SUSPECT LIST:
1. Wheel alignment (camber/toe difference side-to-side)
   Test: 4-wheel alignment printout

2. Brake drag on one side (seized caliper slide pin)
   Test: Drive 5 km, check wheel temperature with IR gun

3. Uneven tyre pressure (lower pressure = more resistance)
   Test: Tyre pressure gauge -- check all four

4. Different tyre sizes or tread depth on same axle
   Test: Visual + tread depth gauge left vs right

5. Damaged suspension component (bent arm, collapsed bushing)
   Test: Visual inspection on lift, check for impact damage

DIAGNOSTIC ORDER: Check tyres first (free, fast, common)
                  --> alignment --> brake drag --> suspension
```

#### Slide 5: Case 1 — Diagnostic Decision Tree
**Visual:** Decision tree flowchart for "pulls to one side" — starting with tyre pressure check, branching to alignment, brake temperature, visual inspection

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Here's how you work through it efficiently. Always start with the simplest, cheapest check. Tyre pressure takes 60 seconds and costs nothing. If pressures are equal, check tyre condition — different sizes or significant tread depth difference? If tyres are good, do a short road test and then immediately check wheel temperatures with an infrared thermometer. If one wheel is significantly hotter, you have brake drag — inspect that caliper. If temperatures are even, you need an alignment check. And if alignment is within spec but the car still pulls, go to the lift and look for physical damage to suspension components.
>
> Notice the logic: cheap and fast first, expensive and slow last. That's efficient diagnostics."

#### Slide 6: Case Study 2 — Vibration at Speed
**Visual:** Illustration of a steering wheel with vibration arrows. Speed-dependent graph showing vibration onset at 80-100 km/h

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Case two. 'My steering wheel vibrates between 80 and 120 km/h, but it's fine at lower speeds and mostly goes away above 140.' Speed-dependent vibration. This is almost always a rotating imbalance — something spinning that's heavier on one side.
>
> Suspect one: wheel balance. A wheel-tyre assembly that's lost a balance weight or was never balanced properly. This is the most common cause by far. The vibration matches the wheel rotation frequency, so it gets worse at a specific speed range.
>
> Suspect two: tyre flat spot. If the car sat for weeks without moving, the tyre can develop a temporary flat spot where it rested on the ground. This usually works itself out after 20 minutes of driving. If it doesn't, the tyre is permanently deformed — usually from heat damage or age.
>
> Suspect three: brake rotor runout. If the rotor has lateral runout — it wobbles as it spins — you'll feel a vibration. But here's the key difference: if the vibration is there ALL the time at that speed, it's probably wheel balance. If it only appears when BRAKING at that speed, it's the rotor. This distinction matters.
>
> Suspect four: worn wheel bearing. A bearing with excessive play allows the hub to wobble slightly. The vibration tends to change with steering input — turn slightly left and it gets better or worse. Also often accompanied by a hum or drone that changes with speed.
>
> Suspect five: bent wheel rim. Impact damage from a pothole. The wheel is no longer round. This creates a vibration similar to imbalance but balancing won't fix it because the rim itself is distorted."

**PPT Content:**
```
CASE 2: VIBRATION AT SPEED

Customer says: "Steering wheel shakes at highway speed."

SUSPECT LIST:
1. Wheel imbalance (lost weight, never balanced)
   Test: Spin balance on wheel balancer
   Clue: Vibration at specific speed range, constant

2. Tyre flat spot (sat too long, or heat-damaged)
   Test: Visual inspection, road test after warm-up
   Clue: Vibration after sitting, may fade with driving

3. Brake rotor runout (warped/uneven disc)
   Test: Dial indicator on rotor face (<0.05 mm spec)
   Clue: Vibration ONLY when braking at speed = rotor

4. Worn wheel bearing (excessive play)
   Test: Jack up wheel, rock top-to-bottom, listen
   Clue: Hum/drone that changes with steering

5. Bent wheel rim (pothole impact)
   Test: Visual on balancer, runout measurement
   Clue: Vibration persists after balancing

KEY DISTINCTION:
  Vibration ALL the time at speed = balance / bearing / bent rim
  Vibration ONLY when braking = rotor runout
```

#### Slide 7: Case 2 — The Rotor Runout Detail
**Visual:** Cross-section of a brake disc on a hub, with a dial indicator positioned against the friction face. Diagram showing lateral runout measurement

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's zoom into rotor runout because this is a measurement you'll make frequently. You mount a dial indicator on a fixed point — the caliper bracket works well — and position the probe against the rotor friction surface. Slowly rotate the rotor one full turn by hand. The dial indicator needle will deflect — that deflection is the total lateral runout.
>
> For most manufacturers, the maximum allowable runout is between 0.03 and 0.05 millimetres. That's thinner than a human hair. If runout exceeds the limit, you either machine the rotor on-car with a brake lathe, or you replace it. Remember from Day 23: you also need to check the rotor thickness. If machining would take it below minimum thickness, replacement is the only option.
>
> The customer complaint for rotor runout is almost always 'pulsating brake pedal.' The pedal pushes back against your foot in rhythm with the wheel rotation. That pulsation is the thick and thin spots on the rotor pushing the pads in and out."

**PPT Content:**
```
ROTOR RUNOUT MEASUREMENT

Tool: Dial indicator (DTI) on magnetic base
Position: Probe against rotor friction surface
Method: Rotate rotor 360 degrees slowly by hand

Read: Total Indicated Runout (TIR)
Spec: Typically 0.03 - 0.05 mm maximum
(Check manufacturer spec -- varies by vehicle)

EXCEEDS SPEC?
  --> Option 1: Machine rotor on-car (if above minimum thickness)
  --> Option 2: Replace rotor (if at or below minimum thickness)

CUSTOMER SYMPTOM:
  Pulsating brake pedal during braking
  = Rotor pushes pads in/out as thick-thin spots rotate

Day 23 link: Always check rotor thickness against discard limit
before deciding to machine vs replace
```

#### Slide 8: Case Study 3 — Noise Over Bumps
**Visual:** Side view of a vehicle going over a speed bump, with annotated noise source locations in the suspension — bushings, ball joints, spring seats, damper mounts, heat shields

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Case three. 'I hear a clunk or rattle when I go over bumps.' This is a classic suspension noise complaint. The challenge is that almost every suspension component can make noise when worn. You need to narrow it down.
>
> Worn bushings — the rubber mounts that connect control arms to the subframe. When the rubber degrades, metal-to-metal contact creates a clunk. You can sometimes see cracks or separation visually.
>
> Loose or worn ball joints. Remember from Day 22 — ball joints are the pivot points that allow the wheel to move up and down and turn left and right simultaneously. When the ball wears in its socket, you get play. Clunking over bumps, sometimes a creaking on turns.
>
> Broken or cracked coil spring. Springs can fracture, usually at the bottom coil where road salt and debris cause corrosion. A broken spring clunks because the two pieces shift against each other. The car may also sit lower on that corner.
>
> Worn damper top mount. The top mount is where the strut assembly meets the body. It contains a bearing that allows the strut to rotate with the steering. When the bearing or rubber fails, you get a knock over bumps — especially slow bumps like speed humps.
>
> And the red herring: a loose exhaust heat shield. It's not a suspension problem at all, but it rattles over bumps and sounds like it's coming from underneath. A simple tap test identifies it."

**PPT Content:**
```
CASE 3: NOISE OVER BUMPS

Customer says: "I hear clunking/rattling over bumps."

SUSPECT LIST:
1. Worn control arm bushings (rubber degraded)
   Test: Visual -- cracks, separation, deformation
   Clue: Clunk from subframe area, both sides common

2. Worn/loose ball joint (play in pivot)
   Test: Pry bar test on lift, check for play
   Clue: Clunk, sometimes creaking on turning

3. Broken coil spring (fractured, usually lower coil)
   Test: Visual inspection on lift
   Clue: Clunk + car sits lower on one corner

4. Worn strut top mount / bearing
   Test: Hand on top mount, bounce car
   Clue: Knock over slow bumps, noise when turning at standstill

5. Loose exhaust heat shield (NOT suspension!)
   Test: Tap with hand -- rattles easily
   Clue: Metallic rattle, sounds like suspension but isn't

DIAGNOSTIC TIP:
  Have someone bounce the car while you listen underneath
  Isolate: front vs rear, left vs right
```

#### Slide 9: Case Study 4 — Brake Pedal Feel Issues
**Visual:** Four pedal diagrams showing: normal pedal, soft/spongy pedal, hard pedal, pulsating pedal — each with a brief label

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Case four. Brake pedal complaints. The pedal is the driver's primary feedback from the entire braking system, so what they feel tells you a lot.
>
> Soft or spongy pedal — the pedal sinks further than normal and feels mushy. This almost always means air in the hydraulic system. Air is compressible; brake fluid is not. Even a tiny air bubble makes the pedal feel spongy. Other causes: a leaking master cylinder allowing fluid to bypass internally, or a leaking caliper or wheel cylinder allowing fluid to escape externally. Check the fluid level first — if it's low, you have a leak somewhere.
>
> Hard pedal — you have to push much harder than normal to get any braking. The most common cause is brake booster failure. Remember from Day 23 — the booster multiplies your foot force using engine vacuum. If the vacuum hose is disconnected, cracked, or the booster diaphragm has failed, you lose that assistance. The brakes still work — they're just very hard to press. Another cause: a blocked brake line, though this is rare.
>
> Pulsating pedal — the pedal pulses against your foot during braking. We just covered this: rotor lateral runout. The thick and thin spots push the pads in and out, and you feel it through the pedal. This is NOT the same as ABS activation, which is a rapid vibration accompanied by the ABS pump noise and warning light. Teach the customer the difference.
>
> Low pedal that pumps up — the pedal is low on first press but comes up with a second pump. This usually indicates excessive rotor runout pushing pads back, or drum brakes with excessive shoe-to-drum clearance. The first press takes up the slack, the second press applies the brakes normally."

**PPT Content:**
```
CASE 4: BRAKE PEDAL FEEL ISSUES

SOFT / SPONGY PEDAL:
  Cause: Air in hydraulic system
         Master cylinder internal leak
         External fluid leak (caliper, line, hose)
  Test:  Check fluid level --> bleed system --> pressure test

HARD PEDAL:
  Cause: Brake booster failure (no vacuum assist)
         Disconnected/cracked vacuum hose
         Blocked brake line (rare)
  Test:  Engine-off pump test (pump pedal, start engine, pedal should drop)
         Check vacuum hose connections

PULSATING PEDAL:
  Cause: Brake rotor lateral runout (Day 23)
  Test:  Dial indicator on rotor face
  Note:  Different from ABS activation! ABS = rapid buzz + ABS light

LOW PEDAL (PUMPS UP):
  Cause: Excessive rotor runout pushing pads back
         Drum brake shoe clearance too wide
  Test:  Rotor runout measurement, drum brake adjuster check

KEY: Pedal feel = direct feedback from hydraulic system health
```

#### Slide 10: Case 4 — The Booster Test
**Visual:** Diagram of the vacuum booster pump-up test sequence: engine off, pump pedal 3-4 times, hold firm, start engine, pedal should drop

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Here's a classic test every technician should know — the brake booster functional test. With the engine off, pump the brake pedal three or four times. This depletes the vacuum reserve in the booster. Now hold the pedal firmly pressed. Start the engine. If the booster is working, the pedal should drop slightly under your foot as engine vacuum restores the booster assistance.
>
> If the pedal doesn't move when you start the engine, the booster is not receiving vacuum — check the hose — or the booster diaphragm has failed. This is a simple, zero-cost test that takes 30 seconds and immediately tells you whether the booster is functional. Day 23 taught you how the booster works. Today you apply that knowledge diagnostically."

**PPT Content:**
```
BRAKE BOOSTER FUNCTIONAL TEST

1. Engine OFF
2. Pump brake pedal 3-4 times (depletes vacuum reserve)
3. Hold pedal firmly pressed -- DO NOT release
4. Start engine while holding pedal

RESULT:
  Pedal drops slightly = Booster WORKING (vacuum restored)
  Pedal stays firm, no change = Booster FAILED or no vacuum

IF FAILED, check:
  - Vacuum hose connection to intake manifold (disconnected?)
  - Vacuum hose condition (cracked? collapsed?)
  - Check valve in hose (one-way valve stuck?)
  - Booster diaphragm (internal failure --> replace booster)

TIME: 30 seconds   COST: Zero   VALUE: Immediate diagnosis
```

#### Slide 11: Case Study 5 — Uneven Tyre Wear Patterns
**Visual:** Six tyre cross-sections showing different wear patterns: inner edge, outer edge, centre, both edges, feathering, cupping — each labeled with its cause

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Case five. This one ties together everything from Day 21 and Day 22. Tyre wear patterns are diagnostic gold. The tyre records its history in the rubber — you just need to read it.
>
> Inner edge wear — the inside shoulder is worn more than the outside. This means excessive negative camber. The top of the wheel is leaning inward too much, overloading the inner edge. Common on vehicles with lowered suspension or worn upper mounts that allow the geometry to shift.
>
> Outer edge wear — the opposite. Too much positive camber. The wheel leans outward at the top, overloading the outer edge. Less common, but seen when a control arm bushing has collapsed outward.
>
> Centre wear — the middle of the tread is worn but the edges are fine. Over-inflation. Remember Day 21: higher pressure makes the tyre crown outward, concentrating the contact patch in the centre. The edges don't touch the road as firmly.
>
> Both-edge wear — the edges are worn but the centre is fine. Under-inflation. The tyre sags, the edges carry more load, the centre lifts slightly. This also causes higher rolling resistance and heat buildup.
>
> Feathering — run your hand across the tread and it feels smooth one direction but rough the other, like saw teeth. This is incorrect toe alignment. Day 22: toe-in scrubs the outer edge, toe-out scrubs the inner edge. The directional scrubbing creates that feathered texture.
>
> Cupping or scalloping — irregular dished-out patches around the circumference. This is almost always worn dampers. When the damper can't control the spring oscillation, the tyre bounces — literally leaving the road surface and landing again repeatedly. Each landing scuffs a cup-shaped patch into the rubber."

**Visual:** Warning triangle icons next to each trap, with brief descriptions

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before we go to the practical stations, let me warn you about common traps. First: confirmation bias. You decide what the problem is before you test, then you only look for evidence that confirms your guess. Stay open to all possibilities until the evidence eliminates them.
>
> Second: skipping the road test. You can't diagnose a vibration at 100 km/h while the car is sitting in the workshop. Drive it. Feel it. Listen to it. Half of chassis diagnostics happens behind the steering wheel.
>
> Third: forgetting to check the obvious. I've seen technicians tear apart a suspension chasing a noise that turned out to be a loose spare tyre in the boot, or a water bottle rolling under the seat. Always check the simple things first.
>
> Fourth: not comparing side to side. Suspension problems are often diagnosed by comparing the affected side to the good side. If the left ball joint has 3 mm of play and the right has none, the left is worn. Without the comparison, you might not know what 'normal' looks like."

**PPT Content:**
```
COMMON DIAGNOSTIC TRAPS

1. CONFIRMATION BIAS
   Deciding the answer before testing
   --> Stay neutral. Let evidence guide you.

2. SKIPPING THE ROAD TEST
   Can't diagnose "vibration at speed" in the workshop
   --> Drive it. Feel it. Listen at the speed it happens.

3. FORGETTING THE OBVIOUS
   Loose items in boot, unsecured heat shields, low tyre pressure
   --> Check simple things BEFORE complex things.

4. NOT COMPARING SIDE TO SIDE
   Suspension is symmetrical -- one side shows you what "normal" is
   --> Always compare left vs right.

5. NOT ASKING THE CUSTOMER ENOUGH QUESTIONS
   "When?" "At what speed?" "Only when braking?" "After hitting a pothole?"
   --> The customer's description narrows your suspect list.
```

#### Slide 23: Week 5 Rapid Review — Days 23-24
**Visual:** Two-column summary: Day 23 key concepts (left) and Day 24 key concepts (right)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Day 23 — brakes. Pascal's Law: pressure applied to a confined fluid is transmitted equally in all directions. This is how a light foot press creates enormous clamping force at the wheels. The master cylinder converts mechanical force to hydraulic pressure. The brake booster multiplies your input force using engine vacuum. Disc brakes: caliper squeezes pads against rotor. Inspection limits: minimum pad thickness typically 2-3 mm, minimum rotor thickness stamped on the rotor. Brake fluid is hygroscopic — it absorbs moisture from the air. DOT 3 has a lower boiling point than DOT 4, which is lower than DOT 5.1. Moisture lowers the boiling point — that's why we test brake fluid condition and change it on schedule.
>
> Day 24 — ABS and ESC. The ABS wheel speed sensor — inductive type generates an AC signal whose frequency corresponds to wheel speed; Hall effect type generates a digital square wave and works down to zero speed. The ABS hydraulic modulator can hold, release, or reapply pressure to each wheel independently. It prevents wheel lock-up during hard braking so the driver maintains steering control. ESC adds a yaw rate sensor and a lateral acceleration sensor. It compares where the driver wants to go — from the steering angle sensor — with where the car is actually going — from the yaw rate sensor. If there's a mismatch, ESC brakes individual wheels to correct the vehicle's path. Traction control is the flip side: it prevents wheelspin during acceleration."

**PPT Content:**
```
WEEK 5 RAPID REVIEW: DAYS 23-24

DAY 23: HYDRAULIC BRAKES
  Pascal's Law: Pressure in confined fluid = equal in all directions
  Master cylinder: Converts pedal force to hydraulic pressure
  Brake booster: Multiplies force using engine vacuum
  Disc brake: Caliper + pads + rotor (friction = heat)
  Inspection: Min pad thickness (~2-3 mm), min rotor thickness (stamped)
  Brake fluid: DOT 3 < DOT 4 < DOT 5.1 (boiling point)
  Hygroscopic: Absorbs moisture --> lower boiling point over time

DAY 24: ABS / ESC / TRACTION CONTROL
  Wheel speed sensor: Inductive (AC) vs Hall effect (digital)
  ABS modulator: Hold / Release / Reapply per wheel
  ABS purpose: Prevent lock-up --> maintain steering during hard brake
  ESC sensors: Yaw rate + lateral acceleration + steering angle
  ESC logic: Where driver steers vs where car goes --> brake correction
  Traction control: Prevents wheelspin on acceleration
```

## Key Takeaways

1. **Diagnosis follows a framework:** Symptom --> System identification --> Targeted test --> Root cause. Never guess and replace.
2. **Tyre wear patterns are diagnostic evidence:** Inner edge = negative camber. Outer edge = positive camber. Centre = over-inflation. Both edges = under-inflation. Feathering = toe. Cupping = worn dampers.
3. **Five common chassis complaints map to structured suspect lists:** pulls, vibrations, noises, pedal feel, uneven wear — each has a ranked diagnostic sequence from cheapest/fastest to most expensive/slowest.
4. **Always check the obvious first:** tyre pressure before alignment, fluid level before master cylinder, heat shield before suspension.
5. **Compare side to side:** suspension is symmetrical by design. One side is your reference for the other.
6. **The pedal tells the story:** soft = air/leak, hard = booster, pulsating = rotor runout, low-pumps-up = excessive clearance.
7. **Diagnostic skill defines your career value** — not wrench speed, but accuracy of diagnosis.
