# Day 3: Hazards, Compliance & the Fluids That Run Through a Car
## Instructor-Led Story Framework for PPT Preparation

**Duration:** 180 minutes (65 min theory + 115 min practical)
**Approach:** Hazard Awareness + Tactile Fluid Identification
**PPT Target:** 27 slides
**Week:** 1 of 8 — "Meet the Machine"

---

## 🎯 Session Objectives (For Instructor)

By the end of this session, learners will be able to:
- Complete a risk assessment using a likelihood x severity matrix
- Identify correct fire extinguisher type for common workshop fire scenarios
- Read a Safety Data Sheet (SDS) and extract key safety information
- Explain environmental compliance requirements for waste fluids and batteries
- Identify all 7 major automotive fluids by purpose, type, colour, and container
- Explain why using the wrong fluid causes system damage

---

## 🔗 Connection to Prior Knowledge

**From Day 1:** Saw all 8 vehicle systems and where fluids live (engine oil in the sump, coolant in the reservoir, brake fluid in the master cylinder).
**From Day 2:** Learned safety fundamentals, PPE, and the 6 hazard categories — today's chemical and thermal hazards connect directly.

**Bridge:** "Yesterday was about personal safety — staying alive around equipment. Today we focus on substances. A car runs on 7 different fluids, and every one can hurt you, hurt the car, or hurt the environment if you handle it wrong."

### **DEVELOPMENT PART 1: Hazard Management** (Slides 4-11, ~45 minutes)

#### Slide 4: Risk Assessment Matrix
**Visual:** 5x5 matrix with colour-coded risk zones (green/yellow/orange/red)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Every hazard can be assessed on two dimensions: how likely is it, and how bad would it be? Multiply them for a risk score. Low likelihood, low severity — green, acceptable. High both — red, work cannot proceed until you mitigate. Your job: identify hazards, assess them, apply controls to move from red toward green."

**PPT Content:**
```
RISK ASSESSMENT MATRIX

LIKELIHOOD (L):         SEVERITY (S):
1 = Rare                1 = Negligible (first aid)
2 = Unlikely            2 = Minor (medical treatment)
3 = Possible            3 = Moderate (lost time)
4 = Likely              4 = Major (permanent disability)
5 = Almost certain      5 = Catastrophic (fatality)

RISK = L x S
1-4: LOW (green)    5-9: MEDIUM (yellow)
10-15: HIGH (orange)    16-25: EXTREME (red)
```

---

#### Slide 5: Worked Example
**Visual:** Scenario with before/after risk scores

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Scenario: changing a tyre without jack stands. Likelihood of jack slipping — 3, possible. Severity — 5, catastrophic. Risk: 15, HIGH. Control: use jack stands and chocks. New likelihood: 1, rare. New score: 5, MEDIUM. Controls reduce likelihood; severity usually stays the same."

**PPT Content:**
```
WORKED EXAMPLE

Hazard: Vehicle falls while changing tyre (no jack stands)
BEFORE: L=3, S=5 → Risk 15 (HIGH)
Control: Jack stands + wheel chocks + flat ground
AFTER: L=1, S=5 → Risk 5 (MEDIUM)

Controls reduce LIKELIHOOD. Severity often unchanged.
```

#### Slide 9: Environmental Compliance
**Visual:** Proper waste storage area with labelled containers

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Everything that comes out of a car during service is regulated waste. Waste oil in sealed drums, never down drains. Coolant collected separately. Brake fluid in sealed containers. Batteries stored upright for recycling. Refrigerant NEVER vented — always recovered. Oil filters drained and collected. Fines for illegal disposal start at 5,000 euros."

**PPT Content:**
```
WASTE DISPOSAL — REGULATED MATERIALS

| Waste | Method | NEVER |
|-------|--------|-------|
| Engine oil | Sealed drum → collector | Pour down drain |
| Coolant | Separate container → recycler | Mix with oil |
| Brake fluid | Sealed → chemical waste | Leave open |
| Batteries | Upright → recycler | Landfill |
| Refrigerant | Recovery machine | Vent to atmosphere |
| Oil filters | Drain 24h → waste bin | General waste |

Fines: €5,000 — €50,000+
```

#### Slide 10: Chemical Storage Rules
**Visual:** Storage cabinet diagram showing segregation

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Flammables in a fire-rated cabinet away from ignition. Acids separate from bases. Gas cylinders chained upright. Everything labelled, everything sealed."

**PPT Content:**
```
CHEMICAL STORAGE
• Flammable liquids → fire-rated cabinet, away from heat
• Acids → separate from bases (they react violently)
• Compressed gas → chained upright, ventilated area
• All containers → labelled, sealed when not in use
• Incompatible materials → NEVER store together
```

### **DEVELOPMENT PART 2: The 7 Automotive Fluids** (Slides 12-19, ~40 minutes)

#### Slide 12: Engine Oil
**Visual:** SAE viscosity chart, API/ACEA symbols, colour spectrum new→used

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Engine oil lubricates, cools, cleans, seals, and protects. SAE grades tell you viscosity — 5W-30 means thin cold, medium hot. API and ACEA ratings indicate quality. Using the wrong grade starves the engine at cold start or provides insufficient protection at temperature. Always match the exact OEM specification."

**PPT Content:**
```
FLUID 1: ENGINE OIL
JOB: Lubricate, cool, clean, seal, protect
COLOUR: Golden (new) → dark brown/black (used)

SAE GRADES: 0W-20, 5W-30, 5W-40, 10W-40
RATINGS: API SN/SP, ACEA A3/B4, C3 (low-SAPS for DPF)

⚠ Wrong grade = accelerated wear, sludge, DPF damage
Always match OEM specification exactly
```

---

#### Slide 13: Coolant
**Visual:** Three coolant types — green (IAT), orange (OAT), pink (HOAT) — "DO NOT MIX"

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Coolant transfers heat and prevents freezing. Three types by chemistry: IAT green, OAT orange, HOAT pink/yellow. Mix IAT with OAT and you get gel that clogs the heater core and radiator. A 1,000 euro repair from a 5 euro mistake. Always match the manufacturer's specification. Test concentration with a refractometer — 50/50 with distilled water."

**PPT Content:**
```
FLUID 2: COOLANT
JOB: Heat transfer, freeze protection, corrosion prevention
• IAT (green) — 2-year life, older vehicles
• OAT (orange/pink) — 5-year life, modern vehicles
• HOAT (yellow/pink) — 5-year life, some European/Asian

⚠ MIXING IAT + OAT = gel/sludge → clogs heater core
50/50 with DISTILLED water, check with refractometer
```

---

#### Slide 14: Brake Fluid
**Visual:** DOT comparison table with boiling points

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Brake fluid transfers your pedal force to the calipers. The critical property is boiling point — if it boils, vapour forms, the pedal goes to the floor, and you don't stop. DOT 3, 4, and 5.1 are glycol-based and hygroscopic — they absorb moisture, which drops the boiling point. DOT 5 is silicone — NOT compatible with the others. Flush every 2 years."

**PPT Content:**
```
FLUID 3: BRAKE FLUID
JOB: Hydraulic force transfer, pedal to caliper

| DOT | Dry Boiling | Wet Boiling | Base |
|-----|-------------|-------------|------|
| 3 | 205°C | 140°C | Glycol |
| 4 | 230°C | 155°C | Glycol |
| 5.1 | 260°C | 180°C | Glycol |
| 5 | 260°C | 180°C | SILICONE |

⚠ DOT 5 NOT compatible with DOT 3/4/5.1
⚠ Hygroscopic — flush every 2 years
⚠ Strips paint — wipe spills immediately
```

---

#### Slide 15: Transmission Fluid
**Visual:** ATF colour spectrum red→brown, gear oil containers

**Instructor Narration:**
> "ATF is red, thin, and specification-critical. Different transmissions require different ATF — Dexron VI, Toyota WS, ZF Lifeguard — NOT interchangeable. Wrong ATF destroys clutch packs. Manual gearbox oil is thicker — GL-4 or GL-5 rated. Always match the exact spec on the fill cap or in the manual."

**PPT Content:**
```
FLUID 4: TRANSMISSION FLUID
ATF: Red/pink, thin — Dexron VI, Mercon, Toyota WS, ZF Lifeguard
Manual: Amber, thick — GL-4 (synchros), GL-5 (hypoid gears)
Differential: Dark amber — GL-5, 75W-90 or 80W-140

⚠ ATF types NOT interchangeable
Wrong ATF = €3,000-5,000 transmission replacement
```

#### Slide 16: Refrigerant
**Visual:** R-134a vs R-1234yf containers, different fittings

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Two types: R-134a being phased out due to high Global Warming Potential, R-1234yf replacing it at much higher cost. Different service fittings prevent cross-contamination. Handling requires certification. Never vent to atmosphere — illegal."

**PPT Content:**
```
FLUID 5: REFRIGERANT
| | R-134a | R-1234yf |
|---|--------|----------|
| GWP | 1,430 | 4 |
| Status | Phasing out | Current |
| Cost/kg | €15-25 | €50-100 |
| Fittings | Standard | Different |

⚠ Never vent — recovery equipment mandatory
⚠ R-1234yf mildly flammable
⚠ Certification required
```

#### Slide 17: Grease Types
**Visual:** Grease samples — lithium (red), moly (black), silicone (clear), copper (copper)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Grease is oil in a thickener — it stays where you put it. Lithium for general purpose. Moly for high-pressure joints like CV. Silicone for rubber seals and brake slide pins. Copper anti-seize for exhaust bolts and spark plugs — but NEVER on rubber, it dissolves it."

**PPT Content:**
```
FLUID 6-7: GREASE
| Type | Colour | Use | NOT on |
|------|--------|-----|--------|
| Lithium EP2 | Red/brown | Bearings, general | High-temp |
| Moly MoS2 | Black | CV joints, high load | Rubber |
| Silicone | Clear | Seals, O-rings, slide pins | High load metal |
| Copper | Copper | Exhaust bolts, plugs | Rubber (dissolves) |
```

## 🔑 Key Takeaways

1. Risk = Likelihood x Severity — controls reduce likelihood
2. SDS Sections 2, 4, 7 are your chemical safety reference
3. Seven automotive fluids, each with types that must NOT be mixed
4. Wrong fluid = system damage — always check specification first
5. Environmental compliance is legal, not optional
