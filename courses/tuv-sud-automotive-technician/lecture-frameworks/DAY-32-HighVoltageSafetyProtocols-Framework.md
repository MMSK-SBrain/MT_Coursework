#### Slide 5: Voltage, Current, and the Human Body
**Visual:** Diagram of the human body with a current path drawn from one hand through the chest to the other hand. Overlay showing resistance values at different body points. Heart highlighted in red along the current path.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's understand the physics. Voltage is the pressure that pushes current through a circuit. Current is what actually does the damage. And resistance determines how much current flows for a given voltage. Ohm's Law — you learned this on Day 7 — current equals voltage divided by resistance.
>
> The human body's resistance varies enormously. Dry skin — about 100,000 ohms. Wet skin — as low as 1,000 ohms. Internal body tissue — only about 500 ohms. So here's the calculation that should keep you awake at night: 400 volts across wet hands through the chest. 400 divided by 1,000 equals 400 milliamps. The lethal threshold for ventricular fibrillation is 30 milliamps. You are thirteen times over the lethal dose.
>
> This is not like 12-volt systems where you might get a tingle. At 400 volts, you don't get a warning. You get cardiac arrest."

**PPT Content:**
```
VOLTAGE, CURRENT, AND YOUR BODY

Ohm's Law: I = V / R
    I = Current (what kills)
    V = Voltage (the push)
    R = Resistance (your body)

BODY RESISTANCE:
    Dry skin:           ~100,000 ohms
    Wet skin:           ~1,000 ohms
    Internal tissue:    ~500 ohms

THE LETHAL CALCULATION:
    400V / 1,000 ohms (wet skin) = 400 mA

#### Slide 6: How Electrical Current Kills
**Visual:** Table showing current ranges and their effects on the human body. A vertical scale from 1 mA to 1,000 mA with labeled zones.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's be specific about what current does to you. At 1 milliamp, you feel a tingle — that's your threshold of perception. At 10 milliamps, muscle contraction begins. This is critical — your hand clamps onto the conductor and you cannot let go. This is called 'let-go threshold.' Your voluntary muscles are overridden by the electrical current.
>
> At 30 milliamps through the chest, ventricular fibrillation begins. Your heart stops pumping blood in a coordinated way and starts quivering. Without a defibrillator within minutes, you die. At 75 milliamps, fibrillation is almost certain. Above 100 milliamps, you may get direct cardiac arrest — the heart simply stops. Above 1 amp, you get severe burns and tissue destruction.
>
> The critical path is hand-to-hand or hand-to-foot through the chest. That is why you will NEVER work on an HV system with both hands. One hand works. The other stays behind your back or in your pocket. This is not a suggestion. It is a survival technique used by every electrical worker in the world."

#### Slide 7: AC vs DC — Both Kill, Differently
**Visual:** Two waveforms side by side — AC sine wave and DC flat line. Comparison table of lethal thresholds.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "You may have heard that AC is more dangerous than DC. That's partially true — at the same voltage, AC is more likely to cause fibrillation because the alternating current interferes with the heart's electrical timing. The lethal threshold for AC is lower — about 30 milliamps AC versus roughly 50 milliamps DC for the same exposure time.
>
> This is why the regulatory thresholds differ: 30 volts AC is considered dangerous, while the threshold for DC is 50 volts. But here's what matters in practice: electric vehicles primarily use DC at 400 to 800 volts. The inverter converts DC to AC to drive the motor — so inside the inverter, you have both. And at these voltage levels, BOTH AC and DC are massively lethal. The academic difference between AC and DC danger is irrelevant when you are at 400+ volts. Both kill. Treat both with identical respect."

**PPT Content:**
```
AC vs DC — BOTH LETHAL AT HV LEVELS

                        AC              DC
Perception threshold    1 mA            5 mA
Let-go threshold        10 mA           50 mA
Fibrillation threshold  30 mA           50 mA
Regulatory danger limit 30V AC          50V DC

WHY AC IS MORE DANGEROUS (at lower voltages):
— Alternating current disrupts heart's natural rhythm
— Lower current needed to cause fibrillation

IN AN EV, BOTH EXIST:
— Battery, DC bus, charging: DC (400-800V)
— Motor drive (inside inverter): AC (3-phase)

AT 400+ VOLTS: THE DISTINCTION IS ACADEMIC.

#### Slide 15: Lock-Out / Tag-Out — The Procedure
**Visual:** Step-by-step photos: (1) Personal padlock with unique key, (2) Padlock applied to MSD location/lockout hasp, (3) Tag filled out with name/date/reason attached to lock, (4) Key in technician's pocket.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Lock-Out/Tag-Out is not automotive-specific. It comes from industrial electrical safety — OSHA 1910.147 in the US, similar standards worldwide. The principle is simple and absolute: when you de-energize a system, you physically lock it so no one can re-energize it. Then you tag the lock with your identity and the reason.
>
> Here's how it works. After removing the MSD, you place YOUR personal padlock on the MSD location — this could be a lockout hasp over the MSD slot, or a lockout device specific to the vehicle. You use a lock that only YOU have the key for. No master keys. No shared locks. YOUR lock. YOUR key.
>
> Then you attach a tag. The tag states your name, the date, the time, and the reason the system is locked out. Simple, specific, non-removable.
>
> The absolute rule: ONLY the person who applied the lock removes the lock. Not the shop foreman. Not the service manager. Not the vehicle owner in a hurry. YOU put it on. YOU take it off. If someone removes your lock without your permission, that is a termination-level safety violation in any professional shop."

**PPT Content:**
```
LOCK-OUT / TAG-OUT (LOTO)

PURPOSE: Prevent re-energization while YOU are working on the system

PROCEDURE:
[1] After MSD removal, place YOUR personal PADLOCK on the MSD location
    — Use a lockout hasp or vehicle-specific lockout device
    — Your lock. Your unique key. No master keys. No shared locks.

[2] Attach a TAG to the lock:
    — Your NAME
    — DATE and TIME
    — REASON for lockout ("HV system isolation for [task]")

[3] Keep the key in YOUR POCKET — on your person at all times

ABSOLUTE RULE:
    ONLY THE PERSON WHO APPLIED THE LOCK REMOVES THE LOCK.
    No exceptions. No manager override. No customer pressure.

    Unauthorized lock removal = termination-level safety violation
```

#### Slide 17: Class 0 Insulating Gloves — Your Primary Protection
**Visual:** Photo of Class 0 orange/red rubber insulating gloves with leather protectors beside them. Markings visible (Class 0, 1000V AC, test date stamp).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Class 0 insulating gloves are rated to 1,000 volts AC, which corresponds to 1,500 volts DC. They are your primary barrier between your hands and lethal voltage. These are not optional. You wear them every time you approach an HV system — even to remove the MSD.
>
> They are made of natural rubber or synthetic rubber compounds and must be tested and certified. Each glove has a date stamp indicating when it was last tested. The industry standard requires retesting every 6 months. If your gloves are past their test date, they are not to be used. Period.
>
> Over the rubber gloves, you wear leather protector gloves. The rubber provides electrical insulation. The leather provides mechanical protection — against cuts, abrasions, and punctures that would compromise the rubber's insulating ability. Never wear the rubber gloves alone. Never wear the leather protectors alone. Always together."

**PPT Content:**
```
CLASS 0 INSULATING GLOVES

RATING: 1,000V AC / 1,500V DC — suitable for EV work (400-800V systems)

CONSTRUCTION:

#### Slide 21: The CAT III Multimeter — Your Verification Tool
**Visual:** Photo of a CAT III rated multimeter with probes. Close-up of the CAT III marking on the meter. Comparison table of CAT ratings (I through IV).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "You learned to use a multimeter on Day 9. Now you learn why the category rating on that meter matters more than anything else about it.
>
> Multimeter CAT ratings indicate the meter's ability to withstand voltage transients — spikes that occur in electrical systems. CAT I is for low-energy electronics. CAT II is for household outlets. CAT III is for distribution-level circuits — building wiring, industrial panels, and automotive HV systems. CAT IV is for utility entrance.
>
> For HV automotive work, you must use a minimum CAT III rated meter, rated for the voltage of the system you're measuring — typically CAT III 600V or CAT III 1000V. Using a CAT II meter on a 400-volt HV bus is dangerous because if a transient spike occurs, the meter cannot contain it. The meter can explode in your hands. This has happened.
>
> Before you use the meter, verify it's working. Test it on a known live source — a 12V battery or a test outlet. If the meter reads correctly on the known source, it's functional. Then take your HV measurements. Then test the known source again. This is called the 'live-dead-live' test, and it proves your meter was working before, during, and after your HV measurement."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE CAT III MULTIMETER

CAT RATINGS:
CAT I    — Low-energy electronics (bench testing)
CAT II   — Single-phase outlets (household)
CAT III  — Distribution level (AUTOMOTIVE HV, industrial)
CAT IV   — Utility entrance (overhead lines)

FOR HV AUTOMOTIVE WORK:
— MINIMUM CAT III, rated for system voltage
— Typically: CAT III 600V or CAT III 1000V
— Using wrong CAT rating: meter can EXPLODE on transient spike

"LIVE-DEAD-LIVE" TEST:
[1] TEST meter on known live source (12V battery) — confirms meter works
[2] MEASURE HV system — should read 0V
[3] TEST meter on known live source again — confirms meter still works

This proves your 0V reading was real, not a broken meter.

#### Slide 22: The Three-Point Verification
**Visual:** Schematic diagram of an HV system showing HV+, HV-, and chassis ground. Three measurement positions clearly marked with probe placement. Each measurement labeled as M1, M2, M3.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "De-energization verification requires THREE measurements. Not one. Not two. Three. And all three must read zero volts.
>
> Measurement one: HV positive to HV negative. This confirms there is no voltage across the main DC bus. Measurement two: HV positive to vehicle chassis ground. This confirms the positive rail is not energized relative to the vehicle body. Measurement three: HV negative to vehicle chassis ground. This confirms the negative rail is not energized relative to the vehicle body.
>
> Why all three? Because a single measurement can miss fault conditions. If HV+ and HV- are both floating at 200 volts above chassis, the measurement between them reads zero — but touching either one to chassis gives you 200 volts through your body. Only the full three-point verification catches every possible energized state.
>
> Set your multimeter to DC voltage, maximum range. Place your probes. Read the value. All three measurements must show zero volts. If any measurement shows any voltage — STOP. Do not proceed. Re-check the isolation procedure. Something is wrong."

**PPT Content:**
```
THREE-POINT DE-ENERGIZATION VERIFICATION

ALL THREE MEASUREMENTS MUST READ 0V:

MEASUREMENT 1: HV+ to HV-
    — Confirms no voltage across the DC bus

MEASUREMENT 2: HV+ to CHASSIS GROUND
    — Confirms positive rail is not energized vs. body

MEASUREMENT 3: HV- to CHASSIS GROUND
    — Confirms negative rail is not energized vs. body

WHY THREE?
— If HV+ and HV- both float at +200V above chassis:
  M1 reads 0V (both same voltage) — MISLEADING
  M2 reads 200V — DETECTED
  M3 reads 200V — DETECTED
— Only 3-point verification catches ALL fault states

IF ANY MEASUREMENT SHOWS VOLTAGE:
STOP. DO NOT PROCEED. RE-CHECK ISOLATION PROCEDURE.
```

#### Slide 23: Insulation Resistance Measurement — Concept
**Visual:** Simplified diagram showing insulation resistance between HV circuit and vehicle chassis. Megohmmeter shown applying test voltage and reading resistance value. Minimum threshold value displayed.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Beyond voltage verification, there's another measurement you should understand: insulation resistance. This measures how well the HV system is isolated from the vehicle chassis — even when de-energized.
>
> A megohmmeter applies a test voltage — typically 500 or 1000 volts — between the HV circuit and chassis, and measures the resistance of the insulation barrier. Good insulation reads in the megohm range — typically 1 megohm or higher per manufacturer specification. Low insulation resistance indicates degraded insulation — possibly from moisture ingress, physical damage, or aging — and means the HV system could energize the chassis even through supposedly intact insulation.
>
> You will not typically perform this measurement on your first day working with HV systems. But you need to understand the concept because insulation monitoring systems in the vehicle perform this check continuously. If the vehicle displays an insulation fault warning, it means the barrier between lethal voltage and the vehicle body has been compromised. That vehicle is treated as fully energized until proven otherwise."

**PPT Content:**
```
INSULATION RESISTANCE MEASUREMENT — CONCEPT

WHAT IT MEASURES:
— Quality of insulation between HV circuit and vehicle chassis
— Even with 0V confirmed, degraded insulation = future risk

HOW:
— Megohmmeter applies test voltage (500V or 1000V)
— Measures resistance between HV circuit and chassis
— Good insulation: > 1 megohm (per manufacturer spec)
— Low resistance: degraded insulation — investigate cause

VEHICLE ONBOARD MONITORING:
— EVs continuously monitor insulation resistance
— Dashboard warning: "Insulation Fault" or "HV Isolation Warning"
— If this warning appears:
  TREAT VEHICLE AS FULLY ENERGIZED until verified safe

COMMON CAUSES OF INSULATION DEGRADATION:
— Moisture ingress (flooding, deep water crossing)
— Physical damage (accident, road debris)
— Cable chafing (vibration wear)
— Connector contamination
— Aging of insulation materials
```

## Key Takeaways

1. 50V DC / 30V AC is the threshold for lethal voltage — EV systems operate at 400-800V, far above this threshold
2. 30 milliamps through the heart causes ventricular fibrillation — at 400V through wet skin, you receive 400 mA (13 times lethal)
3. The 6-step isolation procedure is non-negotiable: secure, de-activate, remove MSD, wait, LOTO, verify
4. LOTO means YOUR lock, YOUR key, only YOU remove it — this is the law, not a guideline
5. Class 0 insulating gloves must be inspected EVERY time before use — inflate test and visual inspection
6. Three-point de-energization verification (HV+ to HV-, HV+ to chassis, HV- to chassis) with live-dead-live confirmation is the ONLY proof the system is safe
7. If in doubt, don't touch — call for help
