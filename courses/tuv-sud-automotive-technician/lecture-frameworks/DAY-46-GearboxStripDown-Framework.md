- Safely remove a manual gearbox from a vehicle or prepare a bench unit for disassembly
- Execute a correct disassembly sequence preserving component relationships
- Use bearing pullers, a hydraulic press, and soft-jaw vices without component damage
- Lay out all disassembled components on a bench in reassembly order

### **THEORY BLOCK: Gearbox Anatomy Review** (Slides 5-8, ~20 minutes)

**Narrative Voice:** Concise refresher — not re-teaching Week 6, just refreshing what they need to know for disassembly.

#### Slide 5: Internal Architecture — Refresh
**Visual:** Cutaway diagram of a 2-shaft manual gearbox showing input shaft, output shaft (mainshaft), layshaft/countershaft, selector forks, synchro hubs

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Quick refresher from Week 6. A manual gearbox has two main shafts — the input shaft driven by the clutch, and the output shaft (or mainshaft) that drives the wheels through the final drive. Gears are in constant mesh — they're always turning together. What changes is which gear is LOCKED to its shaft. That's the synchro hub's job — it slides along the shaft, engages a gear through friction synchronisation, and locks it with dog teeth. Selector forks move the synchro hubs. The shift mechanism moves the selector forks. That's the whole system."

**PPT Content:**
```
MANUAL GEARBOX — INTERNAL ARCHITECTURE

INPUT SHAFT
- Driven by clutch
- Carries fixed gears (always meshed with output gears)

OUTPUT SHAFT (MAINSHAFT)
- Carries free-spinning gears on needle bearings
- Synchro hubs slide to LOCK selected gear to shaft

SELECTOR MECHANISM
- Shift lever → cables/rods → selector shaft → forks → synchro hub

BEARINGS
- Input shaft: Pilot bearing (in crankshaft) + front case bearing
- Output shaft: Front + rear bearings (typically taper roller or ball)
- Layshaft: Front + rear bearings

SEALS
- Input shaft seal (clutch side)
- Output/drive flange seals (diff side)
```

#### Slide 6: Disassembly Sequence — The Map
**Visual:** Numbered flowchart showing the disassembly order — external components first, case split, shaft extraction, gear/synchro removal, bearing extraction

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Here's the sequence we'll follow. It matters. You can't get the shafts out until you split the case. You can't split the case until you remove the external components. And you can't remove the bearings until the shafts are out. Each step depends on the one before. Skip a step, and you'll be undoing your own work. This flowchart stays on the wall all day."

**PPT Content:**
```
DISASSEMBLY SEQUENCE

STAGE 1: EXTERNAL STRIP (30 min)
→ Remove shift mechanism, cables, linkage
→ Remove reverse light switch, speed sensor
→ Remove clutch release bearing and fork
→ Drain residual oil if not already done

STAGE 2: CASE SEPARATION (20 min)
→ Remove case bolts (note different lengths!)
→ Mark case halves with paint pen
→ Separate case halves (soft mallet, pry points — NEVER screwdriver)
→ Photograph internal layout immediately

STAGE 3: SHAFT EXTRACTION (30 min)
→ Remove selector shaft(s) and forks
→ Lift out input and output shaft assemblies together
→ Remove differential assembly

STAGE 4: SHAFT DISASSEMBLY (40 min)
→ Remove gears, synchros, spacers from shafts
→ NOTE THE ORDER — photograph and label everything

STAGE 5: BEARING EXTRACTION (20 min)
→ Bearing pullers for shaft bearings
→ Press for tight-fit bearings
→ NEVER use a hammer directly on a bearing
```

#### Slide 8: Bearing Puller Safety & Technique
**Visual:** Step-by-step photo sequence showing correct bearing puller setup — jaw placement behind bearing race, centre bolt on shaft end, gradual tightening

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Bearing pullers store significant energy when loaded. If a jaw slips, it can fly off with force. Here's the correct setup: jaws BEHIND the bearing inner race, not on the cage or rollers. Centre bolt square on the shaft end — use a cap or washer to protect the shaft thread. Tighten gradually and evenly. If it's not moving, check your jaw placement before applying more force. If you hear cracking, STOP — something is wrong. I'll demonstrate on the press as well — the press is safer but requires correct mandrel selection."

**PPT Content:**
```
BEARING PULLER — SAFE TECHNIQUE

SETUP:
1. Select correct puller size (jaws must reach behind bearing race)
2. Position jaws BEHIND inner race — never on cage or rollers
3. Centre bolt aligned with shaft centreline
4. Protect shaft end with cap/washer

EXTRACTION:
5. Tighten centre bolt hand-tight first
6. Verify jaw grip before applying force
7. Tighten in quarter-turn increments
8. If no movement after firm force — STOP and reassess

HYDRAULIC PRESS (preferred for tight bearings):
- Select correct mandrel diameter (smaller than shaft, larger than bore)
- Support workpiece on V-blocks or bearing support plates
- Apply pressure gradually — watch for misalignment
- ALWAYS wear safety glasses — fragments can fly

NEVER: Hit a bearing with a steel hammer
NEVER: Pull on the outer race when removing from a shaft
NEVER: Reuse a bearing that was forcefully removed
```

#### Slide 10: Stage 2 — Case Bolt Removal & Marking
**Visual:** Photo showing case bolts being removed, with arrows indicating different bolt lengths and their positions marked on a diagram

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Case bolts. This is where people make their first mistake. The bolts are NOT all the same length. Some are longer because they pass through thicker flanges or carry alignment dowels. If you put a long bolt in a short hole during reassembly, it bottoms out and cracks the case. So: as you remove each bolt, either push it into a cardboard template that mirrors the bolt pattern, or tape it to a photo printout at its exact position. This takes two minutes and saves you from a very expensive mistake."

**PPT Content:**
```
STAGE 2: CASE BOLTS

CRITICAL: BOLTS ARE DIFFERENT LENGTHS

Method 1: Cardboard Template
- Trace case joint outline on cardboard
- Punch holes at bolt locations
- Push each bolt into its position as you remove it

Method 2: Photo Map
- Print a photo of the case flange
- Tape each bolt to its position on the printout

Method 3: Labelled bags (less ideal)
- Bag and label by position number

ALSO NOTE:
- Dowel pin locations (alignment pins — may need tapping out)
- Sealant type on joint face (RTV silicone vs gasket)
- Any earth/ground strap bolted to case
```

#### Slide 11: Stage 2 — Case Separation
**Visual:** Photo sequence showing correct case separation — using soft mallet at designated pry points, case halves beginning to part, dowel pins visible

**Instructor Narration:**
> "All bolts are out. Now we separate the case halves. The gasket or sealant is bonding them together, plus the dowel pins are a tight fit. NEVER use a screwdriver blade between the case faces — you'll gouge the aluminium and create a leak path. Use the pry points — there are usually cast lugs specifically for this. **PPT Content:**
```
CASE SEPARATION — THE RIGHT WAY

BEFORE YOU START:
- Verify ALL bolts removed (count against the template)
- Check for hidden bolts behind shift mechanism or bearing caps
- Ensure alignment dowels are identified

SEPARATION:
1. Position gearbox with case split line horizontal
2. Tap at designated pry points with soft-face mallet
3. Alternate sides — work evenly around the perimeter
4. Listen for the seal breaking — it will start to gap
5. Once loose, lift the upper case half STRAIGHT UP
6. DO NOT tilt or twist — shafts and forks may bind

IF IT WON'T BUDGE:
- STOP. Recount bolts.
- Look for a hidden bolt behind a bracket or bearing cap
- Check if a snap ring is preventing separation
- NEVER use a steel tool between case faces

IMMEDIATELY AFTER OPENING:
```

#### Slide 14: Stage 3 — Selector Mechanism Removal
**Visual:** Close-up photo of selector shaft(s), detent springs, interlock pins, and forks — with removal sequence numbered

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Selector mechanism comes out first because it sits on top of the gear shafts. You'll have one, two, or three selector rods depending on the design. Each rod has a detent — a spring-loaded ball that clicks into the gear position. Watch for the interlock pins between the rods — they prevent selecting two gears at once. These are small parts that like to fall into the case and disappear. Use a magnetic pickup tool and tray them immediately."

**PPT Content:**
```
STAGE 3A: SELECTOR MECHANISM

COMPONENTS:
- Selector shaft(s) — one per shift rail
- Selector forks — one per synchro hub
- Detent springs and balls — one per rail position
- Interlock pins/plungers — between adjacent rails
- Reverse idler shaft and gear (may be separate)

REMOVAL ORDER:
1. Note which fork is on which rail (photograph + mark)
2. Remove detent plugs carefully — SPRINGS UNDER COMPRESSION
3. Withdraw detent balls and springs (magnet for balls)
4. Slide selector shafts out — note direction
5. Remove forks as shafts clear them
6. Remove interlock pins (may fall out or need magnet)

INSPECTION:
- Fork pads: Check for wear grooves (depth > 0.5mm = replace)
- Rail detent grooves: Check for rounding
- Interlock pins: Check for wear flats
```

#### Slide 15: Stage 3 — Shaft Assembly Removal
**Visual:** Photo showing both shaft assemblies being lifted out together, with arrows indicating the lift direction and caution about bearing fit

**Instructor Narration:**
> "With the selectors out, the shafts can come out. On most transaxle designs, the input and output shafts lift out together — they're meshed. Support both shafts and lift straight up. If a bearing is retained in the case, you may need to tap the shaft from below. The differential usually lifts out separately — it's heavy, so support it properly. Handle these assemblies carefully — dropping a shaft assembly on the bench can damage gear teeth."

**PPT Content:**
```
STAGE 3B: SHAFT EXTRACTION

INPUT + OUTPUT SHAFTS:
1. Verify no snap rings retain bearings in case
2. Support both shafts — they mesh together
3. Lift STRAIGHT UP — no tilting
4. If tight: gentle tap from below with soft mallet
5. Place on clean bench surface or V-blocks

DIFFERENTIAL:
1. Lift straight out — it's heavier than you expect
2. Note preload shim location (on bearing or in case)
3. Place on clean surface — do not roll off bench

REVERSE IDLER:
1. Usually on a separate shaft with a retaining pin
2. Note shaft orientation (may be directional)

PHOTOGRAPH EACH ASSEMBLY AS REMOVED
LAY OUT IN ORDER: Input shaft | Output shaft | Differential | Reverse idler
```

#### Slide 16: Stage 4 — Gear and Synchro Removal from Shafts
**Visual:** Step-by-step photo sequence showing snap ring removal, synchro hub removal, gear sliding off shaft — each step labelled with position number

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Now the detailed work. Each gear and synchro comes off the shaft in sequence. Start from one end — typically the rear. Remove the snap ring, slide off the synchro hub, then the blocker ring, then the gear. Each component gets laid out on the bench IN THE EXACT ORDER it came off. Left to right, matching the shaft sequence. If you mix up the order, reassembly becomes guesswork. The blocker rings are especially important to keep paired with their gear — they wear together."

**PPT Content:**
```
STAGE 4: SHAFT DISASSEMBLY

WORK FROM ONE END TO THE OTHER — DO NOT SKIP AROUND

For each gear position:
1. Remove snap ring (note groove — internal circlip pliers)
2. Slide off synchro hub assembly (hub + sleeve + springs + keys)
3. Remove blocker ring(s) (one or two per synchro — keep paired)
4. Slide off gear (note needle bearing/bush inside gear bore)
5. Remove spacer/thrust washer if present

LAYOUT:
- Use a long bench or clean board
- Lay parts LEFT TO RIGHT in removal order
- Keep each gear position grouped together
- Photograph the complete layout

CRITICAL NOTES:
- Synchro springs and keys are small — don't lose them
- Needle roller bearings inside gears — count them (36? 42?)
- Some gears have integrated synchro cones — handle with care
- Note any shims between gears and bearings — MEASURE and record
```

#### Slide 19: Inspection Methodology — Look Before You Measure
**Visual:** Flowchart: Visual inspection (eyes) → Tactile inspection (fingers) → Measurement (instruments) → Verdict (replace/reuse/borderline)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Now that everything is apart, we inspect. And we start with our eyes, not our micrometers. Visual inspection catches 80% of faults. A pitted bearing is visible. A chipped gear tooth is visible. A worn synchro ring is visible. Run your fingers over bearing races — roughness you can feel means replacement. Only after visual and tactile inspection do we bring out measuring instruments. That comes tomorrow. Today, we're building a fault list from what we can see and feel."

**PPT Content:**
```
INSPECTION METHODOLOGY

STEP 1: VISUAL (today)
- Surface condition — pitting, scoring, discolouration
- Gear teeth — chips, cracks, wear patterns
- Synchro rings — cone surface condition, dog teeth wear
- Bearings — discolouration (blue = overheated), rust, pitting
- Seals — hardening, cracking, lip wear
- Case — cracks, stripped threads, bore wear

STEP 2: TACTILE (today)
- Bearing rotation — smooth or rough?
- Gear bore — smooth or scored?
- Synchro cone — rough (good) or smooth/glazed (bad)?
- Shaft surfaces — smooth or scored at seal contact?

STEP 3: MEASUREMENT (tomorrow — Day 47)
- Synchro ring clearance
- Bearing preload/endplay
- Gear backlash
- Shaft runout

STEP 4: VERDICT
- REPLACE: Clearly worn beyond service
- REUSE: Meets specification
- BORDERLINE: Measure carefully, decide based on data
```

#### Slide 20: Identifying Worn Synchros
**Visual:** Comparison photos — new synchro ring vs worn synchro ring. Close-ups showing cone face wear, dog tooth rounding, and the brass filings that indicate wear

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Synchro rings are the most common wear item inside a gearbox. The cone face does the work — it synchronises the speed through friction, like a tiny clutch. When the cone wears smooth, it can't do its job, and you get that grinding noise going into gear. Look at the cone surface — a good synchro has visible machining grooves or a rough texture. A worn one is smooth and shiny. The dog teeth on the outer edge should be sharp and square — if they're rounded, the synchro can't hold engagement and the gear pops out. Second and third gear synchros wear fastest because they do the most work."

**PPT Content:**
```
SYNCHRO RING INSPECTION — VISUAL

CONE FACE:
- GOOD: Visible machining grooves, rough texture
- WORN: Smooth, polished, glazed appearance
- FAILED: Deep scoring, material transfer, embedded debris

DOG TEETH (outer engagement teeth):
- GOOD: Square, sharp edges
- WORN: Rounded edges, visible chamfer on engagement side
- FAILED: Chipped, cracked, or broken teeth

COMMON FAILURE PATTERN:
- 2nd gear synchro wears first (most used in city driving)
- 3rd gear synchro next
- 5th gear rarely wears (highway cruising — matched speeds)

LOOK FOR:
- Brass particles in oil (synchro wear debris)
- Uneven wear on cone face (misalignment indicator)
- Spring tension in synchro keys — should snap back firmly
```

#### Slide 21: Identifying Gear and Bearing Faults
**Visual:** Grid of close-up photos — pitted bearing race, spalled gear tooth, scored shaft, chipped gear tooth, blue-tinted overheated bearing, micro-pitting on gear flank

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Gear teeth: look at the contact pattern on each flank. Normal wear shows a smooth, polished contact band in the centre of the tooth face. Pitting — small craters on the surface — means fatigue. A chipped tooth means impact damage or a foreign object went through. Bearings: spin each one by hand. A good bearing is glass-smooth. Any roughness, clicking, or notchiness means replacement. Blue or brown discolouration means it overheated — the heat changed the steel's properties and it MUST be replaced. Shaft surfaces: look at the seal contact areas — scoring means a new seal will leak immediately, and the shaft may need repair."

**PPT Content:**
```
GEAR TOOTH INSPECTION:
- Normal: Smooth contact band, centred on tooth face
- Pitting: Small craters — fatigue failure, progressive
- Spalling: Larger material loss — advanced pitting
- Chipping: Edge damage — impact or foreign object
- Scuffing: Welding/tearing marks — lubrication failure

BEARING INSPECTION:
- Spin test: Smooth = OK, Rough = REPLACE
- Visual: Pitting on races = REPLACE
- Colour: Silver/grey = normal, Blue/brown = overheated = REPLACE
- Play: Excessive radial or axial play = REPLACE

SHAFT INSPECTION:
- Seal contact areas: Scoring or grooves = REPAIR or REPLACE
- Bearing seats: Wear or fretting marks = ASSESS
- Spline condition: Worn or twisted splines = REPLACE
- Thread condition: Stripped or damaged threads = REPAIR

CASE INSPECTION:
- Bearing bores: Wear, corrosion, fretting
- Bolt threads: Cross-threading, stripping
- Mating faces: Gouges, warping, porosity
```

## Key Takeaways

1. Photograph BEFORE you remove — your phone camera is your most important tool during disassembly
2. Label, tray, and order every component — disorder during disassembly creates chaos during reassembly
3. Case bolts are NOT all the same length — map them or risk cracking the case
4. Visual inspection before measurement catches 80% of faults
5. Synchro cone texture and dog tooth sharpness are the primary manual gearbox wear indicators
6. A bearing that feels rough when spun by hand is a failed bearing — no measurement needed
