- Explain break-in guidelines (RPM limits, load restrictions, first oil change timing)
- Conduct post-rebuild verification tests (compression, leak-down, oil pressure, coolant pressure)
- Use a scan tool to verify no DTCs are present after rebuild
- Identify abnormal noises during initial running and correlate to potential causes

### **DEVELOPMENT: Theory & Procedures** (Slides 4-12, ~25 minutes)

#### Slide 4: Pre-Start Checklist — Fluids
**Visual:** Checklist graphic with oil dipstick, coolant reservoir, and bleed valve highlighted

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Fluids first. Engine oil: filled to the correct level on the dipstick using the manufacturer-specified grade. Not overfilled — excess oil foams, aerates, and doesn't lubricate properly. Coolant: filled to the correct level AND bled. Air pockets in the cooling system create localised hot spots that can crack a cylinder head in minutes. Most engines have a bleed valve — usually on the thermostat housing or a high point in the coolant system. Fill, open the bleed valve, fill until coolant flows from the bleed valve with no bubbles, close it. On some engines you need to run the heater with the engine idling to fully bleed the system."

**PPT Content:**
```
PRE-START CHECKLIST — FLUIDS

ENGINE OIL:
□ Correct grade (check manufacturer spec — not 'whatever is on the shelf')
□ Filled to correct level on dipstick (not overfilled)
□ New oil filter installed (gasket lubricated, hand-tight + 3/4 turn)
□ Drain plug installed and torqued

COOLANT:
□ Correct mixture (typically 50:50 coolant/water)
□ Filled to correct level
□ System BLED — no air pockets
□ Bleed valve opened during fill, closed when bubble-free flow
□ All hoses connected and clamps tightened

POWER STEERING / TRANSMISSION (if disturbed):
□ Fluid level checked and correct

"Air in coolant = hot spots. Hot spots = cracked heads."
```

#### Slide 5: Pre-Start Checklist — Connections & Sensors
**Visual:** Engine bay with arrows pointing to key sensor locations — CTS, CKP, CMP, MAP, MAF, O2 sensors, injector connectors

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Every single electrical connector must be seated and locked. I mean every one. The critical ones: crankshaft position sensor — no start without it. Camshaft position sensor — rough running or no start. Coolant temperature sensor — wrong fuelling strategy. MAP or MAF sensor — wrong air calculation. Injector connectors — misfires. Coil pack connectors — misfires. Oil pressure sensor — no warning if pressure fails. Oxygen sensors — won't run closed-loop. Go around the engine twice. First pass: connect everything. Second pass: tug-test every connector to confirm it's locked."

**PPT Content:**
```
PRE-START CHECKLIST — CONNECTIONS & SENSORS

CRITICAL (no-start or immediate damage if missing):
□ Crankshaft position sensor (CKP) — connected and seated
□ Camshaft position sensor (CMP) — connected and seated
□ Coolant temperature sensor (CTS) — connected
□ All injector connectors — clicked and locked
□ All coil/plug connectors — clicked and locked

IMPORTANT (poor running or damage risk):
□ MAF / MAP sensor — connected
□ Throttle position sensor — connected
□ Oil pressure sensor/switch — connected
□ Knock sensor(s) — connected
□ Oxygen / lambda sensors — connected
□ Turbo boost control solenoid — connected
□ All vacuum hoses — connected and routed correctly

METHOD: Two passes around the engine
Pass 1: Connect everything
Pass 2: Tug-test every connector — must click and hold
```

#### Slide 6: Pre-Start Checklist — Mechanical Verification
**Visual:** Mechanic peering inside intake manifold with a torch, checking for foreign objects

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before fluids and electrics even matter: mechanical verification. Number one — and this has destroyed more rebuilt engines than any other single mistake — no tools left inside. Look inside the intake ports. Look inside the exhaust ports. Look under the valve cover if accessible. A 10mm socket sitting on top of a piston will punch a hole straight through it on the first compression stroke. Number two: timing verified. Even though we checked this on Day 43, check the marks again. Number three: battery connected and fully charged. A weak battery that cranks slowly won't build oil pressure fast enough. Number four: exhaust connected or at least clear — back-pressure from a blocked exhaust will stall the engine."

**PPT Content:**
```
PRE-START CHECKLIST — MECHANICAL

FOREIGN OBJECTS:
□ Visually inspect intake ports — NO tools, rags, or debris
□ Visually inspect exhaust ports — clear
□ Check under valve/cam cover — nothing left behind
□ Check around timing cover — no loose items

TIMING:
□ Timing marks STILL aligned (re-verify from Day 43)
□ Timing cover and all guards installed
□ Drive belt(s) installed and tensioned

BATTERY:
□ Battery connected (correct polarity!)
□ Battery fully charged (12.6V+ open circuit)
□ Terminals clean and tight

EXHAUST:
□ Exhaust manifold connected
□ Downpipe/turbo connections secure
□ Exhaust path clear (not blocked)

"A 10mm socket on a piston = a hole through the piston."
```

#### Slide 7: Turbo Oil Pre-Lube — Why It's Critical
**Visual:** Cross-section diagram of turbocharger bearing housing showing the oil feed passage, journal bearings, and the thin oil film that separates shaft from housing

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Yesterday you learned that a turbocharger shaft spins at up to 150,000 RPM on a film of oil thinner than a human hair. When an engine sits after a rebuild, that oil film has drained away completely. The turbo bearings are metal-on-metal. If you just turn the key and fire the engine, the turbo shaft accelerates to tens of thousands of RPM in the first second — with no lubrication. The bearings score, the shaft wobbles, and you've killed the turbo before the engine even reaches idle. The solution: pre-lube. Crank the engine on the starter motor with the fuel system disabled. The oil pump is mechanically driven — it runs whenever the engine turns. Crank for 10-15 seconds, pause, crank again. Watch the oil pressure gauge. When you see pressure — typically 1-2 bar on the gauge at cranking speed — the bearings are coated. NOW you can allow combustion."

**PPT Content:**
```
TURBO OIL PRE-LUBE — WHY

PROBLEM:
• After rebuild, turbo bearings have ZERO oil film
• First combustion → turbo spins to 80,000+ RPM instantly
• Journal bearings running dry = scoring in 2-3 seconds
• Scored bearings = turbo replacement ($1,500-$5,000+)

THE OIL PATH (from cold):
Sump → Oil pump → Filter → Main gallery → Turbo feed line → Bearings

TIME TO FILL: 5-15 seconds of cranking (no combustion)

SOLUTION: PRE-LUBE PROCEDURE
Crank on starter with fuel DISABLED
→ Oil pump runs (mechanically driven)
→ Oil fills galleries, reaches turbo bearings
→ When oil pressure gauge shows 1-2 bar → bearings are coated
→ NOW enable fuel and start

"3 seconds of dry bearings = $3,000 turbo replacement"
```

#### Slide 8: Turbo Oil Pre-Lube — Procedure
**Visual:** Step-by-step photo sequence: fuse box with fuel pump fuse removed, cranking with oil pressure gauge visible, fuse being reinstalled

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Here's the exact procedure. Step one: disable the fuel system. The cleanest method is to pull the fuel pump relay or fuse — this prevents fuel delivery while allowing everything else to function normally. Alternatively, you can unplug the injector connectors, but that's more work. Step two: turn the ignition to crank. Hold the starter for 10 seconds. Do NOT exceed 15 seconds — you'll overheat the starter motor. Release, wait 10 seconds for the starter to cool. Step three: watch the oil pressure gauge or warning light. If the light goes out or you see 1+ bar on the gauge during cranking, oil pressure is established. If not, crank again for another 10 seconds. Step four: once oil pressure is confirmed, reinstall the fuel pump relay/fuse. Step five: reconnect anything you disconnected. You are now ready for first start."

**PPT Content:**
```
TURBO OIL PRE-LUBE — STEP BY STEP

1. DISABLE FUEL DELIVERY
   • Remove fuel pump relay/fuse (check fuse box diagram)
   • OR: Disconnect injector connectors (more effort)
   • Engine will crank but NOT fire

2. CRANK ENGINE — FIRST CYCLE
   • Turn key to START / press starter button
   • Hold for 10 seconds (MAX 15 seconds — starter motor limit)
   • Watch oil pressure gauge / oil warning light

3. PAUSE & CHECK
   • Release starter, wait 10 seconds (starter cool-down)
   • Did oil pressure light go out? → Oil pressure building
   • Did gauge show 1+ bar? → Gallery filling

4. CRANK AGAIN IF NEEDED
   • Repeat crank cycle until oil pressure confirmed
   • Typically 2-3 cycles for a freshly rebuilt engine

5. RESTORE FUEL SYSTEM
   • Reinstall fuel pump relay/fuse
   • Reconnect any disconnected connectors
   • Verify everything restored

→ ENGINE IS NOW PRE-LUBED AND READY FOR FIRST START
```

#### Slide 9: First-Start Protocol
**Visual:** Dashboard oil pressure gauge prominently displayed with green "safe" zone marked, and a stopwatch showing 5:00 minutes

**Instructor Narration:**
> "With oil pressure confirmed from pre-lube, here's the first-start protocol. Fuel is enabled. Key to start. The engine should fire within a few seconds — if it doesn't catch within 10 seconds of cranking, stop and investigate. Do not grind the starter hoping it will catch. Once it fires: immediately look at the oil pressure gauge. You need to see pressure within 3 seconds of the engine running. If you don't see oil pressure within 5 seconds — shut it down immediately. Something is wrong. If oil pressure is good: let it idle. Do not rev it. Do not touch the throttle. Let it idle for a minimum of 5 minutes. During those 5 minutes, you're watching for leaks — oil, coolant, fuel, exhaust. You're listening for abnormal noises — knocking, ticking, rattling, squealing. You're watching the temperature gauge — it should rise slowly and steadily."

**PPT Content:**
```
FIRST-START PROTOCOL

1. ENABLE FUEL (relay/fuse reinstalled from pre-lube step)
2. KEY TO START — engine should fire within 5-10 seconds
   ⚠ If no start in 10 seconds → STOP cranking → Investigate
3. IMMEDIATELY monitor oil pressure gauge
   ✅ Pressure within 3 seconds → GOOD
   ⚠ No pressure within 5 seconds → SHUT DOWN IMMEDIATELY

4. LET IT IDLE — minimum 5 minutes, DO NOT rev
   While idling, monitor:
   □ Oil pressure gauge — steady, within spec
   □ Temperature gauge — rising slowly and steadily
   □ Visual leak check — oil, coolant, fuel, exhaust
   □ Listen — any knock, tick, rattle, or squeal?
   □ Exhaust smoke — brief white = condensation (normal)
     → Persistent white = coolant burning (problem)
     → Blue = oil burning (rings not seated yet — monitor)

5. After 5 min stable idle → gently raise RPM to 2000 for 30 sec
6. Return to idle → re-check all gauges and leak points

"First 5 minutes: eyes on gauges, ears on engine, hands off throttle."
```

#### Slide 10: Break-In Guidelines
**Visual:** RPM gauge with a red zone marked below 3000 RPM for first 30 minutes, gradient chart showing load restrictions over first 1000 km

**Instructor Narration:**
> "If the engine starts, idles, shows good oil pressure, and has no leaks — congratulations. But the job isn't done. The break-in period is where the piston rings seat against the cylinder walls. If you baby the engine — constant gentle cruise at 2000 RPM — the rings glaze and never seat properly. You'll get oil consumption for the life of the engine. If you abuse it — full throttle, redline, maximum load — you'll scuff the bores before the crosshatch pattern has done its job. The sweet spot: vary the RPM. Accelerate, decelerate, use engine braking. But stay under 3000 RPM for the first 30 minutes of running. No more than 75% throttle for the first 500 km. No sustained constant speed for more than 2 minutes. And critically: change the oil at 500 km. That first oil is full of microscopic metal particles from the rings and bores bedding in. You want those particles OUT."

**PPT Content:**
```
BREAK-IN GUIDELINES

FIRST 30 MINUTES:
• Maximum 3000 RPM
• Idle to 3000 RPM range — vary constantly
• No sustained single RPM for more than 60 seconds
• Monitor oil pressure and temperature throughout

FIRST 500 KM:
• Maximum 75% throttle — no full-load
• Vary RPM between 1500-3500 RPM continuously
• Use engine braking (helps ring seating)
• No cruise control (constant speed = ring glazing)
• No towing or heavy loads

500 - 1000 KM:
• Gradually extend to full RPM range
• Still avoid sustained full-load
• Monitor oil consumption (some is normal during break-in)

FIRST OIL CHANGE:
→ AT 500 KM (or per manufacturer recommendation)
→ Break-in oil is full of metal particles from ring seating
→ New oil + new filter — non-negotiable

"Vary RPM, avoid extremes, change oil early."
```

#### Slide 11: Post-Rebuild Verification — Compression & Leak-Down
**Visual:** Split image — compression tester screwed into spark plug hole on left, leak-down tester connected with percentage gauge on right

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Once the engine has run and reached operating temperature, we verify the rebuild quality with tests. Compression test first: remove all spark plugs, disable fuel and ignition, screw in the compression gauge, crank each cylinder for 5-6 compression strokes. Record the reading. All cylinders should be within 10% of each other. If one is low — there's a sealing problem. Leak-down test next: this tells you WHERE the problem is. With the piston at TDC on the compression stroke, you pressurize the cylinder with regulated shop air and measure how much leaks past. Less than 10% leak-down is good — that's a healthy cylinder. 10-20% means investigate — listen at the intake, exhaust, crankcase, and coolant filler to locate the leak path. Over 20% is a problem that needs correction."

**PPT Content:**
```
POST-REBUILD VERIFICATION — COMPRESSION TEST

PROCEDURE:
1. Engine at operating temperature
2. Remove ALL spark plugs
3. Disable fuel and ignition
4. Screw compression gauge into cylinder 1
5. Crank 5-6 compression strokes — record peak reading
6. Repeat for all cylinders

RESULTS:
✅ All cylinders within 10% of each other → GOOD
⚠ One cylinder >10% low → Investigate
⚠ Adjacent cylinders both low → Head gasket suspect

LEAK-DOWN TEST:
1. Piston at TDC compression stroke
2. Apply regulated air (100 PSI / 7 bar typical)
3. Read percentage of air leaking past

< 10% leakage = Good (healthy cylinder)
10-20% leakage = Investigate — find the leak path
> 20% leakage = Problem — correction required

LISTEN FOR LEAK SOURCE:
• Intake → Intake valve not sealing
• Exhaust → Exhaust valve not sealing
• Crankcase (dipstick/oil cap) → Rings not sealing
• Coolant filler (bubbles) → Head gasket issue
```

#### Slide 12: Post-Rebuild Verification — Oil Pressure, Coolant, Scan Tool
**Visual:** Three-panel image: mechanical oil pressure gauge, coolant pressure tester on radiator, scan tool showing "No DTCs Found"

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Oil pressure verification: at idle, a healthy engine typically shows 1-2 bar minimum — check the manufacturer spec. At 3000 RPM, expect 3-5 bar. If pressure is low at idle but comes up at RPM, suspect bearing clearance issues. If it's low across the board, there may be an oil pump issue or a gallery leak. Coolant system pressure test: fit the pressure tester to the radiator or expansion tank, pump to system pressure — typically 1.0 to 1.5 bar — and hold for 10 minutes. If pressure drops, you have a leak. External leaks you can find visually. Internal leaks — into a cylinder or oil passage — are harder and may only show up as white exhaust smoke or milky oil. Finally: connect the scan tool. Read all DTCs. On a correctly rebuilt and connected engine, you should have zero active fault codes. If you see codes — read them, diagnose them, fix them. A DTC after a rebuild usually means a sensor wasn't reconnected or was damaged during the work."

**PPT Content:**
```
POST-REBUILD VERIFICATION — OIL, COOLANT, SCAN TOOL

OIL PRESSURE (mechanical gauge for accuracy):
• At idle (warm): Minimum 1.0 bar (check spec — varies by engine)
• At 3000 RPM: Typically 3.0-5.0 bar
• Low at idle, OK at RPM → Bearing clearance concern
• Low everywhere → Oil pump or gallery leak

COOLANT SYSTEM PRESSURE TEST:
1. Fit pressure tester to radiator/expansion tank cap opening
2. Pump to system rated pressure (typically 1.0-1.5 bar)
3. Hold for 10 minutes
✅ Pressure holds steady → System sealed
⚠ Pressure drops → Leak — find and fix

SCAN TOOL CHECK:
1. Connect OBD-II scan tool
2. Read ALL system DTCs (not just engine)
3. Expected result: ZERO active fault codes
⚠ DTCs present → Likely disconnected or damaged sensor
⚠ Address every DTC — do not clear and ignore

NOISE CHECK (listen with stethoscope or trained ear):
• Knock = bearing or piston issue
• Tick = valve train clearance or exhaust leak
• Rattle = loose component or timing chain
• Squeal = belt tension or pulley alignment
```

SETUP (per cylinder):
1. Rotate engine to TDC COMPRESSION stroke for test cylinder
   (Both valves closed — verify with valve movement)
2. Connect leak-down tester to spark plug hole
3. Apply regulated air (typically 100 PSI / 7 bar)

READING:
< 10% → GOOD — healthy sealing
10-20% → INVESTIGATE — locate leak source
> 20% → PROBLEM — correction needed

LOCATE THE LEAK — LISTEN:
□ Intake manifold → Intake valve leaking
□ Exhaust/tailpipe → Exhaust valve leaking
□ Oil filler cap / dipstick → Piston rings leaking
□ Coolant filler (bubbles) → Head gasket leaking

RECORD:
Cyl 1: ____%    Cyl 2: ____%
Cyl 3: ____%    Cyl 4: ____%

Compare with compression results for full picture
```

#### Slide 20: Phase 6 — Noise Assessment & Road Test
**Instructor Action:**
- With engine running at idle: guided listening exercise
- Use automotive stethoscope or long screwdriver (blade on engine, handle to ear)
- Listen at each cylinder (injector area) for consistent ticking
- Listen at timing cover for chain/belt noise
- Listen at front accessories for bearing noise
- Listen at exhaust manifold for leaks (hissing)
- If engine is in a vehicle: road test at low speed

  - Monitor for vibration, hesitation, or abnormal noise
  - Check oil pressure during acceleration
- If engine is on a stand: extended RPM sweep (idle to 2500 RPM, vary, hold, release)

**PPT Content:**
```

NOISE ASSESSMENT (engine idling):
Use stethoscope or long screwdriver (handle to ear, blade on surface)

LISTEN AT:
□ Each cylinder (injector area) — consistent tick = normal
  → Uneven or loud tick = valve clearance or lifter issue
□ Timing cover — smooth whir = normal

□ Front accessories — smooth = normal

ROAD TEST (if in vehicle):
□ Gentle acceleration through all gears
□ Monitor oil pressure under load
□ Listen for knock under acceleration (timing/fuelling issue)
□ Check for vibration at various speeds
□ Brake test — safe stopping confirmed

ON STAND (if not in vehicle):
□ Idle → 2000 → 2500 → idle → 1500 → 3000 → idle
□ Vary RPM, listen for changes in noise character

## Assessment Checkpoint

- Pre-start checklist completed accurately without instructor prompting
- Turbo oil pre-lube executed correctly (fuel disabled, cranked to oil pressure, fuel restored)
- First-start protocol followed (immediate oil pressure monitoring, 5-minute idle, leak check)
- Can explain break-in guidelines (RPM limits, load limits, first oil change at 500 km)
- Compression test performed correctly — readings recorded, variation calculated
- Leak-down test performed correctly — percentages recorded, leak sources identified by listening
- Oil pressure readings at idle and 3000 RPM recorded and compared to specification
- Coolant pressure test held for 10 minutes — result documented
- Scan tool connected and DTCs read — result documented
- Post-rebuild results sheet complete and signed

---

## Key Takeaways

1. Never start a rebuilt engine without completing the full pre-start checklist — every item matters
2. Turbo oil pre-lube is mandatory: crank with fuel disabled until oil pressure shows on the gauge — 3 seconds of dry turbo bearings costs thousands
3. First-start priority is oil pressure — if you don't see pressure within 5 seconds, shut it down
4. Break-in requires varied RPM, no full-load for 500 km, and first oil change at 500 km
5. Compression test: all cylinders within 10% of each other indicates good uniform sealing
6. Leak-down test: less than 10% is good, 10-20% investigate, over 20% is a problem — listen to locate the leak path
7. A zero-DTC scan tool readout after a rebuild confirms all electronic systems are properly reconnected
8. This week you proved that a systematic, verification-driven approach can take an engine from a pile of parts to a running, tested power unit
