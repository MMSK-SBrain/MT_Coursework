#### Slide 6: Halogen Headlights — The Foundation
**Visual:** Cross-section diagram of a halogen bulb showing the tungsten filament, halogen gas fill (iodine/bromine), quartz glass envelope. Beside it, photos of H1, H4, and H7 bulbs at actual relative size.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "A halogen bulb is an incandescent bulb filled with halogen gas — typically iodine or bromine. The halogen gas creates a chemical cycle: tungsten evaporates from the hot filament, combines with the halogen, and redeposits back on the filament. This extends bulb life from about 200 hours to 500-1000 hours and allows a higher filament temperature, which means whiter, brighter light.
>
> You need to know three bulb types. H1 — single filament, used in projector-type low beams and fog lights. H4 — dual filament, one bulb handles both low beam and high beam. It has three pins and a specific orientation tab. H7 — single filament, the most common halogen headlight bulb in European vehicles for the past 20 years. Each fits only its own socket — they are NOT interchangeable."

**PPT Content:**
```
HALOGEN HEADLIGHTS — THE FOUNDATION

How it works:
- Tungsten filament heated to ~3200 K by electrical current
- Halogen gas (iodine/bromine) recycles evaporated tungsten back to filament
- Extends life vs. standard incandescent (500-1000 hours)

Common bulb types:
  H1: Single filament, 55W, projector low beam / fog light
  H4: DUAL filament, 60W (high) / 55W (low), 3-pin connector
  H7: Single filament, 55W, most common European low beam

Colour temperature: ~3200 K (warm yellowish-white)
Power consumption: 55-60W per bulb

CRITICAL HANDLING RULE:
Never touch the quartz glass with bare fingers.
Skin oils create hot spots → premature bulb failure.
Always handle with clean gloves or a lint-free cloth.
```

#### Slide 7: HID / Xenon Headlights — The Arc Discharge
**Visual:** Diagram showing the complete HID system: ballast (electronic control unit), igniter (high-voltage pulse generator), and D-type bulb. Cross-section of a D2S bulb showing the two electrodes and xenon gas chamber. Separate callout showing the auto-levelling motor on the headlight housing.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "HID stands for High Intensity Discharge. Instead of a filament, two electrodes create an electrical arc through xenon gas inside a small quartz capsule. This arc produces roughly three times the light output of a halogen bulb while using only 35 watts. The light is much whiter — around 4300 Kelvin.
>
> But you can't just apply 12 volts. The system needs a ballast — an electronic module that converts 12V DC to a high-frequency AC supply. And to start the arc, the igniter produces a pulse of 20,000 to 25,000 volts. That's enough to kill you. Never work on a HID system with the ignition on.
>
> The common bulb designations are D1S and D2S. The 'D' stands for discharge, the number indicates the generation, and 'S' means it has an integrated UV-blocking shield — required because the arc produces ultraviolet light that would damage the polycarbonate lens. D1S has the igniter integrated into the bulb base. D2S uses a separate igniter.
>
> Here's the legal point: because HID headlights are so bright, EU regulations require automatic levelling. A sensor on the rear axle measures vehicle load — if the boot is full, the rear drops, the front rises, and the headlights would aim upward into oncoming traffic. The auto-levelling system detects this and adjusts the headlight motor to keep the beam aimed correctly. No auto-levelling, no legal HID headlight. A headlight washer system is also required to keep the lens clean, because dirt on a HID lens scatters the intense light."

**PPT Content:**
```
HID / XENON HEADLIGHTS — ARC DISCHARGE

How it works:
- Electrical arc between two electrodes in xenon gas capsule
- Ballast converts 12V DC → high-frequency AC (approx. 85V operating)
- Igniter produces 20,000-25,000V starting pulse
- Light output: ~3200 lumens (vs. ~1500 for halogen H7)

Bulb types:
  D1S: Igniter integrated in bulb base, 35W
  D2S: Separate igniter, 35W
  "S" = UV shield integrated (required for polycarbonate lenses)

Colour temperature: ~4300 K (bright white)

LEGAL REQUIREMENTS (EU):
  1. Automatic headlight levelling — MANDATORY
     (Rear axle sensor → levelling motor adjusts beam height)
  2. Headlight washer system — MANDATORY
     (Dirt on lens scatters intense HID light)

SAFETY WARNING:
Igniter produces 25,000V — LETHAL
Never work on HID system with ignition ON.
Wait minimum 1 minute after switching off before touching components.
```

#### Slide 8: LED Headlights — Solid State Lighting
**Visual:** Diagram of an LED headlight module showing: LED chip array on a heat sink substrate, driver module (current regulator), heat sink with fins or heat pipe, and the optical lens/reflector that shapes the beam. Photo of a disassembled LED headlight unit showing these components.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "LED headlights are a completely different technology. An LED — Light Emitting Diode — produces light when current flows through a semiconductor junction. No filament, no arc, no gas. The light is produced at the atomic level.
>
> LEDs are incredibly efficient — they produce more light per watt than halogen or HID. They last 15,000 to 50,000 hours compared to halogen's 500. They reach full brightness instantly — no warm-up time like HID. And because each LED is small, designers can create headlight shapes that were impossible with bulb-based systems.
>
> But LED headlights generate heat at the back of the LED chip, not at the front like a bulb. This heat must be managed or the LED degrades and eventually fails. That's why every LED headlight has a substantial heat sink — aluminium fins, a heat pipe, or sometimes a small electric fan. The driver module regulates current precisely. Too much current and the LED overheats and dies. Too little and you lose brightness.
>
> On most LED headlight vehicles, the entire headlight assembly is sealed. You don't replace individual LEDs — you replace the module or the whole headlight unit. This makes LED headlights significantly more expensive to repair than halogen."

**PPT Content:**
```
LED HEADLIGHTS — SOLID STATE LIGHTING

How it works:
- Current flows through semiconductor junction → photon emission
- No filament, no arc, no warm-up time
- Multiple LED chips arranged in array, each precisely aimed

Key components:
  1. LED chip array — the light source (multiple chips per function)
  2. Driver module — regulates current (constant current source)
  3. Heat sink — dissipates heat from rear of LED chips
     (Aluminium fins, heat pipes, or active fan cooling)
  4. Optics — lens and/or reflector shapes the beam pattern

Specifications:
- Colour temperature: ~5500-6500 K (daylight white)
- Lifespan: 15,000-50,000 hours
- Power: 20-40W per headlight (lower than halogen)
- Full brightness: Instant (no warm-up)

SERVICE NOTE:
LED headlights are generally NOT bulb-replaceable.
Failed LED = replace module or complete headlight assembly.
Cost: 500-2000+ per headlight unit.

LEGAL: Auto-levelling REQUIRED (same as HID).
```

#### Slide 9: Matrix LED / Adaptive Headlights — Intelligent Light
**Visual:** Illustration showing a matrix LED headlight with individually addressable LED segments. Beside it, a road scene diagram: the headlight illuminates the full road ahead but creates shadow "holes" around two oncoming vehicles, preventing glare while maintaining full illumination everywhere else. Small inset showing the windscreen-mounted camera that detects oncoming traffic.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Matrix LED is the most advanced headlight technology on the road today. Imagine having high beam on all the time — full illumination ahead — but the system can selectively switch off or dim individual LED segments to avoid blinding oncoming vehicles or the vehicle ahead of you. That's matrix LED.
>
> A camera mounted near the rear-view mirror detects oncoming headlights and the tail lights of vehicles ahead. The headlight control ECU maps those positions onto the LED matrix and dims only the specific segments that would cause glare. The rest of the road stays fully illuminated. Some systems have over 80 individually controllable LED segments per headlight. The newest systems — called digital light or micro-LED — have thousands.
>
> This is a perfect example of cross-system integration. The lighting system depends on a camera — that's an ADAS component. The camera feeds the headlight ECU over the vehicle network. The ECU processes in real time and adjusts the LED matrix faster than the human eye can perceive. Steering angle data from the chassis CAN bus enables cornering light functionality. Speed data adjusts the beam shape for city versus motorway driving."

**PPT Content:**
```
MATRIX LED / ADAPTIVE HEADLIGHTS — INTELLIGENT LIGHT

How it works:
- Array of individually controllable LED segments (32 to 84+ per headlight)
- Camera on windscreen detects oncoming / preceding vehicles
- Headlight ECU maps vehicle positions → dims specific LED segments
- Creates "shadow zones" around other road users
- Full high beam illumination everywhere else — NO glare

System components:
  1. Matrix LED headlight module (multiple individually addressable LEDs)
  2. Front camera (shares with ADAS — lane keeping, sign recognition)
  3. Headlight control ECU (dedicated or integrated in BCM)
  4. Vehicle network data: steering angle, vehicle speed, navigation

CROSS-SYSTEM INTEGRATION:
  Camera (ADAS) + Steering angle sensor (chassis) + Speed (powertrain)
  + Navigation data + BCM control = Adaptive lighting

Advanced variants:
- Digital Light / HD headlights: 1000+ micro-LED pixels
- Projecting lane markings, symbols, or warnings onto the road
```

#### Slide 10: Projector Lens vs Reflector Headlights
**Visual:** Side-by-side cross-section diagrams. Left: reflector headlight showing the bulb at the focal point of a parabolic reflector, light bouncing forward through a clear lens. Right: projector headlight showing the bulb inside an ellipsoidal reflector, light focused through a condenser lens with a sharp cutoff shield visible.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Whatever bulb technology you use, the light has to be shaped into a beam pattern. There are two designs. A reflector headlight uses a carefully shaped mirror behind the bulb. The bulb sits at the focal point, and the reflector bounces light forward. Modern reflector designs use computer-calculated complex surfaces — not a simple parabola — to create a good beam pattern directly. They're simpler and cheaper but generally produce a less sharp beam cutoff.
>
> A projector headlight — sometimes called a lens-type headlight — uses an ellipsoidal reflector that focuses light through a condenser lens. Between the reflector and lens, there's a cutoff shield that creates the sharp horizontal line you see in the beam pattern. This sharp cutoff is critical — it's what prevents the light from shining upward into oncoming drivers' eyes. Projector headlights produce a sharper, more defined beam. HID and many LED systems use projector optics because of the intense light output that needs precise control."

**PPT Content:**
```
HEADLIGHT TYPES: PROJECTOR LENS vs REFLECTOR

REFLECTOR HEADLIGHT:
- Bulb at focal point of shaped reflector
- Light bounces forward through clear lens cover
- Computer-designed complex surface reflectors (modern)
- Simpler, cheaper, lighter
- Less sharp beam cutoff
- Common with: Halogen (H4, H7)

PROJECTOR (LENS) HEADLIGHT:
- Bulb inside ellipsoidal reflector
- Light focused through a condenser lens
- Cutoff shield creates SHARP horizontal beam line
- More precise beam pattern, better light distribution
- More complex, heavier, more expensive
- Common with: HID/Xenon, LED, Matrix LED

WHY IT MATTERS:
The cutoff line determines whether you blind oncoming traffic.
Projector = sharp cutoff = easier to aim precisely.
Reflector = softer cutoff = more tolerance needed in aiming.
```

#### Slide 11: Headlight Aiming and Alignment — Equipment
**Visual:** Photo of a professional beam setter (headlight alignment device on a rail) positioned in front of a vehicle. Inset photos: (1) alignment screen with marked target zones, (2) the adjustment screws on the back of a headlight housing, (3) a headlight levelling motor (small electric motor mounted on the headlight reflector pivot).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Headlight aiming is one of the most common tasks in a workshop, and one of the most commonly done incorrectly. The goal is simple: the low beam cutoff line should illuminate the road ahead without shining into oncoming drivers' eyes. Too high and you blind people. Too low and the driver can't see far enough.
>
> The professional tool is a beam setter — sometimes called an optical aimer. It's a box on a rail that you position in front of each headlight. Inside, it has a lens and a screen that shows you the beam pattern. You align the cutoff line to a reference mark. The advantage: it doesn't require a perfectly flat floor, and it works regardless of vehicle loading.
>
> The simpler method uses a flat wall or alignment screen. You park the car exactly 10 metres from the wall on a level floor, mark the headlight centre height, and adjust until the cutoff line falls to the correct position — typically 1-2% below the headlight centre height. That's 10 to 20 centimetres drop at 10 metres."

**PPT Content:**
```
HEADLIGHT AIMING — EQUIPMENT & METHOD

PROFESSIONAL BEAM SETTER:
- Optical alignment device on floor-mounted rail
- Positions in front of each headlight individually
- Lens and screen display the beam pattern
- Align cutoff to reference marks
- Independent of floor flatness and vehicle load
- Required for MOT/roadworthiness testing stations

ALIGNMENT SCREEN METHOD (workshop wall):
- Flat wall or dedicated alignment screen
- Vehicle on level floor, 10 metres from wall
- Mark headlight centre height on screen
- Low beam cutoff should fall 10-20 cm below centre (1-2% drop)
- Left-hand cutoff flat, right-hand side rises at 15 degrees (EU/RHD)

ADJUSTMENT MECHANISM:
- Static: Adjustment screws on headlight housing
  (separate screws for vertical and horizontal adjustment)
- Dynamic: Levelling motor controlled by headlight range ECU
  (responds to rear axle height sensor / vehicle load)
```

#### Slide 12: Headlight Aiming — Procedure Step by Step
**Visual:** Numbered step-by-step diagram showing: (1) vehicle positioned on level surface, (2) tyres inflated to correct pressure, (3) fuel tank half full / no extra load, (4) beam setter positioned, (5) adjustment screws identified, (6) cutoff line aligned to target. Small inset showing the EU beam pattern with the flat cutoff on the left and the 15-degree rise on the right (for right-hand traffic).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The procedure is precise. Step one: the vehicle must be on a level surface. Step two: tyres at the specified pressure — incorrect tyre pressure changes ride height and therefore beam aim. Step three: the vehicle should be at its standard loading — no heavy items in the boot, fuel tank approximately half full, no passengers except the driver or a 75 kg weight on the driver's seat. Step four: bounce the suspension to settle it. Step five: position the beam setter or mark the screen. Step six: switch on the low beam and identify the cutoff line. Step seven: use the horizontal and vertical adjustment screws on the headlight housing — usually accessible from behind or through the engine bay — to position the cutoff to specification.
>
> The EU low beam pattern has a characteristic shape: flat cutoff on the left side — that's the oncoming traffic side — and a 15-degree upward rise on the right side to illuminate road signs and the verge. This asymmetric pattern is designed for right-hand traffic. UK-spec vehicles have the pattern mirrored."

**PPT Content:**
```
HEADLIGHT AIMING PROCEDURE

PREPARATION:
1. Vehicle on LEVEL surface
2. Tyres at SPECIFIED pressure
3. Standard load only (half tank fuel, no extra weight)
4. Bounce suspension to settle ride height
5. Clean headlight lenses

ALIGNMENT:
6. Position beam setter in front of headlight (or use wall at 10m)
7. Switch on LOW BEAM (one headlight at a time — cover the other)
8. Identify the cutoff line on the screen
9. Adjust VERTICAL screw: cutoff 1-2% below headlight centre
10. Adjust HORIZONTAL screw: cutoff break point centred on headlight axis

EU LOW BEAM PATTERN (right-hand traffic):
- LEFT side: flat horizontal cutoff (protects oncoming traffic)
- RIGHT side: 15-degree upward rise (illuminates signs and verge)
- Break point: where flat meets rise — aligned to headlight centre

DYNAMIC LEVELLING CHECK:
- With ignition on, verify levelling motor responds
- Load rear axle → headlights should dip automatically
```

#### Slide 13: Daytime Running Lights (DRL)
**Visual:** Front view of two vehicles side by side: one with dedicated DRL LED strip running, one with reduced-power headlights serving as DRL. Inset showing the EU regulation symbol for DRL.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Daytime Running Lights — DRLs — are mandatory on all new vehicles sold in the EU since 2011. Their purpose is simple: make the vehicle visible to other road users during daylight. They're not for the driver's visibility — they're for being seen.
>
> There are two implementations. Dedicated DRL: a separate LED strip or light unit that activates automatically when the engine starts and switches off when the headlights are turned on or the vehicle is parked. The second method: reduced-power headlights — the low beam operates at lower current, producing enough light to be visible but not as much as full headlights. The dedicated LED approach is far more common on modern vehicles because it uses very little power — typically 5 to 10 watts total versus 110 watts for two halogen low beams."

**PPT Content:**
```
DAYTIME RUNNING LIGHTS (DRL)

Purpose: Make vehicle visible during daylight — "be seen" not "see"
Legal: Mandatory on all new EU vehicles since February 2011

Two implementations:

1. DEDICATED DRL (most common modern approach):
   - Separate LED strip or light unit in the headlight assembly
   - Activates automatically: engine on / vehicle running
   - Deactivates when: headlights switched on, or vehicle parked
   - Power: 5-10W total (very efficient)
   - Controlled by: BCM

2. REDUCED-POWER HEADLIGHTS:
   - Low beam operates at reduced current (~70% brightness)
   - Higher power consumption than dedicated LED DRL
   - Older vehicles / budget models

DRL INTERACTION:
- DRL on → turn signal activated → DRL on that side may dim or switch off
  (prevents confusion between DRL and indicator)
- DRL on → headlights switched on → DRL deactivates or dims to position light
```

#### Slide 14: Tail Lights — Rear Lighting Systems
**Visual:** Annotated photo of a modern tail light cluster showing: brake light (red, brighter), tail/position light (red, dimmer), turn indicator (amber), reverse light (white), rear fog light (red, single — usually left side). Diagram showing the wiring: common ground, individual feeds from BCM.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The rear of the vehicle carries five mandatory lighting functions, often combined in one or two units. Tail lights — or position lights — are the dim red lights that indicate the vehicle's presence and width. They come on with the headlights. Brake lights share the same colour but are significantly brighter — they're activated by the brake light switch on the pedal. Turn indicators are amber. Reverse lights are white — they illuminate when reverse gear is selected. And the rear fog light is a high-intensity red light — in the EU, at least one is required, and it's usually on the left side only.
>
> Modern tail lights are almost exclusively LED arrays. Each function uses a separate set of LEDs within the cluster. LED tail lights last the life of the vehicle but if one fails, you often replace the entire module — no individual bulb replacement. On older vehicles, these are standard bulb types: P21W for brake and reverse, P21/5W for combined tail/brake, PY21W for amber indicators."

**PPT Content:**
```
TAIL LIGHTS — REAR LIGHTING FUNCTIONS

Five mandatory functions:

1. TAIL / POSITION LIGHT (red, dim)
   - Indicates vehicle presence and width
   - Activated with headlights / parking lights

2. BRAKE LIGHT (red, bright)
   - Signals deceleration — activated by brake pedal switch
   - Third high-mount brake light (CHMSL) — mandatory since 1998

3. TURN INDICATOR (amber)
   - Signals lane change / turning intent
   - Flashes at 1-2 Hz frequency

4. REVERSE LIGHT (white)
   - Illuminates behind vehicle when reversing
   - Activated by reverse gear switch / transmission range sensor

5. REAR FOG LIGHT (red, high intensity)
   - Minimum one required (EU — usually left side)
   - Only used in fog / poor visibility (<50m)
   - Separate switch, warning light on dashboard

LED tail lights: Module replacement (no individual bulb swap)
Bulb-type tail lights: P21W, P21/5W, PY21W common types
```

#### Slide 15: Turn Indicators and Hazard Lights
**Visual:** Diagram showing the turn signal circuit: from the BCM (or flasher relay on older vehicles) through the combination switch, to front indicator, side repeater, rear indicator, and dashboard tell-tale. Inset photo of a sequential LED indicator showing the progressive sweep pattern.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Turn indicators — or blinkers, or flashers — communicate your intention to turn or change lanes. On older vehicles, a thermal flasher relay created the on-off cycle — a bimetallic strip heated up, bent, broke the circuit, cooled, reconnected. If a bulb failed, the relay cycled faster because the load decreased — that's why a fast-flashing indicator tells you a bulb is out.
>
> Modern vehicles have no flasher relay. The BCM — Body Control Module — handles indicator timing digitally. It controls each indicator bulb or LED module individually over the vehicle network. The BCM monitors the current draw on each circuit. If a bulb fails, the BCM detects the reduced current and displays a warning on the instrument cluster.
>
> Sequential indicators are now common on LED-equipped vehicles. Instead of the whole lens flashing on and off, the LEDs illuminate in a sweep pattern from the inside to the outside of the vehicle. This is purely a design feature — the timing and visibility are regulated to the same standards as conventional indicators."

**PPT Content:**
```
TURN INDICATORS & HAZARD LIGHTS

CONVENTIONAL (older vehicles):
- Flasher relay (thermal or electronic) creates on/off cycle
- Combination switch selects left/right
- Fast flash = bulb failure (lower load → faster cycle)
- Standard frequency: 1-2 Hz (60-120 flashes/minute)

MODERN (BCM controlled):
- BCM generates indicator timing digitally
- No separate flasher relay — function integrated in BCM
- BCM monitors current per circuit → detects bulb failure
- Warning on instrument cluster: "bulb out" or specific indicator

HAZARD LIGHTS:
- Both left and right indicators flash simultaneously
- Separate switch (usually red triangle on dashboard)
- Must function even with ignition off (direct battery feed)

SEQUENTIAL LED INDICATORS:
- LEDs illuminate in a progressive sweep (inside → outside)
- Same flash frequency as conventional (1-2 Hz total cycle)
- Controlled by headlight/tail light module or BCM

DIAGNOSTIC: Indicator fault → check BCM, wiring, ground, bulb/LED module
```

#### Slide 17: Adaptive and Automatic Lighting Systems
**Visual:** System diagram showing the automatic lighting chain: light sensor on windscreen → BCM → headlight activation. Below it: auto high-beam chain: camera → image processing → BCM → headlight high/low beam switching. Below that: cornering light chain: steering angle sensor → BCM → cornering fog light or swivelling headlight module.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Modern vehicles don't just have lights — they have intelligent lighting that adapts to conditions. Three key systems. First, automatic headlights: a photodiode light sensor on the windscreen — usually near the rain sensor — measures ambient light. When it falls below a threshold, the BCM automatically switches on the headlights. Drive into a tunnel, headlights come on. Drive out, they turn off after a delay.
>
> Second, automatic high beam assist. A camera — the same ADAS camera used for lane keeping — detects the headlights of oncoming vehicles and the tail lights of vehicles ahead. When the road is clear, high beam activates automatically. When another vehicle is detected, the system switches back to low beam. On matrix LED systems, this becomes the selective dimming we discussed earlier — instead of switching between high and low, it creates shadow zones.
>
> Third, cornering lights. When you turn the steering wheel past a threshold angle — or when the turn indicator is active below a certain speed — the BCM activates an additional light on the side you're turning. This might be a dedicated cornering light module in the bumper, or the fog light on that side, or a swivelling headlight module that physically rotates the low beam in the direction of the turn. The steering angle sensor provides the input, and speed data determines whether the function activates — cornering lights typically only work below 40 km/h."

**PPT Content:**
```
ADAPTIVE & AUTOMATIC LIGHTING SYSTEMS

1. AUTOMATIC HEADLIGHTS:
   Input: Light sensor (photodiode on windscreen)
   Logic: Ambient light < threshold → BCM activates headlights
   Features: Tunnel detection, dawn/dusk sensitivity, off-delay timer

2. AUTO HIGH-BEAM ASSIST:
   Input: Windscreen camera (shared with ADAS functions)
   Logic: Camera detects oncoming headlights / tail lights ahead
   Action: High beam ON when road clear, LOW beam when traffic detected
   Advanced: Matrix LED selective dimming (no full switch — shadow zones)

3. CORNERING LIGHTS:
   Input: Steering angle sensor + vehicle speed + turn indicator
   Logic: Steering angle > threshold AND speed < 40 km/h
   Action: Activate additional side light in direction of turn
   Implementation options:
     a) Dedicated cornering light in bumper
     b) Fog light on turning side activates
     c) Headlight module physically swivels (AFS — Adaptive Front-lighting)

ALL THREE SYSTEMS require:
- BCM software, vehicle network (CAN/LIN), sensor inputs
- Demonstrates Week 8 theme: systems connecting across boundaries
```

#### Slide 18: Lighting System Electrical Architecture Summary
**Visual:** Full system wiring block diagram showing: battery → fuse box → BCM → individual lighting circuits. LIN bus connections from BCM to headlight modules and tail light modules. CAN bus connections from BCM to instrument cluster, ADAS camera module, steering angle sensor. Headlight range levelling motor and rear axle level sensor shown. Brake light switch shown as a separate direct input.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's see the complete picture. The battery feeds the main fuse box. Power distribution goes through the BCM, which is the central controller for all lighting functions. The BCM communicates with headlight driver modules, tail light modules, and interior lighting modules over LIN bus — a lower-speed, lower-cost network perfect for lighting control.
>
> On the CAN bus, the BCM receives data from the instrument cluster, the ADAS camera module, the steering angle sensor, and the engine/body controllers. The brake light switch is typically a separate hardwired input — not network-dependent — because brake light function is safety-critical and must work independently of the vehicle network.
>
> The headlight levelling motor has its own circuit — commanded by the BCM based on the rear axle level sensor. This is a real-time feedback loop: vehicle load changes, sensor reports it, BCM adjusts the motor, headlight beam moves.
>
> When you're diagnosing a lighting fault, you need to think in this architecture. Is it a power supply issue? A ground issue? A BCM output failure? A LIN bus communication fault to the headlight module? A CAN bus input missing — like the steering angle sensor data not reaching the BCM for cornering lights? That's cross-system thinking."

**PPT Content:**
```
LIGHTING SYSTEM — ELECTRICAL ARCHITECTURE

POWER PATH:
Battery → Main fuse box → BCM → Individual lighting circuits
         (each circuit has dedicated fuse protection)

COMMUNICATION:
LIN bus: BCM ↔ Headlight modules, Tail light modules, Interior modules
CAN bus: BCM ↔ Instrument cluster, ADAS camera, Steering angle sensor,
         Engine ECU (speed signal), Navigation (tunnel data)

KEY CIRCUITS:
- Headlight low beam: BCM → relay/driver → headlight module
- Headlight high beam: BCM → relay/driver → headlight module
- DRL: BCM → headlight module (LIN bus command)
- Brake light: Brake switch → BCM → tail light module

- Indicators: BCM → front/rear/side modules (LIN or direct drive)
- Headlight levelling: BCM → levelling motor (based on axle sensor)

DIAGNOSTIC THINKING:
Lighting fault → check:
1. Fuse → 2. Ground → 3. Power supply → 4. BCM output →
5. LIN/CAN communication → 6. Module → 7. Bulb/LED

#### Slide 23: Station 4 — Lighting DTC Reading & Circuit Testing
**Visual:** Photo of an OBD scan tool connected to the vehicle showing lighting-related DTCs on the screen (e.g., "Left low beam circuit — open circuit"). Beside it, a multimeter being used to test voltage at a headlight connector (12V expected) and a continuity test on a tail light ground wire.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The final station combines scan tool diagnostics with hands-on circuit testing. First, connect the scan tool and navigate to the BCM module. Read stored DTCs — I've seeded some faults for you to find. You might see codes like 'left low beam open circuit,' 'right indicator short to ground,' or 'headlight levelling sensor signal implausible.'
>
> For each DTC, use your multimeter to verify the fault. If the code says 'open circuit,' disconnect the headlight connector and test for 12 volts at the BCM output with the headlight switched on. If there's 12V at the fuse but not at the connector, you have a break in the wire. Test the ground path too — check continuity from the headlight ground terminal back to the vehicle ground point.
>
> If the code says 'short to ground,' measure resistance from the signal wire to ground with the connector disconnected. It should be open (infinite resistance). If you read near-zero ohms, the wire is shorted.
>
> This is real diagnostic work. DTCs tell you WHERE to look. The multimeter tells you WHAT you've found."

**PPT Content:**
```
STATION 4: LIGHTING DTC READING & CIRCUIT TESTING

STEP 1: SCAN TOOL DIAGNOSTICS
- Connect OBD scan tool
- Select BCM (Body Control Module)
- Read DTCs — record all lighting-related fault codes
- Note: freeze frame data (conditions when fault occurred)
- Clear faults and check if they return immediately

STEP 2: CIRCUIT TESTING (per each DTC found)

TEST 1 — Supply voltage:
  Multimeter on DC VOLTS, probe headlight connector
  Expected: ~12-14V with headlight commanded ON
  Result <10V → voltage drop issue (wiring, connector, relay)

TEST 2 — Ground integrity:
  Multimeter on CONTINUITY/OHMS
  Probe from lamp ground pin to vehicle chassis ground
  Expected: <0.5 ohm
  Result >1 ohm → poor ground (corroded terminal, broken wire)

TEST 3 — Bulb/LED continuity:
  Disconnect bulb, measure across terminals
  Halogen: low resistance (0.5-5 ohm depending on wattage)
  Open (infinite) → bulb filament broken

STEP 3: DOCUMENT
Record: DTC code | Test performed | Reading | Diagnosis

## Key Takeaways

1. Headlight technology has evolved from halogen through HID to LED and matrix LED — all four are still on the road and a technician must service all of them
2. HID and LED headlights legally require automatic headlight levelling and (for HID) headlight washers — missing components mean the vehicle fails roadworthiness inspection
3. Headlight aiming is a precision task with direct safety implications — too high blinds oncoming traffic, too low reduces the driver's visibility
4. Modern lighting is controlled by the BCM over CAN and LIN networks — a "bulb out" warning can indicate faults in the bulb, wiring, ground, BCM output, or network communication
5. Lighting is a cross-system function: it connects electrical power, body control, ADAS cameras, chassis sensors, and legal requirements — exactly the kind of systems thinking Week 8 demands
