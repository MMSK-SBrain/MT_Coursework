# Day 41: Engine Strip-Down
## Instructor-Led Story Framework for PPT Preparation

**Approach:** Methodical Disassembly — Discipline Before Speed
**PPT Target:** 22 slides

---

## Session Objectives (For Instructor)

By the end of this session, learners will be able to:
- Execute a correct engine disassembly sequence from accessories to block internals
- Apply a component tagging and bagging system that guarantees reassembly accuracy
- Document disassembly progress with photographic evidence at each stage
- Perform cleaning procedures appropriate to each component type
- Organize stripped components on a clean bench in reassembly order

**This is Day 41 — the beginning of the optional rebuild stage.** Learners have diagnosed engines (Week 3), studied lubrication, cooling, and turbo systems (Week 4), and understand measurement (Day 4-5). Now they take an engine apart with surgical precision. The message: tearing things apart is easy. Tearing things apart so they go back together perfectly — that takes discipline.

#### Slide 2: Why Disassembly Sequence Matters
**Visual:** Flowchart showing the consequences of wrong disassembly order — cracked castings, stripped threads, damaged sealing surfaces, lost alignment

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Why can't you just start unbolting things? Three reasons. First, some components are load-bearing — remove the wrong bracket first and something heavy falls and cracks. Second, some bolts are hidden behind other components — if you don't follow the sequence, you'll try to force things and strip threads. Third, some gasket surfaces are fragile — the wrong removal angle gouges a sealing face and now you need to machine it. The factory wrote a disassembly sequence for a reason. We follow it."

**PPT Content:**
```
WHY SEQUENCE MATTERS

Wrong order can cause:
- Cracked castings (unsupported weight)
- Stripped threads (hidden fasteners still engaged)
- Damaged gasket surfaces (wrong removal angle)
- Broken sensors and connectors (still attached when pulling)
- Lost alignment references (removed before marking)

RULE: Follow the service manual disassembly sequence.
When in doubt, accessories first, then external, then internal.
```

#### Slide 3: The Disassembly Hierarchy
**Visual:** Pyramid diagram — top layer: accessories and externals; middle layer: cylinder head and valve train; bottom layer: block internals (pistons, crank, bearings)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Think of engine disassembly as peeling layers. The outermost layer is accessories — alternator, AC compressor, power steering pump, intake manifold, exhaust manifold, sensors, wiring. These come off first. Next layer: the cylinder head — rocker cover, camshaft(s), head bolts, head gasket. Last layer: the block internals — oil pan, oil pump, connecting rods, pistons, main bearing caps, crankshaft. Every layer depends on the one above being removed first. You work from the outside in."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE DISASSEMBLY HIERARCHY

LAYER 1 — ACCESSORIES & EXTERNALS (First off)
  Alternator, AC compressor, power steering pump
  Intake manifold, exhaust manifold, turbo
  Sensors, wiring harness, coolant hoses, fuel lines

LAYER 2 — CYLINDER HEAD (Second)
  Rocker/cam cover, camshaft(s), followers
  Head bolts (reverse of torque sequence)
  Cylinder head, head gasket

LAYER 3 — BLOCK INTERNALS (Last)
  Oil pan, oil pump, timing cover/chain/belt
  Connecting rod caps, pistons (push out from below)
  Main bearing caps, crankshaft, rear main seal

OUTSIDE IN — ALWAYS
```

#### Slide 5: Photo Documentation Protocol
**Visual:** Series of example photos — engine overview before starting, close-up of connector routing, hose orientation marks, bracket bolt patterns

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before you remove ANYTHING, take photographs. Not one photo — a series. Start with an overview of the whole engine from each side. Then as you disconnect each item, photograph it first. Hose connections — photograph the routing. Electrical connectors — photograph the locking tab orientation. Brackets — photograph which bolt goes where. The goal: if you lose your memory tomorrow, the photos will tell you how to put it back together. I've seen professionals use hundreds of photos on a complex rebuild. There's no such thing as too many."

**PPT Content:**
```
PHOTO DOCUMENTATION PROTOCOL

Before ANY disassembly:
  1. Overview — front, back, left, right, top
  2. Close-ups — every connector, hose, bracket

During disassembly:
  3. BEFORE disconnecting — photo showing connection
  4. AFTER disconnecting — photo showing routing/orientation
  5. Any anomaly — broken clip, corroded bolt, damaged seal

Naming convention:
  Photo sequence matches bag numbers
  E.g., Bag #7 = Exhaust manifold bolts → Photos #7a, #7b

RULE: If in doubt, photograph it.
```

#### Slide 6: The Tagging and Bagging System
**Visual:** Close-up of properly tagged bags — each with a number, component name, bolt count, and torque spec written on the bag

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Every fastener that comes off the engine goes into a labelled bag. Every bag gets a number, the component name, the number of bolts inside, their size, and if you know it — the torque spec for reassembly. Bag number 1: alternator bracket bolts. Bag number 2: alternator electrical connector hardware. And so on. When you reassemble, you work backwards through the bag numbers. No guessing. No leftover bolts. No 'I think this one goes here.' This is what separates a rebuild that lasts from one that comes back."

**PPT Content:**
```
TAGGING & BAGGING SYSTEM

Each bag includes:
  - Sequential number (#1, #2, #3...)
  - Component name (e.g., "Alternator mounting bolts")
  - Fastener count (e.g., "3x M10 x 30mm")
  - Torque specification (e.g., "45 Nm")
  - Location note (e.g., "Lower left = longer bolt")

Rules:
  - One component per bag
  - Gaskets stored flat (do not fold or crumple)
  - Small parts (dowels, clips, o-rings) get their own bag
  - Bags stored in sequence on the bench

EVERY BOLT HAS A HOME. YOUR JOB IS TO REMEMBER IT.
```

#### Slide 7: Accessory Removal — Electrical First
**Visual:** Engine bay annotated showing disconnection sequence — battery terminal, ECU connector, sensor connectors, injector harness, coil packs

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Start with electrical. Disconnect the battery — negative terminal first, always. Then systematically unplug every sensor connector, every injector plug, every coil pack connector. Label each one as you go. Most connectors have a locking tab — press or lift before pulling. Never yank. If a connector is stuck, spray penetrant around the seal and wiggle gently. A broken connector means a new harness — and that's expensive."

**Instructor Action:** Demonstrate disconnecting 3-4 connectors, showing the locking tab technique. Learners then disconnect the remaining connectors in pairs, tagging each one.

**PPT Content:**
```
ACCESSORY REMOVAL — PHASE 1: ELECTRICAL

Sequence:
  1. Disconnect battery (negative first!)
  2. Unplug ECU main connector
  3. Label and disconnect sensor connectors:
     - Coolant temp, oil pressure, knock sensors
     - Crank position, cam position sensors
     - Throttle body / MAP / MAF connectors
  4. Unplug injector harness
  5. Unplug coil pack connectors
  6. Unplug alternator connector
  7. Release wiring harness clips and set harness aside

NEVER yank a connector. Press the tab, wiggle, pull straight.
```

**Narrative Voice:** More technical. Emphasise precision and damage prevention.

#### Slide 11: Rocker Cover and Valve Train Access
**Visual:** Cross-section showing rocker cover, cam caps, camshafts, valve springs, and the head bolt pattern

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Layer two. Remove the rocker cover — also called the cam cover or valve cover. These are often sealed with a gasket or RTV sealant. Pry gently at the designed pry points — never pry on the gasket surface or you'll gouge it. Once the cover is off, you can see the camshafts and valve train. Before removing anything, note the timing marks. If this engine has a timing chain, the chain stays on until the head is loose. If it has a timing belt, the belt was already removed as part of the accessory layer."

**Instructor Action:** Demonstrate rocker cover removal, showing correct pry point technique. Point out timing marks. Learners remove rocker cover on their engine.

**PPT Content:**
```
CYLINDER HEAD — PHASE 1: ROCKER COVER

Steps:
  1. Remove rocker cover bolts (note pattern — often a sequence)
  2. Break seal gently at pry points (marked on casting)
  3. NEVER pry on gasket mating surface
  4. Remove cover, inspect gasket condition (replace at reassembly)
  5. Photograph valve train layout

With cover removed, observe:
  - Camshaft(s) — single or double overhead cam
  - Cam caps / bearing journals
  - Valve springs and retainers
  - Timing chain/gear (if chain-driven, visible at front)
  - Oil condition on the valve train (tells a story about maintenance)

NOTE TIMING MARKS before disturbing anything.
```

---

#### Slide 12: Camshaft Removal
**Visual:** Diagram showing cam cap removal sequence — always from outside working inward, even loosening pattern

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Camshaft caps must come off in a specific sequence — always from the outside working in, loosening evenly, half a turn at a time. The valve springs are pushing up on the camshaft. If you release one cap fully while others are tight, the shaft bends and the cap cracks. This is a career-ending mistake on an expensive engine. Half a turn, outside in, work around the pattern until all caps are loose. Then lift each cap, keeping them in order — they are position-specific and cannot be swapped."

**Instructor Action:** Demonstrate the loosening pattern on a DOHC head. Learners loosen cam caps following the sequence. Instructor verifies sequence compliance before caps are lifted.

**PPT Content:**
```
CAMSHAFT REMOVAL

CRITICAL: Loosen cam caps evenly — 1/2 turn at a time

Sequence:
  1. Note cam timing position (mark sprocket to chain link)
  2. Loosen cam cap bolts — outside caps first, work INWARD
  3. Loosen 1/2 turn per pass — multiple passes
  4. When all caps are loose, lift in ORDER
  5. Keep caps in numbered sequence — POSITION-SPECIFIC
     (Cap 1 only fits journal 1, etc.)
  6. Lift camshaft(s) straight up
  7. Bag caps in order with their bolts

WHY: Valve spring pressure will bend the shaft and crack
caps if uneven release occurs. Irreversible damage.
```

---

#### Slide 13: Head Bolt Removal
**Visual:** Typical head bolt loosening pattern — numbered diagram showing the reverse-of-tightening spiral from centre outward

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Head bolts hold the cylinder head to the block and clamp the head gasket. They were tightened in a specific sequence — centre outward, in stages. We reverse that. Start at the outside, work inward, loosening each bolt a quarter turn per pass. This prevents warping the head. Aluminium heads warp easily — uneven release of clamping force distorts the flat surface and now you need a machine shop to resurface it. Patience here saves hundreds later."

**Instructor Action:** Demonstrate head bolt loosening pattern — draw it on the whiteboard. Learners loosen head bolts following the pattern, with instructor checking after each pass.

**PPT Content:**
```
HEAD BOLT REMOVAL

Pattern: REVERSE of tightening sequence
  - Start at OUTSIDE corners
  - Work INWARD toward centre
  - Loosen 1/4 turn per pass
  - Multiple passes until all bolts are free

Typical pattern (4-cylinder inline):
  10 - 6 - 2 - 3 - 7
   9 - 5 - 1 - 4 - 8
  (1 is centre, 10 is outermost)

REVERSE: Start at 10, end at 1

IMPORTANT:
  - Head bolts may be Torque-To-Yield (TTY) — single use
  - If TTY: measure stretch or inspect thread deformation
  - Mark TTY bolts as "DO NOT REUSE"
  - Aluminium heads warp if bolts are released unevenly

Bag bolts in sequence. Note any that felt different (damaged threads).
```

#### Slide 14: Head Lift-Off and Gasket Inspection
**Visual:** Photo showing cylinder head being lifted straight up, with the head gasket visible on the block deck surface. Inset photos of common gasket failures — blown between cylinders, coolant passage breach, oil leak path

**Instructor Narration:**
> "With all bolts out, the head lifts straight up. It may need gentle persuasion — tap with a rubber mallet, never pry between the mating surfaces. Never use a metal tool on the gasket face. Once the head is off, look at the gasket. This is forensic evidence. A gasket blown between two cylinders shows as a burn mark or erosion between the fire rings. Coolant passage breach shows staining. Oil leak paths show dark tracks. Photograph the gasket on both sides — this tells you why the engine was torn down."

**Instructor Action:** Lift the head (assist learners — it's heavy). Lay head gasket flat and analyse failure mode with the group. Learners photograph and document.

**PPT Content:**
```
HEAD LIFT-OFF & GASKET INSPECTION

Removal:
  1. All head bolts removed and bagged
  2. Break seal — tap with rubber mallet at casting lugs
  3. NEVER pry between mating surfaces (damages flatness)
  4. Lift head STRAIGHT UP (heavy — use a partner or hoist)
  5. Place head on clean, soft surface (wood blocks)

Gasket forensics — photograph BOTH sides:
  - Burn/erosion between cylinders = blown head gasket
  - Coolant staining at water jacket = coolant breach
  - Oil tracking from oil gallery = oil leak source
  - Intact with no marks = gasket was likely fine (problem elsewhere)

PRESERVE the old gasket for customer explanation if needed.
Protect block deck surface — cover with clean cloth.
```

#### Slide 16: Timing System, Oil Pan, and Oil Pump
**Visual:** Front of engine block showing timing cover, chain, tensioner, oil pan below, oil pump location

**Instructor Narration:**

**Instructor Action:** Demonstrate timing cover removal and timing mark documentation. Show oil pan removal technique. Learners execute under supervision.

**PPT Content:**
```
BLOCK INTERNALS — PHASE 1: TIMING & OIL SYSTEM

Timing system:
  1. Remove timing cover (multiple small bolts — bag them)
  2. Photograph timing marks (chain link to sprocket dots)
  3. Remove chain tensioner and guides
  4. Remove timing chain
  5. Remove crankshaft and camshaft sprockets (mark orientation)

Oil system:
  6. Invert engine on stand
  7. Remove oil pan bolts (perimeter)
  8. Break sealant bead carefully (plastic scraper)
  9. Remove oil pickup tube and screen
  10. Remove oil pump (location varies — front of block or in pan)

Bag all hardware by component. Photo everything.
```

#### Slide 17: Connecting Rod and Piston Removal
**Visual:** Cutaway diagram showing connecting rod cap, rod bolts, big-end bearing shells, piston pin, and piston rings. Arrows show which direction the piston comes out (upward through the bore)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "This is where marking is critical. Every connecting rod and cap is matched to a specific cylinder — they were machined together and are NOT interchangeable. Before removing a single cap, stamp or mark each rod and cap with its cylinder number. Rod and cap on the same side, same number. Use a number stamp set or even a permanent marker and nail polish for protection. Then remove the rod cap bolts, push the piston up and out through the top of the bore. Keep the bearing shells with their rod and cap — they tell a story about wear."

**Instructor Action:** Demonstrate rod numbering technique on cylinder 1. Show correct rod cap removal — support the rod so it doesn't score the crankshaft journal. Learners remove remaining pistons in pairs.

**PPT Content:**
```
CONNECTING ROD & PISTON REMOVAL

CRITICAL — Mark before removal:
  - Number each rod AND cap (1, 2, 3, 4...)
  - Mark on the SAME SIDE (e.g., both marks face front)
  - Method: number stamps, vibro-etcher, or permanent marker + clear coat

Removal sequence per cylinder:
  1. Rotate crankshaft so piston is at BDC
  2. Remove rod cap bolts
  3. Remove rod cap (keep bearing shell with cap)
  4. Push piston UP through bore (use hammer handle gently)
  5. Protect crankshaft journal from rod bolt contact
     (slip rubber hose over bolt threads during push-out)
  6. Keep piston + rod + cap + bearings together as a SET

NEVER mix rods or caps between cylinders.
NEVER mix bearing shells between positions.
```

#### Slide 18: Crankshaft Removal
**Visual:** Block with pistons removed, showing main bearing caps in sequence, thrust bearing location, and rear main seal

**Instructor Narration:**
> "With all pistons out, the crankshaft is next. Main bearing caps — again, numbered and position-specific. Loosen them in the reverse of the tightening sequence, outside in. Lift the crankshaft straight up — it's heavy, use two people or a hoist strap. The main bearing shells stay in position for inspection. Note the thrust bearing location — usually on the centre main — this controls crankshaft end-play. The rear main seal will be visible once the crank is out. Inspect it for the leak pattern."

**Instructor Action:** Demonstrate main cap numbering verification. Supervise crankshaft lift-out. Learners photograph bearing conditions in each journal.

**PPT Content:**
```
CRANKSHAFT REMOVAL

Sequence:
  1. Verify main cap numbering (should be cast into the cap)
     If not marked: number them NOW before removal
  2. Loosen main cap bolts — reverse of tightening sequence
  3. Remove caps, keep bearing shells in position
  4. Lift crankshaft STRAIGHT UP (heavy — two people or hoist)
  5. Place crankshaft on V-blocks or clean soft surface

Inspect and photograph:
  - Main bearing shells (wear pattern, scoring, overlay loss)
  - Rod bearing shells (same assessment)
  - Thrust bearing surfaces (end-play wear)
  - Rear main seal groove condition
  - Crankshaft journals (visual inspection — measurement tomorrow)

The bearing shells are FORENSIC EVIDENCE of engine health.
```

BLOCK STRIP — FINAL ITEMS

Remaining removal:
  1. Freeze plugs (if block going for hot tank cleaning)
  2. Oil gallery plugs (for thorough cleaning)

  4. Core plugs (if corroded)

#### Slide 20: Cleaning Procedures by Component Type
**Visual:** Three-zone cleaning station — parts washer with solvent, chemical soak tank, and a bead-blasting cabinet (with warning label)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Different parts need different cleaning. The parts washer with solvent handles most external grime — brackets, covers, manifolds. Chemical soak in a hot caustic solution is for heavily carbonised parts like pistons and intake ports — but only cast iron and steel. Aluminium goes in a dedicated aluminium-safe solution or it dissolves. Bead blasting — be aware of it but be cautious. Glass bead blasting cleans surfaces beautifully, but if a single bead gets into an oil gallery or bearing surface, it causes premature failure. If you bead blast, you must follow it with thorough washing and blowing out every passage."

**PPT Content:**
```
CLEANING PROCEDURES

Parts Washer (solvent):
  - External grime, oil, grease
  - Suitable for all metal types
  - First-pass cleaning for most components

Chemical Soak (hot tank / caustic):
  - Heavy carbon deposits, varnish
  - Cast iron and steel ONLY (not aluminium!)
  - Aluminium requires dedicated Al-safe solution

Bead Blasting (glass bead):
  - Surface cleaning, gasket residue removal
  - WARNING: Beads contaminate oil passages
  - MUST blow out ALL passages after blasting
  - Many shops avoid bead blasting engine internals

For all methods:
  - Follow with compressed air blow-out of all passages
  - Oil bare metal surfaces after cleaning to prevent rust
  - Wear gloves and eye protection
```

#### Slide 21: Gasket Surface Preparation
**Visual:** Close-up of a block deck surface being cleaned with a plastic scraper and Scotch-Brite pad, showing the difference between a cleaned section and a section with old gasket residue

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Every mating surface must be perfectly clean for reassembly. Old gasket material, sealant residue, corrosion — all of it must come off. But here's the rule: aluminium surfaces get plastic scrapers and Scotch-Brite pads. Never a metal scraper on aluminium — you'll gouge the surface and create a leak path. Cast iron can tolerate a careful metal scraper, but still prefer plastic. Razor blades are acceptable on flat cast iron surfaces with care. The surface should be clean, smooth, and free of any residue."

**PPT Content:**
```
GASKET SURFACE PREPARATION

Aluminium surfaces (head, intake manifold, covers):
  - Plastic scraper ONLY
  - Scotch-Brite pad (non-metallic)
  - Gasket remover spray (chemical softening)
  - NEVER metal scraper — gouges create leak paths

Cast iron surfaces (block deck, exhaust manifold):
  - Plastic scraper preferred
  - Metal scraper with CARE (light pressure, flat angle)
  - Razor blade held flat (gentle — don't dig)

After cleaning:
  - Wipe with solvent to remove residue
  - Inspect under good light for remaining material
  - Run a straight edge across the surface to check flatness
  - Protect cleaned surfaces — cover or oil immediately

A PERFECT SEAL STARTS WITH A PERFECT SURFACE.
```

## Key Takeaways

1. Disassembly sequence matters — outside in, accessories first, then head, then block
2. Every fastener gets tagged, bagged, numbered, and photographed
3. Photo documentation is your insurance policy against reassembly confusion
4. Aluminium surfaces require plastic scrapers only — no metal tools on soft metal
5. Cam caps and rod caps are position-specific — mark them BEFORE removal
6. The old parts tell a story — bearings, gaskets, and wear patterns are diagnostic evidence
