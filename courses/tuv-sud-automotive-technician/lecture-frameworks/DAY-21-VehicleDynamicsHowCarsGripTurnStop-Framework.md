#### Slide 5: The Tyre — Anatomy of the Only Contact Point
**Visual:** Cross-section cutaway diagram of a radial tyre showing all layers: tread, belts, carcass plies, sidewall, bead wire, inner liner, bead filler. Each layer labeled and colour-coded.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's open up a tyre. It's not just rubber — it's an engineered composite. Starting from the inside: the inner liner is an airtight layer that holds air pressure, like a built-in inner tube. The carcass plies are fabric cords — usually polyester — that give the tyre its shape and carry the load. In a radial tyre, these cords run radially — straight from bead to bead across the tyre, perpendicular to the direction of travel. The belts — steel cords — sit under the tread and stabilise the contact patch. The sidewall protects the carcass and flexes over bumps. The bead is a bundle of steel wire that locks the tyre onto the wheel rim. And the tread — that's your contact patch — has grooves to channel water and compounds tuned for grip.
>
> Every layer has a job. Lose any one, and the tyre fails."

**PPT Content:**
```
TYRE ANATOMY — CROSS-SECTION

TREAD
  — Rubber compound contacting the road
  — Grooves and sipes for water evacuation
  — Pattern determines grip characteristics

BELTS (steel)
  — Stabilise the tread area
  — Keep the contact patch flat under load

CARCASS PLIES (polyester/nylon cords)
  — Structural skeleton of the tyre
  — Carry the load, maintain shape

SIDEWALL
  — Protects carcass from impacts and weather
  — Flexes to absorb road imperfections

BEAD (steel wire bundle)
  — Locks tyre to wheel rim
  — Withstands mounting forces

INNER LINER
  — Airtight rubber layer (replaces inner tube)
  — Maintains air pressure
```

#### Slide 6: Radial vs. Bias Construction
**Visual:** Side-by-side cutaway comparison. Left: radial tyre with cords running perpendicular to travel direction, steel belts visible. Right: bias-ply tyre with cords running diagonally at alternating angles. Arrows showing cord direction.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "There are two fundamental tyre constructions. Radial — used on virtually every passenger car today — has carcass cords running radially from bead to bead, at 90 degrees to the direction of travel. Steel belts sit under the tread. This design lets the sidewall flex independently of the tread, so the contact patch stays flat even when cornering or going over bumps.
>
> Bias-ply — sometimes called cross-ply — has carcass cords running diagonally at alternating 30-40 degree angles. The whole structure is stiffer. You'll still see bias tyres on trailers, some agricultural equipment, and classic vehicles. But for modern cars, it's radial. The 'R' in your tyre size marking — that's what it means."

**PPT Content:**
```
RADIAL vs. BIAS (CROSS-PLY) CONSTRUCTION

RADIAL (R)                          BIAS (CROSS-PLY)
Cords run bead-to-bead at 90°      Cords run diagonally at 30-40°
Steel belts under tread             No separate belt layer
Flexible sidewall                   Stiffer sidewall
Contact patch stays flat            Contact patch distorts under load
Better grip, lower rolling          Higher rolling resistance
  resistance, longer life           Tougher sidewall for rough terrain
99% of passenger cars today         Trailers, off-road, classic cars

The "R" in 225/45R17 = RADIAL construction
```

#### Slide 7: Reading the Sidewall — Size Markings Decoded
**Visual:** Photograph of a tyre sidewall with the marking "225/45R17 91W" zoomed in. Each element highlighted with a colour-coded callout line pointing to an explanation box.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Every tyre tells you exactly what it is — you just need to know the code. Let's decode 225/45R17 91W.
>
> 225 — the section width in millimetres. That's the tyre's width when properly mounted and inflated, measured at the widest point of the sidewall. 45 — the aspect ratio. The sidewall height is 45% of the width. So 45% of 225 is about 101 mm of sidewall. R — radial construction, as we just discussed. 17 — the wheel rim diameter in inches. 91 — the load index. You look this up in a table: 91 means 615 kg per tyre. And W — the speed rating: W means this tyre is approved up to 270 km/h.
>
> This is exam material. You will need to decode tyre markings on the certification exam."

**PPT Content:**
```
TYRE SIZE MARKING DECODED: 225/45R17 91W

225  = Section width (mm)
       Width of tyre from sidewall to sidewall

45   = Aspect ratio (%)
       Sidewall height = 45% of width = 101.25 mm

R    = Radial construction

17   = Rim diameter (inches)
       Wheel the tyre fits

91   = Load index
       Maximum load per tyre (91 = 615 kg)

W    = Speed rating
       Maximum rated speed (W = 270 km/h)

EXAM TIP: You WILL be asked to decode tyre markings.
```

#### Slide 9: Tread Depth — Legal, Recommended, and Reality
**Visual:** Three side-by-side photographs of tyre tread: new tyre (~8 mm), worn to 3 mm (recommended replacement), and worn to 1.6 mm (legal minimum). A tread depth gauge shown in use. Wear indicator bar highlighted.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "A new tyre has about 8 millimetres of tread depth. The legal minimum in most jurisdictions is 1.6 mm — and there are built-in wear indicators in the tread grooves that become flush with the surface at exactly 1.6 mm. But here's the reality: wet-weather braking performance drops dramatically below 3 mm. The grooves can't channel water fast enough, and you get aquaplaning — the tyre rides up on a film of water and loses all grip. That's why the industry recommendation is to replace at 3 mm.
>
> You'll measure tread depth at three points across the tyre: inside edge, centre, and outside edge. If wear is uneven, that tells you something about alignment, inflation, or suspension — which we'll cover on Day 24."

**PPT Content:**
```
TREAD DEPTH — THE NUMBERS THAT MATTER

New tyre:      ~8 mm
Recommended:    3 mm (industry standard replacement point)
Legal minimum:  1.6 mm (most jurisdictions)

WEAR INDICATORS (TWI):
  — Raised bars moulded into tread grooves
  — Flush with tread surface at 1.6 mm
  — Small triangle or "TWI" on sidewall marks location

WHY 3 mm, NOT 1.6 mm?
  — Wet braking distance at 80 km/h:
    8 mm tread: ~26 m
    3 mm tread: ~38 m
    1.6 mm tread: ~52 m (DOUBLE the new tyre)

MEASURE AT 3 POINTS: Inside edge, centre, outside edge
Uneven wear = alignment, inflation, or suspension problem
```

#### Slide 10: The Contact Patch in Detail
**Visual:** Top-down thermal image of a tyre contact patch showing pressure distribution. Beside it, a diagram showing how contact patch size changes with inflation pressure (over-inflated = small centre patch, under-inflated = edges only, correct = even full patch).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let's go deeper into the contact patch. When a tyre meets the road, the rubber deforms to create a flat area. The size and shape of this patch depend on three things: tyre width, inflation pressure, and load.
>
> Over-inflate, and the tyre bulges in the centre — only the middle strip touches the road. Under-inflate, and the centre sags — only the edges carry the load. Both are dangerous and both cause uneven wear. Correct inflation gives you an even, full-width patch. That's maximum grip and maximum tyre life.
>
> This is why TPMS exists — and why checking tyre pressures is part of every service inspection."

**PPT Content:**
```
CONTACT PATCH — PRESSURE DISTRIBUTION

Three factors determine contact patch size:
1. Tyre width (wider = larger patch)
2. Inflation pressure (correct = even distribution)
3. Load (heavier = larger patch)

INFLATION EFFECTS ON CONTACT PATCH:

OVER-INFLATED          CORRECT           UNDER-INFLATED
  [narrow centre]    [even, full width]   [edges only]
  Centre wear         Even wear            Edge wear
  Harsh ride          Optimal grip         Sloppy handling
  Reduced grip        Maximum tyre life    Increased fuel use
                                           Overheating risk

Correct pressure = found on door jamb sticker,
NOT on the tyre sidewall (that's maximum, not recommended)
```

#### Slide 11: Forces on a Vehicle — The Three Axes
**Visual:** 3D diagram of a car with three coloured arrows: green arrow along the length (longitudinal/acceleration-braking), red arrow across the width (lateral/cornering), blue arrow vertical (weight/normal force). Labels for each.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Now let's talk about what the tyre actually has to do. There are three types of force acting through the contact patch. Longitudinal forces — forward for acceleration, rearward for braking. These act along the car's direction of travel. Lateral forces — sideways force generated during cornering. And vertical forces — the car's weight pushing down, and the road pushing back up.
>
> Here's the critical insight: the tyre has a FINITE amount of grip. It's like a budget. If you use all your grip for braking, you have none left for cornering. If you're cornering at the limit and hit the brakes hard, the tyre can't do both — and you slide. This is called the friction circle, and it's the fundamental concept behind ABS and ESC."

**PPT Content:**
```
THREE FORCES ON A VEHICLE

LONGITUDINAL (fore-aft)
  — Acceleration force (engine torque through tyres)
  — Braking force (brake friction opposing rotation)
  — Acts along direction of travel

LATERAL (side-to-side)
  — Cornering force (tyre resists sideways slide)
  — Generated when steering wheel is turned
  — The tyre "walks" slightly sideways (slip angle)

VERTICAL (up-down)
  — Vehicle weight pressing tyres onto road
  — Road reaction force pushing back
  — Changes with load, acceleration, braking, cornering

FRICTION CIRCLE: Total available grip is LIMITED.
Tyre must share its grip budget between all three forces.
More braking = less cornering. More cornering = less braking.
```

#### Slide 12: Weight Transfer — Why the Car Dives, Squats, and Rolls
**Visual:** Three diagrams showing a car in profile and from the front: (1) Braking — nose dives, weight shifts forward. (2) Accelerating — rear squats, weight shifts rearward. (3) Cornering — body rolls, weight shifts to outside wheels. Arrows showing weight transfer direction.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "When you brake, the car's nose dives. That's not just cosmetic — weight is physically transferring from the rear tyres to the front tyres. The front contact patches are now carrying more load and have more grip. The rears have less load and less grip. That's why front brakes are always larger than rear brakes — they do more work.
>
> Accelerating does the opposite — weight shifts rearward, which is why rear-wheel-drive cars have better traction launching from a standstill. And cornering? Weight shifts to the outside wheels. The inside wheels get lighter and have less grip.
>
> This is weight transfer. The suspension's job is to manage it — to control HOW that weight moves and HOW QUICKLY. Too fast and the tyre overloads. Too much body roll and the inside tyre lifts off the road entirely. That's what we'll study tomorrow."

**PPT Content:**
```
WEIGHT TRANSFER — DYNAMIC LOAD CHANGES

BRAKING (nose dive):
  Weight shifts FORWARD
  Front tyres: MORE grip (larger contact patch)
  Rear tyres: LESS grip
  → Front brakes do 60-80% of braking work

ACCELERATION (rear squat):
  Weight shifts REARWARD
  Rear tyres: MORE traction
  Front tyres: LESS traction
  → RWD launches better, FWD can spin front tyres

CORNERING (body roll):
  Weight shifts to OUTSIDE wheels
  Outside tyres: MORE grip
  Inside tyres: LESS grip (can lift off in extreme)
  → Anti-roll bars limit body roll to keep all 4 tyres loaded

SUSPENSION manages WHEN and HOW weight transfers.
Too fast = tyre overload. Too slow = sloppy response.
```

#### Slide 13: How Braking Works — Energy Conversion
**Visual:** Animated-style diagram (or sequence of 3 images) showing: (1) A car in motion with a "KE = 1/2 mv^2" label, (2) Brake pad pressing against rotor with friction arrows and heat radiation lines, (3) Car stopped with temperature reading on the rotor (300-400 degrees C highlighted).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Braking is energy conversion. Nothing more. Your car has kinetic energy — it's moving. The formula is one-half times mass times velocity squared. That 'squared' is important: double your speed and you have FOUR TIMES the energy to get rid of.
>
> Where does that energy go? Into heat. When the brake pad presses against the spinning rotor, friction converts kinetic energy into thermal energy. A hard stop from 100 km/h can heat the rotors to 300-400 degrees Celsius. That's why brake components are designed to handle extreme heat — and why brake fade happens when they can't.
>
> This is the physics behind every brake system you'll ever work on. The design details change — disc vs. drum, hydraulic vs. electric — but the physics never does: motion becomes heat through friction."

**PPT Content:**
```
BRAKING = KINETIC ENERGY → HEAT

Kinetic Energy: KE = 1/2 x mass x velocity²

Double the speed = 4x the energy to dissipate

THE PROCESS:
1. Driver presses brake pedal
2. Hydraulic pressure pushes pads against rotor
3. Friction converts kinetic energy to HEAT
4. Car decelerates

NUMBERS THAT MATTER:
  — 1,500 kg car at 100 km/h: ~578 kJ of kinetic energy
  — All converted to heat in the brake rotors
  — Rotor temperature: 300-400°C in hard braking
  — Emergency stop from 100 km/h: ~40 m (dry road)

Speed squared means: 50 km/h takes ~10 m to stop.
100 km/h takes ~40 m. Double speed = FOUR times the distance.
```

#### Slide 15: TPMS — Direct vs. Indirect
**Visual:** Split diagram. Left: direct TPMS — cutaway of a tyre showing a sensor module mounted on the valve stem inside the tyre, transmitting wirelessly to an ECU. Right: indirect TPMS — diagram showing ABS wheel speed sensors comparing rotational speeds, with a smaller (under-inflated) tyre spinning faster.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Tyre Pressure Monitoring Systems became mandatory in many markets because under-inflation is invisible until it's dangerous. There are two types.
>
> Direct TPMS uses a pressure sensor mounted inside each tyre, usually on the valve stem. It measures actual pressure and temperature and transmits wirelessly to a receiver ECU. It's accurate — gives you real-time pressure readings — but the sensors have batteries that last 5-10 years, and they can be damaged during tyre changes if you're not careful.
>
> Indirect TPMS uses the existing ABS wheel speed sensors. The logic is: an under-inflated tyre has a smaller rolling radius and therefore rotates faster than a correctly inflated one. The system compares wheel speeds and triggers a warning if one is consistently faster. It's cheaper — no extra sensors — but less accurate. It can't tell you which tyre is low in some implementations, and it needs to be reset after any tyre pressure adjustment or rotation."

**PPT Content:**
```
TPMS — TYRE PRESSURE MONITORING SYSTEM

DIRECT TPMS                        INDIRECT TPMS
Sensor inside each tyre            Uses ABS wheel speed sensors
Measures actual pressure            Compares rotational speeds
  & temperature                    Under-inflated tyre = smaller
Wireless transmission to ECU         radius = spins faster
Accurate (± 1 psi)                 Less accurate (detects ~25% loss)
Gives per-tyre readings            May not identify which tyre
Battery life: 5-10 years           No battery needed
Sensors can be damaged             Needs RESET after:
  during tyre change                 — Pressure adjustment
Cost: Higher                         — Tyre rotation
                                     — Tyre replacement
                                   Cost: Lower (uses existing ABS)

Both types: Dashboard warning light when pressure drops
below threshold (typically ~25% below recommended)
```

#### Slide 16: Tyre Rotation Patterns
**Visual:** Three diagrams showing top-down car outlines with arrows indicating tyre movement patterns: (1) Front-to-rear (same side), (2) Cross rotation (X-pattern), (3) Five-tyre rotation (including spare). Label which drive type each suits.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Tyres wear differently depending on their position. Front tyres on a front-wheel-drive car wear faster and on the edges due to steering loads. Rears wear more evenly. Rotation evens out this wear and extends the life of the set.
>
> The simplest pattern is front-to-rear — swap fronts and rears on the same side. This works for directional tyres that can only spin one way. Cross rotation — moving front-left to rear-right and front-right to rear-left — is the most common for non-directional tyres on front-wheel-drive cars. If the car has a full-size spare, a five-tyre rotation brings the spare into the cycle.
>
> Typical interval: every 10,000 to 12,000 km, or every second oil change. Check the manufacturer recommendation."

**PPT Content:**
```
TYRE ROTATION PATTERNS

1. FRONT-TO-REAR (same side)
   FL → RL    FR → RR
   RL → FL    RR → FR
   Use: Directional tyres, some AWD systems

2. CROSS ROTATION (X-pattern)
   FL → RR    FR → RL
   RL → FR    RR → FL
   Use: Non-directional tyres, FWD vehicles

3. FIVE-TYRE ROTATION (with full-size spare)
   FL → RL → Spare
   FR → RR → FL (position varies by pattern)
   Use: Vehicles with full-size matching spare

INTERVAL: Every 10,000-12,000 km
  (or per manufacturer recommendation)

WHY ROTATE?
  — Even out wear differences between positions
  — Front tyres wear faster on FWD (steering + driving)
  — Extends total tyre set life by up to 20%
```

#### Slide 17: Wheel Balancing — Static and Dynamic
**Visual:** Diagram showing: (1) Static imbalance — wheel heavy on one side, bouncing up and down (hop). (2) Dynamic imbalance — wheel heavy on one edge, wobbling side to side (shimmy). (3) A wheel on a balancing machine with clip-on and adhesive weights shown.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Even a new tyre and wheel combination isn't perfectly balanced. Small weight differences cause vibration that increases with speed. There are two types of imbalance.
>
> Static imbalance means one spot on the wheel is heavier. It causes the wheel to bounce — you feel it as a vertical vibration, especially at 80-120 km/h. Dynamic imbalance means weight is uneven across the width of the wheel. It causes side-to-side wobble — shimmy.
>
> Balancing corrects this by adding small counterweights. Clip-on weights go on the rim flange — you'll see them on steel wheels. Adhesive weights stick to the inside barrel of alloy wheels where clip-ons would look ugly or not fit. The balancing machine spins the wheel and tells you exactly where and how much weight to add. You'll do this in the practical session."

**PPT Content:**
```
WHEEL BALANCING — STATIC & DYNAMIC

STATIC IMBALANCE (vertical)
  — Heavy spot on one point of the wheel
  — Causes up-and-down bouncing (hop)
  — Felt as vibration through steering wheel
  — Corrected with weight on ONE plane

DYNAMIC IMBALANCE (lateral)
  — Weight unevenly distributed across wheel width
  — Causes side-to-side wobble (shimmy)
  — Felt as steering wheel shake at speed
  — Corrected with weights on BOTH inner and outer planes

WHEEL WEIGHT TYPES:
  — Clip-on (rim flange) — steel wheels
  — Adhesive (stick-on inside barrel) — alloy wheels

WHEN TO BALANCE:
  — Every tyre fitment (new tyre or remount)
```

#### Slide 19: Station 1 — Tyre Inspection Procedure
**Visual:** Step-by-step photo sequence: (1) Tread depth gauge positioned in main groove, (2) Reading at inner edge, (3) Reading at centre, (4) Reading at outer edge. Below: checklist of damage types to look for with example photos (bulge, crack, nail, uneven wear).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "At this station, you have a tread depth gauge and tyres of varying condition. Here's the procedure. Insert the gauge into the main tread groove — not a sipe or wear indicator groove. Push until the base sits flat on the tread blocks. Read the measurement. Do this at three points: inner edge, centre, and outer edge of the tread.
>
> Record all three readings. If they're even, the tyre is wearing normally. If the centre is more worn than the edges, the tyre has been over-inflated. If the edges are more worn, it's been under-inflated. If one edge is more worn than the other, there's an alignment issue. We'll connect this to alignment geometry on Day 24.
>
> Also inspect for: sidewall bulges — that's internal structural damage, tyre must be replaced immediately. Cracks in the sidewall — age degradation. Foreign objects embedded in the tread. And check the DOT date code — four digits on the sidewall telling you the week and year of manufacture. A tyre over 6 years old should be inspected carefully regardless of tread depth."

**PPT Content:**
```
TYRE INSPECTION PROCEDURE

TREAD DEPTH MEASUREMENT:
1. Use a tread depth gauge (or vernier caliper)
2. Insert into MAIN groove (not sipes or indicator groove)
3. Measure at THREE points across the tread:
   — Inner edge
   — Centre
   — Outer edge
4. Record all three readings

WEAR PATTERN DIAGNOSIS:
  Centre wear only     → Over-inflation
  Both edges worn      → Under-inflation
  One edge worn        → Alignment issue (camber/toe)
  Patchy/cupping wear  → Worn dampers or balance issue

DAMAGE INSPECTION:
  ☐ Sidewall bulges (REPLACE immediately)
  ☐ Sidewall cracks (age/UV damage)
  ☐ Foreign objects (nails, screws, glass)
  ☐ Cuts deeper than 1 mm
  ☐ DOT date code: tyres >6 years — inspect carefully
```

#### Slide 21: Station 2 — TPMS Sensor Identification
**Visual:** Photographs of: (1) A direct TPMS sensor removed from a wheel, showing the pressure transducer, battery, antenna, and valve stem. (2) The ABS wheel speed sensor on a hub (indirect TPMS component). (3) Dashboard TPMS warning light (exclamation mark inside a tyre cross-section).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "At this station, I have a direct TPMS sensor removed from a wheel. Pick it up. Feel how small it is. This tiny module measures pressure, temperature, and transmits wirelessly to the ECU every few seconds. The battery is embedded — you can't replace it. When the battery dies, you replace the whole sensor. Cost: 30-80 euros per sensor.
>

```
TPMS SENSOR IDENTIFICATION

DIRECT TPMS SENSOR:
```

#### Slide 22: Station 3 — Wheel Balancing Demonstration
**Visual:** Photo of a modern wheel balancing machine with a wheel mounted. Callout labels for: mounting cone, wing nut, spin guard, weight placement indicators (inner/outer), and display screen showing weight location.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Watch closely — then you'll do it yourself. Step one: mount the wheel on the balancer using the correct cone and spacers to centre it. Step two: enter the wheel parameters — rim diameter, rim width, and offset. These are usually on a sticker inside the rim or stamped on the back. Step three: lower the guard and spin. The machine measures imbalance and shows you exactly where to place weights — how many grams, at what position on the inner and outer rim.
>
> Step four: apply the weights. Clip-on weights snap onto the rim flange. Adhesive weights — clean the surface first with brake cleaner, peel the backing, press firmly. Step five: verify — spin again. The machine should read zero or near-zero imbalance.
>
> Common mistakes: wrong cone size, incorrect wheel data entry, dirty adhesive surface, and placing the weight in the wrong position. Take your time. A poorly balanced wheel means a vibration complaint coming back to the workshop."

**PPT Content:**
```
WHEEL BALANCING — STEP BY STEP

1. MOUNT wheel on balancer
   — Select correct cone to centre the wheel
   — Tighten wing nut securely

2. ENTER wheel data
   — Rim diameter (inches)
   — Rim width (inches)
   — Offset distance (mm)

3. SPIN the wheel
   — Lower safety guard
   — Machine measures static & dynamic imbalance

4. APPLY weights at indicated positions
   — Clip-on: snap onto rim flange
   — Adhesive: clean surface, peel, press firmly

5. VERIFY — spin again
   — Target: 0g on both inner and outer planes
   — Acceptable: < 5g residual

COMMON ERRORS:
  ✗ Wrong cone = wheel not centred = false reading
```

## Key Takeaways

1. The tyre contact patch is the ONLY connection between a 1,500 kg vehicle and the road — four patches the size of your hand
2. Tyre construction is layered engineering: inner liner, carcass, belts, sidewall, bead, tread — each layer has a specific function
3. Tyre size markings encode width, aspect ratio, construction type, rim diameter, load capacity, and speed rating
4. Legal minimum tread depth is 1.6 mm; recommended replacement is 3 mm; wet braking distance doubles from new to legal minimum
5. Three forces act through the contact patch: longitudinal (accel/brake), lateral (cornering), vertical (weight)
6. Weight transfer under braking, acceleration, and cornering changes how much grip each tyre has
7. Braking converts kinetic energy to heat — and kinetic energy increases with the SQUARE of speed
8. Direct TPMS measures actual pressure via sensors; indirect TPMS infers pressure from ABS wheel speed comparison
9. Tyre rotation extends tyre life; staggered fitment and directional tyres limit rotation options
10. Wheel balancing eliminates vibration from static (vertical) and dynamic (lateral) imbalance
