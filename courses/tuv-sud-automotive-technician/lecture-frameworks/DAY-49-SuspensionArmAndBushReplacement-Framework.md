# Day 49: Suspension Arm & Bush Replacement
## Instructor-Led Story Framework for PPT Preparation

**Duration:** 180 minutes (27 min theory + 153 min practical)
**Approach:** Hands-On Replacement — Press Work, Torque Discipline, Ride-Height Awareness
**PPT Target:** 23 slides
**Week:** 10 of 11 — "The Chassis Specialist: Suspension & Steering" (Optional)

---

## Session Objectives (For Instructor)

By the end of this session, learners will be able to:
- Remove a lower control arm following the correct fastener sequence (weight-on vs weight-off bolts)
- Support the hub/knuckle assembly before disconnecting the arm to prevent brake hose and ABS sensor damage
- Use a hydraulic press safely to extract worn bushes (correct mandrel selection, shielding, controlled pressure)
- Press in new bushes with correct orientation (voided sections aligned per manufacturer specification)
- Remove and replace a ball joint using press or staked-type methods
- Explain and apply the critical concept of torquing suspension bolts at ride height
- State why wheel alignment is mandatory after any suspension arm or bush replacement

#### Slide 2: What Bushes and Ball Joints Actually Do
**Visual:** Annotated diagram of a McPherson strut front suspension showing lower control arm, bush mounting points, and ball joint connecting arm to knuckle

**Instructor Narration:**
> "A control arm connects the subframe to the steering knuckle. At the subframe end, rubber bushes allow the arm to pivot while absorbing vibration. At the knuckle end, a ball joint allows the arm to pivot in multiple planes — up-down for bumps, and left-right for steering. The bush is a rubber-bonded insert pressed into the arm. It's designed to flex in a controlled way — soft enough to absorb road noise, stiff enough to locate the wheel precisely. When the rubber deteriorates — from age, oil contamination, or fatigue — that precision disappears. The wheel starts moving in ways the engineers never intended."

**PPT Content:**
```
CONTROL ARM ANATOMY

SUBFRAME END: Rubber bush(es) — allow controlled pivot
- Absorb road noise and vibration (NVH)
- Permit arm rotation in one plane
- Resist movement in all other planes

KNUCKLE END: Ball joint — multi-axis pivot
- Allows vertical suspension travel
- Allows steering rotation
- Transfers braking and cornering loads

FAILURE SIGNS:
- Clunking over bumps (worn bush or ball joint play)
- Vague steering feel (excess bush compliance)
- Uneven tyre wear (geometry shifted from wear)
- Visual: cracked, split, or oil-soaked rubber
```

#### Slide 3: Why This Job Demands Precision
**Visual:** Side-by-side comparison: car at ride height with arm level vs car on lift with arm hanging at full droop — bush twist angle shown

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Here's the thing that separates a professional job from a parts-swapper job. When you fit a new bush and torque the bolts with the suspension hanging at full droop — like it is on a lift — the bush rubber is twisted because the arm is at its maximum angle. Then when the car is lowered and the suspension sits at ride height, the bush is already pre-loaded with twist. Every time the suspension moves, it's fighting against that pre-twist. The result? The bush fails in 10,000 to 15,000 kilometres instead of lasting 80,000. Today you learn to do it right: torque at ride height."

**PPT Content:**
```
THE CRITICAL CONCEPT: TORQUE AT RIDE HEIGHT

WRONG METHOD (common in fast-fit shops):
1. Lift car, remove arm, fit new bushes
2. Refit arm, torque all bolts on the lift
3. Lower car - bush pre-twists - premature failure (10,000-15,000 km)

CORRECT METHOD (professional approach):
1. Lift car, remove arm, fit new bushes
2. Refit arm, hand-tighten bolts only
3. Lower car OR jack under arm to simulate ride height
4. THEN apply final torque
5. Bush sits at neutral angle - full service life (80,000+ km)

This single step is the difference between a 1-year job and a 5-year job.
```

### **DEVELOPMENT: Techniques and Theory** (Slides 4-9, ~17 minutes)

#### Slide 4: Control Arm Removal — Fastener Sequence
**Visual:** Underside photo of a front lower control arm with numbered callouts showing bolt removal order; annotation showing which bolts need weight-on

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Removing a control arm isn't just 'undo all the bolts'. Some bolts are designed to be tightened — and loosened — with the vehicle's weight on the suspension. These are typically the arm-to-subframe pivot bolts. If they were torqued at ride height, they'll be under stress when the suspension is at full droop on a lift. You may need to crack them loose with the wheels on the ground, or support the arm with a jack to relieve the stress before undoing them. The ball joint pinch bolt or nut at the knuckle end is usually straightforward — but you must support the knuckle before you disconnect it, or the knuckle drops, stretching brake hoses and straining ABS sensor wiring."

**PPT Content:**
```
CONTROL ARM REMOVAL — FASTENER SEQUENCE

STEP 1: Support the vehicle safely on axle stands
STEP 2: Remove wheel
STEP 3: Support hub/knuckle with a jack or stand
  - BEFORE disconnecting arm from knuckle
  - Prevents knuckle dropping onto brake hose/ABS wiring

STEP 4: Disconnect ball joint from knuckle
  - Pinch bolt type: remove pinch bolt, lever joint from taper
  - Nut type: remove castellated nut, use ball joint separator
  - NEVER use a fork-type splitter on a joint you plan to reuse

STEP 5: Remove arm-to-subframe bolts
  - Note: some may need weight-on (check service data)
  - Crack loose with jack under arm if bolt is under stress

STEP 6: Remove arm complete with bushes

DOCUMENT bolt sizes, locations, any shims or spacers
```

#### Slide 5: Hydraulic Press Safety — Before You Press
**Visual:** Hydraulic press with safety shield in place, operator wearing eye protection, annotated with key safety points

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The hydraulic press is one of the most useful — and most dangerous — tools in the workshop. You're applying tonnes of force through a small area. If the workpiece slips, if the mandrel is wrong, if you don't use a shield — metal can fly. Before you press anything: safety glasses on, shield in place, no one standing in line with the press ram. Build your setup carefully. Support the arm properly so it won't tip or rotate under load. Use the correct mandrel — the inside diameter must match the bush outer diameter. Too small and you'll damage the arm bore. Too large and you won't contact the bush evenly."

**PPT Content:**
```
HYDRAULIC PRESS SAFETY

BEFORE PRESSING:
[ ] Safety glasses ON — mandatory, no exceptions
[ ] Press shield in position
[ ] No one standing in line with ram axis
[ ] Workpiece supported securely (cannot tip or rotate)
[ ] Correct mandrel selected

MANDREL SELECTION:
- Press-OUT mandrel: ID matches bush OUTER diameter
  Contacts the bush shell, not the arm bore
- Press-IN mandrel: OD slightly smaller than arm bore
  Guides new bush centrally into bore
- Receiver cup: must be larger than bush OD
  Allows bush to press through and exit cleanly

DURING PRESSING:
- Apply pressure SLOWLY — watch for deflection
- Listen for cracking or popping (stop and investigate)
- Watch for sudden release — keep hands clear of moving parts
- If pressure exceeds expected range - STOP - reassess setup
```

#### Slide 6: Bush Extraction — Press-Out Procedure
**Visual:** Step-by-step photo sequence showing bush being pressed out of a control arm: setup, mandrel contact, mid-press, bush exiting into receiver cup

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Set the arm on the press bed with the bush centred over a receiver cup — the cup catches the bush as it's pressed out. The press-out mandrel sits on top of the bush. It should be sized so it pushes on the bush outer shell without touching the arm bore. Apply pressure slowly and steadily. You'll feel initial resistance as the interference fit breaks — then it usually moves smoothly. If the bush was bonded with adhesive and the arm is alloy, heat the arm bore gently with a heat gun first. Never hammer a bush out — you'll distort the bore, and then the new bush won't seat properly."

**PPT Content:**
```
BUSH EXTRACTION — PRESS-OUT

SETUP:
1. Position arm on press bed — bush centred over receiver cup
2. Select mandrel: contacts bush shell, not arm bore
3. Align mandrel, receiver, and press ram in a straight line
4. Engage shield

PRESSING OUT:
1. Apply pressure slowly — feel for interference break
2. Once moving, maintain steady pressure
3. Bush drops into receiver cup — extraction complete
4. Inspect arm bore: clean, no scoring, no corrosion
5. Clean bore with emery cloth if light surface rust

IF BUSH IS STUBBORN:
- Apply gentle heat to arm bore (heat gun, not flame)
  Alloy expands, releases grip on bush shell
- NEVER hammer — distorts bore
- If bore is scored or oval - arm must be replaced

CHECK BORE DIAMETER — must be within spec for new bush fit
```

#### Slide 7: New Bush Installation — Press-In with Orientation
**Visual:** New bush with visible voided section highlighted; arrow showing required orientation relative to arm geometry; press-in setup diagram

**Instructor Narration:**
> "New bushes aren't symmetrical. Most have a voided section — a gap in the rubber — that must face a specific direction. This void controls the compliance characteristics: it makes the bush softer in one direction and stiffer in another. The engineers designed it so the bush flexes correctly under braking and cornering loads. If you press the bush in 90 degrees off, the car will feel different — and the bush will wear unevenly because it's being stressed in a direction it wasn't designed for. Check the service data or the old bush orientation before you press the new one in."

**PPT Content:**
```
NEW BUSH INSTALLATION — ORIENTATION MATTERS

BEFORE PRESSING IN:
1. Note orientation of old bush before removal (photo it!)
2. Check new bush for orientation marks or voided sections
3. Clean arm bore — light oil film for smooth entry
4. Light smear of rubber-safe lubricant on bush outer shell
   DO NOT use petroleum-based lube on rubber bushes
   Use soapy water or manufacturer-specified lubricant

VOIDED SECTION ORIENTATION:
- Void typically faces direction of primary arm movement
- Allows compliance in that plane (absorbs bumps)
- Stiff axis perpendicular to void (resists lateral loads)
- Incorrect orientation = wrong compliance = poor handling + fast wear

PRESS-IN PROCEDURE:
1. Position bush at bore entry with correct orientation
2. Press-in mandrel contacts bush evenly on outer edge
3. Press slowly — bush must enter STRAIGHT, not cocked
4. Press until bush is flush or at specified depth
5. Verify orientation hasn't rotated during press-in
6. Check bush sits squarely — no lip visible on either side
```

#### Slide 8: Ball Joint Replacement
**Visual:** Exploded view of ball joint in control arm — staked type and press-fit type shown side by side; ball joint separator tool and press adapter

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Ball joints come in two main types: press-fit and staked. Press-fit joints press into the arm bore — removal and installation is the same hydraulic press procedure as a bush. Staked joints are pressed in and then the arm material is peened over a lip to retain them — you need to drill or grind the staking before pressing out, and re-stake with a suitable punch after pressing the new one in. In either case, after installation, check the joint seats fully — you should see the snap ring groove fully exposed, and the joint should have the correct amount of free play. Too tight is as bad as too loose — a tight ball joint binds and causes steering feedback problems."

**PPT Content:**
```
BALL JOINT REPLACEMENT

TYPE 1 — PRESS-FIT:
1. Press out old joint from arm bore (same mandrel technique)
2. Press in new joint — ensure snap ring groove fully visible
3. Fit new snap ring (if applicable)

TYPE 2 — STAKED:
1. Grind or drill the staked material from the retaining lip
2. Press out old joint
3. Press in new joint
4. Re-stake arm material into retaining groove with punch
5. Verify staking holds joint securely

AFTER INSTALLATION:
- Check ball joint articulation — smooth, no binding
- Check dust boot integrity — must be undamaged
- Grease joint if grease nipple fitted (pump until boot firms up)
- Correct seating: snap ring groove fully visible, joint flush

SEPARATION FROM KNUCKLE (for arm-mounted joints):
- Remove pinch bolt/castellated nut
- Use ball joint separator tool — controlled pressure
- NEVER use a fork-type splitter if reusing the joint (tears boot)
```

---

#### Slide 9: Subframe Bolts & Torque Values
**Visual:** Underside view of subframe mounting points with torque values annotated; bolt condition comparison (new vs corroded)

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before you refit the arm, check the subframe bolts. These are some of the most critical fasteners on the car — they hold the entire suspension and steering assembly to the body. If you've disturbed them, or if they show corrosion, check torque values and inspect thread condition. Many subframe bolts are single-use stretch bolts — check service data. When refitting the control arm to the subframe, use new hardware if specified. The arm-to-subframe bolts are typically M12 or M14, grade 10.9 or higher, and torque values are significant — often 100-150 Nm. These are the bolts you'll torque at ride height."

**PPT Content:**
```
SUBFRAME BOLTS & ARM MOUNTING HARDWARE

SUBFRAME-TO-BODY BOLTS:
- Typically M12/M14, grade 10.9 or 12.9
- Torque: 80-150 Nm (varies by vehicle)
- Often stretch bolts — check if single-use
- Check for corrosion, thread damage, elongation

ARM-TO-SUBFRAME PIVOT BOLTS:
- Typically M12/M14
- Torque: 100-150 Nm (ALWAYS check service data)
- May require new bolts (single-use specification)
- MUST be torqued at RIDE HEIGHT

BALL JOINT TO KNUCKLE:
- Pinch bolt: typically 50-80 Nm
- Castellated nut: torque to spec + advance to next slot for split pin

GENERAL RULES:
- Replace any bolt that shows corrosion, galling, or stretch marks
- Apply copper grease to bolt shanks (not threads) to aid future removal
- Never substitute lower-grade hardware
```

#### Slide 12: Stage 1 — Inspection of Removed Arm
**Visual:** Removed control arm on bench with worn bushes and ball joint visible; instructor pointing to cracked bush rubber

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Before we go to the press, look at what we're replacing. See how this bush rubber is cracked and separated from the outer shell? That's age and fatigue. And this one — see the oil contamination? Someone had a leaking engine oil or power steering fluid dripping onto it. Oil attacks rubber. It swells, softens, and loses its damping properties. The ball joint — grab the stud and try to rock it. Feel that play? That's wear in the bearing surface inside the joint. Any of these findings alone is reason to replace."

**PPT Content:**
```
INSPECT THE REMOVED ARM

BUSH CONDITION — WHAT TO LOOK FOR:
- Cracking: surface cracks from age and UV exposure
- Separation: rubber pulled away from outer or inner sleeve
- Distortion: rubber displaced to one side (overloaded or wrong torque)
- Oil contamination: swollen, soft rubber (engine/PS fluid leak)
- Corrosion: outer shell rusted, expanding and cracking rubber

BALL JOINT CONDITION:
- Axial play: grab stud, push/pull — any movement = worn
- Radial play: grab stud, rock side-to-side — no play acceptable
- Boot condition: torn boot = contamination = accelerated wear
- Stiffness: joint should move freely but with slight resistance

DOCUMENT FINDINGS — this justifies the replacement to the customer
```

#### Slide 13: Stage 2 — Bush Press-Out
**Instructor Action:**
- Move to hydraulic press area
- Demonstrate mandrel selection process: measure bush outer diameter with vernier caliper, select mandrel with matching ID
- Show receiver cup selection: must be larger than bush OD to allow clean exit
- Set up arm in press with bush centred over receiver
- Engage safety shield
- Demonstrate slow, controlled pressure application
- Bush exits into receiver — show the old bush and the clean bore
- Repeat for second bush (if arm has two bushes)
- Learners set up and press remaining bushes under supervision

**Key Teaching Points:**
- "If the mandrel is too small, it goes through the bush without pushing it. If it's too large, it contacts the arm bore and damages it. Measure twice, press once."
- "Watch the pressure gauge. If pressure climbs past the expected range and the bush isn't moving, STOP. Something is misaligned."

#### Slide 14: Stage 2 — Bore Inspection After Extraction
**Visual:** Close-up of clean arm bore after bush removal; feeler gauge being used to check for distortion

**Instructor Narration:**
> "With the bush out, inspect the bore. It must be clean, round, and free of scoring or corrosion. Light surface rust can be cleaned with fine emery cloth. Deep pitting or scoring means the bore is damaged — the new bush won't have a uniform interference fit and will eventually work loose. If the bore is out of spec, the arm must be replaced. This is a quick check that prevents a comeback."

**PPT Content:**
```
BORE INSPECTION AFTER BUSH EXTRACTION

CHECK:
- Visual: clean bore, no deep scoring, no pitting
- Measure: bore diameter at two points 90 degrees apart
  Must be within tolerance for new bush interference fit
  Typical interference: 0.05-0.15 mm
- Light corrosion: clean with fine emery cloth + solvent
- Heavy corrosion or scoring: ARM MUST BE REPLACED

PREPARATION FOR NEW BUSH:
- Clean bore thoroughly — solvent wipe, lint-free cloth
- Light oil film or rubber-safe lubricant on bore wall
- No petroleum-based grease on rubber-bonded bushes
  Soapy water or silicone spray is safe

INSPECT BALL JOINT BORE SIMULTANEOUSLY
- Same criteria: clean, round, undamaged
```

#### Slide 15: Stage 3 — New Bush Press-In
**Instructor Action:**
- Show new bush to learners — identify voided section, orientation marks
- Reference photos taken of old bush orientation
- Apply rubber-safe lubricant to bore and bush outer shell
- Set up press: arm supported, new bush aligned at bore entry with correct orientation
- Press-in mandrel selected: OD slightly smaller than bore, contacts bush outer edge evenly
- Press slowly — watch bush enter straight with no cocking
- Press to flush or specified depth
- Verify orientation hasn't rotated during press-in
- Check both sides: bush sits squarely, no lip visible
- Repeat for second bush
- Each learner performs at least one press-in under supervision

**Key Teaching Points:**
- "The bush must go in STRAIGHT. If it enters cocked, the outer shell distorts and the rubber pre-loads unevenly. You might not notice it at first, but the bush will squeak and fail early."
- "Check orientation AFTER pressing. The bush can rotate during the press-in if the lubrication lets it spin. Verify before you move on."

#### Slide 17: Stage 5 — Arm Refitting
**Visual:** Control arm being offered back into position on subframe, with annotations showing hand-tight bolts and supported knuckle

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Now the arm goes back on the car. Offer it up to the subframe and start the pivot bolts hand-tight — do NOT torque them yet. Connect the ball joint to the knuckle and torque the pinch bolt or castellated nut to spec — the ball joint taper seats under torque and that connection is fine to torque now. But the pivot bolts — the bush bolts — stay hand-tight. Those get final torque at ride height."

**PPT Content:**
```
ARM REFITTING PROCEDURE

1. Offer arm to subframe mounting points
2. Insert pivot bolts — HAND-TIGHT ONLY
   DO NOT torque yet — ride height step comes next

3. Connect ball joint to knuckle
   Seat taper fully before tightening
   Torque pinch bolt or castellated nut to spec
   Fit new split pin if castellated nut type

4. Reconnect any anti-roll bar links to arm (if applicable)
   Hand-tight for now

5. Refit wheel — hand-tighten wheel bolts/nuts

PIVOT BOLT STATUS: HAND-TIGHT — awaiting ride height
```

#### Slide 18: Stage 5 — The Ride-Height Torque Procedure
**Visual:** Two methods shown side by side: (1) car lowered onto wheels, torquing from below; (2) jack positioned under control arm to simulate ride height while car is still on lift

**Instructor Narration:**
> "This is the most important step of the entire job. You must torque the arm-to-subframe pivot bolts with the suspension at ride height. There are two ways to achieve this. Method one: lower the car fully onto its wheels, bounce the suspension a few times to settle it, then get underneath with a torque wrench — you'll need ramps or a pit for access. Method two — more practical: keep the car on the lift, but place a trolley jack under the outer end of the control arm and jack it up until the suspension is at the position it would be at ride height. Measure from a reference point if needed. Then torque the pivot bolts. Either method works. What does NOT work is torquing with the arm hanging at full droop."

**PPT Content:**
```
RIDE-HEIGHT TORQUE — THE CRITICAL STEP

METHOD A: Lower car onto wheels
1. Lower car fully, remove axle stands
2. Bounce suspension 4-5 times to settle
3. Access from below (ramps or pit)
4. Torque pivot bolts to spec

METHOD B: Jack to ride height (preferred when lift available)
1. Place trolley jack under outer arm or hub carrier
2. Raise jack until arm is at ride-height angle
   Measure arm angle or arm-to-subframe gap per service data
   Or: level arm approximately horizontal (typical ride position)
3. Torque pivot bolts to spec while jack holds position
4. Remove jack

WHY THIS MATTERS:
- Bush rubber sits at neutral angle - no pre-twist
- Full range of suspension travel available in both directions
- Bush lifespan: 80,000+ km (vs 10,000-15,000 km if torqued at droop)

NEVER SKIP THIS STEP
```

#### Slide 20: Wheel Alignment — Mandatory After Arm Work
**Visual:** Four-wheel alignment screen showing before/after readings; diagram showing how a new bush can change camber and toe

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Every time you remove or replace a control arm, a bush, or a ball joint, you MUST perform a wheel alignment or at minimum check the alignment. Why? The new bush may have slightly different compliance than the worn one — the arm now sits at a subtly different angle. A new ball joint may seat at a slightly different position in the knuckle taper. The result: camber, caster, and toe can all shift. If you hand the car back without checking alignment, the customer gets uneven tyre wear within 5,000 kilometres and you get a warranty comeback. Alignment is not optional after suspension arm work — it's part of the job."

**PPT Content:**
```
WHEEL ALIGNMENT — MANDATORY

WHY ALIGNMENT CHANGES AFTER ARM/BUSH WORK:
- New bush compliance differs from worn bush
  Arm resting angle shifts slightly
- New ball joint taper seat differs from worn surface
  Knuckle position shifts
- Any change in geometry = camber, caster, or toe shift

WHAT NEEDS CHECKING:
- Front camber (may be adjustable via eccentric bolts)
- Front toe (adjusted at tie rod — Day 48 skill)
- Front caster (usually set by arm geometry — not adjustable)
- Rear alignment (if rear arms disturbed)

ALWAYS: Four-wheel alignment, not just front
  Rear toe affects thrust angle which affects front toe setting

CUSTOMER COMMUNICATION:
"We've replaced the suspension arm and bushes. A wheel alignment
is included in this job to ensure your tyres wear evenly and
the car tracks straight."
```

#### Slide 21: Common Mistakes & Comebacks
**Visual:** Table format with three columns: Mistake, Consequence, Prevention

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let me share the mistakes I've seen cause comebacks. Number one: torquing at full droop — the bush dies in under a year. Number two: bush orientation wrong — the car feels vague and the customer can't explain why, but it's because the bush is compliant in the wrong axis. ## Key Takeaways

1. Always support the hub/knuckle before disconnecting the control arm — protect brake hoses and ABS wiring
2. Mandrel selection is critical: ID must match bush outer diameter for extraction; OD must suit bore for installation
3. Hydraulic press safety: shield in place, slow pressure, watch for sudden release, hands clear
4. Bush orientation matters — voided sections must face the correct direction for designed compliance
5. Ball joints must be correctly seated (snap ring groove visible) with undamaged boots
6. TORQUE PIVOT BOLTS AT RIDE HEIGHT — this single step determines whether the bush lasts 1 year or 5+ years
7. Wheel alignment is MANDATORY after any arm, bush, or ball joint replacement — it is part of the job, not an add-on
