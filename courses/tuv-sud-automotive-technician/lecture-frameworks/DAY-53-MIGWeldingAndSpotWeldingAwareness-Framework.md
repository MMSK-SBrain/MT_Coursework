## Session Objectives (For Instructor)

By the end of this session, learners will be able to:
- Set up a MIG welding machine for mild steel — wire speed, voltage, and shielding gas flow rate
- Prepare joints correctly — cleaning, fit-up, and clamping
- Identify and don correct welding PPE (auto-darkening helmet, leather gloves, leather apron, safety boots)
- Perform basic welding practice: butt joint, lap joint, and fillet weld in flat position
- Execute a simulated exhaust repair — patch welding a hole in mild steel tubing
- Inspect welds for common defects: porosity, undercut, cold lap, and incomplete penetration
- Explain resistance spot welding principles and how body panels are joined at the factory
- Determine when welding is appropriate vs when it is not — structural vs non-structural, insurance and certification considerations

**This is an awareness and introductory skill session.** Learners are NOT becoming certified welders today. The goal is to demystify welding, give them basic competency for common workshop repairs (exhaust work, bracket fabrication), and make them aware of the role welding plays in body repair so they can have informed conversations with body shop technicians.

#### Slide 3: MIG Welding — What It Is
**Visual:** Labelled diagram of MIG welding process — wire electrode feeding from spool through torch, shielding gas flowing around arc, weld pool forming on workpiece, completed bead behind.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "MIG stands for Metal Inert Gas — though the correct technical term is GMAW: Gas Metal Arc Welding. Here's how it works. A spool of wire feeds continuously through the welding torch. When you pull the trigger, an electrical arc forms between the wire tip and the workpiece. That arc melts both the wire and the base metal, creating a weld pool. At the same time, shielding gas — usually a mix of argon and CO2 — flows around the arc to protect the molten metal from atmospheric contamination. The wire is both the electrode and the filler material. That's what makes MIG so popular — it's relatively easy to learn and fast to execute."

**PPT Content:**
```
MIG WELDING (GMAW) — HOW IT WORKS

Components:
- Wire electrode: Continuously fed from spool (0.6-1.0mm diameter)
- Shielding gas: Protects weld pool from oxygen/nitrogen
- Arc: Electrical arc between wire tip and workpiece
- Weld pool: Molten mixture of wire + base metal
- Bead: Solidified weld joint

Key parameters (you will set these today):
1. WIRE SPEED — controls deposition rate (metres/min)
2. VOLTAGE — controls arc length and heat input
3. GAS FLOW — controls shielding (litres/min)

Why MIG is the automotive standard:
- Easy to learn compared to TIG or stick welding
- Works well on thin materials (car body = 0.8-2.0mm)
- Fast — high productivity for production and repair
- Clean — minimal spatter with correct settings
```

#### Slide 6: MIG Machine Setup — Wire Speed, Voltage, Gas Flow
**Visual:** Annotated photo of a MIG welder control panel showing wire speed dial, voltage dial, gas flow regulator, and wire spool compartment.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Three settings control your weld quality. Wire speed — measured in metres per minute — controls how fast the filler wire feeds. Too slow and the arc is unstable. Too fast and the wire pushes into the workpiece without melting properly. Voltage controls the arc length — higher voltage means a wider, hotter arc. Too low and the wire stubs into the work with a crackling sound. Too high and you get spatter and undercut. Gas flow rate — typically 12 to 16 litres per minute for workshop conditions. Too low and the shielding is inadequate — you get porosity. Too high and you create turbulence that sucks air INTO the shielding envelope, also causing porosity. Let me show you the starting settings for 1.5mm mild steel."

**PPT Content:**
```
MIG MACHINE SETUP — THE THREE CONTROLS

FOR 1.5mm MILD STEEL (today's practice material):

Wire speed: 4-6 m/min
- Start at 5 m/min, adjust by ear and bead appearance
- Correct: smooth crackling/hissing sound
- Too slow: popping/stubbing, wire melts into ball
- Too fast: wire pushes workpiece, spatters excessively

Voltage: 17-19V
- Start at 18V for 1.5mm material
- Too low: arc won't establish, wire stubs
- Too high: excessive spatter, undercut, burn-through

Gas flow: 12-16 L/min
- Argon/CO2 mix (75/25 or 80/20)
- Check flow at regulator
- Too low: porosity (gas holes in weld)
- Too high: turbulence pulls in atmospheric contamination

Wire: 0.8mm ER70S-6 mild steel wire (standard automotive)

THESE ARE STARTING POINTS — fine-tune by practice.
```

#### Slide 7: Joint Preparation — Clean, Fit, Clamp
**Visual:** Three-panel sequence — (1) grinding/cleaning a joint edge, (2) fitting two pieces with minimal gap, (3) clamping pieces in position with welding clamps.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "The weld is only as good as the preparation. Rule one: CLEAN. Remove all rust, paint, oil, and coating from the joint area — at least 25mm back from the weld zone. Use a flap disc or wire wheel on a grinder. Contamination in the weld pool causes porosity and weak joints. Rule two: FIT. The pieces must fit together with minimal gap — for butt joints on thin material, less than 1mm gap. Gaps cause burn-through. Rule three: CLAMP. The workpieces must be held firmly in position during welding. Metal expands when heated and contracts when cooling — unclamped parts WILL move and distort. Tack weld first, then run the full bead."

**PPT Content:**
```
JOINT PREPARATION — THE THREE RULES

RULE 1: CLEAN
- Remove rust, paint, oil, coating from joint zone
- Clean minimum 25mm back from weld line
- Tools: flap disc, wire wheel, or grinding disc
- Wipe with acetone for oil/grease removal
- Contamination = porosity + weak weld

RULE 2: FIT-UP
- Minimal gap between parts (< 1mm for butt joints)
- Edges aligned (no step/offset between plates)
- For lap joints: 10-15mm overlap minimum
- For fillet: 90-degree angle, tight contact at root

RULE 3: CLAMP
- Welding clamps, magnetic squares, or vice grips
- Hold parts FIRMLY — thermal expansion WILL move them
- Tack weld at ends and middle BEFORE running full bead
- Tack welds prevent distortion during full welding

Good preparation = good weld. Bad preparation = impossible to fix.
```

#### Slide 8: MIG Welding Technique — Torch Angle, Speed, Distance
**Visual:** Diagram showing torch angle (10-15 degrees push), standoff distance (10-15mm), travel direction arrow, and photo of correct hand position on torch.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Three technique variables: torch angle, travel speed, and standoff distance. Hold the torch at 10 to 15 degrees from vertical, tilted in the direction of travel — this is called a push angle, and it gives the best gas coverage and bead profile for MIG. Travel speed controls bead width and penetration — too fast gives a narrow, high bead with poor penetration. Too slow gives excessive width and risks burn-through on thin material. Standoff distance — the gap between the nozzle tip and the workpiece — should be 10 to 15mm. Too close and you clog the nozzle with spatter. Too far and you lose gas shielding."

**PPT Content:**
```
MIG WELDING TECHNIQUE

TORCH ANGLE:
- 10-15 degrees from vertical
- Tilted in direction of travel (PUSH technique)
- Push = better gas coverage, flatter bead
- Drag/pull = deeper penetration (used for thicker material)

TRAVEL SPEED:
- Moderate, consistent speed
- Watch the weld pool — it should be 6-8mm wide
- Too fast: narrow, peaked bead, poor fusion
- Too slow: wide, flat bead, risk of burn-through

STANDOFF DISTANCE:
- 10-15mm nozzle-to-work distance
- Maintain constant distance throughout weld
- Too close: spatter clogs nozzle, arc unstable
- Too far: loss of gas shielding, porosity

STEADY HAND + CONSISTENT SPEED = CLEAN BEAD
It's a motor skill — repetition builds quality.
```

#### Slide 9: Weld Defects — What to Look For
**Visual:** Illustrated cross-sections and surface views of five common weld defects — porosity, undercut, cold lap (lack of fusion), incomplete penetration, and excessive spatter.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "After every weld, you inspect it. Porosity looks like tiny holes or pitting in the bead surface — caused by gas contamination, usually from dirty metal or insufficient shielding. Undercut is a groove melted into the base metal alongside the bead — caused by too much heat or too fast travel speed. Cold lap means the weld metal sits on top of the base metal without fusing into it — caused by insufficient heat or wrong angle. Incomplete penetration means the weld didn't melt through the full thickness — the root of the joint isn't fused. And excessive spatter — those balls of metal stuck everywhere around the weld — means your settings are off, usually voltage too high or wire speed mismatched."

**PPT Content:**
```
WELD DEFECT IDENTIFICATION

POROSITY (gas holes):
- Appearance: pitting, small holes in bead surface
- Cause: dirty base metal, insufficient gas, draught blowing gas away
- Fix: clean better, increase gas flow, block draughts

UNDERCUT (groove beside bead):
- Appearance: channel melted into base metal along weld toe
- Cause: excessive heat, too fast travel, wrong angle
- Fix: reduce voltage, slow down, correct torch angle

COLD LAP / LACK OF FUSION:
- Appearance: bead sits on surface without melting into base
- Cause: insufficient heat, too fast travel, cold base metal
- Fix: increase voltage, slow travel speed, preheat if thick material

INCOMPLETE PENETRATION:
- Appearance: root of joint not fused (visible from back side)
- Cause: insufficient heat, too fast, excessive gap or poor fit-up
- Fix: increase parameters, improve fit-up

EXCESSIVE SPATTER:
- Appearance: metal balls stuck around weld zone
- Cause: voltage/wire speed mismatch, contaminated wire/gas
- Fix: adjust settings, check gas flow, clean wire contact tip
```

#### Slide 10: When to Weld vs When NOT to Weld
**Visual:** Decision matrix — green zone (exhaust repairs, brackets, non-structural), yellow zone (consult body shop), red zone (structural members, safety-critical, insurance-related).

**Instructor Narration:**
> "This is critical professional judgement. As a general service technician, you can weld non-structural repairs — exhaust pipes, mounting brackets, body accessories. But structural welding — chassis members, subframes, roll cages, A/B/C pillars — requires specific certification and often insurance approval. If a vehicle has been in a collision and structural members are damaged, that's a body shop repair with certified welders and specific procedures. Welding on structural components without certification can void insurance, compromise crash safety, and make you personally liable. Know your limits. When in doubt — don't weld it. Refer it to someone qualified."

**PPT Content:**
```
WHEN TO WELD vs WHEN NOT TO WELD

GREEN — Service technician can weld:
- Exhaust pipe and silencer repairs
- Non-structural brackets and mounts
- Accessory fabrication
- Tow bar mounting (with approved kit)
- Workshop equipment repairs

YELLOW — Consult body shop / specialist:
- Outer body panels (fender, door skin)
- Floor pan patches
- Rust repair on non-structural areas

RED — DO NOT weld without certification:
- A/B/C pillars (structural)
- Sill / rocker panels (structural)
- Subframes and cross members
- Suspension mounting points
- Any post-collision structural repair
- Roll cages (motorsport certification required)

Insurance implications:
Unauthorised structural welding = voided insurance policy.
Legal implications:
Structural failure from bad weld = personal liability.
```

#### Slide 12: Demonstration — Striking an Arc and Running a Bead
**Visual:** Photo sequence — trigger pull, arc establishment, steady travel along a marked line, bead complete.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Watch me first. I position the torch at the start of the line, about 10mm standoff. Helmet down. Trigger. The arc starts — you hear that steady crackling hiss. Now I move steadily along the line, maintaining constant distance and speed. Watch the weld pool, not the arc — the pool tells you everything. Too wide means I'm going too slow. Too narrow means I'm going too fast. I reach the end, release the trigger, lift the torch. That's your first bead. Simple in concept, a lifetime to perfect."

**PPT Content:**
```
RUNNING A BEAD — INSTRUCTOR DEMONSTRATION

1. POSITION torch at start point, 10-15mm standoff
2. HELMET DOWN — confirm auto-darkening active
3. TRIGGER — arc establishes, wire feeds
4. MOVE steadily along marked line
5. WATCH the weld pool (6-8mm wide = correct)
6. MAINTAIN constant standoff distance
7. REACH end point — release trigger
8. LIFT torch — arc extinguishes

LISTEN to the arc:
- Steady crackle/hiss = correct settings
- Popping = wire speed too low or voltage too low
- Harsh buzzing = wire speed too high
- Sputtering = gas issue or dirty contact tip

Your first bead will not be pretty. Your tenth will be better.
Your hundredth will be good. That's how craft works.
```

#### Slide 15: Practice Exercise 3 — Lap Joint
**Visual:** Diagram of lap joint — one plate overlapping another by 15mm, fillet weld along the overlap edge.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Exercise three: lap joint. One plate on top of another with 15mm overlap. This joint is common in automotive — panel overlaps, bracket attachments. Weld:

#### Slide 19: Resistance Spot Welding — Factory Body Assembly
**Visual:** Diagram of resistance spot welding — two copper electrodes clamping two sheet metal panels, current flowing through creating a weld nugget at the interface. Factory photo of robot spot welding a car body.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Spot welding is how vehicle bodies are assembled at the factory. Two panels are clamped between copper electrodes. A massive electrical current — thousands of amps — passes through for a fraction of a second. The resistance at the panel interface generates heat, creating a fused nugget that joins the panels. A typical car body has 3,000 to 5,000 spot welds. You won't do spot welding as a service technician, but you need to understand it for two reasons: first, you'll see spot welds when removing trim and panels. Second, if you ever work in or alongside a body shop, you need to know how replacement panels are attached."

**PPT Content:**
```
RESISTANCE SPOT WELDING — HOW BODIES ARE BUILT

Process:
- Two copper electrodes clamp panels together
- High current (5,000-15,000 amps) flows for 0.1-0.5 seconds
- Resistance at panel interface generates heat
- Metal melts at interface, forming a WELD NUGGET
- Electrodes release — nugget solidifies

In the factory:
- 3,000-5,000 spot welds per vehicle body
- Performed by robots (consistency, speed)
- Typical cycle time: 1 spot weld per 2 seconds
- Body-in-white assembly takes ~400 robot welding stations

In body repair:
- Original spot welds drilled out to remove damaged panel
- Replacement panel attached with spot welder or plug welds (MIG)
- Weld count and positions must match OEM specification
- Squeeze-type spot welders used in body shops
```

#### Slide 20: Spot Weld Identification and Significance
**Visual:** Close-up photos of spot welds visible on inner panels — showing the characteristic circular dimple pattern. Photo of a spot weld that's been drilled out for panel removal.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "You'll encounter spot welds every time you remove a door card, lift a carpet, or look inside a wheel arch. They look like small circular dimples — usually in a regular pattern along panel flanges. Count them and note their positions before any panel work. When a replacement panel is fitted, the body shop must replicate the same number of welds in the same positions. Fewer welds = weaker structure. Different positions = stress concentration changes. This matters for crash safety — the crumple zones are designed around those weld positions."

**PPT Content:**
```
SPOT WELD IDENTIFICATION

What they look like:
- Circular dimples (6-8mm diameter typically)
- Regular spacing along panel flanges (20-40mm apart)
- Visible on inner panels, under trim, inside wheel arches

Why they matter for service technicians:
1. Identify before panel removal (don't drill through them accidentally)
2. Count them — body shop needs to replicate exact count
3. Note positions — structural integrity depends on placement
4. Recognise if a panel has been previously replaced (plug welds
   instead of spot welds may indicate collision history)

For body repair awareness:
- Spot welds removed by DRILLING (spot weld cutter bit)
- Panel separated after all spot welds drilled
- Replacement panel fitted and re-welded
- Weld count + position MUST match manufacturer specification
```

#### Slide 21: Professional Boundaries — Certification and Insurance
**Visual:** Decision flowchart — "Is this weld structural?" leading to "Is the vehicle insured/will the repair affect crashworthiness?" leading to certification requirements.

**Instructor Narration:**
> "Let me be very clear about professional boundaries. In many countries, structural welding on vehicles requires specific certification — MIG/MAG to BS EN ISO 9606, or equivalent. Insurance companies require proof of certified welding for any structural repair before they'll cover the vehicle. If you weld a structural member without certification and that joint fails in a crash — you are personally liable. That's not a hypothetical. It's a legal reality. Know what you're qualified to do. Exhaust work, brackets, non-structural repairs — fine. Anything structural — refer to a certified body repair specialist."

**PPT Content:**
```
PROFESSIONAL BOUNDARIES

CERTIFIED WELDER REQUIRED FOR:
- All structural body repairs (pillars, sills, rails)
- Subframe and chassis repairs
- Suspension mounting point repairs
- Roll cage installation (motorsport certification)
- Any repair affecting vehicle crashworthiness

CERTIFICATION STANDARDS:
- BS EN ISO 9606-1 (qualification of welders)
- Manufacturer-specific body repair certification
- Insurance-approved body shop accreditation

YOUR RESPONSIBILITY:
- Know the difference between structural and non-structural
- If unsure — ASK before welding
- Document all welding repairs (photos, materials, procedures)
- Never exceed your certification level

The cost of getting this wrong is not a failed weld.
It's a failed structure in a crash.
```

## Key Takeaways

1. Welding PPE is non-negotiable — auto-darkening helmet, leather gloves, leather apron, safety boots, cotton clothing
2. MIG setup for mild steel: wire speed 4-6 m/min, voltage 17-19V, gas flow 12-16 L/min
3. Joint preparation is 70% of weld quality — clean, fit, clamp
4. Push angle (10-15 degrees), constant standoff (10-15mm), steady speed
5. Five defects to recognise: porosity, undercut, cold lap, incomplete penetration, excessive spatter
6. Spot welding joins body panels at the factory — 3,000 to 5,000 welds per vehicle
7. Structural welding requires certification — know your limits, refer when necessary
