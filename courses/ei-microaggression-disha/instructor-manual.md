### Slide 6: Pair Share

**Visual/Content:** Prompt: "Turn to the person next to you. Share ONE WORD that describes how you are feeling right now."

**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Alright, quick pair activity. Turn to the person next to you — if you are at the end of a row, turn to whoever is closest."
>
> "Share one word that describes how you are feeling right now. Not a sentence, not a story — just one word. Then listen to theirs."
>
> "Go."

*(Let pairs talk for 90 seconds to 2 minutes. The room will start buzzing — that is the energy you want.)*

> "Wonderful. I heard some great words floating around. I do not need you to share them publicly — but notice: you just practised two of the four EI quadrants. Self-awareness — knowing how you feel. And social awareness — listening to how someone else feels."
>
> "That is emotional intelligence in action, and we have not even started the first module yet."

**Facilitator Notes:**
- This is an intentionally low-stakes warm-up — one word is non-threatening
- If the room is very quiet, model it first: "I will go first. My one word right now is 'curious.' Now your turn."
- The bridge to EI quadrants at the end is important — it foreshadows Module 1

### Slide 11: The Amygdala Hijack

**Visual/Content:** Simple diagram of the brain showing the amygdala (deep in the brain, labelled "Alarm system") and the prefrontal cortex (front of the brain, labelled "Thinking brain"). Arrow showing: Threat perceived --> Amygdala fires --> Prefrontal cortex goes offline --> Fight/Flight/Freeze response.

**Timing:** 4 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Let me introduce you to the part of your brain that gets you into trouble."

*(Point to the amygdala on the diagram.)*

> "This is the amygdala. It is your brain's alarm system. It has one job: keep you alive. When it perceives a threat, it floods your body with stress hormones — cortisol, adrenaline — and your thinking brain, the prefrontal cortex, goes offline."
>
> "In prehistoric times, this was lifesaving. Tiger in the bushes? Do not think — run."
>
> "But here is the problem. Your amygdala cannot tell the difference between a tiger and a passive-aggressive email from your manager."

*(Pause for laughter.)*

> "When your boss questions your decision in front of the team, when someone takes credit for your idea, when you receive unfair feedback — your amygdala fires the same way. And suddenly you are reacting, not responding."
>
> "Has this ever happened to you? You said something in a meeting — or sent an email — and immediately thought, 'Why did I do that?' That is an amygdala hijack."
>
> "The good news is that there are techniques to interrupt this hijack. We will learn one in a few minutes. But first, let us watch what an emotional hijack looks like."

**Facilitator Notes:**
- Keep the neuroscience simple — this is not a biology class
- The tiger-versus-email line always gets a laugh. Use it.
- The personal question ("Has this ever happened to you?") is rhetorical — do not wait for answers, just let them nod internally

### Slide 15: The STOP Technique

**Visual/Content:** Large letters S-T-O-P with explanations:
- **S** — Stop what you are doing
- **T** — Take a breath (deep, slow)
- **O** — Observe your emotions (name them)
- **P** — Proceed with awareness (choose your response)

**Timing:** 4 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Alright, I promised you a practical tool to interrupt the amygdala hijack. Here it is. It is called the STOP technique, and it takes about 10 seconds."
>
> "S — Stop. Literally stop what you are doing. If you are about to type that angry reply, take your hands off the keyboard. If you are about to say something sharp in a meeting, close your mouth."
>
> "T — Take a breath. One deep, slow breath. In through the nose, out through the mouth. This activates your parasympathetic nervous system — it tells your amygdala to stand down."

*(Demonstrate a deep breath. Invite participants to do one with you.)*

> "O — Observe. What am I feeling right now? Name it. 'I am angry.' 'I am hurt.' 'I feel disrespected.' Naming the emotion gives your prefrontal cortex — your thinking brain — a chance to come back online."
>
> "P — Proceed with awareness. Now choose your response. Not react — respond. There is a difference."
>
> "You can use STOP before responding to a difficult email. Before a tough conversation. Before giving feedback when you are frustrated. Before reacting to something in a meeting that triggers you."
>
> "It is simple, but it is not easy. Simple because it is four steps. Not easy because in the heat of the moment, your amygdala wants you to skip straight to reacting."

**Facilitator Notes:**
- The guided breath is important — it makes this experiential, not just theoretical
- Consider writing S-T-O-P on the flipchart so it stays visible throughout the day
- You will reference this technique again in Modules 4 and 5

### Slide 18: What Is Bias?

**Visual/Content:** Definition: "A tendency to favour or disfavour a person, group, or thing — often without conscious awareness." Visual: Iceberg metaphor — conscious bias (explicit) above the waterline, unconscious bias (implicit) below the waterline, with the underwater portion being much larger.

**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Welcome back. Let us start Module 2 with a confession: I am biased. You are biased. Every human being on this planet is biased."

*(Pause. Let that land.)*

> "Bias is a tendency to favour or disfavour a person, group, or thing — and here is the tricky part — often without conscious awareness."
>
> "Look at this iceberg. The small part above the water is conscious bias — the biases we KNOW we have. 'I prefer working with people who are punctual.' That is conscious."
>
> "But the massive part below the water? That is unconscious bias. The assumptions you make before you even realize you are making them. The candidate you 'just have a good feeling about.' The colleague whose idea you dismiss without really listening."
>
> "Today is not about blame. None of us chose our biases — they were formed by our upbringing, our culture, our media, our experiences. But once you SEE them, you have a choice about what to do with them."

**Participant Handbook Reference:** Page 5 — "4 Types of Bias"

---

### Slide 19: The 4 Types of Bias

**Visual/Content:** Infographic showing four types with icons and one-line definitions:
1. **Unconscious Bias** — Automatic associations we make without thinking
2. **Affinity Bias** — Favouring people who are like us
3. **Confirmation Bias** — Seeking information that confirms what we already believe
4. **Attribution Bias** — Explaining others' behaviour differently than our own

**Timing:** 5 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Let me introduce four types of bias that show up most often in the workplace."
>
> "First: Unconscious Bias. These are the automatic associations your brain makes. Research shows your brain processes 11 million bits of information per second, but you are only consciously aware of about 40. The rest? Your brain handles on autopilot — and that autopilot is shaped by every stereotype, every cultural message, every experience you have ever had."
>
> "Second: Affinity Bias. This is the tendency to favour people who are like you. Same college, same hometown, same communication style, same background. It is why 'culture fit' in hiring can be code for 'they remind me of myself.'"
>
> "Third: Confirmation Bias. Once you form an opinion about someone, you unconsciously look for evidence that confirms it — and ignore evidence that contradicts it. If you think a team member is uncommitted, you notice every time they leave early, but you do not notice the evenings they stayed late."
>
> "Fourth: Attribution Bias. This is a double standard in how we explain behaviour. When YOU miss a deadline, it is because of circumstances — the client changed scope, you were overloaded. When SOMEONE ELSE misses a deadline, it is because they are disorganized or do not care."
>
> "In your handbook on page 5, write down these four types. And as I described each one, did any of them feel... familiar?"

*(Pause. Let them sit with that.)*

**Facilitator Notes:**
- Do not rush this slide. Each bias type deserves a beat.
- The "familiar" question is rhetorical — do not press for answers. The discomfort of recognition is the point.

### Slide 25: Bias Interruption Strategies

**Visual/Content:** Three strategies displayed with icons:
1. **Slow down decisions** — Do not go with your gut. Take 24 hours before making important people-decisions.
2. **Seek disconfirming evidence** — Actively look for information that challenges your assumption.
3. **Use structured criteria** — Evaluate people against pre-defined criteria, not "overall impression."

**Timing:** 4 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "So what do we DO about bias? Three practical strategies."
>
> "One: Slow down decisions. Your gut feeling is just your bias wearing a disguise. When you are making important decisions about people — hiring, feedback, promotion, project assignments — do not go with your first instinct. Sleep on it. Talk to someone with a different perspective."
>
> "Two: Seek disconfirming evidence. If you think someone is not ready for a promotion, deliberately look for evidence that they ARE ready. If you think someone is the perfect candidate, look for reasons they might not be. Challenge your own conclusions."
>
> "Three: Use structured criteria. Before you evaluate anyone, define what 'good' looks like — in specific, measurable terms. Then evaluate everyone against the same criteria. This takes the 'vibes' out of decision-making."
>
> "In your handbook, there is space to note where you will apply each of these. Write one specific situation for each."

*(Give 1 minute for writing.)*

### Slide 27: What Is a Stereotype?

**Visual/Content:** Definition: "An oversimplified, fixed belief about a group of people." Below: Formation cycle diagram — Exposure --> Generalisation --> Reinforcement --> Perceived as "Truth" (circular arrow).

**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "A stereotype is an oversimplified, fixed belief about a group of people. Notice the words: oversimplified and fixed."
>
> "Here is how they form. First, you are exposed to a pattern — maybe you see a few women colleagues who prioritise family over overtime. Second, your brain generalises — 'women are less committed to their careers.' Third, the stereotype gets reinforced — every time you see a woman leave early, it confirms your belief. You do not notice the women who stay late."
>
> "And over time, the stereotype starts to feel like truth. It does not feel like a belief anymore — it feels like a fact."
>
> "Here is what makes stereotypes especially tricky: they are not always hostile. Sometimes the most limiting stereotypes sound like compliments."

*(Pause.)*

> "'Women are naturally better at multitasking.' 'You handle the emotional stuff so well.' 'You are such a team player — you do not need to be in the spotlight.' These sound nice. But what do they actually do? They pigeonhole. They limit. They keep people in boxes."

**Participant Handbook Reference:** Page 8 — "Stereotype Formation Cycle"

### Slide 28: Where Do Stereotypes Come From?

**Visual/Content:** Four sources with icons:
1. **Family** — Messages from parents, relatives, community
2. **Media** — Films, TV, advertising, social media
3. **Workplace culture** — "How things are done here"
4. **Self** — Internalized beliefs about your own group

**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Where do our stereotypes come from? Four main sources."
>
> "Family: the messages you absorbed growing up. 'Ladkiyaan aise nahi karti.' 'Engineering is for boys.' Even if your family was progressive, the extended family, the community, the cultural water you swam in — it all left deposits."
>
> "Media: think about the films you grew up watching. How were working women portrayed? How were mothers portrayed? How were women leaders portrayed? Those images shape our expectations even when we know they are fiction."
>
> "Workplace culture: every organization has unwritten rules. 'Leaders look like this.' 'Ambitious people do this.' These are stereotypes wearing a professional suit."
>
> "And finally — self. This is the hardest one. Sometimes we internalize stereotypes about our own group. 'Maybe I AM too emotional for this role.' 'Maybe I SHOULD not push for that promotion because I have young children.'"
>
> "I am not going to ask you to share publicly, but I invite you to notice, quietly: which source has had the most influence on you?"

*(Pause for 15 seconds of quiet reflection.)*

**Facilitator Notes:**
- The Hindi phrase ("Ladkiyaan aise nahi karti" — "Girls do not do things like that") will resonate with this audience. Use it naturally.
- If someone wants to share which source influenced them most, welcome it briefly, but do not turn this into a long discussion — the movie clip is next.

### Slide 38: What Are Microaggressions?

**Visual/Content:** Definition: "Brief, everyday exchanges that communicate hostile, derogatory, or negative messages to members of marginalised groups — often unintentionally." — Derald Wing Sue. Below: "Micro does not mean the impact is small."

**Timing:** 3 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Let us start with a definition. Microaggressions are — and I am quoting the researcher Derald Wing Sue — 'brief, everyday exchanges that communicate hostile, derogatory, or negative messages to members of marginalised groups — often unintentionally.'"
>
> "Let me unpack that. Brief — these are not long speeches. They are one-liners, glances, decisions. Everyday — they happen in meetings, emails, hallway conversations. Often unintentionally — the person doing it usually does not know they are doing it."
>
> "And here is the critical point: the word 'micro' does not mean the impact is small. It means the actions are subtle. But they accumulate. And that accumulation is what makes them so damaging."
>
> "I want to be clear about something. Today we are not here to point fingers. Many of us have been on the receiving end of microaggressions. And — if we are honest — most of us have probably committed them too, without knowing. This is about building the awareness to recognize them, no matter which side you are on."

**Participant Handbook Reference:** Page 10 — "Three Types of Microaggressions"

### Slide 39: Three Types

**Visual/Content:** Three types displayed with severity gradient:
1. **Microassaults** — Conscious, deliberate verbal or nonverbal attacks. Examples: using a slur, deliberately excluding someone, displaying hostile body language. "These are the most overt — people usually know they are doing it."
2. **Microinsults** — Rude, insensitive comments or behaviours that demean someone's identity. Examples: "You are so articulate!" (said with surprise to someone from a marginalised group), "You don't look like an engineer." "These are usually unintentional but still hurtful."
3. **Microinvalidations** — Comments or behaviours that dismiss, negate, or exclude the experiences of marginalised groups. Examples: "I do not see gender — I treat everyone equally." "You are being too sensitive." "These are the hardest to spot because they often sound positive or neutral."

**Timing:** 5 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "There are three types of microaggressions, and they vary in how obvious they are."
>
> "First: Microassaults. These are the most overt. They are conscious and deliberate — using a slur, making a sexist joke, deliberately excluding someone. Most people know when they are doing this. These are the easiest to identify."
>
> "Second: Microinsults. These are comments that are rude or insensitive, but the person saying them usually does not realize it. 'You are so articulate!' said with surprise — as if you expected the person NOT to be. 'You got this role? Good for you!' with a tone that implies surprise. 'You do not look like an engineer.' The insult is in the underlying assumption."
>
> "Third — and this is the one I want you to pay close attention to — Microinvalidations. These dismiss or negate someone's lived experience. 'I do not see gender, I treat everyone equally.' 'You are overreacting.' 'It was just a joke.' 'I am sure they did not mean it that way.'"
>
> "Microinvalidations are the hardest to spot because they often sound reasonable. They sound like fairness or kindness. But what they actually do is tell the person: your experience does not matter. What you felt is not valid."

**Facilitator Notes:**
- Slow down on microinvalidations — this is where the most learning happens
- Participants often recognise microassaults immediately but struggle with microinvalidations
- If someone says "But I really DO treat everyone equally" — this is a teachable moment. Respond: "That intention is good. But impact matters more than intention. If someone tells you they felt invalidated, 'I treat everyone equally' does not address what they experienced."

- Cumulative microaggressions correlate with reduced performance, not due to ability but due to cognitive load of processing them

### Slide 47: The 3D Framework

**Visual/Content:** Three strategies displayed clearly:
1. **Direct** — Address it in the moment. "That comment felt dismissive. Can we revisit Priya's point?"
2. **Distract** — Change the subject or redirect. "Hey, can we get back to the agenda item?"
3. **Delegate** — Report to someone with authority or follow up later. "I want to flag something that happened in today's meeting with you."

**Timing:** 5 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "The 3D framework gives you three response strategies. Think of them as three tools in your toolkit — you pick the one that fits the situation."
>
> "First: Direct. This means addressing the microaggression in the moment. It might sound like: 'I want to go back to what Priya was saying — I do not think we fully heard her point.' Or: 'That comment felt dismissive. Can we unpack that?'"
>
> "Direct takes courage. It is the most visible response. But it is also the most powerful, because it names what happened in real time."
>
> "Second: Distract. This means defusing the situation without directly confronting it. 'Hey, we are running short on time — can we get back to the agenda?' Or: 'Let us take a five-minute break.' You are not ignoring the microaggression — you are strategically redirecting the conversation to stop it from continuing."
>
> "Distract is lower-risk. It is useful when you do not feel safe being direct, or when the power dynamic is not in your favour."
>
> "Third: Delegate. This means following up with someone who has the authority or relationship to address it. 'I want to flag something that happened in today's meeting. Can we talk about it?' Or: 'I noticed a pattern in how Priya's contributions are being received. I think it is worth addressing.'"
>
> "Delegate is essential when there is a significant power imbalance, when the microaggression is part of a pattern, or when you need institutional support."
>
> "All three are valid. The 'right' response depends on the situation."

**Participant Handbook Reference:** Page 12 — "The 3D Framework"

**Timing:** 3 minutes

### Case Study 1: Bias in the Performance Review

**Module:** Module 2 (Slide 23--24)
**Focus:** Unconscious bias, affinity bias, confirmation bias, attribution bias
**Time Allocation:** 17 minutes total (2 min reading + 10 min group discussion + 5 min sharing)

#### Full Scenario Text

It is performance review season at Pinnacle Technologies, a mid-sized IT services company in Bengaluru. Sanjay Nair, a Senior Delivery Manager, is reviewing two of his direct reports: Vikram Deshmukh, a Project Lead who has been on his team for three years, and Meena Iyer, a Project Lead who joined the team eighteen months ago after an internal transfer from the Chennai office.

Both Vikram and Meena manage similar-sized projects with comparable complexity. This year, both delivered their projects within budget and received positive client feedback. Vikram's project had a two-week schedule overrun due to a vendor delay. Meena's project came in on time, but she escalated a resource conflict to Sanjay mid-project, requesting his intervention when two team members had a sustained disagreement about architecture decisions.

As Sanjay sits down to write their reviews, he finds himself rating Vikram as "Exceeds Expectations" and Meena as "Meets Expectations." When his HR Business Partner, Aruna, asks him to explain the difference in ratings, Sanjay gives the following reasons:

"Vikram is a natural leader — the team gravitates toward him. The schedule overrun was completely out of his control; the vendor dropped the ball. He handled the pressure brilliantly — stayed calm, kept the team focused, and still delivered a great outcome. He is ready for the next level."

"Meena is solid — she does good work, no question. But I am not sure she is ready for more responsibility yet. She needed to escalate that resource conflict to me instead of handling it herself, which tells me she is still building her leadership muscle. And she has only been on the team for eighteen months — she needs more time to prove herself. Also, I heard she is considering starting a family soon, so I am not sure it is the right time to put more on her plate."

Aruna notices that Sanjay and Vikram both graduated from IIT Bombay, play cricket together on weekends, and frequently have lunch together. Sanjay and Meena interact primarily in formal settings — team meetings and project reviews.

Meanwhile, Meena's previous manager in Chennai had rated her "Exceeds Expectations" in both her prior reviews, noting her collaborative leadership style and her ability to build consensus in complex stakeholder environments. Her escalation of the resource conflict was, in fact, consistent with the company's conflict resolution guidelines, which encourage involving management when team-level resolution fails.

#### Discussion Questions & Expected Responses

**Q1: "What biases can you identify in this scenario?"**

Expected responses by group assignment:

| Bias Type | Evidence in the Scenario |
|-----------|------------------------|
| **Unconscious Bias** | Sanjay's instinctive sense that Vikram is "a natural leader" vs. Meena is "solid" — different language for similar performance. The family planning comment reflects unconscious assumptions about women's priorities. |
| **Affinity Bias** | Sanjay and Vikram share IIT Bombay background, play cricket together, have lunch together. This personal connection is influencing Sanjay's perception of Vikram's leadership ability. Sanjay's relationship with Meena is purely transactional. |
| **Confirmation Bias** | Sanjay interprets Vikram's schedule overrun as "vendor's fault" (external attribution) but interprets Meena's escalation as "not ready" (internal attribution). He is also anchoring on Meena's tenure ("only eighteen months") while ignoring her track record from Chennai. He is seeking evidence that confirms his existing impression. |
| **Attribution Bias** | Vikram's failure (schedule overrun) is attributed to external factors (the vendor). Meena's action (escalation) is attributed to an internal deficit (lack of leadership). This is a classic double standard — the same behaviour interpreted differently depending on who does it. |

**Q2: "How would the outcome differ if the decision-maker was aware of their bias?"**

Expected responses:
- Sanjay would evaluate both against the same objective criteria — delivery, client feedback, team management, adherence to company processes
- He would recognize that Meena's escalation followed company policy and was actually a sign of mature judgment, not weakness
- He would seek Meena's Chennai manager's input rather than relying solely on his own eighteen-month impression
- He would catch himself applying different language ("natural leader" vs. "solid") and examine why
- He would recognize that the family planning comment is irrelevant and inappropriate in a performance evaluation

**Q3: "What process change could prevent this?"**

Expected responses:
- Standardized evaluation rubrics with specific, measurable criteria applied to all employees
- Calibration sessions where managers review ratings with peers and HR to catch inconsistencies
- 360-degree feedback that includes input from team members, not just the manager's impression
- Mandatory bias training for all managers before review season
- Removing non-performance factors (tenure, personal life speculation) from evaluation criteria
- Requiring managers to provide specific behavioral examples for each rating, not general impressions

#### Facilitation Tips

- If participants say "This happens all the time" — validate: "That recognition is exactly the point of this exercise."
- If someone defends Sanjay's reasoning ("But Meena DID escalate") — ask the group: "Is escalation according to company policy a sign of weakness or good judgment? And would we interpret it the same way if Vikram had done it?"
- If the family planning comment sparks strong reactions — let the reaction breathe. Then say: "Notice the emotional charge here. That is your EI system telling you something. This is where bias and emotions intersect."
- If participants get stuck on identifying their assigned bias, help with a leading question: "Look at the relationship between Sanjay and Vikram outside of work. What role might that play?"

#### Debrief Talking Points

- All four biases operate simultaneously in real life — they layer and reinforce each other
- The person experiencing the bias (Meena) may not have enough information to identify it — she may simply feel that the review is "unfair" without being able to name why
- Structural solutions (rubrics, calibration) work better than relying on individual awareness — because bias is, by definition, unconscious
- The family planning comment is not just bias — it potentially crosses into discriminatory conduct. Name this if no one else does.

### Case Study 2: Stereotypes and the Maternity Return

**Module:** Module 3 (Slide 31)
**Focus:** Gender stereotypes, role-based stereotypes, benevolent sexism
**Time Allocation:** 12 minutes total (2 min reading + 10 min discussion)

#### Full Scenario Text

Nandini Sharma is a Senior Manager in the Strategy and Planning division of a large manufacturing conglomerate headquartered in Pune. She has been with the company for nine years, consistently rated as a high performer, and was shortlisted for the General Manager promotion track before she went on maternity leave.

Nandini returns after six months of maternity leave. She is eager to re-engage — she has kept in touch with key projects through email and has already spoken to her team about her return. On her first day back, she meets with her reporting manager, Rajesh Kumar, who is a Vice President.

Rajesh welcomes her warmly. "Nandini, great to have you back. We have missed you. Listen, I have been thinking about your transition back, and I want to make sure we do not overwhelm you. I have moved the Vizag plant expansion project to Abhishek — it has been going well under him and I did not want to disrupt the continuity. Your new assignment is the internal process optimization review — it is important work, and it is less travel-intensive, which I thought would be better for you right now, given your situation."

Nandini is taken aback. The Vizag project was her initiative — she had developed the proposal, secured board approval, and built the project team before her leave. The process optimization review, while legitimate, is an internal project with low visibility and no client exposure. She notices that Rajesh does not ask her what she wants — he has made the decision for her.

Over the next few weeks, Nandini observes a pattern. In leadership meetings, Rajesh introduces topics by saying, "Abhishek is leading the Vizag expansion — he has really grown into the role." When Nandini contributes strategic insights, colleagues respond politely but do not build on her points the way they used to. One colleague says privately, "It must be hard to juggle everything. I do not know how you manage." Another says, "You look great for someone who just had a baby!" A third asks, "So, are you planning a second one soon?"

When the next round of GM promotions is announced, Nandini is not on the list. Abhishek is. When she asks Rajesh about it, he says, "You have been back for only four months, Nandini. Give it some time. Abhishek has had a strong year — the Vizag project has been his flagship. I want to make sure you are settled before we push for promotion. There is no rush."

Nandini realizes that the project she built has become someone else's career credential, and the clock on her "readiness" has been reset — not because of her performance, but because of assumptions about her priorities as a new mother.

#### Discussion Questions & Expected Responses

**Q1: "What stereotypes are operating in this scenario?"**

Expected responses:
- **Gender stereotype:** The assumption that a new mother will be less committed to her career, less available, less willing to travel
- **Role-based stereotype:** The belief that women returning from maternity leave need to be "protected" from demanding assignments — benevolent sexism disguised as care
- **Competence stereotype:** The subtle shift in how Nandini's contributions are received in meetings — as if her credibility has diminished
- **"Ideal worker" stereotype:** The unspoken norm that committed professionals do not take extended leave, do not have caregiving responsibilities, and are available at all times. Nandini is measured against this norm and found lacking.

**Q2: "How do they affect the person's opportunities?"**

Expected responses:
- Nandini lost her flagship project — the one she conceived and built — and with it, her path to promotion
- She was assigned lower-visibility work without being consulted, reducing her exposure to senior leadership and clients
- Her "readiness" clock was reset to zero, while Abhishek's continued to run
- The cumulative effect of colleagues' comments — however well-intentioned — signals that she is now seen as "a mother" before she is seen as "a strategist"
- If this pattern continues, Nandini may self-select out of ambitious career goals — not because she wants to, but because the system has communicated that ambitious goals are incompatible with motherhood

**Q3: "Who is responsible for changing this?"**

Expected responses:
- **Rajesh** — as her manager, he should have asked Nandini what she wanted, not assumed. He should have preserved her project assignment or given her the choice. He should evaluate her based on current performance, not assumptions.
- **HR** — the organization should have a return-from-leave protocol that protects project ownership and promotion pipelines
- **Colleagues** — the comments ("how do you manage," "you look great for a new mom," "are you planning another one") are microaggressions that need to be addressed
- **Nandini** — she has a role in advocating for herself, but the systemic barriers make self-advocacy harder. The burden should not fall solely on her.
- **The organization** — policies around parental leave, project ownership during leave, and promotion criteria need to be examined for built-in bias

#### Facilitation Tips

- This case study often generates strong emotional responses from participants who have experienced similar situations. Let the emotion be present without trying to "solve" it.
- If someone says "But Rajesh was trying to help her" — acknowledge the good intention, then redirect: "Good intent does not equal good impact. What was the actual impact on Nandini's career?" This is the core of benevolent sexism.
- If someone shares a personal story that mirrors this scenario, let them share. Say: "Thank you. Your experience is exactly why this program exists." Do not ask follow-up questions unless they invite it.
- If the group focuses only on what Nandini should have done (self-advocacy), gently redirect: "Nandini's self-advocacy is important. But whose responsibility is it to create a system where she does not have to fight to keep her own project?"

#### Debrief Talking Points

- Benevolent sexism — "protecting" women from challenges they did not ask to be protected from — is one of the most insidious forms of gender bias because it looks like care
- The "ideal worker" norm is a systemic issue, not an individual one. It penalizes anyone with caregiving responsibilities — disproportionately women.
- The cumulative effect of "helpful" comments erodes professional identity over time
- Solutions must be systemic (policies, protocols) AND individual (awareness, allyship)

**Time Allocation:** 15 minutes total (8 min activity + 7 min debrief)

#### Scenario 1

Ritu, a software architect, presents a technical design to the steering committee. After her presentation, the committee chair turns to Ritu's male colleague, Aakash, who was not involved in the design, and asks, "Aakash, what do you think — does this approach hold up technically?" Ritu's design is solid, and Aakash, caught off-guard, says, "I think Ritu covered it well." The chair nods but still directs follow-up technical questions to Aakash for the rest of the meeting.

**Classification:** **Microinsult**
**Explanation:** The chair's behaviour communicates an underlying assumption that Ritu's technical competence needs validation by a male colleague. It is unlikely the chair is consciously aware of this pattern — he probably believes he is being thorough. The insult is in the implication that Ritu's expertise is insufficient on its own.

---

#### Scenario 2

During a team outing, Fatima, a Muslim colleague, declines the wine being passed around. A senior colleague says loudly, "Oh come on, Fatima, one glass will not kill you. Do not be so conservative — you are in corporate India now, not in a madrasa!" Several people at the table laugh nervously.

**Classification:** **Microassault**
**Explanation:** This is a conscious, deliberate verbal attack that targets Fatima's religious identity. The reference to "madrasa" is a hostile stereotype, and the pressure to drink despite a clear refusal is aggressive. The person likely knows this is provocative — the "humour" is a shield for hostility.

---

#### Scenario 3

Ananya, a new team lead, proposes a change to the team's sprint process. Her manager listens and says, "That is an interesting idea, Ananya, but we have tried similar things before and they did not work. I think with more experience here, you will understand why we do things the way we do." He does not ask what specifically Ananya is proposing or how it differs from past attempts.

**Classification:** **Microinvalidation**
**Explanation:** The manager dismisses Ananya's proposal without engaging with its substance. The message is: your perspective does not have enough weight yet. While framed as mentorship ("with more experience"), it invalidates her contribution by suggesting she does not have the standing to propose changes. This is a microinvalidation because it negates her professional voice rather than insulting or attacking her directly.

---

#### Scenario 4

Prerna, a senior manager who uses a wheelchair, arrives at a client site for a critical presentation. The conference room is on the second floor with no elevator access. When she asks about an alternative, the client's office manager says, "Oh, we did not realize... can someone carry your laptop up and you can dial in from the lobby? That might actually be more comfortable for you."

**Classification:** **Microinsult**
**Explanation:** The suggestion to "dial in from the lobby" implies that Prerna's physical presence at her own presentation is optional — that her comfort is more about convenience than professional participation. The phrase "more comfortable for you" masks the failure to provide accessibility by reframing exclusion as accommodation. The client likely thinks they are being helpful, which is why this is a microinsult (insensitive and demeaning) rather than a microassault (deliberate).

---

#### Scenario 5

After a leadership development workshop, a group of colleagues are discussing their career ambitions. Sunita, who is 52, mentions she is interested in the Chief Digital Officer role that just opened. A younger colleague says, "That is great, Sunita, but do you really want to start learning AI and all that at this stage? It is such a young person's game. Maybe something in governance would be a better fit?"

**Classification:** **Microinsult**
**Explanation:** The comment implies that Sunita's age makes her unsuitable for a technology leadership role. The suggestion to pursue "governance" instead reflects an age-based stereotype that older professionals cannot learn new technologies. The colleague likely thinks they are being pragmatic, but the underlying message is demeaning: you are too old for this.

#### Scenario 6

Deepika, who grew up in a small town in Uttar Pradesh, has just been promoted to Vice President at a financial services firm. At the celebratory team lunch, a well-meaning colleague says, "Deepika, your story is so inspiring. Coming from a small town, no connections, no privilege — and look at you now. It just proves that if you work hard enough, anyone can make it." Deepika smiles but feels uncomfortable, though she cannot immediately articulate why.

**Classification:** **Microinvalidation**
**Explanation:** While the comment sounds like a compliment, it contains several invalidating messages. It frames Deepika's success as exceptional rather than earned — as if someone from a small town succeeding is surprising. The phrase "no privilege" makes assumptions about her background. And "if you work hard enough, anyone can make it" invalidates the systemic barriers that exist — it implies that those who have not "made it" simply did not work hard enough. Deepika's discomfort is valid: she is being celebrated for overcoming a narrative she did not choose, rather than for her professional competence.

### Case Study 4: Role-Play — The Stolen Idea

**Module:** Module 5 (Slides 52--53)
**Focus:** Practising the 3D response framework, bystander-to-upstander intervention
**Time Allocation:** 20 minutes total (2 min setup + 12 min role-play + 5 min debrief + 1 min transition)

#### Full Scenario Text

**Setting:** A weekly product review meeting at an e-commerce company in Gurugram. Six people are in the room. The meeting is chaired by Nikhil Mehta, the VP of Product.

**Characters for role-play:**
- **Kavita Rao** (Person A — the target): Senior Product Manager with 8 years of experience. She has been analysing customer churn data for two weeks and has developed a specific proposal.
- **Arjun Kapoor** (Person B — the person committing the microaggression): Product Manager at the same level as Kavita. He is confident, articulate, and well-liked by Nikhil.
- **Deepa Menon** (Person C — the bystander): Another Senior Product Manager. She is junior to neither Kavita nor Arjun and has a good relationship with both.

**The Scenario:**

Kavita is presenting her analysis of customer churn in the premium segment. She has identified that 35% of premium customers who churned cited "inconsistent post-purchase experience" as their primary reason. She proposes a dedicated post-purchase experience team with a 90-day customer journey redesign.

As Kavita is halfway through her proposal, Nikhil interrupts: "Interesting data, Kavita. But I am not sure a whole new team is the right answer — that is a big investment." He turns to the broader group: "What do others think?"

Arjun leans forward. "I think what we need is a focused post-purchase experience initiative — a dedicated squad that maps the entire customer journey for the first 90 days and redesigns the touchpoints. We could pilot it with the premium segment and then scale."

Nikhil's face lights up. "Now THAT is interesting, Arjun. That is exactly the kind of bold thinking we need. Can you put a proposal together by Friday?"

Kavita stiffens. Arjun has just restated her idea in almost identical terms — and received credit for it. She looks around the table. Deepa catches her eye.

**FREEZE. This is where the role-play begins.**

#### Role-Play Instructions

**Round 1 (4 minutes):**
- Person A (Kavita) practises responding using one of the 3Ds.
- Person C (Deepa) practises an upstander intervention.
- Person B (Arjun) stays in character — not hostile, not defensive, just oblivious to what happened.

**Suggested responses for coaching:**

*Kavita (Direct):*
> "Nikhil, I appreciate Arjun's input, but I want to clarify — what Arjun described is exactly the proposal I was presenting. A dedicated squad for the post-purchase journey, piloting with premium. I would love to continue presenting the details, including the implementation plan I have developed."

*Kavita (Distract):*
> "Nikhil, I actually have a detailed implementation plan for exactly this approach. Can I walk the group through it? I think it will address some of the investment concerns."

*Kavita (Delegate — after the meeting):*
> "Nikhil, can I have five minutes after the meeting? I want to discuss how ideas are credited in our product reviews. I believe the proposal Arjun described was the same one I was presenting, and I want to make sure my work is recognized."

*Deepa (Upstander — Direct):*
> "I want to flag something. Kavita was in the middle of presenting this exact proposal — a dedicated post-purchase squad with a 90-day journey redesign for premium. Arjun, I think you are building on Kavita's work, which is great, but I want to make sure Kavita gets the space to finish her presentation."

*Deepa (Upstander — Distract):*
> "Kavita, I thought your analysis was really compelling. Can you walk us through the rest of your proposal? I think there was more detail coming."

**Round 2 (4 minutes):** Rotate roles. Each person plays a different character.

**Round 3 (4 minutes):** Final rotation. Each person has now played all three roles.

#### Facilitation Tips for the Role-Play

- Walk between groups during the role-play. Listen for:
  - Participants who struggle to speak up — coach quietly: "Start with 'I want to go back to...' — that is a low-risk Direct approach."
  - Participants who are too aggressive in their response — coach: "Remember, the goal is to be assertive, not confrontational. How can you make the same point with a calmer tone?"
  - Participants in the bystander role who freeze — coach: "Your role as Deepa is to redirect attention back to Kavita. A simple 'Kavita, can you continue?' is enough."
- If a group finishes early, ask them to discuss: "What made each approach effective or ineffective? Which D felt most natural?"
- If someone refuses to play Person B (Arjun), do not force them. Let them observe and join the debrief discussion.

#### Debrief Talking Points

- The Kavita/Arjun dynamic is one of the most commonly reported microaggressions experienced by women professionals — the "stolen idea" or "amplification gap."
- The bystander (Deepa) role is the most important learning role — because in real life, bystanders have the power to change the dynamic without the target having to advocate for themselves.
- The discomfort of playing Arjun is valuable — it builds empathy for how easily we can commit microaggressions without being aware.
- In real life, the STOP technique from Module 1 is what allows Kavita (and Deepa) to respond rather than react. Self-management is the bridge between awareness and action.
- The EQ Pulse reference fits naturally here: "In organisations that measure psychological safety — tools like EQ Pulse track whether people feel safe to speak up — the upstander rate is a key indicator. When people like Deepa speak up consistently, psychological safety scores rise."

> "This is from Bombay Begums — a Hindi series. Rani is the CEO of a bank. She is the most senior person in this boardroom. Watch how she is treated by her board — and pay attention to the subtle dynamics, not just the words."

**Discussion Facilitation Guide:**
- After the clip, ask: "What microaggressions did you see? Were they microassaults, microinsults, or microinvalidations?"
- Expected responses: "Being talked over — microinsult." "Questions redirected to the male CFO — microinsult." "The 'diversity appointment' comment — depending on whether she was meant to hear it, this could be a microassault (deliberate) or a microinsult (careless)." "The overall pattern of undermining her authority — a combination of microinsults and microinvalidations."
- Key discussion point: "Notice that Rani is the CEO. She has the highest rank in the room. And yet the microaggressions continue. Position and seniority do not protect you. This is important for C3-level women to understand — the dynamics do not automatically disappear as you rise."

**Handling Emotional Reactions:**
- This clip may trigger strong identification from participants who have been in similar situations. If someone shares a personal experience, honour it: "Thank you for sharing that. What you experienced is exactly what this program is addressing."

**Backup Plan (if clip does not work):**

> "In Bombay Begums, Rani Irani is the CEO of a major bank. She is presenting a strategic initiative at a board meeting — her board meeting. But watch the dynamics. Male board members interrupt her mid-sentence. Technical questions are directed not to Rani, but to the male CFO sitting next to her, as if he is the real authority. And then, in a moment that crystallizes everything, she overhears one board member say to another: 'Well, she is our diversity appointment.' Rani freezes. The most powerful person in the room — by title, by authority, by competence — has just been reduced to a box-ticking exercise. What makes this so relevant is that these women in our room today — C3-level professionals — may recognize this dynamic. Seniority does not protect you from microaggressions. Sometimes, the higher you go, the more subtle and insidious they become."

#### Clip 3C: The Intern (2015, English)

**Scene:** Jules Ostin (played by Anne Hathaway), the founder and CEO of an online fashion company, is pressured by her investors and board to hire a "seasoned CEO" — implicitly a male CEO — above her. The pressure is framed as being "for the good of the company" and "to free you up to focus on the creative side," despite Jules having built the company from scratch. The microaggressions are embedded in professional language.

**Start marker:** The meeting or conversation where the investor/board member suggests hiring a CEO "with more experience."
**End marker:** Jules's reaction — anger, self-doubt, and the internal conflict of being told she is not enough for the company she built — approximately 3--4 minutes.

**Setup Script:**

> "This is from The Intern. Jules is the founder and CEO of a successful e-commerce company — she built it from nothing. But her investors have a suggestion. Watch what that suggestion is and how it is framed."

**Discussion Facilitation Guide:**
- After the clip, ask: "What microaggressions are embedded in the professional language?"
- Expected responses: "The suggestion to hire a 'seasoned CEO' implies she is not seasoned enough." "Framing it as being 'for the good of the company' invalidates her leadership." "'Freeing you up for the creative side' is a microinvalidation — it reduces her role from CEO to creative director without saying so directly." "The whole conversation is a microinsult dressed in corporate language."
- Key discussion point: "This is what workplace microaggressions often look like at senior levels: they are wrapped in professional language. 'We need someone more experienced.' 'Maybe you should focus on your strengths.' 'This role needs a different kind of leadership.' They sound reasonable. But the subtext is: you are not enough. And the 'different kind of leadership' they are looking for usually looks like a man."

**Handling Emotional Reactions:**
- Participants who have been pushed aside or had their authority questioned may find this clip triggering. Acknowledge: "If this feels familiar, you are not alone."

**Backup Plan (if clip does not work):**

> "In The Intern, Jules Ostin is the founder and CEO of a successful online fashion company. She conceived the idea, built the product, grew the team, and scaled the business. It is her company. But her investors are concerned about growth, and they have a suggestion: hire a 'seasoned CEO' to run the company while Jules focuses on 'the creative side.' The suggestion is wrapped in the most professional, well-meaning language. 'It is for the good of the company.' 'You would be freed up to do what you do best.' 'We need someone with more operational experience.' But strip away the corporate language and the message is: you are not enough. You built this, but you cannot run it. And the kind of person they envision as more suitable? A man. They never say it. They do not have to. The microaggression is in the assumption — that a woman who built a company from nothing still needs a man to run it properly."

### Slot 4 — Standing Up / Responding

**Use in:** Module 5, Slide 50
**Purpose:** Show examples of people responding to microaggressions or standing up against injustice — modelling the 3D framework in action.

---

#### Clip 4A: Thappad (2020, Hindi)

**Scene:** Amrita (played by Taapsee Pannu) calmly and firmly refuses to accept disrespect after her husband slaps her at a party. The specific scene is not the slap itself, but the aftermath — Amrita's quiet, composed conversation where she states her boundary. She does not scream, she does not beg — she simply says, "Yeh galat tha" (This was wrong), and refuses to normalize it.

**Start marker:** The conversation scene where Amrita tells her husband or a family member that she will not accept what happened.
**End marker:** The moment where her calm refusal shocks the person she is speaking to — approximately 3--4 minutes.

**Setup Script:**

> "We are going to watch a scene from Thappad. Amrita's husband slaps her at a party. What I want you to watch is not the slap — it is what happens after. Pay attention to HOW she responds. Her tone, her words, her composure."

**Discussion Facilitation Guide:**
- After the clip, ask: "Which D did Amrita use?"
- Expected responses: "Direct. She named what happened and stated her boundary."
- Key discussion point: "What gave Amrita the ability to respond this way? Self-awareness — she KNEW this was wrong, even when everyone around her tried to minimize it. Self-management — she stayed calm and composed, which made her message more powerful than any emotional outburst would have been. And conviction — she refused to be talked out of her own experience."
- Bridge to EI: "This is emotional intelligence in action. She felt the full weight of hurt, anger, and betrayal — but she channelled those emotions into a clear, assertive response. She regulated, she chose her words, and she stood firm."

**Handling Emotional Reactions:**
- This clip may be triggering for participants who have experienced domestic violence or relationship abuse. Before playing, add a brief content note: "This scene deals with a moment of disrespect in a marriage. If it feels too close to home, you are welcome to step out — there is no judgment."
- If someone becomes visibly upset, do not draw attention. Catch their eye and nod gently. After the discussion, check in privately during the next break.

**Backup Plan (if clip does not work):**

> "In Thappad, Amrita is a woman whose husband slaps her during a party — in front of guests, impulsively, during an argument. Everyone around her — his family, her family, friends — tells her to move on. 'It was just one slap.' 'He was stressed.' 'It will not happen again.' But Amrita refuses to minimize it. In a quiet conversation — not a confrontation, not a shouting match — she says: 'Yeh galat tha. This was wrong.' She does not scream. She does not beg. She states a fact and a boundary. What makes her response so powerful is its composure. She is not reacting — she is responding. She has felt the pain, processed it, and chosen her words deliberately. That is Direct — the first D — at its most powerful. And it is underpinned by emotional intelligence: the self-awareness to know her own values, and the self-management to communicate them clearly."

#### Clip 4B: Hidden Figures (2016, English)

**Scene:** Katherine Johnson finally confronts her boss, Al Harrison (played by Kevin Costner), about the daily humiliation of running across campus to use the bathroom. She states the facts — clearly, passionately, without apology. Harrison responds by tearing down the "colored" bathroom sign. The scene also works because it shows an ally (Harrison) who, once aware, takes immediate visible action.

**Start marker:** Katherine's outburst/confrontation in the office, standing in her wet clothes.
**End marker:** Harrison walking to the bathroom and tearing down the sign — approximately 3--4 minutes.

**Setup Script:**

> "We return to Hidden Figures. Remember the bathroom scene from earlier? Katherine has been running half a mile every day. In this scene, she has had enough. Watch her response — and watch what her boss does."

**Discussion Facilitation Guide:**
- After the clip, ask: "Which Ds did you see? Katherine's response AND Harrison's response."
- Expected responses: "Katherine used Direct — she stated the facts about her experience." "Harrison used a combination — he could not undo the system immediately, but he took visible, symbolic action by tearing down the sign."
- Key discussion point: "Two things to notice. First, Katherine's response: she did not just say 'the bathroom is far.' She stated FACTS — the distance, the time it takes, the impact on her work. Facts are powerful because they are hard to dismiss. Second, Harrison's response as an ally: once he understood the problem, he did not ask a committee or wait for approval. He walked down the hall and tore the sign down. That is what upstander action looks like when you have power."
- Bridge to bystander role: "Harrison was a bystander who became an upstander. And he could do it because he had power. When you have power — positional, relational, institutional — your upstander action carries more weight."

**Handling Emotional Reactions:**
- This clip often generates both anger (at the injustice) and hope (at the response). Let both emotions coexist.

**Backup Plan (if clip does not work):**

> "Katherine Johnson has been running half a mile every day to use the 'colored' bathroom — in heels, sometimes in rain, losing 40 minutes of work time. One day, she returns to the office soaking wet, and her boss, Al Harrison, asks where she has been. Katherine has had enough. She does not whisper. She does not apologize. She states facts: 'There is no bathroom for me here. I have to walk half a mile to use the bathroom. By the time I get back, I have been gone 40 minutes. And I have the exact same bladder as everyone else in this building.' Harrison is stunned. He had no idea. He walks down the hall, picks up a crowbar, and tears the 'colored' bathroom sign off the wall. In front of everyone. This scene shows two things: the power of stating facts directly — Katherine used the Direct approach — and the power of an ally with authority taking immediate, visible action. Harrison did not form a committee. He did not 'look into it.' He acted."

#### Clip 4C: Nil Battey Sannata (2015, Hindi)

**Scene:** Chanda (played by Swara Bhaskar), a domestic worker and single mother, decides to enroll in her teenage daughter's school to complete her own education. **Handling Emotional Reactions:**

**Backup Plan (if clip does not work):**

Chanda enrolls in her daughter's school to complete her own education. On her first day, she walks into a classroom full of teenagers. They stare. They snicker. A grown woman, sitting at a desk meant for a 14-year-old, with a brand-new notebook and a pencil. The teacher is skeptical. The students are amused. But Chanda sits down. She opens her notebook. And she begins to learn. She does not give a speech. She does not argue. Her presence — steady, dignified, quiet — IS the response. To every stereotype that says domestic workers cannot learn, cannot aspire, cannot grow: Chanda's answer is to sit down and pick up a pencil."

## Energizer Activities

Select one of the following three options for the post-lunch energizer (Slide 34). Choose based on room setup, group energy, and your facilitation style.

---

### Energizer Option 1: "Stand Up If..."

**Duration:** 5--7 minutes
**Setup:** Participants remain at their seats (or stand behind their chairs if space allows).
**Connection to program themes:** Builds awareness of shared experiences, introduces vulnerability in a low-stakes way.

**Instructions:**

> "We are going to do a quick activity to wake up our bodies and our brains. Stay at your seat. I am going to read a series of statements. If the statement is true for you, stand up. Look around — see who is standing with you. Then sit back down."

Read the following statements, pausing for 3--5 seconds after each one to let participants look around:

1. "Stand up if you had to navigate a stereotype about your capability at some point in your career."
2. "Stand up if you have ever been the only woman in a room full of men during a meeting."
3. "Stand up if someone has ever explained something to you that you already knew — and you are an expert in that topic."
4. "Stand up if you have ever stayed silent when you wanted to speak up."
5. "Stand up if you have ever spoken up when it was uncomfortable to do so."
6. "Stand up if you have ever been told you are 'too emotional' at work."
7. "Stand up if you have supported another woman in the workplace this year — championed her idea, recommended her for a project, or backed her publicly."
8. "Stand up if you are ready to learn how to respond to microaggressions this afternoon."

> "Look around this room. You are not alone in any of these experiences. That is what today is about — building a community of awareness and action."

**Facilitator Notes:**
- Keep the pace brisk — this should feel energizing, not heavy
- Do not comment on individual responses — the point is the visual of collective experience
- End on a positive/forward-looking statement (#8) to build momentum into the afternoon

---

### Energizer Option 2: "Two Truths and a Stereotype"

**Duration:** 7--8 minutes
**Setup:** Table groups of 4--5.
**Connection to program themes:** Directly connects to stereotype awareness from Module 3.

**Instructions:**

> "Here is a twist on a classic icebreaker. In your table group, each person will share three statements about themselves. Two are true. One is a common stereotype about a group they belong to — but applied to them personally."
>
> "For example, I might say: (1) I ran a half marathon last year — true. (2) I cry during every movie — true. (3) Women are not good at math — stereotype, and by the way, I topped my class in statistics."
>
> "The group's job is to guess which one is the stereotype. Go."

*(Give 7 minutes. Circulate. Enjoy the laughter and the revelations.)*

> "What did you notice? The stereotypes were easy to spot when they were about someone you KNOW. That is the power of personal connection — it breaks stereotypes. When you know someone as a person, the generalizations fall apart."
