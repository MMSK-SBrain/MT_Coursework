### Bias Interruption Strategies

Three practical ways to interrupt bias in your decisions:

1. **Slow down decisions.** When you catch yourself making a quick judgement about someone, pause. Fast thinking is where bias lives.

2. **Seek disconfirming evidence.** Deliberately look for information that challenges your initial impression. Ask: "What am I not seeing?"

3. **Use structured criteria.** Evaluate people against clear, pre-defined criteria rather than gut feeling. Write your criteria before you evaluate.

## Module 3 — Stereotypes

### Stereotype Formation Cycle

A stereotype is an oversimplified, fixed belief about a group of people. Stereotypes are not always hostile — sometimes the most limiting ones sound like compliments.

```
    ┌──────────────┐
    │   EXPOSURE    │
    │  (encounter   │
    │  with a group)│
    └──────┬───────┘
           │
           ▼
    ┌──────────────┐
    │GENERALISATION │
    │ (one example  │
    │ = all members)│
    └──────┬───────┘
           │
           ▼
    ┌──────────────┐
    │REINFORCEMENT  │
    │ (we notice    │
    │ what confirms)│
    └──────┬───────┘
           │
           ▼
    ┌──────────────┐
    │   "TRUTH"     │
    │ (it feels like│
    │  a fact)      │
    └──────────────┘
```

### 4 Sources of Stereotypes

| Source | How It Works |
|---|---|
| **Family** | Messages we absorbed growing up about "how things are" |
| **Media** | Representations and narratives that shape our expectations |
| **Workplace culture** | Norms about who belongs in which roles and at which levels |
| **Self (internalised)** | Stereotypes we apply to our own identity — "People like me don't..." |

## Module 4 — Recognizing Microaggressions

Microaggressions are brief, everyday exchanges that communicate hostile, derogatory, or negative messages — often unintentionally. The word "micro" does not mean the impact is small. It means the actions are subtle, but they accumulate.

### Three Types of Microaggressions

**1. Microassaults**
Conscious, deliberate verbal or non-verbal attacks meant to hurt or exclude. These are the most overt form and the person committing them is usually aware of what they are doing.

## Module 5 — Responding & Intervening

### The 3D Framework

When you witness or experience a microaggression, you have three response options:

**Direct — Address it.**
Speak up in the moment. Name the behaviour clearly and respectfully.
Example: "That comment felt dismissive. Can we revisit what was said?"

**Distract — Redirect.**
Shift the conversation or situation to break the pattern without direct confrontation.
Example: "Hey, can we get back to the agenda?"

**Delegate — Escalate.**
Report the behaviour to someone in a position of authority, especially when there is a power imbalance or safety concern.
Example: Speaking to a manager, HR, or a trusted colleague after the incident.

### Choosing Your D

The right response depends on context. Consider these factors:

| Factor | Think About |
|---|---|
| **Power dynamic** | Is there a hierarchy between you and the person? |
| **Severity** | How harmful was the comment or action? |
| **Relationship** | Do you have an established relationship with this person? |
| **Safety** | Is it physically and psychologically safe to speak up right now? |

All three Ds are valid. There is no single right answer — context determines the best response.

## Module 6 — From Awareness to Inclusive Action

### The Three Pillars — Connected

Everything you have explored today forms one integrated capability:

```
                    EI
                   / \
                  /   \
                 /     \
                /       \
               /         \
    Bias      /           \    Inclusive
   Awareness ◄─────────────► Action
```

**EI** gives you the self-awareness to notice bias.
**Bias awareness** helps you spot microaggressions.
**Response skills** turn awareness into action.

---

### What Inclusive Spaces Look Like

Five markers of a truly inclusive workspace:

1. People feel safe to speak up without fear of ridicule or retaliation.
2. Differences are valued, not merely tolerated.
3. Feedback flows in all directions — up, down, and across.
4. Mistakes are treated as learning opportunities, not reasons for blame.
5. Everyone has equitable access to opportunities for growth and recognition.

These do not happen by accident. They are built by people who practise what you have been learning today.
