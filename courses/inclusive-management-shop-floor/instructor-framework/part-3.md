### SEGMENT 4.0 — CASE STUDY 1 DEBRIEF: "FIVE MINUTES"
**Time:** 1:00–1:15 | **Duration:** 15 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

Start at exactly 1:00. Don't wait for stragglers — they'll come in and see the room is already working.

> "Welcome back. I hope you had a good lunch. I also hope you were thinking about the case study on your chair — because we're starting right there."

> "Quick show of hands: how many of you think Rajan was wrong? ... How many of you think you might have done the same thing in his position?"

Both hands usually go up from the same people. That's your entry point.

> "Good. That tension — knowing it was wrong and knowing you might have done it anyway — is exactly where learning happens. Let's stay there for a few minutes."

> "One more thing before we start: Meera is 22 years old. First job after ITI. She is exactly the Gen Z employee we talked about before lunch — the one who won't stay silent the way previous generations did. That complaint didn't come from weakness. It came from a generation that knows it has options. Keep that in mind as you discuss."

Form groups of 3–4 (different from their morning groups if possible — vary the dynamics).

> "Five minutes to discuss questions 1 through 3 from the case study. Then each group is going to share the 'better response' they wrote for Rajan — just two or three sentences. Let's go."

Give 5 minutes for group discussion, then debrief.

**Debrief — key points to draw out:**

- **What Rajan got wrong:** Not the decision itself, but the absence of acknowledgment. He didn't say "I hear you." He problem-solved over her, not with her.
- **What Meera needed:** To be seen. Before any accommodation decision was made, she needed 15 seconds of human acknowledgment.
- **The 30-second principle:** A compassionate, professional response in that moment would have cost 30 seconds and prevented 3 lost workdays, an HR complaint, and a damaged relationship.

Read out the sample better response from the case study material:
*"Meera, go sit by the supervisor's desk for five minutes — I'll cover the station. Tell me when you're ready to come back."*

> "Sixteen words. That's it. No therapy. No special treatment. No production loss. Just basic human acknowledgment followed by a practical response. That's what we're building today."

**Bridge to Module 4:**

> "So how do you build the muscle for responses like that? That's what the next 75 minutes is about. We're going to get practical — greetings, listening, feedback, conflict. Real words, real situations, real practice."

### SEGMENT 4.1 — PROFESSIONAL GREETINGS & DAILY COURTESIES
**Time:** 1:15–1:30 | **Duration:** 15 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Let's talk about the most underrated management tool in existence: the good morning."

> "I mean that seriously. How you greet your team members when they arrive sets the tone for everything that follows. It signals: I see you. You matter. You're part of this team."

> "Now, here's a pattern I see consistently in manufacturing environments: male managers greet male team members with a word, a nod, a brief exchange. They may greet female team members with nothing at all — not out of disrespect, but out of uncertainty. They're not sure what's appropriate. So they do nothing. And 'nothing' is read as dismissal."

Show Slide 25.

> "The standard is simple: same greeting, same tone, same eye contact — regardless of gender. If you say 'good morning, Ramu' with a nod and a question about yesterday's target — do the same with Kavitha. Not different. Not overly formal. The same."

> "There are a few specific courtesies that have come up in your organization's feedback. I want to go through them plainly, because clarity matters more than comfort here."

Walk through these on Slide 26, one at a time — conversationally, not as a lecture:

**Offering a seat:**
> "If a woman on your team is standing in your office or at your station for more than two minutes — particularly for a long discussion — offer her a chair. This is basic courtesy. It's what you'd do for a senior colleague or a visitor. It is not special treatment. It is professional behavior."

> "During periods or other health discomfort, if a woman is visibly in pain and there is a stool or chair available, notice it and offer. You don't need to ask why. You just need to offer."

**Using appropriate forms of address:**
> "Use professional titles or first names consistently. 'Ma'am' or 'Madam' is appropriate if that's your workplace norm. What's not appropriate: nicknames, diminutives, or addressing female team members by their role while addressing male counterparts by name."

**Body language and proximity:**
> "In shop floor environments, conversations happen close together because of noise. Be aware of proximity — especially in one-on-one conversations. Keep a professional distance. Face the person. Don't look past them at the line while they're talking to you."

**Quick Activity — 2 minutes:**

> "Turn to the person next to you. One of you is the manager, one is a team member. Manager: give a professional morning greeting. Team member: give feedback — did it feel equal to what you'd receive as a male colleague? Swap. Go."

Debrief in 1 minute: "What's one thing you noticed?"

### SEGMENT 4.2 — ACTIVE LISTENING IN PRACTICE
**Time:** 1:30–1:45 | **Duration:** 15 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Active listening is probably the most-mentioned and least-practiced management skill in existence. Everyone knows what it is. Almost nobody does it consistently."

> "Let me tell you what it actually looks like in a shop floor context — because it's not the same as sitting in a quiet room nodding thoughtfully."

> "Active listening on the floor means: when someone comes to you with a problem, you stop what you're doing — even for 60 seconds — and you give them your physical attention. Eyes. Body. No phone. You listen to what they say, and then you reflect it back before you respond."

Show Slide 27.

> "Reflecting back doesn't mean repeating their words like a robot. It means demonstrating that you understood. For example: 'So if I'm hearing you right, the issue is that the workstation height is causing you back pain by the end of the shift — and you've mentioned it twice before but nothing changed?' That 'nothing changed' addition — that tells her you actually heard the whole message, not just the surface complaint."

**Pairs Exercise — 5 minutes:**

> "I want you to practice one specific skill: the reflection. Partner A will describe a real work frustration — anything from your floor, nothing personal. Partner B will listen, then reflect back in one or two sentences — not a solution, just a reflection. Then swap. Three minutes total, then we debrief."

Give 3 minutes, then:
> "Partner B: how easy or hard was it to resist jumping to a solution? ... That urge to solve — that's your management instinct working against your listening. We're going to slow that down today."

**The Three-Step Listen Model:**

> "When a woman on your team brings you a concern — or any team member, but we'll focus there — here's your three-step sequence."

Write on flipchart:
1. **PAUSE** — put down what you're doing, physically turn to face her
2. **HEAR IT WHOLE** — let her finish without interrupting or redirecting
3. **REFLECT BEFORE RESPONDING** — say back what you heard before you say what you'll do

> "That third step is the one that changes relationships. Because most people — particularly in environments where they've learned not to speak up — are not sure they've been understood until you prove it."

### SEGMENT 4.3 — GIVING FEEDBACK RESPECTFULLY
**Time:** 1:45–2:00 | **Duration:** 15 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Feedback is the most powerful tool a manager has — and the most commonly misused. Let's look at what good feedback actually looks like."

**Show Movie Clip — Chak De! India, Kabir Khan coaching scene (~3–4 minutes)**

After the clip:
> "What made that feedback land? Let me hear two or three observations."

You'll likely hear: "he was direct," "he wasn't personal," "he focused on the behavior not the person," "he was clear about what he wanted instead." Affirm each.

> "Those aren't just coaching instincts. They're a feedback framework. And it works the same way on your floor."

Show Slide 28. Walk through the SBI model — keep it conversational, not academic.

**The SBI Framework:**
- **Situation:** *When* did this happen? Be specific.
- **Behavior:** *What exactly* did you observe? Describe the action, not the character.
- **Impact:** *What was the result* — on the team, the output, the relationship?

> "Notice what's missing from that model: judgment. You're not saying 'you're careless' or 'you're not trying.' You're saying 'yesterday, during the third shift, the quality check on line B was skipped — and that resulted in a return batch from QC this morning.' That's SBI. Objective, specific, actionable."

**The Private Rule:**

> "One more thing that applies particularly to women on your team — though honestly it applies to everyone: feedback must be given in private. Not at the station, not in front of the team, not while walking past. Private. Seated if possible. This isn't about being soft. It's about being effective. Public correction triggers shame, not learning. Private correction triggers reflection."

> "Some of you will say: 'On the floor there's no time for private conversations.' I understand that. For immediate safety or quality corrections, you have to act fast. But for anything that goes beyond 'stop, do this instead' — find a moment, pull them aside, do it properly."

**Quick Check — 2 minutes:**

Give one scenario aloud:
> *"An operator made the same assembly error three times this week. You've noticed it. It's costing rework time. How would you open that feedback conversation using SBI?"*

Take 2–3 responses. Coach the language in real time: "Try removing the word 'always' — make it specific to this week..."

---

**SLIDE 28 — THE SBI FEEDBACK MODEL**
```
Title:    Feedback That Works: SBI
Three panels:
  S — SITUATION:  "During Monday's shift, on Line C..."
                  (specific time and place — not "you always")
  B — BEHAVIOR:   "I noticed the quality check was skipped at step 4..."
                  (observable action — not character or intention)
  I — IMPACT:     "...which resulted in 12 units being returned from QC."
                  (measurable result — not "it was bad")
The rule:  Private. Specific. Behavioral. Forward-looking.
Visual:    Clean three-panel diagram. SBI labels bold.
Note:      "Practice this framework until it becomes automatic.
            It works with men and women equally — but for women,
            the private rule is especially critical. A woman corrected
            publicly on a male-majority floor loses something
            that takes weeks to rebuild."
```

### SEGMENT 4.4 — ROLE-PLAY PRACTICE
**Time:** 2:00–2:25 | **Duration:** 25 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Time to practice. Role-play is uncomfortable — I know that. Engineers especially tend to find it awkward. But here's the truth: if you can't find the words in a safe room with colleagues, you definitely won't find them on a live floor under pressure. So we're going to be a little uncomfortable for 20 minutes and get better for it."

Divide into groups of 3: one person plays the manager, one plays the team member, one is the observer with a feedback checklist (from workbook Page 14).

> "We have three scenarios. Each scenario runs for 3 minutes. Then the observer gives 60 seconds of feedback. Then rotate. By the end, everyone has played all three roles."

**Scenario A — The Conflict Situation:**
*"Rekha, one of your operators, approaches you after the shift briefing looking visibly upset. She tells you that a male colleague — Suresh — made a sarcastic comment in front of the whole team when she asked a question: 'Madam, maybe this work is too technical for you.' Suresh is a senior operator and a strong performer. Rekha says, 'Sir, I don't want to make a big issue — but it keeps happening and I don't feel comfortable anymore.'"*
Manager goal: Acknowledge Rekha's concern fully before doing anything else. Commit to a specific next step. Then address Suresh separately and privately — behavior not character, SBI model.
Observer watches: Does the manager acknowledge before solving? Does he avoid dismissing it ("don't take it personally") or over-promising ("I'll sort him out")? Does he give Rekha a clear, specific next step?

**Facilitator note for Scenario A debrief:** *"Conflict handling follows the same principles as everything else today — acknowledge first, private correction, address the behavior not the person. The additional complexity here: you have two people to manage. Rekha needs to feel heard and protected. Suresh needs a clear, private correction that doesn't humiliate him in front of the team. Both conversations matter."*

**Scenario B — The Feedback Conversation:**
*"Sunita has been late to station twice this week by 5–7 minutes. It's affecting the team's start time. You need to address it. Use the SBI model. It's after the shift."*
Manager goal: Private, specific, behavioral feedback with a clear ask.
Observer watches: Does the feedback follow SBI? Is it private (role-played as a side office)? Is the tone professional, not punishing?

**Scenario C — Responding to a Concern:**
*"Anjali approaches you quietly and says she'd like to talk — the workstation she was moved to last week requires her to stretch significantly to reach the upper panel, and she's getting shoulder pain. She looks uncertain, like she's not sure she should be saying this."*
Manager goal: Acknowledge first. Listen whole. Reflect. Then propose next steps.
Observer watches: Does the manager use the three-step listen model? Does he acknowledge before solving?

After all three rotations, bring the room together:
> "Observers — what was the most common thing managers found hard to do? Not wrong — hard."

Common answers: sitting with silence, not jumping to solve, staying in SBI without going personal. Affirm each.

> "That difficulty you felt — that's the gap we're closing. These three scenarios — conflict, feedback, and responding to a concern — represent the situations that most commonly lead to complaints. You now have practice reps on all three."

## MODULE 5: HEALTH SENSITIVITY & WORKPLACE COURTESIES

> **Note before Module 5:**
> This is the most sensitive module of the day. For some participants, discussing menstrual health in a training setting will be genuinely uncomfortable. That discomfort is the point — because on the floor, that same discomfort leads to situations like Rajan's. Your tone here needs to be matter-of-fact, not clinical, and not apologetic. You are a behavioral scientist discussing a health reality. Treat it accordingly. The Pad Man clip will do most of the tonal work for you.

### SEGMENT 5.1 — UNDERSTANDING MENSTRUAL HEALTH
**Time:** 2:30–2:45 | **Duration:** 15 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "We're moving into Module 5. I want to start with a clip."

**Show Movie Clip — Pad Man, opening scenes (~3–4 minutes)**

After the clip, let 5 seconds pass before speaking.

> "Arunachalam's first reaction when he saw the problem wasn't embarrassment or discomfort. It was practical concern. 'This is a health issue. What can I do about it?' That's the frame we're going to work in for the next hour."

> "Menstrual health is a health issue. Not a personal issue. Not a private problem. A physiological reality that affects approximately 50% of your incoming workforce for a significant portion of their working lives."

> "Let me give you some basic facts — because I know many of you have had no reason to know these before today."

Show Slide 32. Walk through it conversationally, not clinically.

> "The menstrual cycle affects different women differently. Some women experience minimal discomfort. Others — and this is medically documented — experience cramps comparable in intensity to a mild heart attack. Fatigue. Nausea. Back pain. Headaches. And they come to work. Every time. Because the expectation is that they will."

> "What do most of them need on those days? Not a day off, in most cases. They need small adjustments: a chance to sit down for 5–10 minutes, access to a warm drink, proximity to the washroom, and — critically — a manager who doesn't make them feel like a problem for asking."

> "Karnataka introduced a menstrual leave policy in October 2025 — one paid day per month. Even where this isn't yet your organization's policy, your management behavior can make the difference between a woman who shows up and performs, and one who calls in sick for three days because she had no other option."

**Facilitator Note:** If someone asks "but won't women take advantage of this?" — respond directly:
> "That's a fair question. The evidence from companies that have implemented accommodation policies is that productivity increases and absenteeism decreases. Because when people feel seen, they don't need to create distance to protect themselves. Trust works both ways."

### SEGMENT 5.2 — APPROPRIATE ACCOMMODATIONS IN PRACTICE
**Time:** 2:45–3:00 | **Duration:** 15 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Now let's get practical. What does 'appropriate accommodation' actually look like on a shop floor — not in a policy document, but in a real interaction?"

> "Three categories. Each one has a default behavior and a better behavior."

Show Slide 33. Go through each category with a real scenario.

**Category 1 — Seating**

> "Default behavior: 'Everyone stands at their station. If she needs to sit, she should ask HR.'"

> "Better behavior: Have a stool or chair accessible near every station. When you notice someone is struggling, say simply: 'You can use the stool if you need it — let me know if you need anything else.' Then walk away. You've offered. You haven't made it a discussion."

**Category 2 — Washroom Access**

> "Default behavior: Ignore the fact that the nearest women's washroom is 7 minutes away and breaks are scheduled at fixed intervals."

> "Better behavior: Know where the nearest women's washroom is. If a woman on your team needs a bathroom break outside the scheduled slot on a clearly difficult day, allow it. Five minutes does not derail production. Denying it might."

> "The ask you're looking for from your team member is: 'Sir, can I step out for a few minutes?' Your answer is: 'Go ahead. Come back when you're ready.' That's it. No questions about why. No visible note-taking. No tracking that conversation differently from any other break."

**Category 3 — Privacy**

> "If a woman comes to you about a health-related need, the conversation happens somewhere you cannot be overheard. Not at the station, not near male colleagues. If your floor has no private space, step to the side of the room and lower your voice. This communicates: 'What you're telling me is confidential, and I will treat it that way.'"

**Scenario Discussion — 5 minutes:**

> "Here's a real situation. Think about your current floor setup and answer for yourself — you don't need to share."

Read aloud:
*"It's 11:20 AM. A woman operator on your line looks pale and is gripping her workstation. She has not said anything. Your shift target is at 80%. What do you do, step by step?"*

Give 2 minutes of individual thinking, then take 3 shares.

Expected range of answers: "ask if she's okay," "tell her to take a break," "call the nurse." Draw out the sequencing: notice → approach quietly → offer → respect her answer → act on it.

### SEGMENT 5.3 — RESPONDING TO SENSITIVE REQUESTS PROFESSIONALLY
**Time:** 3:00–3:15 | **Duration:** 15 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "The hardest part isn't knowing what to do. It's finding the words in the actual moment when someone comes to you."

> "So let's practice those words."

Show Slide 34. Walk through the response scripts together — not as scripts to memorize, but as patterns to internalize.

> "The pattern is the same every time: acknowledge the ask, respond with action or a clear next step, close with dignity. Short. Professional. No unnecessary commentary."

**Quick Response Practice — 7 minutes:**

In pairs (not groups of 3 this time — more intimate for this topic). One person reads the request, the other gives a professional response. Then switch.

Give pairs these three requests from workbook Page 16:

**Request 1:** *"Sir, I'm not feeling well today. Is it okay if I sit down for a bit?"*
Practice response pattern: Acknowledge + Offer + Close cleanly.
e.g. *"Of course — go use the chair near the QC station. Let me know when you're back."*

**Request 2:** *"Sir, I need to step out to the washroom — I can't wait for the 3 o'clock break."*
Practice response pattern: Simple permission + no interrogation.
e.g. *"Go ahead. I'll note it — come back when you're ready."*

**Request 3:** *"Sir, I wanted to mention — the workstation height has been causing me back pain. I've been managing, but it's getting worse."*
Practice response pattern: Acknowledge + reflect + action.
e.g. *"Thank you for telling me. That shouldn't be ongoing — let me check what adjustment is possible and I'll come back to you by end of shift."*

Debrief with the full group:
> "What felt awkward? ... Usually it's the silence after 'of course.' We want to fill it with explanation or reassurance. You don't need to. The clean close is the professional one."

### SEGMENT 5.4 — BUILDING TEAM NORMS TOGETHER
**Time:** 3:15–3:30 | **Duration:** 15 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "I want to end Module 5 with something constructive. We've talked a lot about what individual managers should do differently. But a manager who changes alone changes slowly. A team that agrees on shared norms — changes fast."

**Small Group Share — 5 minutes:**

## FACILITATOR TIPS — PART 3

**If role-plays stall and nobody wants to go first:**
Go first yourself. Play the manager badly — use a dismissive tone in Scenario A. Then ask: "What did I just do wrong?" Laughter breaks the ice and sets the standard.

**If someone becomes genuinely skilled in the role-play:**
Name it publicly: *"Did everyone notice what [name] just did there? He reflected before he responded. That's exactly it. Remember that."* Positive modelling accelerates the room.

**If the menstrual health segment creates visible discomfort (laughter, side comments):**
Don't ignore it. Address it directly and calmly:
*"I notice some discomfort in the room. That's fine — this is an unusual conversation for a training setting. But here's the thing: that discomfort is exactly what your team members navigate every time they need to ask you something. Today, we practice sitting with it."*

**If someone says "we already have HR for all this":**
*"HR handles escalations. You handle day-to-day reality. By the time something reaches HR, it's already cost you days, morale, and relationship capital. What we're building today keeps it from reaching HR in the first place."*

**On Module 4 role-plays and time:** Role-play almost always runs long when it's working well. If you need to compress, drop Scenario A (greetings) and keep B and C — they generate the most learning. Greetings can be reinforced verbally instead.
