### CASE STUDY 2: "THE REPORT"

**Setting:** Quality Inspection Zone, automotive components plant, North India. Thursday afternoon, 2:55 PM. End of second shift.

**The People:**
- **Vikram** — Group Leader (GL), 14 years' experience. Known as highly competent, demanding, results-driven. Has received performance awards. No previous complaints on record.
- **Anjali** — Quality Inspector, 26 years old, 14 months with the company. One of three women in the inspection zone. Technically strong — flagged as a high-potential employee in her last review.
- **Four other male operators** — present in the zone at the time of the incident.

**What Happened:**

At 2:55 PM, Vikram is reviewing the end-of-shift quality report. He notices that Anjali's inspection log is missing entries for two components from the 2:00 PM batch. He walks directly to her station. Four colleagues are within earshot.

He says loudly: *"Anjali — what is this? Two components not logged? What were you doing at 2 o'clock? This is basic work. Even a new joiner doesn't make this mistake. I don't know why I have to keep checking your work when I don't check anyone else's."*

Anjali says: *"Sir, those components were sent back for rework at 1:50 — I flagged it in the physical register but hadn't updated the log yet."*

Vikram responds: *"That's not a process. Update the log first, rework second. I shouldn't have to explain this to you."*

He walks away. The four male colleagues are quiet. Anjali finishes her shift without another word. The next morning, an email arrives in HR from Anjali, marked to the ICC. Subject line: *"Complaint — hostile and disrespectful treatment by Group Leader."*

Two days later, Vikram is called for a preliminary conversation by the ICC Chairperson.

---

**Discussion Questions (groups of 3–4, 10 minutes):**

1. List every specific behavior in Vikram's response that could form the basis of a complaint. Be precise — not "he was rude," but exactly what he said and how.
2. Vikram believes he did nothing wrong — he was addressing a real quality lapse. Is he right? Where does legitimate performance management end and misconduct begin?
3. Walk through the ICC process from Anjali's complaint. What happens next? Who is involved? What can the outcome be — for Anjali, for Vikram, for the organization?
4. If Vikram had used everything he learned today — SBI, private feedback, acknowledging first — rewrite the interaction in 3–5 sentences. Same concern, different approach.
5. What is the cost to Vikram's credibility, to team morale, and to organizational trust — even if the ICC rules in his favor?

**Facilitator Note:** Have the full case study debrief ready. The key teaching points are in the segments that follow.

### SEGMENT 6.1 — CASE STUDY 2: GROUP DISCUSSION
**Time:** 3:52–4:10 | **Duration:** 18 minutes (10 min groups + 8 min debrief)

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Form your groups of 3–4. Work through the five questions. You have 10 minutes. Focus especially on question 2 — that's where most of the useful tension is."

Circulate. Listen for groups that are debating question 2 (the line between performance management and misconduct) — this is the richest discussion and worth drawing out in the debrief.

At 4:02, bring the room back.

**Debrief — key points to surface (8 minutes):**

**On Question 1 — What Vikram did wrong:**

Take responses, then add anything missed:

- Public location — four witnesses. In complaints, witnesses matter.
- Comparative statement: *"I don't check anyone else's work"* — this singles her out on the basis of perceived incompetence, which can constitute discriminatory treatment.
- Dismissive of her explanation: *"That's not a process"* — she gave a valid reason; he shut it down without investigating.
- Tone and volume — the word "loudly" in the case study is deliberate. Volume in front of colleagues is a power move, not a management tool.
- The generalization: *"Even a new joiner doesn't make this mistake"* — not SBI. That's a character attack dressed as a quality concern.

> "Anjali had an answer. Vikram didn't hear it. That's not a quality conversation — that's a performance. And the audience was standing three feet away."

**On Question 2 — Where the line is:**

> "Vikram had a legitimate concern. The log was incomplete. That needed to be addressed. His failure was not the concern — it was the method. Performance management becomes misconduct not when you address poor performance, but when you address it in a way that demeans, humiliates, or singles someone out on grounds that go beyond the work itself."

> "The test is simple: Would you have addressed a male colleague the same way, with the same words, in the same location, with the same audience? If the answer is no — you've crossed a line."

**On Question 5 — The cost even if he's cleared:**

> "Here's something that often gets missed: even if the ICC rules in Vikram's favor — even if it finds insufficient grounds for formal action — the complaint is on record. The team witnessed it. Anjali is still on his floor. And trust, once cracked, doesn't seal overnight."

> "The complaints that reach HR are not the problem. They're the symptom. The problem is the pattern of behavior that makes a high-potential employee feel that filing a formal complaint is her only option."

### SEGMENT 6.2 — GRIEVANCE REDRESSAL: WHAT ACTUALLY HAPPENS
**Time:** 4:10–4:25 | **Duration:** 15 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Let's talk about what happens after an email like Anjali's lands in HR. Because a lot of managers have a vague idea about the ICC — but not a clear picture of what the process actually looks like, or what it means for them."

**Show Movie Clip — Pink, closing courtroom argument (~3–4 minutes)**

After the clip, let it breathe for a few seconds.

> "That speech is not about a dramatic courtroom case. It's about the principle that is being applied every time an ICC opens a file. The principle that a woman's statement of discomfort — her 'no,' her complaint, her email — is taken seriously. Regardless of the manager's track record. Regardless of his intentions."

Show Slide 39. Walk through the PoSH process clearly but conversationally.

> "Here is what happens when a complaint is filed."

**The ICC Process — step by step:**

> "One: The complaint is received. The ICC Chairperson acknowledges it within 7 days. The respondent — that's you, if you're named — is notified."

> "Two: Conciliation may be offered first, if the complainant agrees. Many cases are resolved here. But if the complainant wants an inquiry, it proceeds."

> "Three: A formal inquiry is conducted. Both parties give statements. Witnesses are interviewed. Documents — shift logs, emails, physical registers — are reviewed. This can take up to 90 days."

> "Four: The ICC submits a report with findings and recommendations. If misconduct is found, penalties range from a written warning to termination, depending on severity. In some cases, the matter is referred to law enforcement."

> "Five: Non-compliance by the organization itself carries separate penalties: up to ₹50,000 for first offense. License suspension for repeat offenses. And now — as of a Supreme Court directive — organizations face compliance surveys with judicial oversight."

> "Here's what I want you to take from this: the process is real, it has teeth, and it protects the complainant's confidentiality. You will not know who gave statements against you until the inquiry is complete. You will not be able to manage around it."

> "The only thing that protects you is not needing to be there."

Pause.

> "And the only thing that keeps you out of an ICC inquiry is the behavior we've been practicing all day."

## FACILITATOR TIPS — PART 4

**If the ICC segment causes visible anxiety or defensiveness:**
> "I understand this feels heavy. And I want to be clear: this session exists precisely so that none of you ever have to sit in front of an ICC. You now have the knowledge and the skills. What you do with them is yours."

**If someone pushes back on the Pink clip ("this isn't relevant to our situation"):**
> "You're right that this is a courtroom, not a shop floor. But the principle Deepak Sehgal articulates — that a woman's stated discomfort is sufficient grounds for a formal inquiry — is exactly the principle your ICC operates under. The film makes it vivid. The law makes it real."

**If the Commitment Circle breaks down into jokes or deflection:**
Don't force it. Say: *"If the sentence doesn't come naturally right now, sit with it. Your action plan has it written. That counts."* Move on. Don't let one person derail the energy.

**If the day runs long and Module 7 gets compressed:**
Protect the action plan time above all else. Compress the commitment circle to a written commitment (signed, dated, shared with one colleague) rather than spoken. But do not skip the action plan — it is the bridge between the training and the floor.

**On the "Sattar Minute" clip — if the internet is unreliable:**
Have it downloaded ahead of time. This clip carries the emotional weight of Module 7's opening. If you can't play it, quote the dialogue directly and explain the context. It still works, but the visual is better.

**On the closing:** Don't thank HR, don't list program objectives again, don't check a box. Speak to them as people. The last two minutes of the day are what they'll remember when they get to their floor on Monday morning.

## 30-DAY REVIEW — FACILITATOR PREPARATION GUIDE

The 30-Day Group Online Review is not a training session — it is an accountability and support conversation. Prepare for it as follows:

**Format:** Online group call, 60–90 minutes. All participants together.

**Opening (10 min):** Ask each person to share one change they made and what the response was. Not outcomes — behaviors.

**Discussion (40 min):** Surface recurring challenges. Common ones:
- "I tried the private feedback but there was nowhere private to go" — operational barrier, escalate to plant management with a specific ask
- "She seemed uncomfortable when I tried to greet her — I didn't know what to do" — likely a response to unfamiliar behavior from her manager; normalize it, coach persistence
- "I didn't have a situation that came up" — probe gently: "Has nothing changed, or have you been avoiding the situations?" Both are real.

**Individual check-in (20 min):** Optional breakout for anyone who wants a private conversation. Flag any situation that sounds like it may need ICC attention — and refer appropriately, don't handle it in the group.

**Closing (10 min):** Each person names one commitment for the next 30 days — building on what they started.
