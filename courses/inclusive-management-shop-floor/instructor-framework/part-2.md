### SEGMENT 3.1 — THE NEUROSCIENCE OF EMPATHY
**Time:** 11:20–11:35 | **Duration:** 15 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Let's start with some science — because I know this room responds to science."

> "Your brain contains neurons called mirror neurons. When you watch someone stub their toe, something in your brain fires as if you stubbed your own toe. When you watch someone smile, your face microexpresses a smile before you're even aware of it. This is not psychology — this is neurobiology. Empathy is literally wired into us."

> "So if empathy is wired in, why does it fail? Why do smart, competent managers who genuinely care about their teams still receive complaints about insensitivity?"

Pause. Let the question sit.

> "Three reasons. First: we're busy. The floor is loud, targets are real, timelines are tight. Empathy requires a moment of stillness that production pressure crowds out. Second: we empathize more easily with people who are like us. Remember affinity bias from this morning? It applies to empathy too. Third: we've been trained — consciously or not — that emotions are not professional. Especially in manufacturing. 'Leave your problems at the gate.' That's a real thing that gets said."

Show Slide 16.

> "But here's what the neuroscience also tells us: empathy can be practiced. It's not fixed. The more you deliberately take someone else's perspective, the more your brain builds those neural pathways. This is literally a trainable skill."

**Show Movie Clip — Inside Out, Joy and Sadness scene (~3 minutes)**

After the clip, don't debrief immediately. Move straight into the next segment — let the image of Joy trying to fix sadness sit in the room as you introduce empathy vs. sympathy.

### SEGMENT 3.2 — EMPATHY VS. SYMPATHY
**Time:** 11:35–11:45 | **Duration:** 10 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "I want to make a distinction that sounds small but changes everything in practice."

> "Sympathy says: 'I feel sorry for you.' It keeps you on the outside, looking in. It's the manager who hears that a woman on his team is having a hard day and says 'Okay, okay, it'll be fine, get back to work.' He meant well. He acknowledged it. But she felt more alone after that conversation than before."

> "Empathy says: 'I can imagine what that feels like, and I'm with you in it.' It doesn't mean you need to cry with her. It doesn't mean you become her therapist. It means you stay present with the reality of her experience for long enough to actually hear it."

Write on the flipchart:
**SYMPATHY = I feel for you** (arrow pointing down from above)
**EMPATHY = I feel with you** (arrow pointing across, at the same level)

> "Notice the difference in posture. Sympathy positions you above — you're the one who's okay, looking at someone who isn't. Empathy puts you at the same level. You're not fixing. You're understanding."

> "And here's the management application: when a woman on your floor comes to you with a problem — a workload issue, a health need, an interpersonal conflict — your job in that first moment is not to solve it. Your job is to hear it. Completely. That's empathy. The solution comes second."

**Discussion — 4 minutes:**

> "Think about the last time someone really listened to you — not to respond, but to understand. What did that feel like? And think about the last time you felt dismissed. What was the difference in how the other person was physically present?"

Take 3–4 shares. You're listening for the word "presence" — being fully there, not distracted, not rushing to solve.

### SEGMENT 3.3 — UNDERSTANDING HER EXPERIENCE: THE SHOP FLOOR THROUGH HER EYES
**Time:** 11:45–12:00 | **Duration:** 15 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Now I want to do something a bit unusual. I'm going to ask you to leave your own head completely."

**Show Movie Clip — English Vinglish, opening scenes (~4 minutes)**

Let it play. Don't comment during. After it ends, give 10 full seconds of silence before speaking.

> "What did you notice?"

Take 2–3 responses. You'll likely hear: "she looked sad," "her family doesn't respect her," "she's competent but invisible." Affirm each one.

> "Now — here's the exercise. I want you to imagine Kavitha. She's one of your operators. She's been on your line for two years. She's technically skilled. But she joined a team of 18 men, she's one of three women on the floor, and the physical environment — tools, workstations, PPE — was designed for the average male body. She arrives 20 minutes early every morning because she needs to find PPE that fits. The closest women's washroom is a 7-minute walk. Twice this month, a male colleague made a comment about her 'taking too long' for a break."

> "Open your workbooks to Page 10. I want you to answer four questions — from Kavitha's perspective. Not yours. Hers."

**Workbook Exercise — 7 minutes:**

1. *What does Kavitha feel when she arrives at the plant gate each morning?*
2. *What does she wish her supervisor understood about her day that he probably doesn't know?*
3. *What would make her feel genuinely valued and included on this team?*
4. *What small thing — one thing — could her manager do this week that would change her experience?*

After 5–6 minutes, bring the room back.

> "I'm not going to ask you what Kavitha thinks. I'm going to ask: what surprised you — in your own answers?"

Take 3–4 shares. This is where the room opens up. Affirm without commentary. Write key themes on the flipchart: *PPE*, *physical space*, *washroom access*, *being watched*, *comments*, *being taken seriously.*

> "You just did empathy mapping. What you wrote there — that's data. That's information your floor is generating every day. Most of it never reaches you because she's learned not to say it."

### SEGMENT 3.4 — EMPATHY MAPPING EXERCISE
**Time:** 12:00–12:10 | **Duration:** 10 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "I want to give you a tool you can actually use back on your floor. It's called an Empathy Map — and it takes about five minutes."

> "An empathy map has four quadrants: what she **thinks**, what she **feels**, what she **says**, and what she **does**. The gap between what she feels and what she says — that's the gap you need to close as a manager. Because what she says is filtered. What she feels is real."

Instruct participants to use Page 11 of the workbook.

> "Think of one specific woman on your team — someone you manage directly. Don't write her name. Just picture her. Now fill in the four quadrants based on what you know — and what you're realizing you might not know."

Give 5 minutes. Then in pairs:

> "Share one thing from the 'Feels' quadrant that surprised you — something you weren't sure about before this exercise."

Take 2 shares with the full group.

> "Here's the uncomfortable truth: for most of the quadrants in that map, you were guessing. That's normal. You can't know what someone feels unless you create the conditions for them to tell you. That's what inclusive management is — creating those conditions, consistently."

## LUNCH BREAK — 12:15 to 1:00 PM | 45 minutes

**Facilitator Notes for Lunch:**

This break is more important than it looks. The morning has been emotionally intense for some participants — possibly more than they expected. Here's how to use it:

**Circulate, don't retreat.** Move through the lunch area. Don't eat separately or stay behind with your laptop. Be present. Light conversation. If someone wants to continue talking about the content, let them — but don't push. If someone is processing quietly, give them space.

**Watch for these signals:**
- Someone who looks shaken or unusually quiet — check in gently, privately. The content sometimes surfaces real experiences.
- Someone who is animatedly telling colleagues what they learned — this is your champion for the afternoon. Note them.
- Someone who is dismissive or making jokes about the morning — don't challenge it. They're managing discomfort. The afternoon's practical work will bring them back.

**Reset the room during lunch:**
- Move to the next section of slides
- Place Case Study 1 materials on each chair (participants will find them when they return)
- Write on the flipchart: "Case Study 1 — We'll discuss at 1:30"
- This creates anticipation without pressure

### CASE STUDY 1: "FIVE MINUTES"

**Setting:** Automotive component manufacturing plant, South India. Wednesday morning, 10:40 AM. Assembly Line B.

**The People:**
- **Rajan** — Group Leader, 11 years' experience. Known as fair but brisk. Runs a tight line.
- **Meera** — Operator, 22 years old, 8 months on the job. First job after ITI certification. One of two women on Line B.

**The Situation:**

Meera approaches Rajan quietly at his workstation. She looks pale. She speaks in a low voice: *"Sir, I'm not feeling well. Can I sit down for five minutes? I'll make up the time."*

Rajan is in the middle of reviewing a quality report. There's a micro-stoppage on another machine that he's tracking. He looks up briefly and says: *"Meera, we're 40 units behind. Everyone needs to be at their station. Can you push through for another hour? Take your regular break at 11."*

Meera nods and walks back to her station. She doesn't take a break at 11. She stays quiet for the rest of the shift. The next morning, she calls in sick. Three days later, her team lead receives a written complaint through HR.

---

**Discussion Questions (participants discuss in small groups — 10 minutes, then debrief):**

1. What did Rajan get wrong? What, if anything, did he get right?
2. What did Meera need in that moment — beyond just sitting down?
3. If Rajan had understood the Gen Z context, the health sensitivity context, and the empathy principles from this morning — what might he have said instead?
4. Write Rajan's response — the one that handles both the production pressure AND Meera's need with dignity. Keep it to 2–3 sentences.
5. What would have happened to the complaint if Rajan had responded differently in that 30-second interaction?

**Facilitator Note for Case Study Debrief:**
The point is not that Rajan is a bad manager. He's a realistic manager — under pressure, trying to hold output. The learning is: a 30-second response, done right, would have cost nothing and prevented everything that followed. That's the ROI of empathy in practice.

A sample "better response" for Rajan:
*"Meera, go sit by the supervisor's desk for five minutes — I'll cover the station. Tell me when you're ready to come back."*

That's 16 words. That's empathy in action.

## FACILITATOR TIPS — PART 2

**If the room gets emotional during the perspective exercise:**
Don't redirect. Hold the space. Say: *"That's exactly what this exercise is for. Stay with that for a moment."* If someone gets visibly upset, offer a quiet check-in during the break.

**If someone says "but we treat everyone the same":**
*"Treating everyone the same sounds fair. But equal treatment of unequal situations produces unequal outcomes. Kavitha has a 7-minute walk to the washroom. Her male colleague has 30 seconds. Same policy. Very different experience. That's the gap we're closing."*

**If someone challenges the Gen Z framing ("they're just entitled"):**
*"That's a common reaction. What I'd offer is: their expectations are higher — not unreasonable, higher. And the plants that meet those expectations retain talent. The ones that don't, lose it — and file complaints. Which outcome do you prefer?"*

**If the Empathy Map exercise feels superficial to some:**
*"You're right — five minutes isn't enough to deeply know someone's experience. The map isn't about having all the answers. It's about making you ask the questions. The value is in the habit, not the output."*

**On timing:** The perspective activity (11:45–12:00) is the most important segment of Part 2. If you're running short, compress the empathy vs. sympathy discussion — but protect the perspective exercise.
