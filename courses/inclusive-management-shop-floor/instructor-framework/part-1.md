Say something like:

> "Today is going to be a little different from the technical training you're used to. We're not going to talk about processes or systems. Do a quick go-around — first name and how many years they've been managing. Keep it light. This tells you your room composition (you'll have everything from 2-year supervisors to 25-year veterans) and gets everyone talking early.

**Facilitator Note:** If anyone looks visibly uncomfortable or arms-crossed, make gentle eye contact, smile, and don't call them out. They'll warm up. Your credibility as a female behavioral scientist is itself a gentle disruption — let it work quietly.

### SEGMENT 1.2 — GROUND RULES & DAY OVERVIEW
**Time:** 9:10–9:20 | **Duration:** 10 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Before we get going, I want to spend a few minutes setting up how we'll work today. These aren't rules imposed on you — I'd like us to build them together."

Ask the group: *"What would make this a safe space for an honest conversation today?"*

Take 3–4 answers on a flipchart. You'll almost always get: confidentiality, no judgment, phones away, respect. Add anything missing:

- **What's said here, stays here.** We'll discuss patterns and situations — never individuals or specific incidents by name.
- **Phones on silent, screens down** — unless we're using them for an activity.
- **All questions are valid.** There are no stupid questions, especially on a topic this new for most of you.
- **Disagree respectfully.** If something doesn't land for you, say so. I'd rather know than lose you.
- **Participation over perfection.** You don't need to have the right answer. You just need to show up and think.

Then walk them through the day's map.

### SEGMENT 1.3 — THE BUSINESS CASE
**Time:** 9:20–9:40 | **Duration:** 20 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Let me start with something I know you all understand: numbers."

This is your anchor for the engineering mindset in the room. Lead with data before you lead with empathy.

> "Your plant is moving toward 40% women on the shop floor. That's not a distant future — that's your next two to three production cycles. The question isn't whether you'll be managing women. You already are. The question is: how ready are you to do it well?"

Show Slide 5.

> "I want you to think about this for a moment: what does it cost when a good employee leaves? Recruitment, onboarding, knowledge loss, team disruption. Estimates put it at anywhere from 6 to 9 months of that person's salary. Now multiply that by even two or three women per shift who leave because the environment felt hostile."

> "And beyond the money — there are legal implications. The Prevention, Protection and Redressal of Sexual Harassment at the Workplace Act — PoSH — is not optional. Non-compliance means criminal liability for the organization and for you as a manager."

Pause here.

> "I'm not telling you this to scare you. I'm telling you because the business case for respectful management is overwhelming. It's not a soft skills nicety. It's an operational necessity."

**Show Movie Clip — Hidden Figures Bathroom Scene (if using)**

After the clip, ask:
> "What did Al Harrison not know? And what changed when he found out?"

Take 2–3 responses. Key insight to draw out: **Awareness + Authority + Action = Change.** You are all in that position today.

### SEGMENT 1.4 — LEGAL FRAMEWORK
**Time:** 9:40–9:55 | **Duration:** 15 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "I want to spend a few minutes on the legal landscape — not to give you a law lecture, but to make sure you're clear on what you're responsible for as a manager."

> "The Prevention, Protection, and Redressal of Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace Act, 2013 — commonly called PoSH — applies to every organization with ten or more employees. Your plant is covered. You are covered."

> "As a manager or supervisor, you are not just responsible for your own behavior. You are responsible for the environment your team operates in. If someone on your floor harasses a woman employee and you knew — or should have known — and did nothing, you carry liability."

> "And I want to introduce something slightly broader today: the concept of a Hostile Work Environment. It doesn't require physical contact. Consistent rudeness, dismissiveness, denial of basic courtesies, exclusion from decisions — these can all constitute a hostile work environment under Indian labor law."

> "This isn't about ticking compliance boxes. It's about understanding that your team members have rights — and when those rights are violated, there are consequences."

Show Slide 7. Keep this conversational — don't read from the slide.

**Facilitator Note:** Some participants may ask specific legal questions. Answer what you know and say clearly: *"For specific compliance queries, your HR team and your Internal Complaints Committee are the right resource. What I want you to take from today is the principle — not just the law."*

### SEGMENT 2.1 — WHAT IS UNCONSCIOUS BIAS?
**Time:** 10:15–10:35 | **Duration:** 20 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Welcome back. I'm going to ask you to do something engineers rarely get to do: watch a cartoon at work."

**Show Movie Clip — Zootopia, Judy Hopps meter maid scene (~3 minutes)**

After the clip:

> "Quick question — what was the bias? Who held it? Who was harmed? And here's the critical one: did anyone intend harm?"

Take 3–4 responses. What you're listening for: the recognition that **intent and impact are two different things.** Most people will say "no one intended harm" — and that's the perfect doorway to the neuroscience of unconscious bias.

> "That's the thing about unconscious bias. By definition, you don't know you have it. It doesn't mean you're a bad person. It means you're a person — with a brain that's been sorting and categorizing information your entire life."

Show Slide 10.

> "Your brain processes 11 million bits of information every second. Your conscious mind handles about 40. The rest — the 10,999,960 bits — are processed below your awareness. Your brain takes shortcuts. It has to. And those shortcuts are built from every experience, every message, every movie, every assumption you've absorbed since childhood."

> "In a manufacturing environment, most of you were trained by, worked alongside, and were managed by — who? Mostly men. Your brain built a pattern: manager = male. Engineer = male. Leader = male. That's not your fault. But it is your responsibility."

### SEGMENT 2.2 — SOCIAL INTELLIGENCE FRAMEWORK
**Time:** 10:35–10:55 | **Duration:** 20 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "Now let's talk about something more actionable: Social Intelligence. This is the ability to read a room, read a person, and respond in a way that builds rather than breaks the relationship."

> "Daniel Goleman — whose work we'll reference throughout today — describes Social Intelligence as having two core dimensions: Social Awareness and Social Facility. I'll translate that into shop floor language."

Show Slide 12.

> "Social Awareness is noticing. Noticing that someone looks uncomfortable. Noticing that a woman on your floor hasn't spoken in a team meeting in two weeks. Noticing that your tone changes when you're stressed, and the impact that has."

> "Social Facility is responding. Responding appropriately when you notice. Not overreacting, not ignoring — responding with proportion and professionalism."

> "In a manufacturing context, you already use both. When you walk the line and something looks off with a machine, you notice and you act. Today we're training that same instinct for people."

**Activity — The Observation Exercise (5 minutes)**

> "I'm going to describe three short workplace moments. For each one, I want you to write one thing you notice and one thing you would do. Use the box on page 6 of your workbook."

Read slowly:

**Moment 1:** *"It's 10 AM. Kavitha, one of your operators, has been standing at her station for 4 hours. She looks pale and is holding her stomach. She hasn't asked for anything."*

**Moment 2:** *"You're about to give feedback to Priya, a team member who made a quality error. Three other operators are standing nearby."*

**Moment 3:** *"In a team briefing, you ask for ideas on a workflow problem. Two men speak immediately. Deepa, who usually has strong ideas, says nothing."*

After 3 minutes of individual writing, ask:
> "What did you notice? What would you do?"

Take 2–3 responses per scenario. Don't give the "right" answer yet — that comes in Module 4. Right now you're surfacing their current instincts.

### SEGMENT 2.3 — MY TRIGGERS — REFLECTIVE EXERCISE
**Time:** 10:55–11:15 | **Duration:** 20 minutes

**Facilitator Script:**

> "We're almost at the end of Part 1, and I want to end with something that takes some courage. We're going to look at your triggers."

> "A trigger is any situation that causes an automatic emotional reaction in you — before your conscious mind has a chance to respond. On the shop floor, triggers might look like: an operator who keeps making the same mistake, a complaint that feels unfair, a team member who pushes back on your instructions."

> "When you're triggered, your prefrontal cortex — the part that handles empathy and sound judgment — goes partially offline. You react from a more primitive part of your brain. And that's when most of the interactions that lead to complaints happen."

> "This isn't weakness. This is biology. But it's biology you can work with."

**Activity — My Trigger Map (10 minutes)**

Instruct participants to open workbook to Page 8.

> "I want you to fill in four boxes. Take your time with this — it's personal."

Box 1: *A situation at work that regularly frustrates me* (be specific)
Box 2: *How I typically react when that happens* (honestly)
Box 3: *Who usually sees that reaction?* (your team, specific people)
Box 4: *What I wish I did instead*

Give 7 minutes for individual reflection. Play soft background music if possible.

Then:

> "You don't need to share this with anyone. But I'd like two or three people who are comfortable to share — not the trigger itself, but what they noticed about the gap between Box 2 and Box 4."

Take shares. Affirm every contribution:
- *"That gap you described — that's self-awareness. That's exactly what we're building today."*
- *"The fact that you can name it means you can work with it."*

**Closing the segment:**

> "Here's what I want you to hold on to from this morning: bias is not about being a bad person. Lack of social intelligence isn't a moral failing. Having triggers is human. What we're doing today is building the gap between stimulus and response — because that gap is where your leadership actually lives."

Show Slide 14.

> "We'll break for lunch in a few hours. Before then, we're going to go deeper — into empathy. Into actually understanding what it feels like to be a woman on your floor. That's where the real work begins."

## FACILITATOR TIPS — PART 1

**If the room is quiet and resistant (common with senior DHs):**
Don't push. Say: *"I know some of you are seasoned managers — and you may have seen training like this come and go. What I ask is: stay curious for just this morning. If nothing lands for you, we'll talk. But give it a chance."*

**If someone says "this is just political correctness":**
Don't argue. Say: *"That's a fair frustration. What I'd ask is — what's the cost of getting this wrong? Legally, personally, operationally. That's not politics. That's risk management."*

**If someone shares something that's clearly a real complaint:**
Acknowledge warmly, redirect gently: *"That sounds like a real situation that deserves proper attention — through HR or your ICC. What we're doing here is building the tools so those situations happen less. Can we hold that specific case and come back to the principle?"*

**If someone is very engaged and dominates:**
Thank them, include others: *"Really valuable — and I want to make sure we hear from some of the quieter voices in the room. [Name], what's your take?"*

**On timing:** Module 1 can run long if discussion is rich. That's a good sign — don't cut the conversation, trim the lecture. The self-check at 9:55 can be shortened to 3 minutes if needed.
